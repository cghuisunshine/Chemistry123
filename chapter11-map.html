11.1
A Molecular Comparison of Gases, Liquids, and Solids
States of matter
R
Roots ‚Äì Core ideas & definitions
How kinetic energy competes with intermolecular attractions.
‚ñ∂

The physical state of a substance is controlled by a balance between the kinetic energy of its particles and the intermolecular attractions holding them together.

  • Gases: molecules are far apart, move freely and randomly, and have kinetic energies much larger than intermolecular attractions. They fill the container, are highly compressible, have low densities, and diffuse rapidly.
  • Liquids: molecules are close together but can slide past one another. Intermolecular forces are strong enough to give a definite volume and low compressibility, but not strong enough to lock molecules in place. Liquids flow and diffuse slowly.
  • Solids: molecules, ions, or atoms are packed closely into fixed positions with only vibrational motion. Intermolecular attractions dominate, giving a rigid shape, definite volume, very low compressibility, and extremely slow diffusion.
  • Condensed phases: liquids and solids are both considered condensed phases because their particles are close together compared with gases.
  • Changing temperature or pressure can shift the balance between kinetic energy and attractions, driving changes between gas, liquid, and solid.

Intermolecular Forces
IMFs & structure‚Äìproperty links
R
Roots ‚Äì Core ideas & definitions
Types of intermolecular forces and their origin.
‚ñ∂
  • Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are attractions between separate molecules; they are much weaker than the covalent or ionic bonds within molecules.
  • All IMFs are ultimately electrostatic‚Äîthey involve attractions between positive and negative charges or partial charges.
  • Dispersion forces (London forces) arise from temporary distortions of electron clouds that create instantaneous dipoles and induce dipoles in neighbors. They operate in all molecules and atoms.
  • Polarizability measures how easily a cloud of electrons can be distorted. More electrons and more diffuse electron clouds mean higher polarizability and stronger dispersion forces.
  • Dipole‚Äìdipole interactions occur between polar molecules with permanent dipole moments; Œ¥‚Å∫ on one molecule attracts Œ¥‚Åª on another.
  • Hydrogen bonding is a strong special case of dipole‚Äìdipole attraction when H is bonded to N, O, or F and a nearby N, O, or F has a lone pair.
  • Ion‚Äìdipole forces are attractions between ions and polar molecules, crucial for dissolving ionic compounds in polar solvents.
  • For molecules with similar sizes, the ordering of IMF strength is roughly: dispersion < dipole‚Äìdipole < hydrogen bonding; ion‚Äìdipole forces can be stronger still.

Intermolecular forces are weaker than ionic or covalent bonds because the interacting charges are smaller and spaced further apart, but the same basic electrostatic idea applies.

  • q‚ÇÅ, q‚ÇÇ are interacting charges (full ionic charges in salts, small partial charges in molecular dipoles).
  • r is the distance between charges.

Example 1 ‚Äì Dispersion and molecular shape

n‚ÄëPentane and neopentane both have formula C‚ÇÖH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇ, but n‚Äëpentane has a higher boiling point. The long, linear shape of n‚Äëpentane allows more surface contact between molecules, strengthening dispersion forces, while neopentane is compact and nearly spherical, reducing contact area.

For group 14 hydrides (CH‚ÇÑ ‚Üí SnH‚ÇÑ), boiling points increase smoothly with molar mass. 11.3
Select Properties of Liquids
Viscosity, surface tension, capillarity
R
Roots ‚Äì Key properties
Viscosity, surface tension, cohesive vs adhesive forces.
‚ñ∂
  • Viscosity is a liquid‚Äôs resistance to flow. Thick liquids like honey or motor oil have high viscosity; water and gasoline have low viscosity.
  • Viscosity increases with stronger intermolecular forces and with longer, more flexible molecules that can tangle.
  • Viscosity decreases as temperature increases because particles move faster and can overcome attractions more easily.
  • Surface tension is the energy required to increase a liquid‚Äôs surface area by a unit amount. It reflects the net inward pull experienced by surface molecules.
  • Stronger IMFs (especially hydrogen bonding) give larger surface tension. Water‚Äôs surface tension is high; mercury‚Äôs is even higher.
  • Cohesive forces are IMFs between molecules of the same substance. Adhesive forces are attractions between a liquid and another surface.
  • If adhesion > cohesion, the liquid wets and climbs the surface (concave meniscus, like water in glass). If cohesion > adhesion, the liquid beads up (convex meniscus, like mercury in glass).
  • Competition between adhesion, cohesion, and gravity produces capillary action, where liquids rise or fall in narrow tubes.

Example 1 ‚Äì Viscosity in a hydrocarbon series

At 20 ¬∞C, hexane, heptane, octane, nonane, and decane show steadily increasing viscosities. Each step adds CH‚ÇÇ units, increasing molar mass and surface area. Stronger dispersion forces and greater ‚Äúspaghetti-like‚Äù tangling of chains slow flow, so viscosity rises.

Example 2 ‚Äì Surface tension and insects on water

Water striders can stand on ponds because water‚Äôs surface behaves like a stretched elastic film. Surface molecules are pulled inward by neighbors, and the insect‚Äôs mass is not enough to break those hydrogen-bond-supported forces.

Example 3 ‚Äì Capillary action in everyday life

In a narrow glass capillary, water rises above the bulk level: adhesion between water and glass plus surface tension pulls the liquid up until gravity balances the upward force. This same process allows paper towels and technical wicking fabrics to draw moisture away from surfaces.

11.4
Phase Changes
Energy & state changes
R
Roots ‚Äì Names & energy directions
Melting, freezing, vaporization, condensation, sublimation, deposition.
‚ñ∂
  • Melting (fusion): solid ‚Üí liquid; endothermic (system absorbs heat to allow particles to move more freely).
  • Freezing: liquid ‚Üí solid; exothermic (system releases heat as particles lock into place).
  • Vaporization: liquid ‚Üí gas; endothermic (particles must break most intermolecular attractions).
  • Condensation: gas ‚Üí liquid; exothermic (particles form attractions and release energy).
  • Sublimation: solid ‚Üí gas; endothermic (solid skips the liquid stage and directly forms gas).
  • Deposition: gas ‚Üí solid; exothermic (gas directly forms solid).
  • On a heating curve, temperatures rise with added heat until a phase-change temperature is reached. During the phase change, temperature stays constant while energy goes into changing the arrangement of particles, not their average kinetic energy.
  • Critical temperature (Tc): highest T at which a liquid can exist; above Tc, no amount of pressure produces a separate liquid phase.
  • Critical pressure (Pc): pressure needed to liquefy a gas at Tc.
  • Above Tc and Pc, the substance is a supercritical fluid, with no distinct boundary between liquid and gas.

At temperatures above Tc, molecules move so energetically that even very high pressures cannot pull them into a separate liquid; instead, the system becomes a dense supercritical fluid. 11.5
Vapor Pressure
Volatility & boiling
R
Roots ‚Äì Vapor‚Äìliquid equilibrium
Evaporation, condensation, and volatility.
‚ñ∂
  • Evaporation: molecules at the surface of a liquid that have enough kinetic energy escape into the gas phase.
  • Condensation: gas molecules lose energy and return to the liquid phase.
  • In a closed container, these processes reach dynamic equilibrium where rates of evaporation and condensation are equal.
  • The pressure of the vapor at this equilibrium is the liquid‚Äôs vapor pressure at that temperature.
  • A liquid with a high vapor pressure at a given temperature is called volatile; it evaporates readily.
  • Stronger IMFs hold molecules in the liquid more tightly, giving a lower vapor pressure; weaker IMFs give a higher vapor pressure.
  • Vapor pressure increases rapidly with temperature because more molecules have energies above the escape threshold.
  • Boiling point: the temperature at which the vapor pressure equals the external pressure acting on the liquid.
  • Normal boiling point: boiling point at 101.3 kPa (1 atm). Changing external pressure changes the boiling point.
T

  • Graphing ln P vs 1/T gives a straight line whose slope is ‚ÄìŒîHvap/R, so the slope‚Äôs magnitude tells you the enthalpy of vaporization.

Example 1 ‚Äì Pressure cookers

In a pressure cooker, steam is trapped so that the pressure above boiling water rises above 1 atm. The higher pressure shifts the boiling point upward, so the water can reach temperatures above 100 ¬∞C. Food cooks faster because chemical reactions run more quickly at higher temperature.

Example 2 ‚Äì High-altitude cooking

At high elevations, atmospheric pressure is below 101.3 kPa. Water‚Äôs vapor pressure equals the lower external pressure at a temperature below 100 ¬∞C, so water boils at a lower temperature. That means boiling water is not as hot and cooking times for foods like pasta or potatoes must be longer.

Example 3 ‚Äì Comparing volatility: CCl‚ÇÑ vs CBr‚ÇÑ

At 25 ¬∞C, both CCl‚ÇÑ and CBr‚ÇÑ are nonpolar, so dispersion forces dominate. CBr‚ÇÑ has more electrons and is more polarizable, giving stronger dispersion forces and lower vapor pressure. CCl‚ÇÑ is therefore more volatile at 25 ¬∞C.

11.6
Phase Diagrams
T‚ÄìP maps
R
Roots ‚Äì What a phase diagram shows
Regions, curves, triple point, critical point.
‚ñ∂
  • A phase diagram plots pressure vs temperature and shows which phase (solid, liquid, gas, supercritical fluid) is stable under each combination.
  • The diagram includes three key curves:
    • Sublimation curve: boundary between solid and gas where these phases are in equilibrium.
    • Fusion (melting) curve: boundary between solid and liquid.
    • Vaporization curve: boundary between liquid and gas (this is essentially the vapor pressure curve).
  • The triple point is the unique combination of T and P where solid, liquid, and gas coexist in equilibrium.
  • The critical point marks the end of the liquid‚Äìgas boundary; at and above this point, the substance is a supercritical fluid with no distinct liquid or gas phases.
  • For most substances, the fusion curve slopes upward (melting point increases with pressure), reflecting solids being denser than liquids.
  • Water is unusual: its fusion curve slopes slightly downward because ice is less dense than liquid water, so increasing pressure favors the liquid phase.
T
Trunk ‚Äì How to read and use the diagram
Paths in T‚ÄìP space and phase changes.
‚ñ∂

To use a phase diagram, treat (T, P) as a point moving around on the map:

  • Find the point corresponding to the current conditions; whichever region it lies in tells you the phase.
  • Moving horizontally (changing T at fixed P) or vertically (changing P at fixed T) can cross boundaries; each crossing corresponds to a phase change.
  • At pressures below the triple point pressure, heating a solid can only make it sublime (solid ‚Üí gas); a liquid phase is not stable.
  • If you compress a gas at a temperature below Tc, you eventually hit the vaporization curve and liquefy the gas. Above Tc, compression just gives a denser gas-like supercritical fluid.

Example 1 ‚Äì CO‚ÇÇ and dry ice

In the CO‚ÇÇ phase diagram, the triple point is at a pressure well above 1 atm. At 1 atm, the fusion curve is not accessible, so solid CO‚ÇÇ cannot melt‚Äîit sublimes directly at about ‚Äì78.5 ¬∞C. That is why dry ice goes straight from solid to gas at room pressure.

Example 2 ‚Äì Methane phase changes

At 100 kPa and 0 ¬∞C, methane lies in the gas region. If you start with solid methane at 100 kPa and heat it, the trajectory crosses the solid‚Äìliquid boundary (melting) and then the liquid‚Äìgas boundary (vaporization). If instead you compress methane at 0 ¬∞C to above the critical pressure, you obtain a supercritical fluid without a distinct boiling transition.

Example 3 ‚Äì Water and pressure-induced melting

Because ice is less dense than liquid water, increasing pressure at temperatures near 0 ¬∞C slightly favors melting. This contributes to phenomena like ice skating, where high pressure under a blade promotes a thin film of liquid water that reduces friction, although other factors (like frictional heating) also play roles.

Liquid Crystals
Intermediate phases
R
Roots ‚Äì What liquid crystals are
Between solid and liquid, with partial order.
‚ñ∂
  • Liquid crystals are phases that have some order like a solid but flow like a liquid; they occur over limited temperature ranges between the solid and liquid phases.
  • They often appear as viscous, milky liquids in the liquid crystalline region and clear liquids above it.
  • Molecules that form liquid crystals are usually rod‚Äëshaped and somewhat rigid (e.g., multiple double bonds and benzene rings), with polar groups that promote alignment.
  • Because the intermolecular forces organizing the molecules are relatively weak, modest changes in temperature, pressure, or electric field can reorient them and change the material‚Äôs optical properties.
T
Trunk ‚Äì Types of liquid crystal order
Nematic, smectic, and cholesteric phases.
‚ñ∂
  • Liquid phase: molecules are arranged randomly in position and orientation.
  • Nematic liquid crystal:
    • Long axes of molecules tend to point in the same direction (‚Äúdirector‚Äù), but molecule positions are not layered.
    • Molecules can flow and rotate around their long axes.
  • Smectic A and C liquid crystals:
    • Molecules maintain nematic-like alignment but also organize into layers.
    • Smectic A: long axes are perpendicular to the layer planes.
    • Smectic C: long axes are tilted relative to the layer planes.
  • Cholesteric (chiral nematic) liquid crystals:
    • Form layers where the orientation of molecules twists from one layer to the next, creating a helical structure.
    • This periodic structure interacts with light, often producing vivid colors that change with temperature or viewing angle.

Leaves ‚Äì Examples & technology
From cholesteryl benzoate to LCDs and e-paper.
‚ñ∂

Example 1 ‚Äì Cholesteryl benzoate

Cholesteryl benzoate melts from a solid to a milky, viscous liquid-crystalline phase at an intermediate temperature, and then becomes a clear liquid at a higher temperature. On cooling, it retraces these transitions in reverse. This behavior revealed the existence of liquid crystalline phases distinct from both solid and ordinary liquid states.

Example 2 ‚Äì Twisted nematic LCDs

In a twisted nematic display, a layer of nematic liquid crystal sits between two polarizers oriented at right angles. With no voltage, the liquid crystal twists the polarization of light so it passes both polarizers and is reflected, making the pixel appear bright. When a voltage is applied, the molecules align with the electric field and no longer twist the light, so the second polarizer blocks it and the pixel appears dark.

Example 3 ‚Äì Cholesteric liquid crystals and e-paper

Cholesteric liquid crystals reflect particular wavelengths of light depending on their pitch (twist period) and orientation. Small changes in temperature or electric fields can tune these properties. This makes them useful for temperature sensors, skin thermometers, and low-power ‚Äúelectronic paper‚Äù displays that can hold text or images with very little energy.
