Chapter 16 Tutorial — Acid–Base Equilibria

16.1 Acid–Base Equilibria

Key ideas

  • Acids donate H⁺ and bases accept H⁺ in the Brønsted-Lowry sense; water can act as either.
  • Every acid–base reaction is an equilibrium between protonated and deprotonated species.
  • Strong acids/bases have equilibria that lie far to one side (complete dissociation).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Acid dissociation: K_a = [H₃O⁺][A⁻]/[HA]. Large K_a indicates a strong acid.

Short example(s)

HCl dissociates almost entirely in water so its K_a is huge and [H⁺] ≈ initial acid concentration.

16.2 The Autoionization of Water

Key ideas

  • Water autoionizes: 2 H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻, establishing baseline hydronium and hydroxide concentrations.
  • K_w depends on temperature, so “neutral pH” is not always exactly 7.00 except at 25 °C.
  • In any aqueous solution, if you know [H₃O⁺] you can find [OH⁻] from K_w (and vice versa).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Ion-product constant: K_w = [H₃O⁺][OH⁻] ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25 °C.
  • Neutral condition: In pure water, [H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻], so each equals √K_w (at 25 °C that’s 1.0×10⁻⁷ M).

Short example(s)

Neutral water has [H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M, leading to pH 7.

16.3 The pH Scale

Key ideas

  • pH measures acidity: pH = −log[H₃O⁺], and pOH = −log[OH⁻].
  • At 25 °C, pH + pOH = 14, so knowing one fixes the other.
  • Because pH is logarithmic, it is best treated with significant figures: the number of decimal places in pH matches the number of significant figures in [H₃O⁺].

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Relationship: [H₃O⁺] = 10^{-pH}; a change of 1 pH unit equals a tenfold change in [H₃O⁺].
  • Convert via K_w: [OH⁻] = K_w/[H₃O⁺] (useful when you find one ion concentration first).

Short example(s)

A solution with [H₃O⁺] = 1 × 10⁻³ M has pH 3 and is acidic.

16.4 Strong Acids and Bases

Key ideas

  • Strong acids/bases dissociate completely, so equilibrium lies nearly all the way to products.
  • For strong acids, treat [H₃O⁺] as the analytical concentration after dilution/mixing; for strong bases, treat [OH⁻] similarly.
  • In very dilute solutions, water’s own 1.0×10⁻⁷ M contribution can matter; otherwise it is negligible.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Strong acid: [H₃O⁺] ≈ [acid]_initial, so pH is calculated directly from concentration.
  • Strong base: [OH⁻] ≈ [base]_initial, then pOH = −log[OH⁻] and pH = 14 − pOH at 25 °C.

Short example(s)

0.10 M HNO₃ gives pH 1.

16.5 Weak Acids

Key ideas

  • Weak acids partially dissociate; equilibrium constants and ICE tables determine [H⁺].
  • The weaker the acid (smaller K_a), the smaller the fraction that ionizes, so pH stays closer to neutral for the same formal concentration.
  • Always check the “small‑x” assumption (percent ionization); if it’s not small, solve the quadratic.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Weak acid: K_a = x²/([HA]_0 − x); with small K_a, approximate x ≪ [HA]_0 to get [H₃O⁺] ≈ √(K_a·[HA]_0).
  • Percent ionization: % ionization = ([H₃O⁺]_eq/[HA]_0)×100%; a quick diagnostic for approximation validity.

Short example(s)

0.10 M acetic acid (`K_a ≈ 1.8 × 10⁻⁵`) gives [H⁺] ≈ 1.34 × 10⁻³ M and pH ≈ 2.87.

16.6 Weak Bases

Key ideas

  • Weak bases partially hydrolyze; treat analogously to weak acids but use K_b and solve for [OH⁻].
  • After you find [OH⁻], convert to pOH then pH using pH + pOH = 14 (at 25 °C).
  • Conjugate relationships connect acid and base strength: weak bases have strong conjugate acids and vice versa.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Weak base: K_b = x²/([B]_0 − x); for small K_b, take [OH⁻] ≈ √(K_b·[B]_0).
  • Conjugates: K_aK_b = K_w for a conjugate acid–base pair at the same temperature.

Short example(s)

0.10 M NH₃ (`K_b ≈ 1.8×10⁻⁵`) yields [OH⁻] ≈ 1.34×10⁻³ M, pOH ≈ 2.87, and pH ≈ 11.13.

16.7 Acid–Base Properties of Salt Solutions

Key ideas

  • Salts of strong acid + strong base are neutral; weak component salts hydrolyze to produce acidic/basic solutions.
  • Identify whether the ions are conjugates of weak acids/bases: conjugate bases of weak acids make solutions basic; conjugate acids of weak bases make solutions acidic.
  • Polyprotic ions (like HCO₃⁻ or HPO₄²⁻) can be amphiprotic, acting as either acid or base depending on what they encounter.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Hydrolysis: For NH₄Cl, NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺ with K_a corresponding to the conjugate acid.
  • Conjugate link: If you know K_a for HA, then K_b = K_w/K_a for A⁻ (useful for basic salts like acetate).

Short example(s)

NaCH₃COO solution is basic because CH₃COO⁻ reacts with water to produce OH⁻.

16.8 Acid–Base Behavior and Chemical Structure

Key ideas

  • Acid strength depends on bond strength/polarity and conjugate-base stability (resonance, inductive effects).
  • Across a period, acidity of H–X tends to increase with electronegativity (more stable conjugate base); down a group, acidity can increase as H–X bonds weaken.
  • For oxyacids, adding electronegative atoms (like O) and electron-withdrawing substituents stabilizes the conjugate base, increasing acidity.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Structural rules: Weaker H–X bond and better charge delocalization in the conjugate base increase acidity.
  • Resonance effect (pattern): More resonance forms for the conjugate base generally means lower energy and therefore a stronger acid.

Short example(s)

HCl is stronger than HF because H–Cl is weaker (longer) and Cl⁻ is better stabilized by larger size.
