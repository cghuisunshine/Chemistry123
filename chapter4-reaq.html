Chapter 4: Reactions in Aqueous Solution

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe what an aqueous solution and an electrolyte are.
  • Predict and write equations for precipitation, acid–base, and redox reactions in water.
  • Write molecular, complete ionic, and net ionic equations.
  • Use molarity and solution stoichiometry to solve quantitative problems.

Key Vocabulary

  • aqueous solution: a homogeneous mixture with water as the solvent.
  • solute: substance being dissolved.
  • solvent: the dissolving medium (here, water).
  • electrolyte: substance that forms ions in water and conducts electricity.
  • nonelectrolyte: dissolves but does not form ions (no conductivity).
  • strong electrolyte: completely forms ions (for example, strong acids, soluble ionic salts).
  • weak electrolyte: partially ionizes (for example, weak acids, weak bases).
  • molecular equation: shows all substances as compounds.
  • complete ionic equation: shows all strong electrolytes as ions.
  • net ionic equation: shows only species that actually change.
  • spectator ions: ions that do not change during the reaction.
  • precipitate: an insoluble solid formed in solution.
  • molarity (M): moles of solute per liter of solution.

1. Aqueous Solutions and Electrolytes

Ionic compounds may dissociate into ions in water (for example, NaCl dissolving into Na+ and Cl−).

Molecular compounds can behave in different ways:

  • Stay as molecules (for example, sugar → nonelectrolyte).
  • Ionize to some extent (for example, acetic acid → weak electrolyte).

Examples:

  • NaCl(s) → NaCl(aq) → Na+(aq) + Cl−(aq): strong electrolyte.
  • HCl(aq) → H+(aq) + Cl−(aq): strong electrolyte (strong acid).
  • NH3(aq) ↔ NH4+(aq) + OH−(aq): weak electrolyte (weak base).
  • C6H12O6(aq) (glucose): nonelectrolyte.

2. Molecular, Complete Ionic, and Net Ionic Equations

Procedure

  1. Write the balanced molecular equation.
  2. Identify strong electrolytes (soluble ionic compounds, strong acids, strong bases) and split them into ions → complete ionic equation.
  3. Cancel spectator ions → net ionic equation.

Example (Precipitation)

Mixing sodium sulfate and barium chloride:

  1. Molecular equation
    Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2 NaCl(aq)
  2. Complete ionic equation
    2 Na+(aq) + SO42−(aq) + Ba2+(aq) + 2 Cl−(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2 Na+(aq) + 2 Cl−(aq)
  3. Net ionic equation (cancel 2 Na+, 2 Cl−)
    Ba2+(aq) + SO42−(aq) → BaSO4(s)

3. Precipitation Reactions and Solubility

A precipitation reaction occurs when two aqueous solutions form an insoluble ionic solid.

Useful solubility guidelines (simplified):

  • Most nitrates (NO3−) and acetates (CH3COO−) are soluble.
  • Most alkali metal (Li+, Na+, K+) and NH4+ salts are soluble.
  • Most chlorides, bromides, iodides are soluble except with Ag+, Pb2+, Hg22+.
  • Most sulfates (SO42−) are soluble except with Ba2+, Pb2+, Ca2+, Sr2+.
  • Most carbonates (CO32−), phosphates (PO43−), sulfides (S2−), hydroxides (OH−) are insoluble except with alkali metals and NH4+ (and some Ba(OH)2, Ca(OH)2 are slightly soluble).

Example

What precipitate forms when KI(aq) and Pb(NO3)2(aq) are mixed? Write the net ionic equation.

  1. Exchange ions:
    2 KI(aq) + Pb(NO3)2(aq) → 2 KNO3(aq) + PbI2(s)
    KNO3 is soluble; PbI2 is insoluble (precipitate).
  2. Complete ionic equation:
    2 K+(aq) + 2 I−(aq) + Pb2+(aq) + 2 NO3−(aq) → 2 K+(aq) + 2 NO3−(aq) + PbI2(s)
  3. Net ionic equation:
    Pb2+(aq) + 2 I−(aq) → PbI2(s)

4. Acid–Base Reactions (Neutralization)

Arrhenius definitions:

  • Acid → produces H+ in water (for example, HCl).
  • Base → produces OH− in water (for example, NaOH).

Strong acids (for example, HCl, HNO3, H2SO4) and strong bases (group 1/2 hydroxides) are strong electrolytes.

Neutralization: acid + base → salt + water.

Key pattern (strong acid + strong base)
The net ionic equation is almost always:
H+(aq) + OH−(aq) → H2O(l)

Example (Stoichiometry)

What volume of 0.200 M NaOH is required to neutralize 25.0 mL of 0.100 M H2SO4?

  1. Reaction:
    H2SO4(aq) + 2 NaOH(aq) → Na2SO4(aq) + 2 H2O(l)
  2. Moles of H2SO4:
    n(H2SO4) = 0.100 M × 0.0250 L = 2.50 × 10−3 mol
  3. Stoichiometry: 1 mol H2SO4 needs 2 mol NaOH:
    n(NaOH) = 2 × 2.50 × 10−3 = 5.00 × 10−3 mol
  4. Volume of NaOH:
    V = n / M = (5.00 × 10−3 mol) / (0.200 mol L−1) = 0.0250 L = 25.0 mL

5. Oxidation–Reduction (Redox) Reactions

  • Oxidation: loss of electrons (oxidation state increases).
  • Reduction: gain of electrons (oxidation state decreases).
  • Oxidizing agent: gets reduced.
  • Reducing agent: gets oxidized.

Simple rules for oxidation numbers:

  • Free element: 0 (for example, Zn(s), O2(g)).
  • Monatomic ion: equals its charge (Na+ → +1, Cl− → −1).
  • Oxygen: usually −2 in compounds (except peroxides).
  • Hydrogen: +1 with nonmetals, −1 with metals.
  • Sum of oxidation numbers = overall charge of species.

Example

Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)

Oxidation states:

  • Zn(s): 0 → Zn2+: +2 (oxidized, reducing agent).
  • Cu2+: +2 → Cu(s): 0 (reduced, oxidizing agent).

Net ionic equation:
Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) → Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)

6. Concentration of Solutions (Molarity and Dilution)

Molarity: M = n / V (n in moles, V in liters).

Moles: n = M × V

Dilution (no reaction, just adding solvent): M1V1 = M2V2

Example (Molarity from Mass)

Prepare 250.0 mL of 0.500 M NaCl solution. How many grams of NaCl?

  1. Moles needed: n = M × V = 0.500 mol L−1 × 0.2500 L = 0.125 mol.
  2. Molar mass NaCl ≈ 58.44 g mol−1.
  3. Mass = n × Mr = 0.125 mol × 58.44 g mol−1 ≈ 7.30 g.

Example (Dilution)

How many mL of 1.00 M HCl are needed to make 500.0 mL of 0.100 M HCl?

Use M1V1 = M2V2:

1.00 × V1 = 0.100 × 0.500 → V1 = 0.0500 L = 50.0 mL.

7. Solution Stoichiometry

General strategy for reactions in solution:

  1. Start from given volume and molarity → moles.
  2. Use the balanced equation (mole ratio) to find moles of the desired substance.
  3. Convert moles to grams, volume, or molarity, as asked.

Example (Precipitation Stoichiometry)

What mass of BaSO4(s) forms when 50.0 mL of 0.200 M BaCl2 is mixed with excess Na2SO4?

  1. Reaction:
    BaCl2(aq) + Na2SO4(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2 NaCl(aq)
  2. Moles BaCl2: n = 0.200 M × 0.0500 L = 0.0100 mol.
  3. Mole ratio BaCl2 : BaSO4 is 1:1 → n(BaSO4) = 0.0100 mol.
  4. Molar mass BaSO4 ≈ 233.4 g mol−1.
  5. Mass BaSO4 = 0.0100 mol × 233.4 g mol−1 ≈ 2.33 g.

8. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

Try these problems. You can check your work with a teacher or tutor, or ask for detailed solutions.

  1. Classify each as strong electrolyte, weak electrolyte, or nonelectrolyte in water:
    (a) KBr    (b) HF    (c) C2H5OH (ethanol)    (d) Na2SO4
  2. Predict whether a precipitate forms when solutions of Na2CO3(aq) and CaCl2(aq) are mixed.
    If yes, give the balanced molecular and net ionic equations.
  3. Write molecular, complete ionic, and net ionic equations for the reaction between HNO3(aq) and KOH(aq).
  4. A 25.0 mL sample of 0.150 M NaOH is completely neutralized by HCl.
    (a) How many moles of HCl reacted?
    (b) What is the molarity of the HCl if the volume used was 18.5 mL?
  5. In the reaction between Al(s) and Cu2+(aq) to form Al3+(aq) and Cu(s):
    (a) Assign oxidation numbers to all species.
    (b) Identify what is oxidized, what is reduced, and the oxidizing and reducing agents.
  6. What volume of 0.300 M AgNO3 is needed to completely precipitate all Cl− from 50.0 mL of 0.200 M NaCl?
    Reaction: AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO3(aq).
  7. A solution is prepared by dissolving 5.00 g of KNO3 in enough water to make 200.0 mL of solution.
    (a) What is the molarity of KNO3?
    (b) How many moles of K+ ions are present?
