Chapter 4: Reactions in Aqueous Solution

4.1 General Properties of Aqueous Solutions

Key ideas

  • Aqueous solutions consist of solutes dispersed and solvated by polar water molecules; ionic solids dissociate into ions while most molecular solutes remain neutral.
  • Electrolytes carry charge because they yield ions in water: strong electrolytes are essentially fully ionic, weak electrolytes keep most molecules intact, and nonelectrolytes stay molecular.
  • Solvation stabilizes ions and lets them diffuse, which explains why water conducts electricity when ionic compounds dissolve.

Important formulas & their meaning

Weak electrolyte ionization CH3COOH(aq) ⇌ CH3COO- (aq) + H+ (aq)

The half-arrows show that acetic acid molecules are mostly un-ionized but a small fraction dissociates into acetate and hydrogen ions; the forward and reverse processes reach equilibrium so the ion concentrations stay low.

Strong electrolyte ionization HCl(aq) → H+ (aq) + Cl- (aq)

Strong acids like hydrochloric acid dissociate nearly completely, so their aqueous solutions contain ions that freely conduct electricity. The one-way arrow indicates the ionized form is overwhelmingly favored.

Short example(s)

A 0.10 M NaCl solution donates 0.10 M Na+ and 0.10 M Cl- ions to the water, so the total ionic concentration is 0.20 M, explaining why its conductivity is much higher than that of 0.10 M sucrose, which remains molecular.

4.2 Precipitation Reactions

Key ideas

  • When two soluble electrolytes mix, oppositely charged ions can form an insoluble solid (precipitate) whose formation is predicted using solubility rules.
  • Metathesis (exchange) reactions follow AX + BY → AY + BX and are the basis for precipitation and many acid-base reactions.
  • Molecular, complete ionic, and net ionic equations clarify which species change during the reaction; spectator ions are removed in the net ionic form.

Important formulas & their meaning

Lead iodide precipitation Pb(NO3)2(aq) + 2 KI(aq) → PbI2(s) + 2 KNO3(aq)

Lead(II) and iodide ions pair to give the insoluble PbI2 solid; potassium and nitrate stay dissolved. Because PbI2 is a strong precipitate, the reaction is driven by the ion pairing that overcomes solvation.

Net ionic equation Pb2+ (aq) + 2 I- (aq) → PbI2(s)

The net ionic equation retains only the ions that participate in the formation of PbI2, showing how the charges (2+ and two 1-) balance to zero in the neutral solid.

Short example(s)

Mixing Mg(NO3)2 and NaOH yields Mg(OH)2 precipitate because hydroxide ions are generally insoluble except with the alkali metals; sodium nitrate remains aqueous so the net ionic equation is Mg2+ + 2 OH- → Mg(OH)2(s).

4.3 Acids, Bases, and Neutralization Reactions

Key ideas

  • Acids donate protons (H+) and increase H+ concentration; bases accept protons or generate OH- ions, and strong/weak labels follow electrolyte strength.
  • Neutralization between a strong acid and a strong base yields water and a salt and is a special kind of metathesis reaction.
  • Some acid-base reactions produce gases (H2S, CO2) through intermediate species like carbonic acid.

Important formulas & their meaning

Diprotic acid ionization H2SO4(aq) → H+(aq) + HSO4-(aq), HSO4-(aq) ⇌ H+(aq) + SO42-(aq)

Sulfuric acid supplies two protons. The first dissociation is complete (strong acid), while the second is only partial so the second equilibrium arrow shows weak electrolyte behavior.

Neutralization H+(aq) + OH-(aq) → H2O(l)

Any strong acid-base neutralization reduces H+ and OH- to water; the net ionic equation eliminates spectator ions and highlights proton transfer.

Gas-forming neutralization H+(aq) + HCO3-(aq) → H2O(l) + CO2(g)

Carbonate and bicarbonate react with acids to form carbonic acid, which quickly decomposes to water and gaseous CO2, producing fizz in antacid tablets.

Short example(s)

Adding Ba(OH)2 to acetic acid produces Ba(CH3COO)2 and water: 2 CH3COOH + Ba(OH)2 → 2 H2O + Ba(CH3COO)2. Breaking soluble species into ions leaves the net ionic equation CH3COOH + OH- → H2O + CH3COO-.

4.4 Oxidation–Reduction Reactions

Key ideas

  • Redox reactions transfer electrons: oxidation increases the oxidation state (loss of electrons), reduction decreases it (gain of electrons).
  • Oxidation numbers assign hypothetical charges according to established rules, letting us track which atoms change charge during a reaction.
  • Activity series rank metals by ease of oxidation, predicting whether a metal will reduce another ion or react with acids.

Important formulas & their meaning

Oxidation by oxygen 2 Ca(s) + O2(g) → 2 CaO(s)

Calcium atoms go from 0 to +2 (oxidation), while O2 is reduced to O2- ions; the electron transfer is the essential redox process in formation of CaO.

Metal + acid Mg(s) + 2 HCl(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)

Magnesium oxidizes to Mg2+, and H+ reduces to H2 gas; Cl- ions are spectators. The net ionic form is Mg + 2 H+ → Mg2+ + H2.

Short example(s)

Iron metal reacts with nickel(II) salts because Fe is higher in the activity series than Ni. The net ionic equation Fe + Ni2+ → Fe2+ + Ni shows iron oxidized while nickel ions become metal.

4.5 Concentrations of Solutions

Key ideas

  • Molarity (M) expresses concentration as moles of solute per liter of solution and allows conversion between moles, mass, and volume.
  • Ionic compounds contribute multiple ions; the molar concentration of each ion equals the molarity of the dissolved compound multiplied by the stoichiometric coefficient.
  • Dilution conserves solute moles (M_i V_i = M_f V_f), so adding solvent lowers molarity while maintaining the solute quantity.

Important formulas & their meaning

Molarity M = moles solute / liters solution

Dividing the amount of solute by the total solution volume (in liters) gives the concentration. For example, dissolving 0.165 mol Na2SO4 in 0.125 L yields 1.32 M.

Dilution Mconc Vconc = Mdil Vdil

The moles of solute before and after dilution are equal, so molarity times volume stays constant. This lets you calculate the volume of stock solution needed to reach a target concentration.

Short example(s)

To make 250 mL of 0.100 M CuSO4 from a 1.00 M stock, you need Vconc = (0.100×250 mL)/1.00 = 25.0 mL; diluting this volume to 250 mL preserves the total moles of CuSO4.

4.6 Solution Stoichiometry and Chemical Analysis

Key ideas

  • Stoichiometric calculations involving solutions combine molarity and molar volume to convert between moles of solutes.
  • Titrations compare a solution of unknown concentration with a standard solution until a stoichiometric equivalence point is reached, often indicated by an acid-base indicator.
  • Precipitation or gas-forming titrations use balanced equations to relate reagent volumes to analyte quantities.

Important formulas & their meaning

Stoichiometric chain for solutions V · M → mol → mol₂ → V₂ · M₂

You convert volume and molarity of a known solution to moles, use mole ratios to get moles of the unknown, and then convert back to volume or mass using the final solute’s molarity or molar mass.

Standard titration result M₁V₁/n₁ = M₂V₂/n₂

When two solutions react according to stoichiometric coefficients n₁ and n₂, the numbers of moles at the equivalence point satisfy this ratio; use it to find the unkown molarity or volume.

Short example(s)

Titrating 20.0 mL of unknown NaOH with 45.7 mL of 0.500 M H2SO4 (where 1 mol H2SO4 reacts with 2 mol NaOH) gives mol NaOH = (0.0457×0.500×2) = 0.0457 mol; dividing by 0.0200 L yields [NaOH] = 2.28 M.
