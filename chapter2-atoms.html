Chapter 2: Atoms, Molecules, and Ions

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe the basic structure of the atom and define atomic number and mass number.
  • Relate isotopes to average atomic mass.
  • Write and name simple ionic and molecular (covalent) compounds.
  • Recognize common polyatomic ions and use them in formulas.

Key Vocabulary

  • Atom: smallest unit of an element that retains its chemical identity.
  • Atomic number (Z): number of protons in the nucleus.
  • Mass number (A): total number of protons and neutrons.
  • Isotopes: atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons.
  • Ion: charged particle formed by gaining or losing electrons.
  • Cation: positively charged ion (loss of electrons).
  • Anion: negatively charged ion (gain of electrons).
  • Polyatomic ion: a charged group of covalently bonded atoms (for example, NO3−).

1. Subatomic Particles and Isotopes

  • Protons: +1 charge, mass ~1 amu, in nucleus.
  • Neutrons: 0 charge, mass ~1 amu, in nucleus.
  • Electrons: −1 charge, tiny mass, in space around nucleus.
  • Notation: ^{A}_{Z}X. Neutrons = A − Z.
  • Average atomic mass is a weighted average of isotopic masses using natural abundances.

2. The Periodic Table and Groups

  • Columns (groups) often share similar properties (for example, alkali metals, halogens, noble gases).
  • Main-group metals tend to form cations; nonmetals often form anions.

3. Ionic and Molecular Compounds

Formulas

  • Ionic compounds: cations + anions in ratios that give net charge 0 (for example, Na+ and Cl− → NaCl).
  • Use subscripts to balance charges (for example, Ca2+ and Cl− → CaCl2).

Naming

  • Binary ionic: name cation, then anion with -ide ending (for example, sodium chloride).
  • Transition metals: use Roman numerals for charge (for example, FeCl3 is iron(III) chloride).
