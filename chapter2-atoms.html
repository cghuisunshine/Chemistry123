Chapter 2: Atoms, Molecules, and Ions

2.1 The Atomic Theory of Matter

Key ideas

  • Dalton’s atomic theory explains the laws of chemical combination by asserting that each element consists of tiny, indivisible atoms and that compounds form when atoms combine in fixed ratios.
  • The law of constant composition says that a given compound always contains the same relative numbers of atoms (and therefore the same mass proportions).
  • The law of multiple proportions states that when the same two elements form different compounds, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are whole-number multiples.

Important formulas & their meaning

Law of multiple proportions mass of B in compound 1 ÷ mass of B in compound 2 = whole-number ratio

When hydrogen and oxygen form water and hydrogen peroxide, the mass of oxygen per gram of hydrogen is 8.0 in H2O and 16.0 in H2O2, so the ratio 16.0/8.0 = 2 is a small whole number. This pattern arises because atoms combine in simple numerical ratios, reinforcing Dalton’s postulates.

Short example(s)

Water (H2O) and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) both contain hydrogen and oxygen, yet their masses of oxygen per gram of hydrogen differ by a factor of two, showing that atoms pack in distinct whole-number ratios.

2.2 The Discovery of Atomic Structure

Key ideas

  • Cathode-ray experiments revealed negatively charged electrons, showing atoms contain smaller particles.
  • Millikan’s oil-drop experiment measured the charge of a single electron, which allowed the charge-to-mass ratio to yield the electron’s mass.
  • Alpha-particle scattering led Rutherford to conclude that an atom’s mass and positive charge are concentrated in a tiny nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space.

Important formulas & their meaning

Charge-to-mass for the electron e/m = 1.76 × 108 C/g

Thomson measured the ratio of the electron’s charge e to its mass m by deflecting cathode rays with electric and magnetic fields. Because the ratio is known and Millikan later measured e, we can compute the tiny mass of the electron, confirming that atoms contain extremely lightweight particles.

Coulomb’s law F = k Q1 Q2 / d2

The force F between two charges is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Here k ≈ 8.99 × 109 N·m²/C², Q1 and Q2 are charges in coulombs, and d is separation in meters. Coulomb’s law explains why electrons cluster around a positive nucleus and why opposite charges attract.

Short example(s)

Millikan found that oil drops carried charges that were always multiples of 1.602 × 10−19 C. Knowing this fundamental charge and Thomson’s charge-to-mass ratio let scientists deduce that an electron has mass ≈ 9.11 × 10−31 kg.

2.3 The Modern View of Atomic Structure

Key ideas

  • Atoms have a tiny, massive nucleus containing protons and neutrons, while electrons occupy the surrounding space.
  • Atomic number (Z) counts protons and fixes the element; mass number (A) equals protons plus neutrons and defines the isotope.
  • Atoms are electrically neutral because the number of electrons equals the number of protons.
  • Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons; they may differ in stability but share chemical behavior.

Important formulas & their meaning

Mass number A = Z + N

The mass number A equals the number of protons Z plus neutrons N. It appears as the superscript in isotopic notation (e.g., 35Cl). This lets you identify which isotope you are using when calculating average atomic masses.

Short example(s)

Titanium with 22 protons and 26 neutrons has mass number 48 and is written as 48Ti. If that ion carries a 3+ charge (three fewer electrons), its symbol becomes 48Ti³⁺, which is critical when writing formulas for ionic compounds.

2.4 Atomic Weights

Key ideas

  • Atoms are assigned masses in atomic mass units (u), where 1 u = 1.66054 × 10−24 g and 12C is defined to weigh exactly 12 u.
  • Most elements consist of isotopic mixtures, so their reported atomic weight is an average weighted by isotopic abundance.
  • Mass spectrometers separate ions by mass-to-charge ratio, providing the precise isotope masses needed to compute atomic weights.

Important formulas & their meaning

Atomic weight Atomic weight = ∑ (isotope mass × fractional abundance)

This weighted average adds each isotope’s mass multiplied by its natural abundance (expressed as a decimal). Chlorine, for example, averages 0.7578 × 34.969 u + 0.2422 × 36.966 u = 35.45 u, which lies between the isotope masses.

Short example(s)

Because chlorine is about 76% 35Cl and 24% 37Cl, its atomic weight is closer to 35 than 37. That average explains why chlorine’s periodic table entry lists 35.45 instead of a whole number.

2.5 The Periodic Table

Key ideas

  • Elements are arranged by increasing atomic number; repeating patterns in properties define the periodic law.
  • Groups (vertical columns) contain elements with similar valence electron configurations and predictable chemistry.
  • The stepped line divides metals (left, often forming cations) from nonmetals (right, often forming anions).
  • Periods (rows) mark the filling of successive electron shells.

Important formulas & their meaning

Section 2.5 explains the structure and predictive power of the periodic table rather than numerical formulas.

Short example(s)

Lithium, sodium, and potassium sit just below the noble gases in each period and behave as soft, reactive metals. They all form 1+ cations and share similar reactivity patterns, which is why the periodic table groups them together.

2.6 Molecules and Molecular Compounds

Key ideas

  • Molecular formulas show exact numbers of atoms, while empirical formulas show only the simplest ratios.
  • Structural, ball-and-stick, and space-filling models help visualize how atoms connect and how molecules pack.
  • Molecules composed purely of nonmetals are called molecular compounds, and some elements exist as diatomic molecules (H2, O2, N2, halogens).

Important formulas & their meaning

Empirical vs. molecular formulas n = molar mass ÷ empirical formula mass

The molecular formula equals the empirical formula multiplied by an integer n. Determining n requires the molar mass (from experiments such as vapor density) divided by the empirical mass. For example, glucose has empirical formula CH2O and molar mass 180 g/mol, so n = 180 ÷ 30 = 6, giving the molecular formula C6H12O6.

Short example(s)

If an analysis yields an empirical formula HO (ratios 1:1) but the molar mass is already 34 g/mol (twice the empirical mass 17), then the molecular formula must be H2O2, not HO, showing why additional data beyond empirical formulas matters.

2.7 Ions and Ionic Compounds

Key ideas

  • Ions form when atoms gain or lose electrons—metals tend to lose electrons (cations), nonmetals tend to gain them (anions).
  • Cations and anions combine so that total positive charge equals total negative charge, producing electrically neutral ionic compounds.
  • Polyatomic ions such as NH4+ and NO3− behave as single charged units.

Important formulas & their meaning

Charge balance rule total positive charge = total negative charge

The subscripts in the empirical formula result from balancing charges. For Mg2+ and N3−, you need three Mg2+ ions (total +6) and two N3− ions (total −6), producing Mg3N2 so the compound is neutral.

Short example(s)

NaCl forms by Na losing an electron to become Na+ and Cl gaining one to become Cl−; their opposite charges attract and yield the ionic solid sodium chloride.

2.8 Naming Inorganic Compounds

Key ideas

  • Cations usually take the metal name; transition metals with variable charges require Roman numerals (e.g., iron(III) for Fe3+).
  • Anions derived from single elements end with -ide (oxide, chloride); oxygen-containing anions end with -ate or -ite, and prefixes per-/hypo- adjust for extra or fewer oxygens.
  • Acid naming links to the anion: -ide becomes hydro-...-ic acid, -ate becomes ...-ic acid, -ite becomes ...-ous acid, and oxyanion prefixes stay with the acid name.
  • Binary molecular compounds use Greek prefixes (mono-, di-, tri-, etc.) to show atom counts; the more metallic element is named first.

Important formulas & their meaning

This section covers nomenclature rules rather than numerical equations, but the rules themselves act like formulas for translating between names and formulas.

Short example(s)

K2SO4 is potassium sulfate because the cation is potassium (K+) and the anion is sulfate (SO42−). HNO3 is nitric acid because it derives from the nitrate ion (NO3−), and COCl2 becomes carbonyl dichloride using prefixes for the two chlorine atoms.

2.9 Some Simple Organic Compounds

Key ideas

  • Organic chemistry studies carbon-containing compounds; hydrocarbons consist only of carbon and hydrogen.
  • Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons where each carbon bonds to four atoms; their names end in -ane.
  • Alcohols are alcohols have an -OH functional group replacing a hydrogen; the location of the OH (1-propanol vs. 2-propanol) defines different isomers.
  • Isomers share the same molecular formula but differ in how atoms are connected, yielding distinct physical and chemical properties.

Important formulas & their meaning

Alkane general formula CnH2n+2

For a straight-chain alkane with n carbon atoms, there are 2n + 2 hydrogens. Methane (n=1) is CH4, ethane (n=2) is C2H6, and pentane (n=5) is C5H12. This formula shows why increasing carbon length adds more hydrogen in a predictable way.

Short example(s)

Replacing one hydrogen in propane (C3H8) with an OH group gives propanol. If the OH is on an end carbon, the compound is 1-propanol; if it’s on the middle carbon, it’s 2-propanol. These two molecules are structural isomers with identical formulas but different geometries and surface properties.
