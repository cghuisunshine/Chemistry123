Chapter 2: Atoms, Molecules, and Ions

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe the structure of the atom in terms of protons, neutrons, and electrons.
  • Use atomic number and mass number to determine the number of subatomic particles in an atom or ion.
  • Explain what isotopes are and how they lead to average atomic masses on the periodic table.
  • Distinguish between molecular and ionic substances and relate this to their formulas.
  • Write and name simple ionic and molecular compounds, including those that contain common polyatomic ions.

Key Vocabulary

  • Atom: smallest unit of an element that retains its chemical identity.
  • Atomic number (Z): number of protons in the nucleus; defines the element.
  • Mass number (A): total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus.
  • Isotopes: atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A).
  • Ion: charged particle formed when atoms gain or lose electrons.
  • Cation: positively charged ion (loss of electrons).
  • Anion: negatively charged ion (gain of electrons).
  • Polyatomic ion: charged group of covalently bonded atoms (for example, NO3−, SO42−).
  • Molecule: a group of atoms held together by covalent bonds.

1. Subatomic Particles and Isotopes

Building Blocks of Atoms

  • Protons: charge +1, mass ≈ 1 amu, located in the nucleus.
  • Neutrons: charge 0, mass ≈ 1 amu, located in the nucleus.
  • Electrons: charge −1, mass ≈ 1/1836 amu, move in the space around the nucleus.

Atomic Notation and Counting Particles

Isotopes are written using mass number and atomic number, for example AZX.

  • Number of protons = Z.
  • Number of neutrons = A − Z.
  • Number of electrons in a neutral atom = Z; in an ion adjust for the charge.

Worked Example 1 – Counting Protons, Neutrons, Electrons

For 3517Cl and 3717Cl, and the ion Cl−, find p, n, and e−.

  1. 3517Cl: p = 17, n = 35 − 17 = 18, e− (neutral) = 17.
  2. 3717Cl: p = 17, n = 37 − 17 = 20, e− (neutral) = 17.
  3. Cl− (any isotope): protons do not change (still 17), electrons = 18 (one extra for the −1 charge).

2. Average Atomic Mass

The atomic mass shown on the periodic table is a weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes:

average mass = Σ(fractional abundance × isotopic mass)

Worked Example 2 – Interpreting Average Atomic Mass

Chlorine has two main isotopes: about 75% 35Cl and 25% 37Cl. Explain why the average atomic mass (≈ 35.45 amu) lies between 35 and 37, but closer to 35.

  1. The average is weighted toward more abundant isotopes.
  2. Since 35Cl is three times as abundant as 37Cl, the average is pulled closer to 35 amu.

3. Periodic Table and Ionic Charges

  • Group 1 metals (alkali metals) commonly form 1+ cations (Na+, K+).
  • Group 2 metals form 2+ cations (Mg2+, Ca2+).
  • Group 17 (halogens) often form 1− anions (Cl−, Br−).
  • Group 16 nonmetals often form 2− anions (O2−, S2−).

Worked Example 3 – Predicting Ion Charges

Predict likely ionic charges for Na, O, and Al in simple binary ionic compounds.

  1. Na is in Group 1 → tends to lose 1 e− → Na+.
  2. O is in Group 16 → tends to gain 2 e− → O2−.
  3. Al is in Group 13 → tends to lose 3 e− → Al3+.

4. Molecular vs Ionic Compounds

  • Ionic compounds:
    • Formed between metals and nonmetals (or with polyatomic ions).
    • Composed of ions in a 3D lattice; overall electrically neutral.
    • High melting points; conduct electricity when molten or dissolved.
  • Molecular (covalent) compounds:
    • Formed between nonmetals.
    • Discrete molecules held together by covalent bonds.
    • Often lower melting/boiling points; do not conduct electricity as pure substances.

5. Formulas and Naming

Ionic Compounds

  • Formula shows the smallest whole-number ratio of ions so that total positive and negative charges balance.
  • For a metal with a fixed charge (main-group metals):
    • Name = metal name + nonmetal with -ide ending (for example, NaCl: sodium chloride).
  • For transition metals with variable charge:
    • Use a Roman numeral in parentheses for the metal charge (for example, FeCl3: iron(III) chloride).
  • Polyatomic ions keep their names: NaNO3 is sodium nitrate; (NH4)2SO4 is ammonium sulfate.

Molecular Compounds

  • Use Greek prefixes for the number of each atom: mono-, di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, etc.
  • Example: CO = carbon monoxide; CO2 = carbon dioxide; N2O5 = dinitrogen pentoxide.

Worked Example 4 – Writing Formulas from Names

Write the formulas for: (a) calcium nitrate, (b) ammonium sulfate, (c) dinitrogen tetroxide.

  1. Calcium nitrate: Ca2+ and NO3− → Ca(NO3)2.
  2. Ammonium sulfate: NH4+ and SO42− → (NH4)2SO4.
  3. Dinitrogen tetroxide: two N atoms and four O atoms → N2O4.

6. Common Polyatomic Ions (Selected)

  • Nitrate: NO3−; nitrite: NO2−.
  • Sulfate: SO42−; sulfite: SO32−.
  • Carbonate: CO32−; bicarbonate (hydrogen carbonate): HCO3−.
  • Phosphate: PO43−.
  • Hydroxide: OH−; ammonium: NH4+.
