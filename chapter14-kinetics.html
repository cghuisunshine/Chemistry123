Chapter 14: Chemical Kinetics

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Define reaction rate and relate it to the change in reactant or product concentration over time.
  • Write and interpret rate laws and reaction orders using experimental rate data.
  • Use integrated rate laws and half‑life expressions for zero‑, first‑, and second‑order reactions (conceptually, with emphasis on first order).
  • Apply collision theory and the Arrhenius equation to relate rate constants to activation energy and temperature.

Key Vocabulary

  • Reaction rate: change in concentration per unit time.
  • Rate law: relationship between rate and reactant concentrations, often determined empirically.
  • Order of reaction: exponent of a concentration term in the rate law (overall order is sum of exponents).
  • Half‑life (t1/2): time required for concentration to fall to half its initial value.
  • Activation energy (Ea): energy barrier that must be overcome for reaction to occur.
  • Arrhenius equation: k = A e^{-E_a/(RT)}.
  • Catalyst: substance that speeds up a reaction without being consumed; lowers effective Ea.

1. Reaction Rates

For a simple reaction A → products, the rate can be expressed as:

\text{rate} = −Δ[A]/Δt

  • Minus sign ensures the rate is positive because [A] decreases.
  • For more complex reactions, stoichiometric factors relate rates of consumption and formation.

2. Rate Laws and Reaction Orders

General form for A → products:

\text{rate} = k[A]^n

  • k is the rate constant (depends on temperature, not on concentrations).
  • n is the order in A; it must be determined from experimental data, not from the balanced equation.

Worked Example 1 – Finding Order from Initial Rates

Suppose doubling [A] doubles the rate, while [B] is held constant. Then the reaction is first order in A. If doubling [A] quadruples the rate, it is second order in A.

3. Integrated Rate Laws and Half‑Life

  • Zero‑order: [A]_t = [A]_0 − kt.
  • First‑order: \ln([A]_t/[A]_0) = −kt; half‑life t_{1/2} = 0.693/k.
  • Second‑order: 1/[A]_t = 1/[A]_0 + kt.

Worked Example 2 – First‑Order Half‑Life

A first‑order reaction has k = 0.025 s−1. What is its half‑life?

  1. Use t_{1/2} = 0.693/k.
  2. t_{1/2} = 0.693 / 0.025 ≈ 27.7\,\text{s}.

4. Collision Theory and Arrhenius Equation

  • Reactions occur when molecules collide with enough energy (≥ Ea) and proper orientation.
  • Arrhenius equation: k = A e^{-E_a/(RT)}.
  • Taking natural logs: \ln k = \ln A − E_a/(RT) → plot ln k vs 1/T gives a straight line with slope −Ea/R.

Effect of Temperature

  • Increasing temperature increases the fraction of molecules with energy ≥ Ea, so k and rate increase.
  • A common rule of thumb: rates roughly double for every 10 °C increase (not exact, just a quick estimate).

5. Catalysis (Qualitative)

  • Catalysts provide an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy.
  • They are not consumed overall; they appear in elementary steps but are regenerated.
  • Biological catalysts (enzymes) are highly specific and operate under mild conditions.

6. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. For A → products, rate data show that doubling [A] doubles the rate while tripling [A] triples the rate. What is the order in A and the general form of the rate law?
  2. A first‑order reaction has k = 0.0100 min−1. (a) What is its half‑life? (b) How much time is needed for 75% of A to react?
  3. Sketch ln[A] vs t and 1/[A] vs t for first‑ and second‑order reactions and indicate which should be linear.
  4. The rate constant doubles when temperature is increased from 300 K to 310 K. What does this suggest qualitatively about the activation energy barrier?
  5. Describe how a catalyst can lower Ea and give one everyday example of catalysis (for example, catalytic converters, enzymes in digestion).
