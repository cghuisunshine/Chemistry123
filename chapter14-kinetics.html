Chapter 14: Chemical Kinetics

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Define reaction rate and express it in terms of reactant or product concentration.
  • Write and interpret rate laws and reaction orders from experimental data.
  • Use integrated rate laws and half‑life expressions for first‑ and second‑order reactions.
  • Apply collision theory and the Arrhenius equation to relate rate constants to temperature.

Key Vocabulary

  • Reaction rate: change in concentration per unit time.
  • Rate law: mathematical relationship between rate and reactant concentrations.
  • Order of reaction: exponent of concentration term in rate law (overall order is sum of exponents).
  • Half‑life: time required for concentration to fall to half its initial value.
  • Activation energy (Ea): energy barrier that must be overcome for reaction to occur.
  • Arrhenius equation: k = A e^{-E_a/(RT)}.
  • Catalyst: substance that speeds a reaction without being consumed.

1. Rate Laws and Reaction Orders

  • General form for A → products: rate = k[A]^n.
  • Determine n and k from initial‑rate data by comparing experiments.
  • Zero‑order: rate independent of [A]; first‑order: rate ∝ [A]; second‑order: rate ∝ [A]2.

2. Integrated Rate Laws and Half‑Life

  • First‑order: ln([A]_t/[A]_0) = −kt; half‑life t_{1/2} = 0.693/k.
  • Second‑order: 1/[A]_t = 1/[A]_0 + kt.

3. Collision Theory and Temperature Dependence

  • Reactions require collisions with enough energy (≥ Ea) and proper orientation.
  • Increasing temperature increases fraction of molecules with sufficient energy, so k and rate increase.
  • Arrhenius plots (ln k vs 1/T) give slope = −Ea/R.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. For A → products, rate data show that doubling [A] doubles the rate. What is the order in A and the general form of the rate law?
  2. A first‑order reaction has k = 0.025 s−1. What is its half‑life?
  3. Sketch ln[A] vs t and 1/[A] vs t for first‑ and second‑order reactions and indicate which should be linear.
  4. The rate constant doubles when temperature is increased from 300 K to 310 K. Qualitatively, what does this say about the activation barrier?
  5. Describe how a catalyst can lower Ea and give one everyday example of catalysis.
