Chapter 6: Electronic Structure of Atoms

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe the wave–particle duality of light and how it leads to quantized energy levels.
  • Explain the Bohr model for hydrogen and its limitations.
  • Define and assign the four quantum numbers (n, l, ml, ms).
  • Sketch and interpret simple s, p, and d orbitals qualitatively.
  • Write ground‑state electron configurations using the Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion, and Hund’s rule.

Key Vocabulary

  • Photon: packet (quantum) of electromagnetic radiation with energy E = hν.
  • Quantization: restriction of a quantity (such as energy) to discrete values.
  • Orbital: region in space where there is a high probability of finding an electron.
  • Principal quantum number (n): energy level (1, 2, 3, …); larger n → higher energy and larger orbital.
  • Angular momentum quantum number (l): shape of orbital (0 = s, 1 = p, 2 = d, 3 = f).
  • Magnetic quantum number (ml): orientation of an orbital (−l … 0 … +l).
  • Spin quantum number (ms): electron spin (+1/2 or −1/2).
  • Aufbau principle: electrons fill lowest‑energy orbitals first.
  • Hund’s rule: electrons fill degenerate orbitals singly with parallel spins before pairing.

1. Light, Energy, and the Bohr Model

Wave–Particle Duality

  • Light behaves like a wave (wavelength λ, frequency ν) and like a particle (photon with energy E = hν).
  • Relationship between wavelength and frequency: c = λν, where c is speed of light.

Bohr Model for Hydrogen

  • Electrons move in circular orbits with specific radii; each orbit corresponds to a quantized energy.
  • Transitions between orbits emit or absorb photons with energy equal to the difference in energy levels.
  • Bohr model explains the line spectrum of hydrogen but fails for multi‑electron atoms.

2. Quantum Numbers and Orbitals

  • Each electron is described by a unique set of four quantum numbers (n, l, ml, ms).
  • Allowed combinations:
    • n = 1, 2, 3, …
    • l = 0, 1, …, n − 1.
    • ml = −l, …, 0, …, +l.
    • ms = +1/2 or −1/2.
  • Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers.

Shapes of Orbitals (Qualitative)

  • s orbitals (l = 0): spherical, centered at the nucleus.
  • p orbitals (l = 1): dumbbell‑shaped, oriented along x, y, or z axes.
  • d orbitals (l = 2): more complex shapes (cloverleaf, etc.).

3. Electron Configurations

Orbital Energy Ordering

Approximate order of filling:

1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f …

Writing Configurations

  • Follow Aufbau: fill from lowest to highest energy.
  • Apply Hund’s rule in p, d, f subshells: put one electron in each orbital with parallel spins before pairing.
  • Use noble‑gas shorthand: start from closest noble gas then add remaining electrons (for example, [Ne] 3s2 3p5 for Cl).

Worked Example 1 – Electron Configuration of Oxygen

Write the ground‑state electron configuration for O (Z = 8).

  1. Order of filling: 1s, 2s, 2p.
  2. Place electrons: 1s2 2s2 2p4 (total 8).
  3. Noble‑gas shorthand: [He] 2s2 2p4.

Worked Example 2 – Electron Configuration of Fe

Write the ground‑state electron configuration and count unpaired electrons for Fe (Z = 26).

  1. Shorthand: [Ar] 4s2 3d6.
  2. In 3d6, follow Hund’s rule: five d orbitals → fill each singly before pairing; 3d6 has four unpaired electrons.

4. Core and Valence Electrons; Periodic Trends

  • Core electrons: inner electrons, not involved directly in bonding.
  • Valence electrons: outer electrons; largely determine chemical behavior.
  • Elements in the same group have similar valence configurations and similar chemistry.

  2. List all possible quantum numbers (n, l, ml) for the 3p subshell.
  3. Write full and noble‑gas electron configurations for: N, Cl, and Br.
  4. How many unpaired electrons are present in the ground‑state electron configuration of: N, O, Fe?
