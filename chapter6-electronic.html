Chapter 6: Electronic Structure of Atoms

6.1 The Wave Nature of Light

Key ideas

  • Electromagnetic radiation behaves as a wave with a wavelength λ and frequency ν, and all types travel at the speed of light, c.
  • Wavelength and frequency are inversely related: longer λ means lower ν, and vice versa.
  • Different regions of the electromagnetic spectrum have vastly different wavelengths—γ rays are very short; radio waves are very long, with visible light a narrow slice in between.

Important formulas & their meaning

Wave relation ν = c / λ

The frequency ν (cycles per second) equals the speed of light c (≈ 3.00×10⁸ m/s) divided by the wavelength λ (meters). Fixing c ensures that shorter wavelengths correspond to higher frequencies.

Short example(s)

Blue light has λ ≈ 450 nm. Dividing c by 450×10⁻⁹ m gives ν ≈ 6.7×10¹⁴ s⁻¹, a much higher frequency than red light (around 4.0×10¹⁴ s⁻¹).

6.2 Quantized Energy and Photons

Key ideas

  • Planck proposed that radiant energy is delivered in packets called quanta; the energy of each quantum is proportional to the frequency of the radiation.
  • Einstein extended this idea to light itself, treating electromagnetic waves as photons with particle-like behavior.
  • Electrons in atoms absorb or emit photons only in discrete amounts, so energy changes are quantized.

Important formulas & their meaning

Photon energy E = h ν

The energy E of a photon equals Planck’s constant h (6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s) times the frequency ν. Higher-frequency light carries more energy per photon.

Photon energy via wavelength E = h c / λ

Substituting ν = c/λ yields this form, showing that shorter wavelengths have higher-energy photons.

Short example(s)

A UV photon with λ = 250 nm has energy E = (6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s)(3.00×10⁸ m/s)/(250×10⁻⁹ m) ≈ 7.95×10⁻¹⁹ J, about five times the energy of a visible photon.

6.3 Line Spectra and the Bohr Model

Key ideas

  • Atoms emit or absorb radiation only at certain wavelengths, giving rise to line spectra that reflect quantized energy differences.
  • Bohr modeled the hydrogen atom with electrons restricted to circular orbits; energy differences between orbits correspond to photon energies.
  • The Rydberg formula and Bohr energies explain observed wavelengths in hydrogen’s emission spectrum.

Important formulas & their meaning

Energy levels for hydrogen En = -2.18×10⁻¹⁸ J (Z² / n²)

Each level n has energy inversely proportional to n²; Z is the nuclear charge (1 for hydrogen). Lower n values have more negative (lower) energy.

Photon energy from Bohr ΔE = Efinal - Einitial = h ν

A photon is emitted if ΔE is negative (electron drops to lower n) and absorbed if ΔE is positive. The Bohr model shows only certain ΔE values are allowed.

Short example(s)

The Balmer series lines arise when electrons fall to n=2 from higher levels. For n=3→2, ΔE corresponds to λ ≈ 656 nm, giving the familiar red line.

6.4 The Wave Behavior of Matter

Key ideas

  • De Broglie proposed that matter has wave properties with wavelengths inversely proportional to momentum.
  • Electron diffraction experiments confirmed the wave nature of particles, so electrons cannot be described only as point particles.
  • The dual wave-particle behavior introduces fundamental limits on simultaneously knowing position and momentum (Heisenberg uncertainty).

Important formulas & their meaning

de Broglie wavelength λ = h / (m v)

Any particle of mass m moving at speed v has an associated wavelength λ. For macroscopic objects the wavelength is vanishingly small, but for electrons it is comparable to atomic dimensions.

Short example(s)

An electron moving at 1.0×10⁶ m/s has λ = (6.626×10⁻³⁴)/(9.11×10⁻³¹×1.0×10⁶) ≈ 0.73 nm, about the size of atoms, demonstrating why electrons behave as waves near atoms.

6.5 Quantum Mechanics and Atomic Orbitals

Key ideas

  • The Schrödinger equation describes electrons as wavefunctions ψ whose squared magnitude gives probability densities.
  • Quantum numbers (n, l, ml, ms) label allowed orbitals and states: principal, angular momentum, magnetic, and spin.
  • Atomic orbitals are stationary states with characteristic energies and shapes; nodes represent regions of zero probability.

Important formulas & their meaning

Schrödinger equation (symbolic) Ĥ ψ = E ψ

The Hamiltonian operator Ĥ (kinetic plus potential energy) acting on the wavefunction ψ yields the energy eigenvalue E. Solutions produce orbitals labeled by n, l, and ml.

Short example(s)

The 1s orbital (n=1, l=0) has a spherical probability density with no nodes, while the 2p orbitals (n=2, l=1) are dumbbell-shaped along different axes.

6.6 Representations of Orbitals

Key ideas

  • Orbital representations include contour surfaces showing where electron probability is high and cross-sections showing nodal structure.
  • s orbitals are spherical, p orbitals have two lobes separated by a nodal plane, and d orbitals show more complex shapes with angular nodes.
  • Radial distribution diagrams help visualize how electron probability changes with distance from the nucleus.

Important formulas & their meaning

Orbitals are labeled by n, l, and ml

The principal quantum number n sets the energy and size; l determines the shape (0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f); ml determines orientation. Each spherical surface has an electron density defined by |ψ|².

Short example(s)

A 3d orbital has l=2 and shows four lobes or a donut-plus-lobes pattern, each corresponding to a different ml value, while the 3s orbital has two radial nodes.

6.7 Many-Electron Atoms

Key ideas

  • Electron-electron repulsions split orbital energies in atoms beyond hydrogen, so orbitals with the same n are no longer degenerate.
  • Shielding and penetration modify the effective nuclear charge felt by electrons; electrons in inner orbitals shield outer ones.
  • Orbital energies increase with n, but subshell ordering (e.g., 4s before 3d) reflects a balance between penetration and shielding.

Important formulas & their meaning

Effective nuclear charge Zeff ≈ Z - S

An electron experiences an effective nuclear charge Zeff equal to the actual nuclear charge Z minus the shielding constant S from other electrons. Higher Zeff means tighter binding.

Short example(s)

In sodium, the 3s electron feels more nuclear charge than the 3p electron because the 3s orbital penetrates closer to the nucleus, making 3s lower in energy than 3p despite sharing n=3.

6.8 Electron Configurations

Key ideas

  • Electron configurations list occupied orbitals using the Aufbau principle, Hund’s rule, and the Pauli exclusion principle.
  • Orbitals fill in order of increasing energy (n + l rule) and accommodate two electrons with opposite spins.
  • Ionic states and excited states can rearrange electron configurations when energy ordering shifts (e.g., Cu and Cr anomalies).

Important formulas & their meaning

Electron capacity max = 2(2l + 1)

Each subshell of angular momentum l contains 2(2l + 1) orbitals, each holding two electrons, so s holds 2, p holds 6, d holds 10, and f holds 14 electrons.

Short example(s)

Elemental chromium uses an unexpected configuration [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ because half-filled d subshells are especially stable, illustrating that actual order may deviate slightly from a naive Aufbau list.

6.9 Electron Configurations and the Periodic Table

Key ideas

  • The periodic table reflects electron configurations: s-block elements fill s orbitals, p-block fill p, and d-block fill d orbitals.
  • Periodic trends (ionization energy, atomic radius) arise from how orbitals fill and how Zeff changes across periods and down groups.
  • Electron configurations help identify valence electrons, which determine chemical reactivity and oxidation states.

Important formulas & their meaning

Valence electrons Valence = electrons in highest n shell

The outermost shell electrons contribute to bonding. For sodium [Ne] 3s¹, the single valence electron is why Na⁺ forms and the element is highly reactive.

Short example(s)

Transition metals can lose different numbers of d electrons, which explains why Fe can form Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺; these multiple oxidation states reflect the energetic accessibility of d orbitals near the valence shell.
