Chapter 6: Electronic Structure of Atoms

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe the wave–particle duality of light and matter.
  • Explain the Bohr model and why quantum mechanics is needed.
  • Assign quantum numbers and sketch simple atomic orbitals.
  • Write ground‑state electron configurations using Aufbau, Pauli, and Hund rules.

Key Vocabulary

  • Photon: a quantum (packet) of electromagnetic radiation.
  • Quantization: restriction to discrete energy levels.
  • Orbital: region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron.
  • Principal quantum number (n): energy level (1, 2, 3, …).
  • Angular momentum quantum number (l): orbital shape (0 = s, 1 = p, 2 = d, 3 = f).
  • Magnetic quantum number (ml): orientation of the orbital.
  • Spin quantum number (ms): electron spin (+1/2 or −1/2).
  • Aufbau principle: fill lower‑energy orbitals first.
  • Hund’s rule: maximize unpaired electrons with parallel spins in degenerate orbitals.

1. Light, Energy, and the Bohr Model

  • Light behaves as both a wave and a particle (E = h\nu, c = \lambda\nu).
  • Bohr model: electrons move in circular orbits with quantized energies; works well for hydrogen‑like species.
  • Limitations of Bohr model led to full quantum mechanical (wave) treatment of electrons.

2. Quantum Numbers and Orbitals

  • Each electron in an atom is described by the set (n, l, ml, ms).
  • s orbitals are spherical, p orbitals are dumbbell‑shaped, d orbitals have more complex shapes.
  • The Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers.

3. Electron Configurations

  • Order of filling roughly follows increasing energy: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, …
  • Core/valence distinction: valence electrons govern chemical behavior.
  • Use noble‑gas shorthand to simplify (for example, Ne, Ar, Kr).

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. For light of wavelength 500 nm, calculate the frequency and energy of one photon.
  2. List all possible quantum numbers (n, l, ml) for the 3p subshell.
  3. Write full and noble‑gas electron configurations for O, Fe, and Br.
  4. How many unpaired electrons are in the ground‑state electron configuration of N, O, and Fe?
  5. Explain why the 4s orbital fills before 3d in the usual Aufbau order but 4s electrons are often lost first when transition metals form cations.
