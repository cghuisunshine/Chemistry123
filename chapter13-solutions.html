Chapter 13: Properties of Solutions

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe factors that affect solubility (intermolecular forces, temperature, pressure for gases).
  • Express solution concentration in molarity, mass percent, mole fraction, and molality.
  • Apply colligative property equations for boiling‑point elevation, freezing‑point depression, and osmotic pressure.

Key Vocabulary

  • Solution: homogeneous mixture of solute in solvent.
  • Solubility: maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a given amount of solvent at a given temperature.
  • Saturated solution: contains maximum dissolved solute in equilibrium with undissolved solute.
  • Unsaturated solution: contains less solute than the saturation limit.
  • Supersaturated solution: temporarily contains more solute than predicted by solubility; unstable.
  • Colligative property: depends only on number of solute particles, not their identity.

1. Solubility and “Like Dissolves Like”

  • Polar solvents (such as water) dissolve ionic and polar solutes well through ion–dipole and dipole–dipole interactions.
  • Nonpolar solvents (such as hexane) dissolve nonpolar solutes well through dispersion forces.
  • Solubility of most solids in water increases with temperature; solubility of gases generally decreases with temperature but increases with pressure.

2. Concentration Units

  • Molarity: M = n / V (moles of solute per liter of solution).
  • Mass percent: (mass solute / mass solution) × 100%.
  • Mole fraction: χ_i = n_i / n_{total}.
  • Molality (m): m = n_{solute} / \text{kg solvent}; useful for colligative properties since it does not change with temperature.

Worked Example 1 – Molarity

Calculate the molarity of a solution made by dissolving 5.00 g of KCl in enough water to make 250.0 mL of solution.

  1. Molar mass KCl ≈ 74.55 g·mol−1.
  2. Moles KCl: 5.00 g ÷ 74.55 g·mol−1 ≈ 0.0671 mol.
  3. Volume = 0.2500 L → M = 0.0671 / 0.2500 ≈ 0.268\,\text{M}.

3. Colligative Properties

  • Boiling‑point elevation: ΔT_b = i K_b m.
  • Freezing‑point depression: ΔT_f = i K_f m.
  • Osmotic pressure: π = iMRT.
  • i is the van ’t Hoff factor (number of particles per formula unit in solution in the ideal limit).

Worked Example 2 – Freezing‑Point Depression

A 0.500 m aqueous solution of a nonelectrolyte is prepared. If Kf for water is 1.86 °C·kg·mol−1, what is the expected freezing point?

  1. ΔT_f = i K_f m; for a nonelectrolyte, i = 1.
  2. ΔT_f = (1)(1.86)(0.500) = 0.93 °C.
  3. Pure water freezes at 0.00 °C → solution freezes at −0.93 °C.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Explain why NaCl dissolves readily in water but not in hexane (C6H14).
  2. Calculate the molarity of a solution made by dissolving 12.0 g of NaOH in 400.0 mL of solution.
  3. A 0.250 m aqueous solution of NaCl is prepared. Assuming ideal behavior (i ≈ 2), what is the expected freezing point of the solution?
  4. Compare the freezing‑point depression of 0.10 m NaCl and 0.10 m CaCl2, assuming ideal dissociation. Which has the lower freezing point and why?
  5. Describe qualitatively what happens when a red blood cell is placed in (a) pure water, (b) a concentrated salt solution, in terms of osmosis.
