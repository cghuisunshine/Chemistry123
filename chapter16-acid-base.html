Chapter 16: Acid–Base Equilibria

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Apply Brønsted–Lowry definitions of acids and bases and identify conjugate acid–base pairs.
  • Write equilibrium expressions for weak acids and weak bases using Ka and Kb.
  • Use Ka, Kb, and Kw to calculate pH for weak acid and weak base solutions.
  • Explain trends in acid strength and the effect of structure, electronegativity, and resonance.

Key Vocabulary

  • Brønsted–Lowry acid: proton (H+) donor.
  • Brønsted–Lowry base: proton (H+) acceptor.
  • Conjugate acid–base pair: species differing by one H+.
  • Acid dissociation constant (Ka): equilibrium constant for HA ⇌ H+ + A−.
  • Base dissociation constant (Kb): equilibrium constant for B + H2O ⇌ BH+ + OH−.
  • Ion‑product of water: K_w = [H^+][OH^-] ≈ 1.0 × 10^{−14} at 25 °C.
  • pH: pH = −\log[H^+] (base‑10 log).

1. Conjugate Pairs and Relative Strengths

  • HA (acid) ⇌ H+ + A− (conjugate base).
  • NH3 (base) + H2O ⇌ NH4+ (conjugate acid) + OH−.
  • Strong acid → weak conjugate base; weak acid → relatively stronger conjugate base.
  • In water, strong acids are completely dissociated; weak acids establish equilibrium.

2. Using Ka, Kb, and Kw

  • For a weak monoprotic acid HA: Ka = [H^+][A^-]/[HA].
  • For a weak base B: Kb = [BH^+][OH^-]/[B].
  • Relationship for conjugate pairs: K_a K_b = K_w.

Worked Example 1 – Weak Acid pH

A 0.10 M solution of a weak acid HA has Ka = 1.8 × 10−5. Estimate the pH.

  1. Write HA ⇌ H+ + A−.
  2. ICE table: initial [HA] = 0.10, [H+] ≈ 0, [A−] = 0; change −x, +x, +x; equilibrium 0.10 − x, x, x.
  3. Expression: K_a = x^2 / (0.10 − x).
  4. Assume x ≪ 0.10 → K_a ≈ x^2 / 0.10 → x = \sqrt{K_a × 0.10}.
  5. Compute: x ≈ \sqrt{(1.8 × 10^{−5})(0.10)} ≈ 1.3 × 10^{−3} M.
  6. pH = −log x ≈ −log(1.3 × 10−3) ≈ 2.89.

Worked Example 2 – Conjugate Base Kb

Given Ka for acetic acid (HC2H3O2) is 1.8 × 10−5, find Kb for acetate (C2H3O2−).

  1. Use K_a K_b = K_w.
  2. K_b = K_w / K_a = (1.0 × 10^{−14}) / (1.8 × 10^{−5}) ≈ 5.6 × 10^{−10}.

3. Structural Effects on Acid Strength

  • For binary acids (H–X): down a group, bond strength decreases, so acid strength often increases.
  • For oxyacids H–O–X: electronegativity of X and number of oxygens influence acid strength.
  • More electronegative X or more O atoms (which stabilize negative charge) → stronger acid.

Worked Example 3 – Comparing Oxyacid Strengths

Explain qualitatively why HClO4 is a stronger acid than HClO.

  1. Both are H–O–Cl acids, but HClO4 has three additional oxygen atoms.
  2. These oxygens help delocalize and stabilize the negative charge on the conjugate base.
  3. More stabilization of the conjugate base means stronger acid.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Identify the conjugate acid–base pairs in: NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH−.
  2. A 0.10 M solution of a weak acid HA has pH 3.20. Calculate [H+], Ka, and percent dissociation.
  3. Given Ka for HF, compute Kb for F− using Kw and describe whether F− acts as a weak base.
  4. Compare qualitatively the acid strengths of HCl, HBr, and HI. Use trends in bond strength and stability of halide ions in your explanation.
