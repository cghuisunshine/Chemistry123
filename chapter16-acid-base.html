Chapter 16: Acid–Base Equilibria

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Apply Brønsted–Lowry definitions of acids and bases.
  • Write acid–base equilibria and identify conjugate acid–base pairs.
  • Use Ka, Kb, and Kw to calculate pH for weak acids and bases.
  • Explain trends in acid strength and the effect of structure.

Key Vocabulary

  • Brønsted–Lowry acid: proton (H+) donor.
  • Brønsted–Lowry base: proton (H+) acceptor.
  • Conjugate acid–base pair: species that differ by one H+.
  • Acid dissociation constant (Ka): equilibrium constant for HA ⇌ H+ + A−.
  • Base dissociation constant (Kb): equilibrium constant for B + H2O ⇌ BH+ + OH−.
  • Ion‑product of water: K_w = [H^+][OH^-] (≈ 1.0 × 10−14 at 25 °C).
  • pH: pH = −log[H^+].

1. Conjugate Pairs and Relative Strengths

  • Strong acid → weak conjugate base; weak acid → relatively stronger conjugate base.
  • In water, strong acids are completely dissociated; weak acids establish equilibrium.

2. Using Ka, Kb, and Kw

  • For a weak monoprotic acid HA: set up ICE table, Ka = [H^+][A^-]/[HA], and solve for [H+].
  • For conjugate pairs: K_a K_b = K_w.

3. Structural Effects on Acid Strength

  • For oxyacids H–O–X: electronegativity and oxidation state of X affect acidity.
  • Across a period, acid strength increases as the attached atom becomes more electronegative.
  • Down a group, stronger bonds weaken more slowly; trends depend on bond strength and polarity together.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Identify the conjugate acid–base pairs in: NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH−.
  2. A 0.10 M solution of a weak acid HA has pH 3.20. Calculate [H+], Ka, and the percent dissociation.
  3. Given Ka for acetic acid, compute Kb for acetate ion using Kw.
  4. Explain qualitatively why HClO4 is a stronger acid than HClO.
