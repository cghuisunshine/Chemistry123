Chapter 17: Additional Aspects of Aqueous Equilibria

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe buffer solutions and calculate pH using the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation.
  • Sketch and interpret qualitative acid–base titration curves for strong–strong and weak–strong systems.
  • Use solubility-product constants (Ksp) to predict precipitation and calculate molar solubility in simple cases.

Key Vocabulary

  • Buffer: solution that resists pH change when small amounts of acid or base are added; contains a weak acid and its conjugate base or a weak base and its conjugate acid.
  • Henderson–Hasselbalch equation: pH = pKa + log([base]/[acid]).
  • Titration: gradual addition of one solution to another to reach an equivalence point.
  • Equivalence point: point in titration where moles of titrant equal moles of analyte according to stoichiometry.
  • Solubility product (Ksp): equilibrium constant for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble salt.
  • Common-ion effect: reduced solubility or ionization due to presence of an ion already in solution.

1. Buffers and Henderson–Hasselbalch

  • Buffers are made by combining a weak acid with its conjugate base (for example, acetic acid + acetate) or a weak base with its conjugate acid.
  • As long as both acid and base components are present in comparable amounts, the pH changes only slightly when small amounts of strong acid or base are added.
  • Good buffer range: pH ≈ pKa ± 1.

Worked Example 1 – Buffer pH

Calculate the pH of a buffer made from 0.10 M acetic acid (HC2H3O2) and 0.10 M sodium acetate (NaC2H3O2). Given pKa = 4.76.

  1. Use Henderson–Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([base]/[acid]).
  2. [base] = [acetate] = 0.10; [acid] = [acetic acid] = 0.10.
  3. pH = 4.76 + log(0.10/0.10) = 4.76 + log(1) = 4.76.

2. Acid–Base Titrations (Qualitative)

  • Strong acid–strong base: pH starts low or high and passes through pH 7 at equivalence.
  • Weak acid–strong base: initial pH higher; buffer region before equivalence; equivalence point pH > 7.
  • Weak base–strong acid: initial pH high; buffer region; equivalence pH < 7.

3. Solubility and Ksp

  • For a salt MX(s) ⇌ M+ + X−, Ksp = [M+][X−].
  • If pure water is the solvent, molar solubility s gives [M+] = s and [X−] = s → Ksp = s2.
  • In the presence of a common ion, expressions change and solubility typically decreases.

Worked Example 2 – Ksp and Solubility

Suppose Ksp for AgCl is 1.8 × 10−10. Find the molar solubility of AgCl in pure water.

  1. AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag+ + Cl−.
  2. Let s = molar solubility = [Ag+] = [Cl−] at equilibrium.
  3. Ksp = s2 = 1.8 × 10−10 → s = √(1.8 × 10−10) ≈ 1.3 × 10−5 M.
