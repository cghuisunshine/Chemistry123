Chapter 17: Additional Aspects of Aqueous Equilibria

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe buffer solutions and calculate pH using the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation.
  • Sketch and interpret simple acid–base titration curves.
  • Use solubility‑product constants (Ksp) to predict precipitation.

Key Vocabulary

  • Buffer: solution that resists pH change when small amounts of acid or base are added.
  • Henderson–Hasselbalch equation: pH = pK_a + log([base]/[acid]).
  • Titration: gradual addition of one solution to another to reach an equivalence point.
  • Equivalence point: point in titration where moles of titrant equal moles of analyte (based on stoichiometry).
  • Solubility product (K_sp): equilibrium constant for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble salt.
  • Common‑ion effect: reduced solubility or ionization due to presence of a shared ion.

1. Buffers and Henderson–Hasselbalch

  • Construct a buffer from a weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and conjugate acid).
  • Use Henderson–Hasselbalch when both acid and base components are present in significant amounts.
  • Good buffer range: pH ≈ pKa ± 1.

2. Acid–Base Titrations (Qualitative)

  • Strong acid–strong base: pH starts low or high and passes through pH 7 at equivalence.
  • Weak acid–strong base: initial pH higher; equivalence point pH > 7; buffer region before equivalence.

3. Solubility and Ksp

  • For a salt MX(s) ⇌ M+ + X−, K_sp = [M^+][X^-].
  • Use Ksp and ion concentrations to compute Q and decide if a precipitate will form (Q > Ksp).
  • Common‑ion effect can substantially lower solubility.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Describe how you would prepare a buffer with pH ≈ 4.76 using acetic acid and sodium acetate.
  2. Calculate the pH of a buffer made from 0.10 M acetic acid and 0.10 M sodium acetate, given pKa = 4.76.
  3. Sketch qualitatively the titration curve for a weak acid with strong base and label the buffer region and equivalence point.
  4. Given Ksp for AgCl and the concentration of Cl−, compute the maximum [Ag+] before precipitation begins.
