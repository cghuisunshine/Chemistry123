Chapter 11 Tutorial — Liquids and Intermolecular Forces

11.1 A Molecular Comparison of Gases, Liquids, and Solids

Key ideas

  • The three states differ because of the balance between kinetic energy (temperature-driven motion) and intermolecular attractions.
  • Gases have widely spaced particles and negligible attractions, liquids have closer particles that remain mobile, and solids have tightly bound particles fixed in place.
  • Heating boosts kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular forces, while increasing pressure brings particles closer so attractions dominate.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Average kinetic energy: KE_avg ∝ T. Higher temperature increases molecular motion so particles resist attraction, which explains why solids melt and gases expand when heated.
  • Density trend: ρ_gas ≪ ρ_liquid ≈ ρ_solid. Denser phases reflect stronger intermolecular forces because particles pack more tightly.

Short example(s)

The halogens illustrate the concept: I₂ is a solid, Br₂ a liquid, and Cl₂ a gas at room temperature because dispersion forces weaken going up the group, so kinetic energy overcomes attraction more readily.

11.2 Intermolecular Forces

Key ideas

  • Intermolecular forces include dispersion (instantaneous dipoles), dipole–dipole, hydrogen bonding, and ion–dipole interactions, all electrostatic in nature.
  • Dispersion forces depend on polarizability: larger, more polarizable electron clouds (heavy atoms, extended molecules) produce stronger induced dipoles and larger boiling points.
  • Polar molecules add permanent dipole attractions, and hydrogen-bonding partners (N, O, F) create especially strong, directional forces.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Dispersion energy scale: E_disp ∝ α₁α₂/r⁶, where α is polarizability and r is distance. Big, squishy electron clouds yield large α values and stronger dispersion (Figure 11.5).
  • Dipole–dipole strength: E_dd ∝ μ²/r³; molecules with larger permanent dipoles (e.g., CH₃CN) have stronger attractions than nonpolar analogs (propane).

Short example(s)

Acetonitrile (bp 355 K) vs propane (bp 231 K) demonstrates dipole–dipole effects—similar molar masses but different polarities cause a higher boiling point for the polar molecule.

HF boils at 293 K because of hydrogen bonding, while HCl boils at 188 K even though both are similar in size; hydrogen bonding adds a strong additional IMF.

11.3 Select Properties of Liquids

Key ideas

  • Viscosity is a liquid’s resistance to flow and increases with stronger intermolecular forces or more entangled, larger molecules; temperature reduces viscosity by providing kinetic energy to break those interactions.
  • Surface tension measures the energy cost of creating new surface area; surface molecules experience a net inward pull, so liquids like water bead up and support light insects.
  • Capillary action occurs when adhesive forces to a surface outweigh liquid cohesion, allowing liquids to climb narrow tubes or fabrics to wick away moisture.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Surface tension: γ = E/A. It equals the energy needed to increase surface area by 1 m², so higher γ (e.g., mercury 0.146 J/m²) reflects stronger cohesion.
  • Viscosity trend: η ∝ exp(Eₐ/RT). The activation energy Eₐ represents the IMFs that molecules must overcome to flow, so raising temperature drops η exponentially (octane’s viscosity halves between 0 and 40 °C).

Short example(s)

Hexane through decane show increasing viscosity because longer carbon chains experience stronger dispersion and entanglement (Table 11.3).

Capillary rise in a glass tube occurs because water–glass adhesive forces exceed water–water cohesion, giving a concave meniscus, whereas mercury forms a convex meniscus because cohesion dominates.

11.4 Phase Changes

Key ideas

  • Phase changes between solid, liquid, and gas absorb or release energy: fusion, vaporization, sublimation, and their reverse processes.
  • Heating curves display horizontal plateaus at constant temperature while a phase change occurs, because added energy breaks intermolecular forces rather than raises temperature.
  • Above the critical temperature, a distinct liquid cannot form because kinetic energy overwhelms attractions even under high pressure, leading to supercritical fluids.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Enthalpy partition: ∆H_sub = ∆H_fus + ∆H_vap. Sublimation requires both melting and vaporization energies, so ∆H_sub for water is ~47 kJ/mol from its fusion (6 kJ/mol) and vaporization (40 kJ/mol).
  • Heat of vaporization: ∆H_vap measures the energy needed to separate liquid molecules into vapor; for water it is 40.7 kJ/mol, explaining why evaporation cools skin and why refrigerators reject heat during condensation.

Short example(s)

Water’s heating curve shows plateaus at 0 °C and 100 °C where melting and boiling occur; once all the ice melts or water vaporizes, the temperature rises again (Figure 11.22).

At 374 °C (critical temperature) and 22.06 MPa (critical pressure), water cannot condense into a distinct liquid; raising pressure above this point produces a compressible supercritical fluid.

11.5 Vapor Pressure

Key ideas

  • Vapor pressure is the pressure of a vapor in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid (or solid) at a given temperature.
  • At higher temperature, more molecules have enough kinetic energy to escape the liquid, so vapor pressure increases.
  • Stronger intermolecular forces hold molecules in the liquid more tightly, so they reduce vapor pressure and raise boiling point.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Boiling condition: A liquid boils when P_vap = P_ext. The normal boiling point is when P_ext = 1 atm (or 1 bar by some conventions).
  • Clausius–Clapeyron (two-temperature form): ln(P2/P1) = −(ΔH_vap/R)(1/T2 − 1/T1). It relates how vapor pressure changes with temperature using ΔH_vap (J/mol), R (8.314 J·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹), and absolute temperature T (K).

Short example(s)

Because ethanol has a lower normal boiling point than water, it has a higher vapor pressure at room temperature. That is why ethanol evaporates more readily and produces a stronger “cooling” sensation on skin.

11.6 Phase Diagrams

Key ideas

  • A phase diagram maps which phase (solid/liquid/gas) is stable at different temperatures and pressures.
  • The triple point is where solid, liquid, and gas coexist; the critical point marks the end of the liquid–gas boundary.
  • Water is unusual: its solid–liquid boundary slopes “backward” because ice is less dense than liquid water, so increasing pressure favors liquid.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Phase-boundary slope (Clapeyron equation): dP/dT = ΔH/(TΔV). It explains why the solid–liquid line slopes depend on volume change ΔV between phases.

Short example(s)

Above the critical temperature, increasing pressure cannot liquefy the gas; instead you get a supercritical fluid with properties of both phases (high density like a liquid, high diffusivity like a gas).

11.7 Liquid Crystals

Key ideas

  • Liquid crystals are phases that flow like liquids but maintain some orientational order like solids.
  • Nematic phases have aligned molecular axes but no layered structure; smectic phases have both alignment and layering.
  • Electric fields can reorient liquid-crystal molecules, changing how they transmit polarized light—this is the basis of LCD screens.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Birefringence (qualitative): Many liquid crystals have different refractive indices along different directions (often summarized as Δn = n_e − n_o), which is why their optical properties change when molecules reorient.

Short example(s)

In an LCD pixel, applying voltage changes molecular alignment, which changes light polarization through the layer. The polarizers then block or pass light, producing dark or bright pixels.
