8.0 Overview — Chemical Bonds and Valence Electrons

Big picture: what bonds are & which electrons matter

Whenever atoms or ions are held together strongly, we say there is a chemical bond between them. The three main bonding ‚Äúflavors‚Äù are:

  • Ionic bonds: electrostatic attractions between positive and negative ions, usually a metal cation and a nonmetal anion, arranged in a repeating ionic lattice.
  • Covalent bonds: two nonmetal atoms share one or more pairs of electrons, forming discrete molecules like H‚ÇÇO or CO‚ÇÇ.
  • Metallic bonds: many metal atoms share a ‚Äúsea of electrons‚Äù that can move throughout the solid, explaining properties like conductivity and malleability.

The electrons that matter most for bonding are the valence electrons, which are in the outermost occupied shell. These determine how an atom will bond and what ions or molecules it tends to form.

Think: ‚Äúcore electrons stay home; valence electrons go out to bond.‚Äù

Common bonding patterns in main-group chemistry:

  • Metal + nonmetal ‚Üí often forms an ionic compound with cations (from metal) and anions (from nonmetal).
  • Nonmetal + nonmetal ‚Üí usually forms covalent molecules by sharing electron pairs.
  • Metal + metal in bulk ‚Üí metallic bonding with delocalized electrons, giving high electrical and thermal conductivity.

As you move through the chapter, you‚Äôll repeatedly connect these three patterns back to the same basic idea: valence electrons either transfer, share locally, or delocalize.

Salt in water. Sodium chloride (NaCl) contains Na‚Å∫ and Cl‚Åª ions held in an ionic lattice. When you put it in water, the polar water molecules surround and separate the ions.

Water in a glass. H‚ÇÇO has covalent bonds between H and O. The molecules attract each other through weaker intermolecular forces, not ionic bonds.

Metal spoon. In stainless steel, metal atoms are packed together and share delocalized electrons. This ‚Äúsea‚Äù can carry charge and heat, unlike in ionic or molecular solids.

Use these familiar items to anchor your intuition: salt = ionic, water = covalent, metal = metallic.

8.1 Lewis Symbols and the Octet Rule

Representing valence electrons & why ‚Äú8‚Äù is special

Valence electrons are the outermost electrons and are responsible for bonding. For most main-group elements, they are the electrons in the highest principal energy level (n).

A Lewis symbol represents an atom‚Äôs valence electrons as dots drawn around the element symbol. Each dot is one valence electron.

  • Dots are placed on four sides of the symbol; each side can hold up to two dots.
  • Dots are spread out as much as possible before pairing them.
  • The number of dots equals the number of valence electrons.

The octet rule says that atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons until they are surrounded by eight valence electrons, like the noble gases (except He, which is stable with two).

Group number tells valence electrons: Group 1 ‚Üí 1, Group 16 ‚Üí 6, Group 17 ‚Üí 7, etc., for main-group elements.

Lewis symbol: element symbol + 1 dot per valence e‚Åª

This is a simple bookkeeping tool: it tracks only valence electrons, which are the ones that will be lost, gained, or shared in bonding.

The octet corresponds to filled s and p subshells (ns¬≤np‚Å∂). Atoms that reach a noble-gas configuration are generally low in energy and relatively stable.

For main-group elements (representative elements):

  • Group 1 ‚Üí 1 valence electron (e.g., Li, Na).
  • Group 2 ‚Üí 2 valence electrons (e.g., Be, Mg).
  • Group 13 ‚Üí 3 valence electrons, ‚Ä¶, Group 18 ‚Üí 8 (except He).

8.2 Ionic Bonding

Electron transfer, ionic lattices, and lattice energy

An ionic bond results from electron transfer from one atom to another, forming positively charged cations and negatively charged anions that attract each other strongly.

Ionic substances typically form when:

  • A metal with low ionization energy loses electron(s) to become a cation.
  • A nonmetal with high electron affinity gains electron(s) to become an anion.

In the solid, ions are arranged in a three-dimensional ionic lattice, not as separate molecules. This lattice leads to properties such as high melting points, brittleness, and crystalline structure.

8.3 Covalent Bonding

Sharing electrons & how many bonds atoms like to make

In a covalent bond, two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. This sharing allows each atom to reach a more stable electron configuration, often an octet.

  • Single bond: one shared pair of electrons between two atoms.
  • Double bond: two shared pairs (four electrons) between the same two atoms.
  • Triple bond: three shared pairs (six electrons) between the same two atoms.

Multiple bonds (double or triple) generally make the atoms closer together and the bond stronger than a single bond between the same atoms.

Typical bonding patterns for second-row nonmetals:

  • Group 17 (F, Cl) ‚Üí 1 bond.
  • Group 16 (O, S) ‚Üí 2 bonds.
  • Group 15 (N, P) ‚Üí 3 bonds.
  • Group 14 (C, Si) ‚Üí 4 bonds.
Bond order: single = 1, double = 2, triple = 3

For a given pair of atoms (for example, C‚ÄìC), a higher bond order means more electron density between the nuclei, resulting in a shorter, stronger bond.

Second-row nonmetals often follow ‚Äúdefault‚Äù valence expectations when neutral:

  • C tends to form 4 covalent bonds.
  • N tends to form 3 covalent bonds and 1 lone pair.
  • O tends to form 2 bonds and 2 lone pairs.
  • F tends to form 1 bond and 3 lone pairs.

These patterns connect directly to their valence electron counts and the octet rule.

Example: H‚ÇÇ.

  • Each H has 1 valence electron.
  • Sharing a pair of electrons gives each H access to 2 electrons, like He.
  • The Lewis structure is H‚ÄìH.

Example: Cl‚ÇÇ.

  • Each Cl has 7 valence electrons.
  • Each shares 1 electron to form a single bond; each now has 8 electrons around it (octet).
  • Structure: Cl‚ÄìCl, each Cl with 3 lone pairs.

Multiple bonding examples.

  • CO‚ÇÇ: O=C=O. C forms two double bonds (4 pairs shared); each O forms one double bond plus two lone pairs.
  • N‚ÇÇ: N‚â°N. Each N shares three pairs; the triple bond is short and strong, explaining why N‚ÇÇ is very stable and relatively inert.

If a neutral C, N, or O appears with ‚Äútoo few‚Äù bonds in your drawing, check whether a multiple bond is needed to give octets.

8.4 Bond Polarity and Electronegativity

How unequally shared electrons produce dipoles

Bond polarity describes how equally or unequally the electrons in a bond are shared.

  • Nonpolar covalent bond: electrons shared equally (e.g., H‚ÇÇ, Cl‚ÇÇ, N‚ÇÇ).
  • Polar covalent bond: electrons shared unequally; one atom pulls electron density more strongly (e.g., H‚ÄìF, H‚ÄìCl).
  • Ionic bond: extreme case where electron transfer is nearly complete (e.g., NaCl).

Electronegativity (EN) is the ability of an atom in a molecule to attract electrons to itself.

  • Fluorine is the most electronegative element (EN ‚âà 4.0); Cs is among the least (EN ‚âà 0.7).

8.5 Drawing Lewis Structures & Formal Charge

Systematic drawing + choosing the best structure

A Lewis structure shows which atoms are bonded, how many bonds there are, and where lone pairs are placed. It encodes the valence electron picture of a molecule or polyatomic ion.

General procedure for drawing a Lewis structure:

  1. Count valence electrons for all atoms; adjust for overall charge (add electrons for negative charge; subtract for positive).
  2. Choose a central atom (often the least electronegative, not H) and connect surrounding atoms with single bonds.
  3. Complete octets for outer atoms by adding lone pairs.
  4. Place remaining electrons on the central atom.
  5. If the central atom still lacks an octet, form multiple bonds by converting lone pairs on surrounding atoms into bonding pairs.

Formal charge helps you choose the most reasonable structure when more than one satisfies the octet rule.

Formal charge = valence e‚Åª ‚àí ¬Ω(bonding e‚Åª) ‚àí (nonbonding e‚Åª)

To compute formal charge for an atom in a Lewis structure:

  • Count all its lone-pair electrons (nonbonding).
  • Add half of the bonding electrons (or simply count bonds and multiply by 1).
  • Subtract that total from the valence electrons of the neutral atom (from the periodic table).

Guidelines for choosing the dominant Lewis structure:

  • Prefer structures where formal charges are small in magnitude (0, ¬±1).
  • When negative formal charges are unavoidable, they should reside on more electronegative atoms.
  • The sum of all formal charges must equal the overall charge on the molecule/ion.

Example: PCl‚ÇÉ.

  • Valence electrons: P (5) + 3√óCl (3√ó7) = 26 e‚Åª.
  • Skeleton: Cl‚ÄìP‚ÄìCl‚ÄìCl with three P‚ÄìCl single bonds ‚Üí 6 e‚Åª used.
  • Complete Cl octets (3√ó6 = 18 e‚Åª) ‚Üí total 24 used.
  • Remaining 2 e‚Åª go as a lone pair on P, giving P eight electrons.
  • Formal charges: all atoms 0 ‚Üí this is the preferred structure.

Example: HCN.

  • Valence electrons: H (1) + C (4) + N (5) = 10 e‚Åª.
  • Skeleton: H‚ÄìC‚ÄìN ‚Üí 4 e‚Åª in bonds.
  • Place remaining electrons to satisfy octets; you find C needs more bonds.
  • Convert lone pairs on N into bonding pairs until C has an octet ‚Üí H‚ÄìC‚â°N.
  • Final structure has a triple bond (C‚â°N) and satisfies the octet rule for C and N.

If you have a valid Lewis structure but high formal charges or negative charge on an atom that‚Äôs not very electronegative, look for an alternative with multiple bonds and better formal charges.

8.6 Resonance Structures

Blending multiple valid Lewis pictures

Sometimes a single Lewis structure cannot explain the experimentally observed structure of a molecule or ion. In these cases, we use resonance structures.

Resonance structures are multiple valid Lewis structures that:

  • Have the same arrangement of atoms.
  • Differ only in the placement of electrons (bonds and lone pairs).

The actual molecule is a resonance hybrid, a blend of all significant resonance structures. It does not flip back and forth between them; instead, its true electron distribution is intermediate.

Resonance often leads to bonds that are all the same length, intermediate between single and double bonds, and to extra stability (as in benzene).

Rules for drawing resonance structures:

  • Keep atom positions fixed; only move electrons.
  • Preserve the overall number of electrons and the overall charge.
  • Each resonance structure should satisfy the octet rule as much as possible and have reasonable formal charges.

Effective bond order in resonance hybrids:

  • If two equivalent resonance structures place a double bond in different positions, each of those bonds in the hybrid has bond order between 1 and 2.
  • Example: in ozone (O‚ÇÉ), the two O‚ÄìO bonds have an effective bond order of about 1.5 each.
  • In NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, three equivalent resonance structures lead to three equal N‚ÄìO bonds with bond order between 1 and 2.

Example: Ozone, O‚ÇÉ.

  • Lewis structures: one with double bond on the left, one with double bond on the right.
  • Experiments show two identical O‚ÄìO bond lengths, both intermediate between a single and a double bond.
  • Conclusion: the real molecule is the resonance hybrid of both structures.

Example: Nitrate ion, NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª.

  • Three equivalent structures, each with one N=O and two N‚ÄìO single bonds, but with the double bond in a different place.
  • All N‚ÄìO bonds are experimentally equal and intermediate in length.

Example: Benzene, C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÜ.

  • Two structures with alternating single and double C‚ÄìC bonds arranged in a hexagon.
  • Experimentally, all C‚ÄìC bonds are the same length, between a single and a double bond.

8.7 Exceptions to the Octet Rule

When atoms don‚Äôt follow ‚Äú8 electrons‚Äù exactly

The octet rule works for many main-group molecules, but there are important exceptions. They fall into three main categories:

  1. Odd-electron species: molecules or ions with an odd number of total valence electrons. An octet cannot be given to every atom.
  2. Incomplete octet (electron-deficient): the central atom has fewer than eight valence electrons (common for B and Be).
  3. Expanded octet (hypervalent): the central atom has more than eight valence electrons (10, 12, etc.), possible only for atoms in period 3 or below.

Hypervalent molecules are more common when a large central atom is surrounded by several small, very electronegative atoms such as F or Cl.

Odd-electron species.

  • Total valence electrons are odd (e.g., 11, 17).
  • Examples: NO, NO‚ÇÇ, ClO‚ÇÇ, O‚ÇÇ‚Åª.
  • These molecules often contain an unpaired electron and are radicals, usually quite reactive.

Incomplete octet (electron-deficient).

  • Central atom has less than 8 electrons, often 6.
  • Common elements: B, Be.
  • Sometimes giving them an octet creates unfavorable formal charges (e.g., putting positive charge on a very electronegative atom), so the electron-deficient structure is actually preferred.

Expanded octet (hypervalent).

  • Central atom has more than 8 electrons (10, 12, etc.).
  • Only atoms from period 3 onward (e.g., P, S, Cl, Br, I) can do this, because they are larger.
  • The main reason is spatial: a larger atom can accommodate more bonded atoms around it without severe crowding.

Example: odd-electron NO.

  • N (5 e‚Åª) + O (6 e‚Åª) = 11 valence electrons.
  • Any reasonable Lewis structure ends up with one unpaired electron.
  • NO is a radical and relatively reactive.

Example: incomplete octet in BF‚ÇÉ.

  • Total valence electrons: B (3) + 3√óF (3√ó7) = 24 e‚Åª.
  • Simple structure: B with three B‚ÄìF single bonds and three lone pairs on each F.
  • B has only 6 electrons; F atoms have octets.
  • Alternative structures with B=F double bonds give B‚Åª and F‚Å∫, placing positive charge on highly electronegative F ‚Üí disfavored.
  • Conclusion: the electron-deficient 6-electron B structure is dominant.

Example: expanded octet in PF‚ÇÖ.

  • Valence electrons: P (5) + 5√óF (5√ó7) = 40 e‚Åª.
  • Lewis structure: central P with five P‚ÄìF single bonds and no lone pairs.
  • P has 10 electrons around it (expanded octet), which is allowed because P is a third-period element.
  • An analogous NCl‚ÇÖ would require N (a second-period element) to hold 10 electrons; this doesn‚Äôt occur under normal conditions.

When an atom from period 3 or lower is surrounded by five or six small, electronegative atoms, suspect a stable expanded-octet structure.

8.8 Strengths and Lengths of Covalent Bonds

Relating bond order, bond energy, and bond length

Bond enthalpy (bond energy) is the energy required to break one mole of a given bond in the gas phase. It is always positive because breaking bonds requires energy.

Bond length is the distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms, usually measured in picometers (pm, 10‚Åª¬π¬≤ m).

For a given pair of atoms:

  • Single bond ‚Üí longest and weakest.
  • Double bond ‚Üí shorter and stronger.
  • Triple bond ‚Üí shortest and strongest.
