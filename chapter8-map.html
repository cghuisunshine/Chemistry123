8.0 Overview — Basic Concepts of Chemical Bonding

Valence electrons, bond types, and why the octet matters

Chemical bonds arise from valence electrons seeking stable configurations: electron transfer gives ionic solids, electron sharing gives covalent molecules, and delocalized electrons give metallic bonding.

Lewis symbols expose the valence shell, while the octet rule explains why s- and p-block elements prefer eight electrons around them.

Valence electrons = group number (main-group)

Counting valence electrons is the first step whether you are writing Lewis symbols, drawing structures, or predicting bonds.

Octet = 8 valence electrons (full s + p)

The octet rule defines the goal: ionic, covalent, and many resonance structures exist so every atom reaches a noble-gas configuration.

Remember: metals + nonmetals ‚Üí ionic; nonmetals sharing electrons ‚Üí covalent; tune your picture using Lewis symbols, formal charges, or lattice energetics.

8.1 Lewis Symbols and the Octet Rule

Counting valence electrons and aiming for eight

Lewis symbols place a dot for each valence electron around the elemental symbol, showing how electrons might pair when atoms bond.

The octet rule says that s- and p-block atoms usually surround themselves with eight electrons (or two electrons for hydrogen) to mimic noble gases.

Valence = group number (main-group); p-block: group number - 10

This is why Group 16 elements like S have six valence electrons (16 - 10 = 6) and why halogens have seven.

Octet = 8 valence electrons

Eight electrons fill the s and p orbitals, so drawing Lewis dots that sum to eight captures the target configuration.

Sulfur‚Äôs Lewis symbol‚ÄîS with six dots‚Äîshows the six valence electrons; when sulfur forms SO‚ÇÇ it shares electron pairs until it reaches an octet on each atom.
