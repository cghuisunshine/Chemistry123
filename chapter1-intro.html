Chapter 1: Introduction, Matter, Energy, and Measurement

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe how chemistry fits within the scientific method and why measurement is central to the subject.
  • Classify matter by state (solid, liquid, gas) and by composition (element, compound, homogeneous mixture, heterogeneous mixture).
  • Distinguish clearly between physical vs. chemical properties and changes, using everyday examples.
  • Use SI units and metric prefixes correctly, converting between common length, mass, volume, and temperature units.
  • Apply significant‑figure rules to measurements, calculated answers, and scientific notation.
  • Use dimensional analysis systematically to set up and solve multistep unit‑conversion problems, including density.

Key Vocabulary

  • Matter: anything that has mass and occupies space.
  • Element: substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.
  • Compound: substance composed of two or more elements in a fixed ratio.
  • Mixture: physical combination of two or more substances; composition may vary.
  • Physical property: can be observed without changing composition (for example, color, melting point).
  • Chemical property: describes how a substance reacts to form new substances (for example, flammability).
  • Accuracy: closeness of a measurement to the true value.
  • Precision: closeness of repeated measurements to each other.
  • Significant figures: digits in a reported value that are known with certainty plus one final estimated digit.
  • Density: mass per unit volume, \rho = m/V.

1. Classification of Matter

States of Matter

  • Solids: particles packed closely; fixed shape and volume; low compressibility.
  • Liquids: particles still close but able to flow; fixed volume but assume shape of container.
  • Gases: particles far apart; neither fixed shape nor fixed volume; highly compressible.

Pure Substances vs Mixtures

  • Pure substances have constant composition:
    • Elements (for example, O2, Cu).
    • Compounds (for example, H2O, NaCl) with definite ratios.
  • Mixtures have variable composition:
    • Homogeneous (solutions): uniform throughout (saltwater, air).
    • Heterogeneous: non‑uniform (oil and water, salad).

Worked Example 1 – Classifying Matter

Classify each as element, compound, homogeneous mixture, or heterogeneous mixture: sugar, brass, orange juice with pulp, oxygen gas.

  1. Sugar (table sugar, sucrose) has fixed composition (C, H, O in a fixed ratio) → compound.
  2. Brass is a uniform solid solution of Cu and Zn → homogeneous mixture.
  3. Orange juice with pulp has visible solid pieces suspended in liquid → heterogeneous mixture.
  4. Oxygen gas (O2) is a single element → element.

2. Properties, Changes, and Energy

Physical vs Chemical Changes

  • Physical changes: same substance before and after (melting ice, boiling water, dissolving salt in water).
  • Chemical changes: new substances formed (burning, rusting, digestion, gas formation in a reaction).

Energy in Physical and Chemical Processes

  • Energy can be stored as potential energy (position, composition) or kinetic energy (motion).
  • Chemical reactions often involve exchange of energy with surroundings in the form of heat.

3. Measurement, SI Units, and Prefixes

  • Base SI units used in general chemistry:
    • Length: meter (m).
    • Mass: kilogram (kg); in the lab we often use grams (g).
    • Time: second (s).
    • Temperature: kelvin (K); Celsius (°C) is common in lab.
    • Amount of substance: mole (mol).
  • Common prefixes: kilo (k, 103), centi (c, 10−2), milli (m, 10−3), micro (µ, 10−6), nano (n, 10−9).

Worked Example 2 – Unit Prefix Conversion

Convert 3.45 km to meters and centimeters.

  1. Write conversion factors: 1 km = 1000 m; 1 m = 100 cm.
  2. Convert to meters: 3.45\,\text{km} \times (1000\,\text{m} / 1\,\text{km}) = 3.45 \times 10^3\,\text{m}.
  3. Convert to centimeters: 3.45 \times 10^3\,\text{m} \times (100\,\text{cm} / 1\,\text{m}) = 3.45 \times 10^5\,\text{cm}.

4. Significant Figures and Scientific Notation

  • Counting significant figures:
    • All nonzero digits are significant.
    • Zeros between nonzero digits are significant (for example, 101 has three sig figs).
    • Leading zeros are never significant (0.0032 has two sig figs).
    • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant (2.300 has four sig figs).
  • Calculations:
    • Add/subtract: answer has same number of decimal places as the measurement with the fewest decimal places.
    • Multiply/divide: answer has same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest significant figures.
  • Use scientific notation to express very large or small numbers clearly and to match the correct sig figs.

Worked Example 3 – Significant Figures

Compute (12.53 g + 1.8 g) / 0.055 g and report with correct significant figures.

  1. Add masses: 12.53 g + 1.8 g = 14.33 g → round to 14.3 g (one decimal place, limited by 1.8 g).
  2. Divide: 14.3 g / 0.055 g ≈ 259.09 (dimensionless).
  3. Sig figs: 14.3 has three sig figs; 0.055 has two sig figs → answer should have two sig figs.
  4. Final answer: 2.6 × 102.

5. Dimensional Analysis and Density

Dimensional analysis (factor‑label method) is the default tool for almost every numerical chemistry problem.

General Strategy

  1. Write down the starting quantity with its units.
  2. Multiply by conversion factors so units cancel, leaving desired units.
  3. Keep track of significant figures and check if the answer is reasonable.

Density as a Conversion Factor

  • Density links mass and volume: \rho = m/V.
  • Use density to convert between mass and volume when doing mixture or solution problems later in the course.

Worked Example 4 – Using Density

A metal sample has mass 12.8 g and volume 4.00 mL. Calculate its density and comment on possible identity.

  1. Compute density: \rho = m/V = 12.8\,\text{g} / 4.00\,\text{mL} = 3.20\,\text{g mL}^{-1}.
  2. Sig figs: 12.8 (three), 4.00 (three) → density has three sig figs (3.20 g·mL−1).
  3. Compare with tabulated densities (from your text or lab manual) to suggest a plausible metal.

6. Common Pitfalls and Study Tips

  • Always write units in every step; most mistakes are unit mistakes.
  • Underline or circle the given and the target quantity before starting a calculation.
  • Check whether your final answer makes sense (for example, size, sign, order of magnitude).
  • Practice converting between °C and K, and between grams/milliliters and kilograms/liters, until it feels automatic.

Try these without looking at your notes, then compare with solutions in your textbook or with a tutor.

  1. Classify each of the following as an element, compound, homogeneous mixture, or heterogeneous mixture: air, NaCl(s), brass, granite, blood.
  2. Identify each change as primarily physical or chemical: boiling water, burning paper, dissolving sugar, rusting iron, shredding paper.
  3. Convert 6.50 × 102 mg to grams and kilograms using dimensional analysis; show all unit‑cancellation steps.
  4. A student measures the mass of a liquid as 15.2 g and its volume as 12.3 mL. Calculate the density and express your answer with the correct significant figures.
  5. Room temperature is often approximated as 25.0 °C. Express this temperature in kelvin and in degrees Fahrenheit.
  6. A block of metal has dimensions 2.40 cm × 1.80 cm × 0.500 cm and mass 21.6 g. (a) Calculate its volume. (b) Determine its density. (c) Suggest a possible identity based on a density table.
