Chapter 15: Chemical Equilibrium

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Write equilibrium constant expressions (Kc and Kp) for homogeneous and heterogeneous equilibria.
  • Relate the reaction quotient Q to K and predict the direction of shift for nonequilibrium mixtures.
  • Set up and solve ICE (Initial–Change–Equilibrium) tables for concentration‑based equilibrium problems.
  • Apply Le Châtelier’s principle to predict the qualitative response of a system to disturbances in concentration, pressure, and temperature.

Key Vocabulary

  • Equilibrium: dynamic state where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal; concentrations become constant in time.
  • Equilibrium constant (K): ratio of product to reactant activities at equilibrium, raised to stoichiometric powers.
  • Reaction quotient (Q): same expression as K but evaluated for any set of concentrations or pressures.
  • Le Châtelier’s principle: if a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts in the direction that tends to counteract the disturbance.

1. Writing K and Q

  • For a generic reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, the equilibrium constant in terms of concentration is:

K_c = [C]^c[D]^d / ([A]^a[B]^b)

  • Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the expression (activity ≈ 1).
  • For gases, you can write Kp in terms of partial pressures; Kp and Kc are related by K_p = K_c(RT)^Δn, where Δn is (moles gas products − moles gas reactants).

2. Using Q to Predict Direction

  • Q < K: system has too much reactant relative to product → reaction shifts right (toward products).
  • Q > K: system has too much product → reaction shifts left (toward reactants).
  • Q = K: system is at equilibrium; no net shift.

Worked Example 1 – Comparing Q and K

For N2O4(g) ⇌ 2 NO2(g), suppose Kc = 0.36 at a certain temperature. If initially [N2O4] = 0.10 M and [NO2] = 0.010 M, what is Q, and which way will the system shift?

  1. Q = [NO_2]^2/[N_2O_4] = (0.010)^2 / 0.10 = 1.0 × 10^{−3}.
  2. Q (1.0 × 10−3) < K (0.36) → reaction shifts right (toward producing more NO2).

3. ICE Tables and Approximations

ICE = Initial, Change, Equilibrium. Strategy:

  1. Write balanced equation and K expression.
  2. Fill an ICE table with given initial concentrations.
  3. Represent changes using a variable (often x) consistent with stoichiometry.
  4. Substitute equilibrium values into K expression and solve for x.
  5. Check any approximations (for example, if x is small relative to initial values).

Worked Example 2 – Weak Acid Dissociation Sketch

For a weak acid HA ⇌ H+ + A− with initial [HA] = 0.10 M and no added H+ or A−, the ICE table would be:

  • Initial: [HA] = 0.10, [H+] ≈ 0 (ignore 10−7 from water), [A−] = 0.
  • Change: [HA] → −x, [H+] → +x, [A−] → +x.
  • Equilibrium: [HA] = 0.10 − x, [H+] = x, [A−] = x.

Substitute into Ka expression and solve for x; for small Ka, the approximation 0.10 − x ≈ 0.10 is often valid.

4. Le Châtelier’s Principle

  • Adding reactant or removing product → equilibrium shifts toward products.
  • Removing reactant or adding product → equilibrium shifts toward reactants.
  • Changing volume (for gases): decreasing volume (increasing pressure) favors the side with fewer moles of gas; increasing volume favors more moles of gas.
  • Temperature changes effectively alter K. For exothermic reactions (ΔH < 0), increasing T shifts equilibrium left; for endothermic (ΔH > 0), increasing T shifts equilibrium right.

Worked Example 3 – Le Châtelier and Haber Process

N2(g) + 3 H2(g) ⇌ 2 NH3(g), ΔH < 0 (exothermic).

  • Increasing pressure (by decreasing volume) favors the side with fewer moles of gas → products (2 mol) vs reactants (4 mol) → more NH3 at equilibrium.
  • Increasing temperature adds heat to an exothermic system → shifts equilibrium left (toward reactants), lowering NH3 yield.
