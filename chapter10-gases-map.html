Characteristics of Gases
Macroscopic behavior
R
Roots ‚Äì Core ideas
How gases differ from liquids and solids.
‚ñ∂
  • Gases expand spontaneously to fill any container; their volume equals the container‚Äôs volume.
  • Gases are highly compressible: applying pressure greatly reduces volume, unlike most liquids and solids.
  • Different gases mix completely to form homogeneous mixtures regardless of identity or proportion (e.g., air is mostly N‚ÇÇ and O‚ÇÇ).
  • Gas molecules are far apart; only a tiny fraction of the total volume is actually occupied by particles, so each molecule behaves almost independently.
  • Substances that are liquids or solids at room conditions can appear as vapors (gaseous form) at higher temperature or lower pressure (e.g., water vapor).
T
Trunk ‚Äì Conceptual links
Why gases share similar physical laws.
‚ñ∂

Because gas molecules are so widely spaced and interactions are weak, the detailed chemical identity of a gas matters much less for its bulk physical behavior. As a result:

  • Very different gases (N‚ÇÇ vs O‚ÇÇ vs CO‚ÇÇ) obey the same simple PV‚ÄìT‚Äìn relationships under similar conditions.
  • We can describe generic ‚Äúideal gas‚Äù behavior and then treat most real gases as approximately ideal, especially at low pressure and moderate temperature.
  • Large intermolecular distances are the root of high compressibility, full-container expansion, and easy mixing.

Example ‚Äì Air as an almost ideal gas

Although O‚ÇÇ supports life and combustion and N‚ÇÇ is relatively inert, their molecular masses and sizes are similar and their intermolecular forces are weak. Physically, they behave so similarly that we can treat ‚Äúair‚Äù as a single ideal gas in many calculations.

Example ‚Äì Vapor vs gas

Water is often a liquid at room conditions, but water molecules in the gas phase are called water vapor. The same thermodynamic ideas apply: water vapor expands to fill its container and mixes uniformly with other gases, but we keep the term ‚Äúvapor‚Äù to remind ourselves that the substance is normally liquid or solid at that temperature.

Roots ‚Äì Definition & atmospheric pressure
P = F/A and what ‚Äú1 atm‚Äù means physically.
‚ñ∂
  • Pressure is the force per unit area on a surface: many tiny molecular impacts summed over that area.
  • Atmospheric pressure comes from the weight of the air column above a point on Earth‚Äôs surface.
  • Near sea level, atmospheric pressure is about 1.0 √ó 10‚Åµ Pa = 1 bar ‚âà 14.7 psi.
  • A barometer uses a column of mercury supported by atmospheric pressure; the height of the column (‚âà760 mm at standard conditions) measures the pressure.
  • A manometer compares gas pressure to atmospheric pressure using mercury in a U‚Äëtube; the height difference h tells you how much higher or lower the gas pressure is than atmospheric.
  • Common pressure units include Pa, kPa, bar, atm, mm Hg, torr, and psi; 1 atm = 760 torr = 101.325 kPa ‚âà 1.01325 bar.

The Gas Laws
Boyle, Charles, Avogadro
R
Roots ‚Äì Basic relationships
P‚ÄìV, V‚ÄìT, and V‚Äìn for a fixed amount of gas.
‚ñ∂
  • Boyle‚Äôs law: at constant temperature and amount, gas volume decreases when pressure increases (inverse relationship).
  • Charles‚Äôs law: at constant pressure and amount, gas volume increases when absolute temperature increases (direct relationship).
  • Avogadro‚Äôs law: at constant temperature and pressure, gas volume is proportional to the number of moles.
  • These laws each describe a slice of gas behavior when two of the four variables (P, V, n, T) are held constant.
  • Gay-Lussac‚Äôs law of combining volumes showed that gaseous reactants and products often combine in simple volume ratios, motivating Avogadro‚Äôs hypothesis that equal volumes at the same T and P contain equal numbers of molecules.

Example ‚Äì Breathing and Boyle‚Äôs law

When you inhale, your chest expands and lung volume increases. At roughly constant temperature, this increase in V reduces the gas pressure in your lungs below atmospheric pressure, and air flows in. Exhalation reverses this cycle, neatly illustrating P‚ÄìV inverse proportionality.

Example ‚Äì Hot air balloons (Charles + density)

Heating the air in a balloon at essentially constant pressure causes the air‚Äôs volume to expand, lowering its density. The lower density makes the balloon buoyant, so it rises relative to the cooler, denser surrounding air.

Example ‚Äì Combining volumes in reactions

In reactions like 2 H‚ÇÇ(g) + O‚ÇÇ(g) ‚Üí 2 H‚ÇÇO(g), Gay-Lussac observed that gases combine in simple volume ratios (2 volumes of H‚ÇÇ with 1 volume of O‚ÇÇ) at the same T and P. Avogadro‚Äôs hypothesis explains this: equal volumes contain equal numbers of molecules, so volume ratios reflect mole ratios.

The Ideal Gas Equation
PV = nRT & consequences
R
Roots ‚Äì PV = nRT
Connecting P, V, n, and T for an ideal gas.
‚ñ∂

The three simple gas laws (Boyle, Charles, Avogadro) combine into one powerful relationship: the ideal gas equation. It assumes that gas molecules have negligible volume and no intermolecular forces, which is a good approximation under many conditions.

PV = nRT
  • P = pressure, V = volume, n = moles, T = temperature in K, R = gas constant.
  • Explains Boyle‚Äôs law (holding n, T constant), Charles‚Äôs law (holding n, P constant), and Avogadro‚Äôs law (holding P, T constant).
  • Defines molar volume at STP: ~22.4 L/mol for an ideal gas at 0 ¬∞C and 1 atm.
  • Is the foundation for gas stoichiometry, gas-density calculations, and many thermodynamic estimates.

Ideal gas equation variations

P = nRT / V
V = nRT / P
n = PV / RT
T = PV / (nR)

Density and molar mass of a gas

d = P M / (R T)
M = d R T / P
  • d = density (e.g., g/L), M = molar mass (g/mol), P, T, R as usual.
  • Higher M or P increase d; higher T decreases d (hot gases are less dense).
  • Measuring d at known P and T gives M, which can help identify an unknown gas.

Gas Mixtures and Partial Pressures
Dalton & mole fractions
R
Roots ‚Äì Independent behavior of gases in a mixture
Partial pressure concept.
‚ñ∂
  • In an ideal gas mixture, each gas behaves as if the others were absent; it ‚Äúsees‚Äù the full container volume and temperature.
  • The pressure that an individual gas would exert alone in the container at the same T is its partial pressure.
  • The total pressure is just the sum of these partial pressures, because each gas‚Äôs collisions add to the total forces on the walls.
  • Dalton‚Äôs law of partial pressures provides the macroscopic statement of this microscopic independence.

The Kinetic-Molecular Theory of Gases
Microscopic model
R
Roots ‚Äì Postulates of the theory
Random motion, negligible volume, elastic collisions, and energy‚Äìtemperature link.
‚ñ∂
  • Gases are made of many tiny particles (molecules or atoms) in continuous, random motion.
  • Particle volumes are negligible compared with container volume for an ideal gas.
  • No intermolecular forces are assumed except during collisions, which are perfectly elastic (no kinetic-energy loss in collisions).
  • Average kinetic energy is proportional to absolute temperature: at the same T, all gases have the same average kinetic energy, even though their masses and speeds differ.
  • These postulates explain the gas laws: more collisions per area or harder impacts (from higher speed) give higher pressure; increasing T increases average kinetic energy and thus pressure at fixed volume.

Example ‚Äì Comparing molecular speeds at 298 K

At 298 K, lighter gases (like H‚ÇÇ or He) have significantly higher u_rms than heavier ones (like Kr or Xe), but average kinetic energies are equal. This explains why lighter gases diffuse and effuse faster, even though all gases share the same temperature.

Example ‚Äì Compression at constant T

When a gas is compressed at constant temperature, average kinetic energy and u_rms do not change. However, molecules strike the walls more often because they have less space, increasing the collision rate and thus the pressure, consistent with Boyle‚Äôs law.

Molecular Effusion and Diffusion
Rates & molar mass
R
Roots ‚Äì Effusion vs diffusion
Two ways gases spread out.
‚ñ∂
  • Effusion is the passage of gas molecules through a tiny hole into vacuum or low-pressure region; collisions near the hole are negligible.
  • Diffusion is the spread of a gas throughout another substance or space, driven by random thermal motion and concentration gradients.
  • At the same temperature, lighter molecules move faster on average (higher u_rms) and thus effuse and idealize diffuse faster than heavier molecules.
  • Real diffusion is slower than molecular speeds suggest because frequent collisions (mean free path effects) interrupt straight-line motion.
T

‚ñ∂

Graham‚Äôs law relates the effusion rates of two gases to their molar masses when T and P are the same:

r‚ÇÅ / r‚ÇÇ = ‚àö(M‚ÇÇ / M‚ÇÅ)
  • r‚ÇÅ, r‚ÇÇ = effusion rates (e.g., volume or moles per unit time).
  • M‚ÇÅ, M‚ÇÇ = molar masses (same units for both gases).
  • The lighter gas (smaller M) has the larger effusion rate.

Since u_rms ‚àù 1/‚àöM, we can view Graham‚Äôs law as saying that effusion rate is proportional to the average molecular speed.

Real Gases: Deviations from Ideal Behavior
van der Waals corrections
R
Roots ‚Äì When and why gases are nonideal
High P, low T, finite size, and attractions.
‚ñ∂
  • For 1 mol of ideal gas, PV/RT = 1 at any pressure and temperature.
  • For real gases:
    • At moderate pressures, PV/RT often dips below 1 due to attractive forces lowering the effective pressure.
    • At high pressures, PV/RT rises above 1 because the finite volume of molecules makes the gas occupy more space than ideal theory predicts.
  • Real gases approach ideal behavior:
    • At low pressures (molecules far apart; volume and attractions less important).
    • At high temperatures (molecules move fast enough that attractions matter less).
  • Kinetic-molecular theory assumptions (point particles, no forces) break down under crowded or cold conditions, leading to deviations.
T
Trunk ‚Äì van der Waals equation
Correcting PV = nRT for real gases.
‚ñ∂

The van der Waals equation modifies the ideal gas equation by correcting both pressure and volume:

(P + n¬≤ a / V¬≤)(V ‚àí n b) = n R T
  • a corrects for attractions:
    • Attractive forces reduce the measured pressure; adding n¬≤a/V¬≤ approximates the ‚Äúlost‚Äù pressure due to attractions.
    • Larger a means stronger attractions.
  • b corrects for finite molecule volume:
    • nb approximates the volume actually occupied by molecules.
    • Subtracting nb gives the free volume available for molecular motion.
    • Larger b means larger molecular size.
  • a and b are experimentally determined and increase roughly with molar mass and molecular size (heavier, bulkier molecules have larger a and b).

For 1 mol N‚ÇÇ at 300 K, PV/RT ‚âà 1 at low pressure. As P rises, PV/RT dips below 1 because attractions lower the effective pressure. This non-monotonic behavior reflects the competing influences of attractions (lowering PV/RT) and repulsive size effects (raising PV/RT).
