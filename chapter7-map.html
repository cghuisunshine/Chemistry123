7.0 Overview — Periodic Properties

How the table encodes size, energy, and reactivity patterns

The periodic table arranges atoms so that similar valence-electron structures line up in columns and the net nuclear pull (Zeff) rises left to right while electrons occupy higher shells down a group.

  • Zeff controls atomic radius, ionization energy, and electron affinity.
  • Atomic radius shrinks across periods and grows down groups as electrons feel different pulls.
  • Chemical reactivity (metals vs nonmetals) follows those size and energy patterns.

7.1 Development of the Periodic Table

Why arranging by atomic number unifies the table
  • Mendeleev and Meyer arranged elements by increasing atomic weight and noticed recurring physical and chemical traits.
  • Mendeleev left gaps for unknown elements, predicting their properties so that similar elements stayed in the same column.
  • Moseley‚Äôs X-ray work assigned each element a unique atomic number Z, which equals the number of protons and fixes the modern periodic structure.
Properties repeat when elements are ordered by increasing Z

Z is the atomic number (dimensionless proton count), so ordering by Z aligns families with similar valence-electron structures.

Z = number of protons in the nucleus

The periodic law works because Z determines the nuclear charge that dictates electron configuration and chemistry.

Prediction success. Eka-silicon (later germanium) was pinned down with density ‚âà4.7 g/cm¬≥ and formulas similar to GeO‚ÇÇ and GeCl‚ÇÑ; when discovered, the measured properties matched, showing the value of leaving blanks.

Ar vs K. Argon (Z=18) sits with noble gases, while potassium (Z=19) moves into alkali metals, even though Ar has a higher atomic mass than K; atomic number ordering honors their chemistry.

The periodic table is a map of electron structures, not mass.

7.2 Effective Nuclear Charge

How screening shapes the force felt by valence electrons

Zeff is the net positive charge a valence electron experiences after accounting for shielding from other electrons; it is always less than Z because electron-electron repulsions cancel some attraction.

Across a period Zeff grows quickly because new protons are added while shielding changes little; down a group Zeff rises slowly because larger shells and extra electrons offset the added protons.

7.3 Sizes of Atoms and Ions

Translating electron clouds into a measurable radius

Because atoms have fuzzy boundaries, we define radii using internuclear distances: bonding (covalent) radii use half the distance in a shared pair, while van der Waals radii use the distance between nonbonded neighbors.

  • Atoms get larger down a group as electrons occupy higher principal quantum numbers.
  • Atomic radius drops across a period because Zeff grows, pulling electrons inward.
  • Cations contract (fewer electrons, stronger pull) and anions expand (extra electron-electron repulsion), and isoelectronic series shrink as Z increases.

7.5 Electron Affinity

Energy released when an atom gains an electron

Electron affinity is the energy change when a gaseous atom captures an electron; negative values mean energy is released.

  • EA tends to become more negative across a period as Zeff grows.
  • Down a group, EA becomes less negative because added shells make the electron feel less attraction and more repulsion.
  • Some atoms (Group 2, noble gases, and Group 15) have unusually small or positive EA values due to stable configurations.

7.6 Metals, Nonmetals, and Metalloids

How periodic position predicts metallic behavior
  • Metals lose electrons easily, conduct electricity, and have basic oxides; nonmetals gain electrons, form covalent bonds, and have acidic oxides.
  • Metalloids sit along the stair-step and show mixed properties (e.g., silicon is brittle yet partially conductive).
  • Metallic character increases down a group and decreases across a period as Zeff strengthens.
