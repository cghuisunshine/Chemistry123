7.0 Overview — Periodic Properties of the Elements

How the periodic table encodes repeating properties

The periodic table was designed so that as you go across or down, certain atomic properties change in regular, repeating ways. These periodic properties include:

  • Effective nuclear charge (Z‚Çëff): the net positive charge felt by valence electrons after accounting for shielding by inner electrons.
  • Atomic and ionic radii: how large atoms and ions are, based on average distances of electrons from the nucleus.
  • Ionization energy: the energy required to remove electrons from gaseous atoms or ions.
  • Electron affinity: the energy change when electrons are added to gaseous atoms.

These microscopic properties explain macroscopic behavior: whether an element is a metal, nonmetal, or metalloid, and how reactive different groups (like alkali metals or halogens) are.

Big picture: periodic trends in attraction between nucleus and electrons drive observed trends in size, energy, and reactivity.

E‚Çë‚Çó = kQ‚ÇÅQ‚ÇÇ / d

Coulomb‚Äôs law tells you how strongly charged particles interact:

  • E‚Çë‚Çó: electrostatic potential energy between charges.
  • Q‚ÇÅ, Q‚ÇÇ: charges (with sign).
  • d: distance between charges.
  • Stronger interaction when charges are larger in magnitude and closer together.

This same idea underlies why small, highly charged nuclei hold electrons tightly (high Z‚Çëff, small radius, high ionization energy) and why big, diffuse atoms hold them more weakly.

As you move across a period from Na to Ar:

  • Z‚Çëff increases ‚Üí atoms get smaller.
  • Ionization energies increase ‚Üí electrons are harder to remove.
  • Electron affinities often become more negative ‚Üí atoms more ‚Äúwilling‚Äù to gain electrons.

As you move down a group from Li to Cs:

  • Atoms get larger because electrons occupy higher principal levels (n).
  • Ionization energies drop ‚Üí valence electrons are more easily removed.
  • Metallic character increases; elements become more reactive metals in Groups 1 and 2.

Use this overview as a map: every later section (Z‚Çëff, size, IE, EA, metals/nonmetals) is a deeper dive into one of these trend patterns.

7.1 Development of the Periodic Table

How arranging elements by atomic number revealed periodicity

As more elements were discovered in the 19th century, chemists began to see patterns in their properties. In 1869, Mendeleev and Meyer independently proposed that:

  • If you arrange elements in order of increasing atomic weight, certain chemical and physical properties repeat periodically.
  • Elements with similar properties should be grouped in the same columns (families/groups).

Mendeleev went further by:

  • Leaving blank spaces where no known element fit, predicting the existence and properties of elements not yet discovered (e.g., eka-silicon ‚Üí germanium).
  • Reordering elements when their properties demanded it, even if atomic weight order was broken.

Later, Moseley (1913) showed that the fundamental ordering principle is atomic number (Z), not atomic mass. Arranging elements by Z fixes anomalies and gives the modern periodic law.

Modern periodic law:

When elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number, their physical and chemical properties show a periodic pattern.

Atomic number (Z):

  • Defined as the number of protons in the nucleus.
  • Moseley used X-ray frequencies of elements to show each has a unique Z that increases in integer steps.

Using atomic number rather than atomic mass:

  • Places Ar (Z = 18) before K (Z = 19) even though Ar has a higher atomic mass.
  • Ensures that elements line up with others sharing similar valence electron configurations and properties.

Mendeleev‚Äôs prediction for eka-silicon.

  • Predicted an element below Si (‚Äúeka-silicon‚Äù) with specific atomic weight, density, and formulas like XO‚ÇÇ and XCl‚ÇÑ.
  • When germanium was discovered, its actual properties (e.g., GeO‚ÇÇ, GeCl‚ÇÑ, density ~4.7 g/cm¬≥) matched Mendeleev‚Äôs predictions very closely.

Ar vs K order problem.

  • Atomic masses: Ar > K, but properties of Ar match Ne and Kr (noble gases), not Na and Rb.
  • By Z: Ar (18) ‚Üí noble gas column; K (19) ‚Üí alkali metal column. This ordering matches chemical behavior.

Historical take-away: the periodic table is not arbitrary. Its structure reflects deep patterns in atomic structure (Z and electron configuration).

7.2 Effective Nuclear Charge (Z‚Çëff)

How shielding modifies nuclear attraction on valence electrons

In a many-electron atom, each electron feels both:

  • Attraction to the positively charged nucleus.
  • Repulsion from other electrons, which ‚Äúscreen‚Äù it from the full nuclear charge.

We define the effective nuclear charge, Z‚Çëff, as the net positive charge experienced by an electron after accounting for screening:

Think of Z‚Çëff as ‚Äúhow strongly the nucleus actually pulls on a given electron.‚Äù

Key trends:

  • Valence electrons feel a much lower Z‚Çëff than core electrons do.
  • Z‚Çëff on valence electrons increases across a period (left ‚Üí right).
  • Z‚Çëff changes only modestly down a group because additional shells increase both Z and shielding.
Z‚Çëff = Z ‚àí S

Where:

  • Z is the nuclear charge (atomic number).
  • S is the screening constant, representing how much other electrons reduce the net attraction.

Simple approximation (used in this chapter):

  • Treat S ‚âà number of core electrons.
  • Valence electrons are assumed to contribute little to S in this simple model.

Refinement via Slater‚Äôs rules (qualitative idea):

  • Electrons in the same shell partially screen (e.g., 0.35 each).
  • Electrons in the next inner shell (n ‚àí 1) screen more strongly (0.85 each).
  • Electrons in deeper shells (n ‚àí 2 and lower) screen almost fully (1.00 each).

The exact numbers are less important than this concept: core electrons shield strongly; valence electrons do not.

Lithium vs beryllium.

  • Li: 1s¬≤2s¬π ‚Üí 3 protons, 2 core electrons ‚Üí simple S ‚âà 2 ‚áí Z‚Çëff ‚âà 1‚Å∫ for the 2s electron.
  • Be: 1s¬≤2s¬≤ ‚Üí 4 protons, 2 core electrons ‚Üí S ‚âà 2 ‚áí Z‚Çëff ‚âà 2‚Å∫ for each 2s electron.
  • Be‚Äôs valence electrons feel a stronger pull than Li‚Äôs; Be is smaller and has a higher ionization energy.

Across a period: Na ‚Üí Mg ‚Üí Al ‚Üí ‚Ä¶ ‚Üí Ar.

  • Core electrons (Ne-like) remain the same.
  • Nuclear charge Z increases from 11 to 18.
  • Z‚Çëff on valence electrons increases steadily ‚Üí valence shell pulled closer to nucleus, radii shrink, and ionization energies rise.

Down a group (Li ‚Üí Na ‚Üí K):

  • New shells are added; valence electrons are further from the nucleus.
  • Shielding also increases, so Z‚Çëff increases only slightly.
  • Net effect: valence electrons are easier to remove down the group (lower ionization energies).

When in doubt about a periodic trend, ask: ‚ÄúHow is Z‚Çëff changing?‚Äù It‚Äôs the underlying driver for size, IE, and EA trends.

7.3 Sizes of Atoms and Ions

How we define and compare atomic/ionic radii

Atoms do not end sharply; their electron density fades gradually. We define practical sizes from internuclear distances:

  • Nonbonding atomic radius (van der Waals radius): half the distance between nuclei of atoms that are close but not bonded.
  • Bonding atomic radius (covalent radius): half the distance between nuclei in a covalent bond (smaller than nonbonding radius).

Unless specified otherwise, ‚Äúatomic radius‚Äù in this chapter usually means the bonding atomic radius.

Key trends for atomic radius:

  • Increases as you move down a group (more electron shells, higher n).
  • Decreases as you move across a period (left ‚Üí right) because Z‚Çëff increases and pulls electrons in.

For ions:

  • Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (lost electrons ‚Üí less repulsion, often a shell lost).
  • Anions are larger than their parent atoms (gained electrons ‚Üí more repulsion, electron cloud expands).
r(atom) = ¬Ω √ó d(bond length between identical atoms)

This definition applies to covalent radii: measure the distance between nuclei in a homonuclear bond and divide by two.

Ionic radii trends:

  • For ions with the same charge, radius increases down a group (higher n).
  • Isoelectronic series: ions with the same number of electrons; radius decreases as nuclear charge increases.

For example, in an isoelectronic series:

  • More protons ‚Üí stronger attraction ‚Üí smaller radius.
  • Fewer protons ‚Üí weaker attraction ‚Üí larger radius.

Example: atomic radius across a period.

  • B, C, N, O, F all lie in the same period.
  • Measured covalent radii show: C radius < B; O radius < N, etc.
  • This systematic decrease is due to increasing Z‚Çëff pulling valence electrons closer.

Cations vs atoms vs anions (Figure 7.8 trend).

  • Na ‚Üí Na‚Å∫: losing one electron reduces electron‚Äìelectron repulsion and often removes the outer shell, so Na‚Å∫ is much smaller than Na.
  • Cl ‚Üí Cl‚Åª: gaining an electron increases repulsion among valence electrons, causing the electron cloud to expand; Cl‚Åª is larger than Cl.

Example: isoelectronic series O¬≤‚Åª, F‚Åª, Na‚Å∫, Mg¬≤‚Å∫, Al¬≥‚Å∫.

  • All have 10 electrons; nuclear charges are 8, 9, 11, 12, 13.
  • Order of decreasing size: O¬≤‚Åª > F‚Åª > Na‚Å∫ > Mg¬≤‚Å∫ > Al¬≥‚Å∫.
  • Al¬≥‚Å∫ has the highest Z for the same number of electrons, so its radius is smallest.

When you see ions with the same electron count, list them by increasing Z to quickly predict which is smallest or largest.

7.4 Ionization Energy

Energy required to remove electrons & periodic trends

Ionization energy (IE) is the energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom or ion:

  • First ionization energy, I‚ÇÅ: X(g) ‚Üí X‚Å∫(g) + e‚Åª.
  • Second ionization energy, I‚ÇÇ: X‚Å∫(g) ‚Üí X¬≤‚Å∫(g) + e‚Åª, etc.

Trends in I‚ÇÅ:

  • Generally increases across a period (left ‚Üí right).
  • Generally decreases down a group.
  • Shows sharp jumps when moving from valence electrons to core electrons (after valence electrons are removed).

Ionization energy is strongly linked to Z‚Çëff and atomic radius: stronger nuclear attraction and smaller radius ‚Üí higher IE.

X(g) ‚Üí X‚Å∫(g) + e‚Åª (ŒîH = I‚ÇÅ)

IE is always positive because you must input energy to remove an attracted electron.

Key patterns:

  • Within a period: Z‚Çëff increases and radius decreases ‚Üí I‚ÇÅ rises.
  • Within a group: outer electrons are further from the nucleus and more shielded ‚Üí I‚ÇÅ falls.
  • Transition metals show more gradual variation in I‚ÇÅ; s- and p-block elements show stronger stepwise trends.

Successive IEs reveal shell structure:

  • Each additional IE is larger than the last (removing an electron from a more positive ion).
  • A massive jump in IE indicates removal of a core electron.

Subtle anomalies across a period.

  • Be (2s¬≤) vs B (2s¬≤2p¬π): B‚Äôs 2p electron is higher in energy and less tightly held than Be‚Äôs 2s electrons ‚Üí I‚ÇÅ(B) < I‚ÇÅ(Be).
  • N (2p¬≥) vs O (2p‚Å¥): in O, one 2p orbital has a paired set of electrons, increasing repulsion and making it slightly easier to remove one ‚Üí I‚ÇÅ(O) < I‚ÇÅ(N).

Huge jumps show core electrons.

  • Table values for Na: I‚ÇÅ = 496 kJ/mol, I‚ÇÇ = 4562 kJ/mol.
  • I‚ÇÅ removes the 3s valence electron; I‚ÇÇ removes a 2p core electron.
  • The enormous jump between I‚ÇÅ and I‚ÇÇ signals that Na has one valence electron; the rest are core.

Example ordering: Ne, Na, P, Ar, K.

  • I‚ÇÅ increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  • Order of increasing I‚ÇÅ: K < Na < P < Ar < Ne.

If you see a step in IE after removing n electrons, that usually means the element has n valence electrons (its group in the s/p blocks).

7.5 Electron Affinity

Energy change when atoms gain electrons

Electron affinity (EA) is the energy change when a neutral gaseous atom gains an electron to form an anion:

X(g) + e‚Åª ‚Üí X‚Åª(g)

Sign convention in this chapter:

  • Negative EA ‚Üí energy released (favorable, exothermic process).
  • Positive EA ‚Üí energy absorbed (unfavorable process).

Trends are less smooth than for ionization energies, but key patterns include:

  • Halogens have highly negative EAs (strong tendency to gain one electron).
  • Group 2 and Group 18 elements often have positive or less negative EAs because the added electron must go into a higher-energy or previously empty subshell.
  • Group 15 elements (like N, P) often have less negative EAs than neighbors due to the stability of half-filled p subshells.
Cl(g) + e‚Åª ‚Üí Cl‚Åª(g) (ŒîH = EA(Cl))

For Cl, EA is significantly negative (energy released when Cl forms Cl‚Åª).

Relationship to ionization of the anion:

Reversing the EA process gives the ionization of the anion:

  • Cl‚Åª(g) ‚Üí Cl(g) + e‚Åª has ŒîH = ‚àíEA(Cl).
  • So the first ionization energy of Cl‚Åª equals the magnitude of EA(Cl) but with opposite sign.

Qualitative EA trends:

  • Most negative for halogens (Group 17).
  • Less negative (or even positive) for Group 15 and Group 2 because electron must enter a higher-energy or partially filled subshell, increasing repulsions.
  • Often positive for noble gases because the added electron must go into a new shell.

Halogens: F, Cl, Br, I.

  • All are one electron short of ns¬≤np‚Å∂; gaining one electron gives a noble-gas-like configuration.
  • Thus, EAs are strongly negative; Cl is a classic case with one of the most negative EA values.

Group 2: Be, Mg.

  • Valence configuration ns¬≤ (full s subshell).
  • Adding an electron forces it into a higher-energy p subshell; this is unfavorable, so EA is positive or only slightly negative.

Group 15: N, P, As.

  • Valence p¬≥ (half-filled p subshell), which is relatively stable.
  • Adding an electron requires pairing in a p orbital, increasing e‚Åª‚Äìe‚Åª repulsion; EA is less negative than for neighbors.

Noble gases: Ne, Ar, etc.

  • Filled valence shell; adding an electron means going to a new shell with higher n.
  • EA is positive; gaseous noble gas atoms do not readily gain electrons.

If you see a filled or half-filled subshell, expect electron affinity to become less favorable (less negative or positive).

7.6 Metals, Nonmetals, and Metalloids

Classifying elements and their oxides

The periodic table is broadly divided into:

  • Metals (left and center): shiny, malleable, ductile, good conductors, tend to form cations.
  • Nonmetals (upper-right): dull, brittle (if solid), poor conductors, tend to form anions or covalent compounds.
  • Metalloids (border line): intermediate properties, often semiconductors (e.g., Si, Ge).

Metallic character increases down a group and decreases across a period (left ‚Üí right). This is the inverse trend of ionization energy.

Oxides often reveal metallic vs nonmetallic behavior:

  • Most metal oxides are basic and ionic.
  • Most nonmetal oxides are acidic and molecular.

Metal oxides are basic.

  • In water: metal oxide + water ‚Üí metal hydroxide.
  • With acid: metal oxide + acid ‚Üí salt + water.
Na‚ÇÇO(s) + H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚Üí 2NaOH(aq)
NiO(s) + 2HNO‚ÇÉ(aq) ‚Üí Ni(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ(aq) + H‚ÇÇO(l)

Nonmetal oxides are acidic.

  • In water: nonmetal oxide + water ‚Üí acid.
  • With base: nonmetal oxide + base ‚Üí salt + water.
CO‚ÇÇ(g) + H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ(aq)
CO‚ÇÇ(g) + 2NaOH(aq) ‚Üí Na‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ(aq) + H‚ÇÇO(l)

Metallic character trend.

  • Moving down a group (e.g., from C to Pb in Group 14), elements become more metallic.
  • Moving across a period from left to right, metallic character decreases: Na (metal) ‚Üí Mg (metal) ‚Üí Al (metal) ‚Üí Si (metalloid) ‚Üí P, S, Cl (nonmetals).

Nonmetal oxides and acid rain.

  • Sulfur in fuels burns to SO‚ÇÇ and SO‚ÇÉ; these dissolve in water to give acids (H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÉ, H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ).
  • These acidic solutions fall as acid rain, affecting lakes, soils, and buildings.

Metalloids as semiconductors.

  • Silicon (Si) has a metallic-looking luster but is brittle and only moderately conducts electricity.
  • By adding small amounts of dopants, its conductivity can be tuned, making it ideal for integrated circuits and computer chips.

If an oxide reacts with acid to make salt + water, it‚Äôs behaving as a base (typical of metals). If it reacts with base to make salt + water, it‚Äôs behaving as an acid (typical of nonmetals).

7.7 Trends for Group 1 and Group 2 Metals

Alkali and alkaline earth metals: structures, reactivity, patterns

Group 1: Alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs):

  • Valence configuration: ns¬π.
  • Very low ionization energies; extremely reactive metals.
  • Soft, low-melting, easily oxidized; react violently with water and halogens.
  • Form M‚Å∫ ions and ionic compounds like NaCl, KBr.

Group 2: Alkaline earth metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba):

  • Valence configuration: ns¬≤.
  • Higher ionization energies than Group 1 but still relatively low.
  • Harder, denser, higher melting points than alkali metals.
  • Form M¬≤‚Å∫ ions and ionic compounds like MgO, CaCl‚ÇÇ.

Reactivity trends:

  • Both groups become more reactive down the group as valence electrons are more loosely held.

Alkali metals with water:

2M(s) + 2H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚Üí 2MOH(aq) + H‚ÇÇ(g)

Releases hydrogen gas and produces a basic solution (metal hydroxide).

Alkaline earth metals with water:

  • Be: no reaction with water or steam.
  • Mg: slow with liquid water, faster with steam:
Mg(s) + H‚ÇÇO(g) ‚Üí MgO(s) + H‚ÇÇ(g)
  • Ca and heavier: react readily with water at room temperature:
Ca(s) + 2H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚Üí Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ(aq) + H‚ÇÇ(g)

Reactions with halogens:

  • Alkali metals: 2M(s) + Cl‚ÇÇ(g) ‚Üí 2MCl(s).
  • Alkaline earth: M(s) + Cl‚ÇÇ(g) ‚Üí MCl‚ÇÇ(s).

Reactions with oxygen:

  • Alkali metals often form oxides, peroxides, or superoxides depending on the metal.
  • Alkaline earth metals form simple oxides (e.g., MgO, CaO).

Vigorous reactions of alkali metals.

  • Na with water: bright flame, H‚ÇÇ gas, hot basic solution of NaOH.
  • Reactivity increases from Li to Cs because valence electrons are more weakly bound (lower IE) in larger atoms.

Calcium with water (text example).

  • Ca(s) bubbles vigorously when immersed in water.
  • Bubbles are H‚ÇÇ gas; the solution contains Ca¬≤‚Å∫ and OH‚Åª (Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ).

Magnesium and calcium in ionic compounds.

  • MgO and CaO are basic oxides used in tonnage quantities as building materials and in industry.
  • Mg and Ca are essential cations in biology (bones, teeth, muscle function).

Recognize that both Group 1 and 2 metals ‚Äúwant‚Äù to reach a noble-gas electron configuration by losing 1 or 2 electrons respectively, which drives their chemistry.

7.8 Trends for Selected Nonmetals

Hydrogen & the oxygen group as models for nonmetal behavior

Hydrogen occupies a unique position:

  • Electron configuration 1s¬π, similar to alkali metals‚Äô ns¬π, but behaves as a nonmetal.
  • Colorless, diatomic gas (H‚ÇÇ) under normal conditions.
  • Ionization energy is high compared to metals, so it tends to share electrons rather than lose them completely.
  • Can form H‚Å∫ (proton) in acids or H‚Åª (hydride) in metal hydrides; very versatile.

Group 16 (Oxygen family: O, S, Se, Te, Po):

  • As you go down the group, elements shift from nonmetallic behavior (O, S, Se) to metalloid (Te) to metallic (Po).
  • Common valence configuration ns¬≤np‚Å¥; often form 2‚Åª anions or share two electrons in covalent bonds.
  • Oxides and hydrides (e.g., H‚ÇÇO, H‚ÇÇS) display a range of acid‚Äìbase and bonding behaviors that change with size and metallic character.

  • H‚ÇÇ + N‚ÇÇ ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ (ammonia, via Haber process under specific conditions).
  • H‚ÇÇ + C (various) ‚Üí hydrocarbons (e.g., CH‚ÇÑ).

  • With very electropositive metals, H can gain an electron to form H‚Åª in ionic metal hydrides (MH, where M is an alkali metal).

  • Heavier chalcogens (Se, Te, Po) follow similar patterns but with increasing metallic character and different oxidation states.

  • ¬ΩH‚ÇÇ(g) + ¬ΩCl‚ÇÇ(g) ‚Üí HCl(g): electrons are shared in a polar covalent bond.

  • More realistically, H‚Å∫ is attached to water as H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫, but for stoichiometry we often write H‚Å∫(aq).
