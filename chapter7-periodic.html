Chapter 7: Periodic Properties of the Elements

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Explain how electron configuration and effective nuclear charge lead to periodic trends.
  • Describe and compare trends in atomic radius, ionic radius, ionization energy, and electron affinity.
  • Recognize metallic vs nonmetallic behavior and how it changes across a period and down a group.
  • Use periodic trends to predict relative reactivity for selected families (alkali metals, halogens).

Key Vocabulary

  • Effective nuclear charge (Zeff): net positive charge experienced by valence electrons after accounting for shielding.
  • Atomic radius: size of an atom, often defined as half the distance between two bonded nuclei.
  • Ionic radius: size of an ion; cations are usually smaller than their parent atoms, anions larger.
  • Ionization energy (IE): energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom or ion.
  • Electron affinity (EA): energy change when a gaseous atom gains an electron.
  • Isoelectronic series: atoms/ions with the same number of electrons but different nuclear charges.

1. Effective Nuclear Charge and Shielding

  • Core electrons “shield” valence electrons from the full nuclear charge.
  • Across a period, nuclear charge increases while shielding changes only slightly → Zeff increases → electrons pulled closer to nucleus.
  • Down a group, added shells increase distance and shielding → outer electrons feel weaker attraction on average.

2. Trend: Atomic and Ionic Radius

  • Atomic radius decreases left → right across a period (increasing Zeff).
  • Atomic radius increases down a group (more shells, larger distance from nucleus).
  • Cations are smaller than corresponding neutral atoms (loss of electron shell and stronger pull on remaining electrons).
  • Anions are larger than neutral atoms (more electron–electron repulsion for same nucleus).

Worked Example 1 – Comparing Radii

Arrange N, O, and F in order of increasing atomic radius.

  1. All in period 2; Z increases from N (7) → O (8) → F (9).
  2. Higher Z → higher Zeff → smaller radius.
  3. Order: F < O < N (smallest to largest).

3. Trend: Ionization Energy and Electron Affinity

  • Ionization energy generally increases left → right (harder to remove electrons from smaller, tightly held orbitals).
  • Ionization energy decreases down a group (outer electrons farther from nucleus, more shielding).
  • Electron affinity often becomes more negative across a period (atoms more eager to gain electrons) with some irregularities.

Worked Example 2 – Comparing First Ionization Energies

Which has the higher first ionization energy: Mg or Al?

  1. General trend would suggest Al (further right) has higher IE.
  2. However, Al starts filling the 3p subshell, which is slightly higher in energy than 3s.
  3. It is slightly easier to remove a 3p electron from Al than a 3s electron from Mg → Mg actually has the slightly higher IE.

4. Reactivity Patterns

  • Alkali metals (Group 1): low IE, large radii → very reactive metals; reactivity increases down the group.
  • Halogens (Group 17): high electron affinity, strong oxidizing agents; reactivity tends to decrease down the group as orbitals get larger.

Worked Example 3 – Reactivity Comparison

Which metal is more reactive with water, Li or Cs?

  1. Down Group 1, IE decreases and radius increases.
  2. Cs loses its valence electron more easily than Li.
  3. Cs is therefore more reactive with water than Li.

5. Isoelectronic Series

  • Example: O2−, F−, Na+, Mg2+ all have 10 electrons.
  • Nuclear charge increases from O (Z = 8) → Mg (Z = 12).
  • As Z increases for the same number of electrons, the radius decreases; O2− is largest, Mg2+ smallest.

6. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Arrange the following in order of increasing atomic radius: N, O, C, F, B.
  2. Which atom in each pair has the higher first ionization energy? (a) Na or K; (b) Mg or Ca; (c) Cl or S.
  3. Rank the ions O2−, F−, Na+, Mg2+ in order of increasing radius. Explain your reasoning.
  4. Explain why metals tend to form cations while nonmetals tend to form anions using periodic trends in IE and EA.
  5. Predict qualitatively which is the stronger oxidizing agent, F2 or Br2, and justify using periodic trends.
