Chapter 7: Periodic Properties of the Elements

7.1 Development of the Periodic Table

Key ideas

  • By 1869, chemists such as Mendeleev and Meyer noticed that arranging known elements by increasing atomic weight caused patterns in chemical and physical behavior to repeat periodically.
  • Mendeleev left blank spaces for undiscovered elements and predicted specific properties for eka-elements (e.g., eka-silicon) so that each column grouped similar elements together.
  • Moseley later tied the ordering to atomic number, the number of protons, which explains why elements such as Ar and K switch places compared to an atomic-weight sequence.

Important formulas & their meaning

Modern periodic law Elements ordered by increasing Z display repeating patterns in their properties.

The atomic number Z is the count of protons, so arranging atoms by Z aligns families with the same electron configurations; the “periodicity” refers to the repeating rise and fall of properties such as reactivity, radii, and ionization energy.

Atomic number Z = number of protons in the nucleus

Protons carry a +1 charge, so Z also equals the positive charge that holds the electrons in place; it is a dimensionless integer that uniquely identifies each element.

Short example(s)

Mendeleev predicted an element below silicon (eka-silicon) with density ≈ 4.7 g/cm³ and oxide formulas XO₂ and XCl₄; when germanium was found, it matched his values closely. Ordering by Z also puts Ar (Z=18) among noble gases and K (Z=19) with the alkali metals, matching their observed chemistry.

7.2 Effective Nuclear Charge

Key ideas

  • Each electron feels attraction from the positive nucleus and repulsion from the other electrons; repulsions act like a “screen” that weakens the pull.
  • The effective nuclear charge Zeff is the net positive pull a valence electron actually experiences after accounting for screening.
  • Zeff increases steadily across a period (more protons raise Z while shielding stays nearly constant) and rises only slightly down a group because extra shells add distance and additional screening.

Important formulas & their meaning

Coulomb interaction (guiding idea) E = k Q₁ Q₂ / d

Energy E (in joules) is proportional to the product of charges Q₁ and Q₂ and inversely proportional to distance d between them; larger charge or shorter distance means a stronger attraction or repulsion, so electrons closer to a powerful nucleus bind more tightly.

Effective nuclear charge Zeff = Z − S

Z is the actual nuclear charge (# of protons), and S is the shielding constant reflecting how much other electrons cancel the attraction. Both Z and S are dimensionless counts, so Zeff is also dimensionless. Core electrons screen almost fully (≈1.00 each), electrons in the same subshell screen partially (≈0.35 each), and electrons in the n−1 shell screen quite well (≈0.85 each) under Slater’s rules.

Short example(s)

In sodium, the 3s valence electron “sees” Z=11 and about S≈10 from the ten core electrons, so the simple Zeff estimate is 1. However, because the 3s orbital can spend some time near the core, the true S is closer to 8.5, giving Zeff≈2.5; this stronger pull explains why Na still binds its valence electron fairly tightly. Across a period (Na → Ar) cores stay the same while Z grows, so Zeff climbs and radii shrink.

7.3 Sizes of Atoms and Ions

Key ideas

  • The atomic radius is not a sharp edge; chemists define it by distances between nuclei: the bonding (covalent) radius is half a bond length, and the van der Waals radius is half the distance between nonbonded neighbors.
  • Atomic radius increases down a group because electrons occupy higher principal quantum numbers (n) and decreases across a period because rising Zeff pulls valence electrons closer.
  • Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (fewer electrons and stronger pull), and anions are larger (extra electrons push each other apart). Isoelectronic ions shrink as Z increases because the same electrons feel a stronger pull.

Important formulas & their meaning

Bonding radius definition r(atom) ≈ ½ × d(A–A bond)

The distance d between two identical atoms forming a single bond is measured (often in picometers, pm); halving it gives an estimate of how close the electron cloud extends from the nucleus.

Isoelectronic radius trend radius ∝ 1 / Zeff (for constant electron count)

When atoms or ions share the same number of electrons, increasing the nuclear charge Z increases Zeff, squeezing electrons in tighter and reducing the radius even though the electron count stays fixed.

Short example(s)

Within Period 2, atomic radii shrink from N to O to F because more protons raise Zeff. In the isoelectronic series O²⁻, F⁻, Na⁺, Mg²⁺ (all 10 electrons), the radii drop as nuclear charge climbs from 8 to 12.

7.4 Ionization Energy

Key ideas

  • The first ionization energy is the energy needed to remove one electron from a gaseous atom; each successive ionization energy removes another electron from an ever-more-positively charged ion and therefore requires more energy.
  • Ionization energy generally increases across a period (smaller radius and higher Zeff make electrons harder to remove) and decreases down a group as valence electrons are farther from the nucleus.
  • Subshell structure causes anomalies: removing a 3p electron from Al is slightly easier than removing a 3s electron from Mg even though Al lies to the right of Mg.

Important formulas & their meaning

First ionization X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻

The energy required (IE₁, in kilojoules per mole) is always positive because work must be done to overcome the attraction between the electron and the nucleus; X(g) is the neutral atom, and e⁻ is the removed electron.

Successive ionization X⁺(g) → X²⁺(g) + e⁻

The second ionization energy IE₂ removes another electron from an already positive ion, so IE₂ > IE₁. When IE jumps dramatically, it usually signals that the electron is being taken from a lower principal shell.

Short example(s)

Magnesium has a higher IE₁ than aluminum because Mg removes a 3s electron (closer to the nucleus), while Al removes a more energetic 3p electron; the 3s orbital is held tighter despite Mg being left of Al.

7.5 Electron Affinity

Key ideas

  • Electron affinity is the energy change when a gaseous atom accepts an electron; negative values mean energy is released, so the process is exothermic.
  • Affinity generally becomes more negative across a period because higher Zeff makes atoms more eager to gain another electron; it gets less negative down a group as atoms get larger and add electron repulsion.
  • Exceptions occur: Group 2 and noble gases have near-zero or positive affinities, and Group 15 atoms (like N) resist pairing electrons and therefore have less negative values.

Important formulas & their meaning

Electron affinity process X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g)

EA (in kJ/mol) is the energy difference ΔE between the left and right sides. A negative EA means the atom releases energy when capturing an electron, while a positive EA means energy must be supplied.

Short example(s)

Chlorine releases about −349 kJ/mol when it gains an electron because completing its 3p⁵ shell is stabilizing. Sulfur gains an electron less easily than expected because the added electron must pair up in a 3p orbital, increasing repulsion, so its EA is not as negative.

7.6 Metals, Nonmetals, and Metalloids

Key ideas

  • Metals (left side) are shiny, conductive, and form cations easily because they have low ionization energies; nonmetals (right side) gain electrons, form anions or covalent bonds, and their oxides tend to be acidic.
  • Metalloids sit along the diagonal and show mixed behavior; their bonding and conductivity place them between metals and nonmetals.
  • Metallic character increases down a group (electrons are lost more readily), and decreases across a period as Zeff tightens hold on the electrons.

Important formulas & their meaning

Metal oxide + water M₂O(s) + H₂O(l) → 2MOH(aq)

Alkali and alkaline earth oxides dissolve to give hydroxides (strong bases) because metal cations disperse into solution and release OH⁻ ions.

Nonmetal oxide + water SO₂(g) + H₂O(l) → H₂SO₃(aq)

Nonmetal oxides usually dissolve to form acidic solutions; additional oxidation (SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄) yields stronger acids.

Short example(s)

Sodium oxide dissolves to make NaOH, so sodium behaves as a metal with basic oxides. Sulfur dioxide forms sulfurous acid, illustrating how nonmetal oxides give acids.

7.7 Trends for Group 1 and Group 2 Metals

Key ideas

  • Group 1 (ns¹) and Group 2 (ns²) elements have low ionization energies and readily form M⁺ or M²⁺ ions to reach noble-gas configurations.
  • Reactivity increases down each group because valence electrons sit in larger, more shielded orbitals.
  • These metals react with water, halogens, and oxygen to form ionic compounds and release hydrogen, halide salts, or oxides.

Important formulas & their meaning

Alkali metals + water 2M(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2MOH(aq) + H₂(g)

Each metal donates an electron to hydrogen, producing hydrogen gas and a soluble hydroxide.

Alkaline earth + water M(s) + 2H₂O(l) → M(OH)₂(aq) + H₂(g)

Group 2 metals behave similarly but deliver two positive charges, so they form hydroxides with M²⁺.

Metal + chlorine 2M(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2MCl(s)

Halogens grab electrons easily, so the metals transfer electrons to form ionic chlorides (MCl₂ for alkaline earth metals uses the same idea with two electrons).

Short example(s)

Cesium reacts more violently with water than lithium because its valence electron sits farther from the nucleus (lower IE), so Cs loses it faster and releases more heat and hydrogen.

7.8 Trends for Selected Nonmetals

Key ideas

  • Hydrogen has a 1s¹ configuration like alkali metals but usually behaves as a nonmetal, forming covalent bonds or serving as a proton (H⁺) when paired with very electronegative atoms.
  • Group 16 (ns²np⁴) starts with strongly nonmetallic oxygen and shifts toward metalloid and metallic behavior (tellurium, polonium) as atoms grow larger and less electronegative.
  • Nonmetal oxides and hydrides often form acids or share electrons to make covalent molecules.

Important formulas & their meaning

Hydrogen chloride formation H₂(g) + Cl₂(g) → 2HCl(g)

The atoms share electrons in a polar covalent bond; the resulting molecule can dissolve in water.

Acid dissociation HCl(g) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

In water, the bond polarizes so HCl releases a proton, behaving as a strong acid.

Sulfur oxides + water SO₂(g) + H₂O(l) → H₂SO₃(aq)

SO₂ forms sulfurous acid, while SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ gives sulfuric acid; heavier chalcogens follow similar patterns with less acidic oxides.

Short example(s)

Sodium plus chlorine makes ionic NaCl, while hydrogen plus chlorine gives covalent HCl, showing hydrogen’s nonmetallic character. Burning sulfur in oxygen gives SO₂, which dissolves to form acids that contribute to acid rain.
