Week 1 – Acid–Base & General Review

Large grain: Chem 123 is built on acid–base and equilibrium language. This week rebuilds that language so thermo/kinetics make sense later.
pH / pOH Kw Strong vs weak acids/bases Conjugate pairs ICE tables

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • Remember how solutions behave: acids donate H⁺, bases accept H⁺.
  • Use pH, pOH, and Kw as your “language of water”.
  • Bridge Chem 11/12 & Chem 121 knowledge into Chem 123.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • Water autoionization: Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻].
  • pH, pOH, and pH + pOH = 14 (25 °C).
  • Strong vs weak acids/bases; conjugate acid–base pairs.
  • ICE tables for weak acids/bases and buffers.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Convert between [H⁺], [OH⁻], pH, pOH quickly.
  • Set up ICE tables for HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻ or B + H₂O ⇌ BH⁺ + OH⁻.
  • Identify buffer pairs (weak acid + conjugate base, etc.).
  • Estimate pH of strong acid/base and simple buffers.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • Kw comes from equilibrium of H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻.
  • pH is a log scale: convenient for tiny concentrations.
  • Conjugate pairs differ by exactly one H⁺.
  • Leveling effect: strong acids are “leveled” to H₃O⁺ in water.
Teach-It Tips: Explain pH with a 0–14 number line, then map [H⁺] onto it. Make learners say conjugate pairs out loud: “HA / A⁻”, “NH₃ / NH₄⁺” etc.
Checkpoint: can you do 5 pH/pOH conversions in a row mentally?

Week 2 – Thermodynamics Foundations & First Law

Large grain: describe energy changes in reactions using systems, surroundings, and state functions.
System vs surroundings State variables Internal energy U Work & heat (w, q) Ideal gas ΔU = q + w

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • We want a precise way to talk about energy changes in reactions.
  • Focus on the system (reacting chemicals) vs surroundings.
  • First Law: energy is conserved; it only moves around.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • System vs surroundings; open, closed, isolated systems.
  • State functions (U, P, V, T) vs path functions (q, w).
  • PV work: w = −PΔV (for expansion/compression).
  • Ideal gas law as an approximation for real gases.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Classify processes: energy entering or leaving the system?
  • Use sign conventions: q > 0 (heat into system), w > 0 (work on system).
  • Compute ΔU from q and w, including PV work.
  • Handle simple piston/ideal gas expansion/compression problems.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • Internal energy as microscopic kinetic + potential energy.
  • Why state functions ignore path: only initial/final states matter.
  • PV work as force × distance at molecular level.
Teach-It Tips: Use cartoons of a piston being pushed vs pushing back. Always ask: “From the system’s viewpoint, did energy go in or out?”
Checkpoint: you can assign correct signs to q, w, and ΔU for any basic scenario.

Week 3 – Enthalpy, Calorimetry, Entropy & Laws of Thermo

Large grain: connect heat changes (ΔH) and dispersal of energy/disorder (ΔS) to predict tendencies.
Heat capacity Calorimetry Enthalpy ΔH Hess’s Law Entropy ΔS 2nd & 3rd Laws

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • ΔH tracks heat flow at constant pressure (lab conditions).
  • Entropy measures energy spreading / number of microstates.
  • Thermo laws give global rules for what can/can’t happen.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • q = m·c·ΔT and q = n·Cm·ΔT for calorimetry.
  • ΔH from calorimetry vs from tabulated ΔH°f.
  • Hess’s Law: enthalpy is a state function; add reactions like vectors.
  • Entropy trends: phase, temperature, number of particles, mixing.
  • Third Law: S° → 0 as T → 0 K for perfect crystals.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Calculate q and use it to get ΔHrxn.
  • Use Hess’s Law to build ΔHrxn from other reactions.
  • Estimate ΔS qualitatively from particle pictures.
  • Use S° tables to compute ΔS° for reactions.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • Entropy and multiplicity: S = k ln W (statistical view).
  • Why nature “likes” high-multiplicity (more possible arrangements).
  • Microscopic justification of heat capacity and energy storage modes.
Teach-It Tips: Use phase-change examples: ice → water → steam; ask where S is largest. For Hess’s Law, treat each reaction like a LEGO piece you can flip and add.
Checkpoint: you can solve calorimetry problems and predict relative entropy changes intuitively.

Week 4 – Spontaneity, Gibbs Free Energy & Equilibrium

Large grain: tie together ΔH and ΔS into ΔG to decide if a process is spontaneous and where equilibrium lies.
Gibbs free energy ΔG Spontaneous vs non-spontaneous Equilibrium constant K ΔG° = −RT ln K van’t Hoff plots Intro to rate laws

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • ΔG combines ΔH and ΔS into a single “go/no-go” criterion.
  • Equilibrium is the balance point where ΔG = 0.
  • K tells composition at equilibrium; kinetics tells how fast you get there.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • ΔG = ΔH − TΔS and the four sign cases (“always”, “never”, “T-dependent”).
  • Standard vs non-standard conditions (ΔG vs ΔG°).
  • ΔG° = −RT ln K and ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q.
  • van’t Hoff: ln K vs 1/T slope = −ΔH°/R.
  • Rate laws & orders preview (for kinetics blocks).

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Given ΔH and ΔS, decide spontaneity at a given T.
  • Convert between K and ΔG°.
  • Interpret van’t Hoff plots to find ΔH° and ΔS°.
  • Decide if a reaction is product- or reactant-favoured.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • ΔG as maximum non-PV work available from a process.
  • Equilibrium as a minimum in the Gibbs energy landscape.
  • Boltzmann view: K related to relative microstate probabilities.
Teach-It Tips: Use a 2×2 table of ΔH/ΔS sign combos. Connect to real life: ice melting vs freezing around 0 °C to show T dependence.
Checkpoint: you can quickly go from ΔH, ΔS → ΔG → “spontaneous?” → rough K.

Week 5 – Rate Laws, Integrated Forms & Data Analysis

Large grain: quantify how fast reactions go and how concentration changes with time.
Rate laws Rate constant k Reaction order Integrated laws Half-life Graphical tests

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • Thermo: can it happen? Kinetics: how fast?
  • Rate laws link rate to concentration.
  • Integrated laws predict [A] at any time t.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • Rate expressions from stoichiometry.
  • Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n; meaning of orders m, n.
  • Integrated forms for zero, first, second order.
  • Half-life t½ and its concentration dependence.
  • Method of initial rates; straight-line testing.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Use experiments to find reaction order and k.
  • Pick the right integrated law and compute [A] at time t.
  • Use half-life expressions to check order.
  • Make and interpret plots: [A] vs t, ln[A] vs t, 1/[A] vs t.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • Collision theory: frequency and orientation of collisions.
  • Arrhenius: k = A e^(−Eₐ/RT); T dependence of k.
  • Energy barriers and simple reaction coordinate diagrams.
Teach-It Tips: Keep a “graph decision tree”: which plot is straight? → order. Emphasize careful units and axis labels; that’s where marks are lost.
Checkpoint: you can look at a table of [A] vs t, decide order, and calculate k.

Week 6 – Mechanisms, Rate-Determining Step & Catalysts

Large grain: connect observed rate laws to plausible multi-step mechanisms and understand catalysis.
Elementary steps Molecularity Intermediates Rate-determining step Steady-state Catalysts

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • Real reactions proceed through several microscopic steps.
  • Mechanisms must match both stoichiometry and rate law.
  • Catalysts speed reactions by lowering Eₐ, not by changing ΔG°.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • Elementary vs overall reactions; molecularity.
  • Intermediates vs catalysts in multi-step schemes.
  • Rate-determining step (RDS) as bottleneck.
  • Steady-state and pre-equilibrium approximations.
  • Homogeneous vs heterogeneous catalysis; enzyme analogy.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Given a proposed mechanism, derive the predicted rate law.
  • Check consistency: does mechanism fit experimental rate law?
  • Identify intermediates and catalysts from steps.
  • Draw reaction coordinate diagrams with and without catalyst.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • Transition state theory and activated complexes.
  • Potential energy surfaces and multi-step pathways.
  • Why catalysts change Eₐ and k but not equilibrium K.
Teach-It Tips: Always line up: mechanism → derived rate law → experimental rate law. Use colour when you write mechanisms to track intermediates that cancel.
Checkpoint: you can sketch at least one reasonable mechanism that fits a given rate law.

Week 7 – Organic Structure, Bonding & Resonance

Large grain: move from “physical chem world” into “organic world” – still the same atoms, new style of thinking.
Lewis structures Octet & formal charge VSEPR & 3D shape Hybridization (sp, sp², sp³) Resonance

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • Organic chemistry = carbon-based structures and their reactivity.
  • Good structures → correct charge, geometry, and hybridization.
  • Resonance is about delocalized electrons, not “flipping structures”.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • Drawing Lewis structures with valence electrons and octet rule.
  • Formal charge vs oxidation state; where charge “lives”.
  • VSEPR geometries: linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, etc.
  • Hybridization: sp, sp², sp³ and their bond angles.
  • Resonance structures and resonance hybrids.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Draw correct Lewis structures for organic fragments (C, H, O, N, X).
  • Assign formal charges correctly and choose the best resonance form.
  • Identify geometry and hybridization of atoms in a structure.
  • Draw valid resonance contributors and the resonance hybrid.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • Connection between hybridization and atomic orbitals (s + p mixing).
  • Why resonance lowers energy: electron delocalization.
  • σ vs π bonds; where electrons are located in space.
Teach-It Tips: Always start organic questions with: “Draw the structure cleanly.” Use colour for lone pairs and charges; mark resonance arrows clearly.
Checkpoint: you can look at any small organic molecule and instantly state shapes & hybridization.

Week 8 – Functional Groups & Reactivity Maps

Large grain: memorize the “cast of characters” in organic chemistry and what each likes to do.
Functional groups Alkanes, alkenes, alkynes Alcohols, halides, carbonyls Polarity & electron-rich sites Reactivity maps

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • Functional groups define behaviour; C skeleton is the “frame”.
  • Reactions happen at electron-rich or electron-poor sites.
  • Seeing functional groups = seeing possible reactions.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • Recognize common groups: alkane, alkene, alkyne, alcohol, ether, halide, carbonyl (aldehyde, ketone, carboxylic acid, ester, amide), amine.
  • Electronegativity and bond polarity (δ⁺ vs δ⁻ sites).
  • Nucleophiles vs electrophiles, leaving groups.
  • “Reactivity map” of a molecule: where arrows will start/end.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Circle functional groups in a structure and label them.
  • Identify nucleophilic vs electrophilic atoms.
  • Predict which bonds are easiest to break or attack.
  • Classify carbon centers (primary, secondary, tertiary).

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • How polarity and orbital overlap control reactivity.
  • Why carbonyl C is electrophilic (π* LUMO, resonance with O).
  • Leaving group ability vs conjugate acid strength.
Teach-It Tips: Turn functional groups into a visual “flashcard wall”. Ask: “If I were a nucleophile, where would I attack in this molecule?”
Checkpoint: you can quickly annotate any structure with functional groups and likely reaction sites.

Week 9 – Stereochemistry & 3D Thinking

Large grain: chemistry is 3D; the shape of molecules controls properties and reactivity.
Chirality Stereocenters R/S configuration Enantiomers & diastereomers Newman projections

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • 3D arrangement affects physical properties and biological activity.
  • Chiral molecules can behave differently in chiral environments.
  • Stereochemistry is about tracking 3D information carefully.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • Chiral center: tetrahedral atom with four different groups.
  • R/S naming rules (Cahn–Ingold–Prelog priorities).
  • Enantiomers vs diastereomers vs meso compounds.
  • Newman projections and conformational analysis basics.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Locate stereocenters in a structure.
  • Assign R or S configuration correctly.
  • Determine if two drawings are identical, enantiomers, or diastereomers.
  • Draw simple Newman projections and compare conformer stability.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • How potential energy changes with rotation around σ bonds.
  • Why biology is sensitive to chirality (chiral receptors/enzymes).
  • Relationship between symmetry planes and achirality/meso structures.
Teach-It Tips: Have learners physically build models or use hands as analogies. Make them practise flipping molecules in 3D without losing connectivity.
Checkpoint: you can quickly assign R/S and decide enantiomer vs diastereomer for exam-style questions.

Week 10 – Organic Acid–Base & Mechanistic Thinking

Large grain: use pKa and stability arguments to predict which way protons and electrons move.
pKa scale Conjugate stability Inductive effects Resonance stabilization Arrow-pushing

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • Many organic reactions are acid–base in disguise.
  • Stronger acid → weaker conjugate base and vice versa.
  • Mechanisms = stories about electrons moving to more stable places.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • pKa as a quantitative measure of acidity.
  • Stability factors: atom, resonance, induction, hybridization, solvation.
  • Bronsted vs Lewis acid/base in organic context.
  • Curved arrow notation for mechanisms (arrow starts at electrons).

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Compare relative acidity/basicity using pKa and structural effects.
  • Predict direction of acid–base reactions (which side favoured).
  • Draw simple proton transfer and resonance delocalization mechanisms.
  • Explain why certain intermediates are especially stable or unstable.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • How energy changes along a reaction coordinate for proton transfer.
  • Connection between pKa and ΔG° via Ka and ΔG° = −RT ln K.
  • Orbital view of donating vs accepting electrons (HOMO/LUMO).
Teach-It Tips: Always ask “which conjugate is more stable and why?” before pushing arrows. Have students narrate mechanisms out loud: “lone pair attacks… bond breaks…”.
Checkpoint: you can predict which side of an organic acid–base equilibrium is favoured and justify it.

Week 11 – Substitution & Elimination (SN1 / SN2 / E1 / E2)

Large grain: understand competing pathways when a nucleophile/base meets an alkyl halide.
SN2 SN1 E2 E1 Substrate structure Solvent effects

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • Same starting materials, different pathways → different products.
  • Pathway choice depends on structure, nucleophile/base, solvent, temperature.
  • These mechanisms unify a lot of exam questions in first-year organic.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • SN2: one-step, backside attack, inversion of configuration.
  • SN1: carbocation intermediate, racemization, rearrangements.
  • E2: one-step β-elimination, anti-periplanar geometry.
  • E1: carbocation then elimination; competition with SN1.
  • Factors: primary/secondary/tertiary, strength of nucleophile/base, solvent type.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Predict dominant pathway (SN1/SN2/E1/E2) from conditions.
  • Draw detailed mechanisms with curved arrows and intermediates.
  • Predict stereochemistry of products (inversion, mixtures, etc.).
  • Identify major vs minor elimination products (Zaitsev vs Hofmann).

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • Energy profiles: number of steps, height of barriers.
  • Why tertiary carbocations/stabilized alkenes are favoured.
  • Solvent effects on ion formation vs concerted pathways.
Teach-It Tips: Make a 4-cell summary chart: SN1, SN2, E1, E2 with key conditions. Practise “rapid classification” drills: 10 scenarios, shout out the pathway.
Checkpoint: you can classify any standard SN/E question in under 10 seconds and sketch the mechanism.

Week 12 – Integration, Synthesis & Review

Large grain: connect thermo, kinetics and organic into one mental map; prepare to teach and to write the exam.
Concept map Multi-step problems Thermo + kinetics + mechanism Exam strategy Teaching mini-lessons

Big Picture (Large Grain)

  • All three pillars connect: ΔG (thermo), k (kinetics), mechanism (organic).
  • You’re moving from “course taker” to “course teacher”.
  • Use MG-SF to compress the whole course into a small, teachable model.

Key Concepts (Medium Grain)

  • Link ΔG ↔ K ↔ reaction favourability with mechanisms that achieve it.
  • Recognize how rate laws reflect underlying elementary steps.
  • See organic mechanisms as specific kinetic pathways over an energy surface.
  • Identify your top 10 “anchor concepts” for the whole course.

Skills & Problem Types (Fine Grain)

  • Solve mixed problems that use thermo + kinetics + organic together.
  • Turn each week into a 2–3 minute mini-lesson with one flagship example.
  • Build a one-page cheat sheet summarizing formulas and patterns.
  • Design your own practice questions and solution skeletons.

Deeper Theory (Micro Grain)

  • See the whole course as “energy + probability + structure”.
  • Understand how microscopic collisions and orbitals lead to macroscopic K and k.
  • Reflect on which topics connect most directly to second-year courses.
Teach-It Tips: Create a final “story of the course” slide: start at acid–base, end at mechanisms. Practise explaining each week to an imaginary student in under 2 minutes.
Checkpoint: you can teach a friend the whole course structure without looking at notes.
