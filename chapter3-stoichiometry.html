Chapter 3: Chemical Reactions and Stoichiometry

3.1 The Conservation of Mass, Chemical Equations, and Stoichiometry

Key ideas

  • The law of conservation of mass means atoms are neither created nor destroyed; every balanced equation simply rearranges the same atoms.
  • Chemical equations list reactants on the left, products on the right, and coefficients that reflect relative quantities of molecules or moles in the reaction.
  • Stoichiometry is the study of these numerical relationships so we can predict how much of each substance is consumed or produced.

Important formulas & their meaning

General balanced equation a A + b B → c C + d D

Coefficients a, b, c, and d show how many formula units (and thus how many moles) of each substance participate. Keeping the same number of atoms of each element on both sides keeps mass conserved. The numbers are kept as the smallest whole-number set so the equation stays meaningful.

Short example(s)

Burning methane: CH4 + 2 O2 → CO2 + 2 H2O. Carbon and hydrogen atoms are balanced (1 C, 4 H) and oxygen atoms total four on each side (2 O2 => 4 O, CO2+2 H2O => 4 O), so the equation obeys conservation of mass.

3.2 Simple Patterns of Chemical Reactivity

Key ideas

  • Combination reactions merge two or more reactants to form a single product.
  • Decomposition reactions split a single reactant into two or more products, often after heating.
  • Combustion reactions use O2 to oxidize hydrocarbons (and their derivatives), producing CO2, H2O, and heat.

Important formulas & their meaning

Combination A + B → C

Two substances combine to make one product. Coefficients must match charges (for ionic solids) or molecular counts so mass is conserved. Example: 2 Mg + O2 → 2 MgO demonstrates a metal and nonmetal forming an ionic solid.

Decomposition C → A + B

One reactant breaks apart into two or more products. The total number of each atom appearing in the products equals the count in the original reactant. Example: 2 NaN3 → 2 Na + 3 N2 is used in airbags; each NaN3 yields one Na atom and 1.5 N2 molecules.

Combustion Hydrocarbon + O2 → CO2 + H2O

A hydrocarbon or oxygenated fuel reacts with O2, always forming carbon dioxide and water. The coefficients reflect the composition of the fuel, e.g., C3H8 + 5 O2 → 3 CO2 + 4 H2O.

Short example(s)

Propane combustion (C3H8 + 5 O2 → 3 CO2 + 4 H2O) powers stoves and furnaces. Both reactant and product oxygen counts match (10 O on each side), so the flame obeys stoichiometry.

3.3 Formula Weights and Elemental Compositions

Key ideas

  • The formula weight adds the atomic weights of all atoms in the formula unit (molecular or ionic).
  • Formula weights allow chemists to link a formula to a measurable mass and to compute percent elemental composition.
  • Compositional analysis reveals what fraction of a compound’s mass comes from each element, which helps identify unknowns.

Important formulas & their meaning

Formula weight FW = Σ (number of atoms × atomic weight)

Add the atomic weight of each atom in the formula. Units are atomic mass units (amu). The FW of H2SO4 is 2×1.0 + 32.1 + 4×16.0 = 98.1 amu. For ionic solids (like CaCl2), use the empirical formula instead.

Percent mass composition % element = (atoms × atomic weight / formula weight) × 100%

Shows what percent of the total mass each element contributes. In H2SO4, sulfur contributes (32.1 / 98.1)×100 ≈ 32.7% because one sulfur atom carries 32.1 amu of the 98.1 amu total.

Short example(s)

Sucrose (C12H22O11) has FW = 12×12.0 + 22×1.0 + 11×16.0 = 342.0 amu. The percent hydrogen is (22×1.0 / 342.0)×100 ≈ 6.4%, while oxygen contributes ≈ 51.5%.

3.4 Avogadro’s Number and the Mole; Molar Mass

Key ideas

  • The mole is a counting unit of 6.02×1023 entities (Avogadro’s number), defined so that 1 mol of 12C atoms weighs 12 g.
  • The molar mass (g/mol) equals the formula weight (amu) expressed in grams per mole; it tells the mass of Avogadro’s number of formula units.
  • Mole conversions link measurable masses, microscopic particles, and macroscopic amounts.

Important formulas & their meaning

Moles from mass mol = mass (g) / molar mass (g/mol)

Divide a sample’s mass by the molar mass to find how many moles it contains. The units cancel to leave mol. Example: 5.38 g C6H12O6 ÷ 180 g/mol ≈ 0.0299 mol.

Particles from moles particles = mol × NA

Multiply mol by Avogadro’s number (6.02×1023 mol-1) to get atoms, molecules, or formula units. A 3 g copper penny (molar mass ≈ 63.5 g/mol) holds (3/63.5)×6.02×1023 ≈ 3.0×1022 atoms.

Short example(s)

0.433 mol Ca(NO3)2 weighs 0.433×164.1 g/mol ≈ 71.1 g because the molar mass equals the formula weight of 164.1 amu expressed in g/mol.

3.5 Empirical and Molecular Formulas

Key ideas

  • Empirical formulas give the simplest whole-number ratios of atoms; molecular formulas are whole-number multiples of them.
  • Percent composition data (mass %) can be converted to mole ratios by assuming a 100 g sample.
  • Combustion analysis (CO2 and H2O produced) determines how many moles of C and H were present originally, letting you deduce the empirical formula even with oxygen.

Important formulas & their meaning

Whole-number multiple n = molecular weight / empirical formula weight

Divide the measured molecular weight (from experimentation) by the empirical formula weight; the result must be a whole number. Multiply each empirical subscript by this number to retrieve the molecular formula. Ascorbic acid (empirical C3H4O3, weight 88.0 amu) has molecular weight 176 amu, so n=2 and the molecular formula is C6H8O6.

Short example(s)

Analyzing ascorbic acid: 40.92% C, 4.58% H, 54.50% O gives mole ratios ≈ 3.41 mol C : 4.54 mol H : 3.41 mol O ⇒ empirical C3H4O3. Because the molecular weight is 176 amu, the molecular formula is double that: C6H8O6.

3.6 Reaction Stoichiometry

Key ideas

  • With a balanced equation, coefficients give stoichiometric equivalents for both molecules and moles.
  • Dimensional analysis converts grams of a reactant to moles, uses mole ratios to get moles of a product (or other reactant), then converts back to grams.
  • Every conversion step preserves units, so checking units is a convenient error check.

Important formulas & their meaning

Stoichiometric chain grams A → mol A → mol B → grams B

Use the molar mass of A to convert grams to moles, then apply the mole ratio (from coefficients) to convert to B, then use B’s molar mass to return to grams. All masses are in grams and moles in mol.

Short example(s)

Burning 1.00 g of butane (molar mass 58.0 g/mol) gives 3.03 g CO2. Convert 1.00 g C4H10 to mol (1/58), use the 2:8 mole ratio to get CO2 mol (×8/2), then multiply by 44.0 g/mol to reach 3.03 g CO2.

3.7 Limiting Reactants and Reaction Yields

Key ideas

  • When reactants are not provided in stoichiometric ratios, the reactant that runs out first—the limiting reactant—determines how much product can form.
  • Excess reactants remain after the limiting reactant is consumed, and you can calculate their leftover amounts using mole ratios.
  • Theoretical yield is what you calculate from the limiting reactant, the actual yield is what you physically collect, and percent yield compares them.

Important formulas & their meaning

Percent yield percent yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%

Actual yield (in grams or moles) comes from the experiment. The theoretical yield is computed assuming the limiting reactant fully converts. Percent yield stays below 100% and tells you how much product you recovered compared to the ideal.

Short example(s)

With 3.0 mol N2 and 6.0 mol H2, combustion of ammonia uses H2 as the limiting reactant (needs 3 mol H2 for every mol N2 but only 6 mol H2 is available). The balanced equation gives 2 mol NH3 per 3 mol H2, so you get 4.0 mol NH3. If only 3.5 mol is collected, percent yield is (3.5 / 4.0)×100% = 87.5%.
