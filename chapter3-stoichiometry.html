Chapter 3: Chemical Reactions and Stoichiometry

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Identify and balance common types of chemical reactions.
  • Use the mole concept to relate masses, moles, and particles.
  • Perform stoichiometric calculations using balanced equations.
  • Identify limiting reactants and compute theoretical, actual, and percent yield.

Key Vocabulary

  • Stoichiometry: quantitative relationships between reactants and products in a chemical reaction.
  • Mole: amount of substance containing Avogadro’s number of entities.
  • Molar mass: mass of 1 mol of a substance (g·mol−1).
  • Limiting reactant: reactant that is completely consumed, limiting product formed.
  • Excess reactant: present in greater quantity than needed to react completely.
  • Theoretical yield: maximum product predicted from stoichiometry.
  • Actual yield: amount of product actually obtained.
  • Percent yield: (actual / theoretical) × 100%.

1. Balancing Equations and Reaction Types

  • Balance atoms by adjusting coefficients, not subscripts.
  • Common types: combination, decomposition, combustion, single-replacement, double-replacement, acid–base neutralization.
  • Check that total charge and number of each type of atom match on both sides.

2. Mole Ratios and Stoichiometric Steps

  1. Convert given mass (or volume, particles) to moles using molar mass or Avogadro’s number.
  2. Use coefficients from the balanced equation to convert moles of given species to moles of desired species.
  3. Convert moles of desired species to required units (mass, volume, number of particles).

3. Limiting Reactant and Yields

  • Strategy: compute moles of product from each reactant; the smaller value comes from the limiting reactant.
  • Theoretical yield comes from the limiting reactant only.
  • Percent yield tells how efficient the reaction is compared to the ideal case.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Balance: C3H8 + O2 → CO2 + H2O. Classify the reaction type.
  2. How many moles of CO2 are produced when 0.750 mol of propane (C3H8) burns in excess oxygen?
  3. Calculate the mass of NaCl produced when 25.0 g of Na react with excess Cl2 via 2 Na + Cl2 → 2 NaCl.
  4. For N2 + 3 H2 → 2 NH3, if 5.0 g N2 react with 5.0 g H2, identify the limiting reactant and compute the theoretical yield of NH3.
  5. A reaction has a theoretical yield of 12.0 g but an actual yield of 9.0 g. Calculate the percent yield.
