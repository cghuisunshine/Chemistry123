Chapter 3: Chemical Reactions and Stoichiometry

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Interpret and balance chemical equations, including physical states.
  • Classify common types of reactions (combination, decomposition, combustion, displacement, acid–base, precipitation).
  • Use molar mass and Avogadro’s number to convert between mass, moles, and number of particles.
  • Carry out stoichiometric calculations using balanced equations.
  • Identify the limiting reactant and compute theoretical, actual, and percent yields.

Key Vocabulary

  • Stoichiometry: quantitative relationship between reactants and products in a chemical reaction.
  • Mole: 6.022 × 1023 entities (Avogadro’s number).
  • Molar mass: mass of 1 mol of a substance (g·mol−1).
  • Limiting reactant: reactant that is completely consumed; determines the maximum amount of product.
  • Excess reactant: present in more than enough quantity to react with the limiting reactant.
  • Theoretical yield: maximum amount of product predicted by stoichiometry from the limiting reactant.
  • Actual yield: amount of product actually obtained in an experiment.
  • Percent yield: (actual / theoretical) × 100%.

1. Balancing Chemical Equations

  • Coefficients in a balanced equation show relative numbers of molecules or moles, not masses.
  • Law of conservation of mass: total number of each type of atom must be the same on both sides.

Worked Example 1 – Balancing Combustion of Propane

Balance: C3H8(g) + O2(g) → CO2(g) + H2O(l).

  1. Balance C: 3 C on left → put 3 in front of CO2: C3H8 + O2 → 3 CO2 + H2O.
  2. Balance H: 8 H on left → need 4 H2O on right (4 × 2 = 8): C3H8 + O2 → 3 CO2 + 4 H2O.
  3. Balance O: right side has 3×2 + 4×1 = 10 O atoms → need 5 O2 on left: C3H8 + 5 O2 → 3 CO2 + 4 H2O.

2. Moles, Masses, and Particles

  • Conversions often go through moles:
    • Mass → moles: n = m / Mr.
    • Moles → particles: particles = n × NA.

Worked Example 2 – Mass to Mole to Atoms

How many oxygen atoms are in 18.0 g of H2O?

  1. Molar mass H2O ≈ 18.0 g·mol−1.
  2. Moles of H2O: 18.0 g ÷ 18.0 g·mol−1 = 1.00 mol.
  3. Each molecule has 1 O atom → 1 mol H2O contains 1 mol O atoms.
  4. Number of O atoms: 1.00 mol × 6.022 × 1023 ≈ 6.02 × 1023 O atoms.

3. Stoichiometric Calculations

General strategy for a mass–mass stoichiometry problem:

  1. Write and balance the chemical equation.
  2. Convert given mass to moles.
  3. Use mole ratio from the balanced equation to find moles of desired substance.
  4. Convert moles of desired substance to requested units (mass, volume, etc.).

Worked Example 3 – Mass–Mass Stoichiometry

How many grams of NaCl can be produced from 10.0 g of Na reacting with excess Cl2?

  1. Balanced equation: 2 Na + Cl2 → 2 NaCl.
  2. Moles of Na: 10.0 g ÷ 22.99 g·mol−1 ≈ 0.435 mol.
  3. Mole ratio: 2 mol Na → 2 mol NaCl (1:1), so n(NaCl) ≈ 0.435 mol.
  4. Mass NaCl: 0.435 mol × 58.44 g·mol−1 ≈ 25.4 g (3 significant figures).

4. Limiting Reactant and Yields

Identifying the Limiting Reactant

  1. Convert each reactant amount to moles.
  2. Use stoichiometric ratios to compute how many moles of product each reactant could produce.
  3. The reactant that gives fewer moles of product is the limiting reactant.

Worked Example 4 – Limiting Reactant

For N2 + 3 H2 → 2 NH3, if 5.0 g N2 react with 5.0 g H2, which reactant is limiting?

  1. Moles N2: 5.0 g ÷ 28.0 g·mol−1 ≈ 0.179 mol.
  2. Moles H2: 5.0 g ÷ 2.0 g·mol−1 = 2.5 mol.
  3. Based on N2: 0.179 mol N2 → 2 × 0.179 = 0.358 mol NH3.
  4. Based on H2: 2.5 mol H2 × (2 mol NH3/3 mol H2) ≈ 1.67 mol NH3.
  5. N2 produces less NH3 → N2 is the limiting reactant.

Theoretical, Actual, and Percent Yield

  • Theoretical yield: amount of product predicted from the limiting reactant.
  • Actual yield: amount produced in the lab (measured).
  • Percent yield: (actual / theoretical) × 100%.

5. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Balance each equation and classify the reaction type: (a) Fe + O2 → Fe2O3; (b) HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O.
  2. How many moles of CO2 are produced when 0.750 mol of propane (C3H8) burns in excess oxygen?
  3. Calculate the mass of CO2 formed when 15.0 g of C3H8 combust completely.
  4. In the reaction 2 Al + 3 Cl2 → 2 AlCl3, if 10.0 g Al react with 35.0 g Cl2, identify the limiting reactant and determine the theoretical yield of AlCl3.
  5. A reaction has a theoretical yield of 12.0 g but an actual yield of 9.0 g. Calculate the percent yield and interpret what it tells you about the efficiency of the reaction.
