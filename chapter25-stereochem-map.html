25.1 Stereochemistry in Organic Chemistry

Stereochemistry traces how a compound‚Äôs atoms occupy three-dimensional space, shaping not only bulk properties but also how molecules fit into enzymes or receptors.

Stereoisomers share formulas and connectivities but differ by spatial arrangement; include geometric isomers (cis/trans), enantiomers (non-superimposable mirror images), and diastereomers (stereoisomers that are not mirrors).

  • Constitutional isomers rearrange bonds, stereoisomers preserve connectivity but reorganize positions in 3D.
  • Enantiomers are always paired; they differ only in a chiral environment, while diastereomers can have distinct physical constants.

Limonene enantiomers smell like oranges vs lemons despite identical formula and connectivity‚Äîonly the ‚Äúhandedness‚Äù changes how the nose receptors bind.

25.2 Cis‚ÄìTrans Isomerism in Cycloalkanes

Geometric (cis/trans) isomers appear when substituents on a ring point to the same side (cis) or opposite sides (trans) of the plane.

Ring strain constrains rotation, so cycloalkanes keep these orientations fixed unless a C‚ÄìC bond is broken.

  • Use wedge (solid triangle) for bonds toward the viewer, dashed wedge for away, and plain lines for in-plane bonds.
  • Naming adds stereodescriptors, e.g., cis-1-ethyl-2-methylcyclohexane if both substituents stick out the same way.

1,2-Dimethylcyclohexane has three stereoisomers (cis plus two trans) that cannot interconvert without breaking the ring, demonstrating geometric constraints.

25.3 Chirality in Organic Compounds

Chiral molecules lack an internal mirror plane and have non-superimposable mirror images called enantiomers.

A stereogenic center (often a carbon with four different groups) guarantees chirality unless symmetry reappears.

  • Achiral molecules usually possess a plane of symmetry; meso compounds are special cases where stereocenters are present but overall molecule remains achiral.
  • Enantiomers have identical physical properties (melting point, boiling point) but respond differently in chiral environments, like smell receptors.

Carvone enantiomers illustrate how chirality changes a smell: (+)-carvone smells like spearmint, (‚Äì)-carvone like caraway, even though the structures differ only by mirror-image arrangement.

25.5 Absolute Stereochemistry

The Cahn‚ÄìIngold‚ÄìPrelog system assigns priorities to substituents based on atomic number and chain comparisons; highest gets 1, lowest 4.

Orient the molecule with priority 4 away, then trace from 1‚Üí3: clockwise means R, counterclockwise means S.

  1. Identify the stereocenter and the four atoms directly attached.
  2. Rank atoms by atomic number (Br>Cl>O>N>C>H); break ties by examining the next atoms along each branch.
  3. Arrange so the lowest priority group points away; read the sequence 1‚Üí3.

Using the rules on (R)-2-butanol yields R configuration once priorities are assigned (OH>CH2CH3>CH3>H).

Chiral drugs illustrate why R/S matter: (R)-albuterol opens airways while (S)-albuterol blocks it, and (S)-ibuprofen provides pain relief while the R isomer is less active until enzymatically converted.

25.6 Molecules with More than One Stereocenter

Number of stereoisomers = 2^n

n equals the number of stereocenters; this predicts the maximum total of enantiomers and diastereomers.

  • Two stereocenters produce four stereoisomers: two enantiomer pairs (e.g., (R,R)/(S,S) and (R,S)/(S,R)).
  • Diastereomers are not mirror images and show different physical/chemical properties, so you can separate them.
  • Meso compounds (like (R,S)-2,3-dibromobutane) possess internal symmetry and are achiral despite multiple stereocenters.

2-bromo-3-chlorobutane has (R,R), (S,S), (R,S), (S,R) stereoisomers; (R,R) and (S,S) are enantiomers, the mixed pairs are diastereomers.

Resolution strategy: convert a racemic acid to diastereomeric salts (e.g., with (R)-Œ±-methylbenzylamine); diastereomers have different solubilities, so you can crystallize and liberate the purified enantiomer.
