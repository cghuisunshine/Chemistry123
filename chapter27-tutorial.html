Chapter 27 Tutorial — Alcohols, Haloalkanes, and Ethers

27.1 Alcohols: Structure, Properties, and Nomenclature

Key ideas

  • Alcohols contain OH groups; the oxygen is sp³ hybridized with two lone pairs, enabling hydrogen bonding that raises boiling points and water solubility.
  • As the alkyl tail grows, the molecule behaves more like a hydrocarbon, reducing water solubility but increasing nonpolar solvent compatibility.
  • Names derive from the longest chain containing the OH; consider stereochemistry if chiral centers exist.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Hydrogen bonding: OH–O attractions (~20 kJ/mol) keep alcohols cohesive and miscible with water.
  • Solubility trend: Ethanol is miscible with water, whereas heptanol is only slightly soluble because the hydrophobic portion dominates.

Short example(s)

Glycerol, with three OH groups, dissolves in water easily, while cholesterol (one OH) is waxy and largely immiscible.

27.2 Haloalkanes

Key ideas

  • Haloalkanes replace an H with X (Cl, Br, I) and serve as electrophilic substrates for substitution or elimination.
  • Carbon-halogen bond strengths decrease down the group; iodides are easiest to substitute because I⁻ is the best leaving group.
  • Haloalkanes arise from alcohols (via acid or SOCl₂/PCl₃) or by addition to alkenes.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alcohol to haloalkane: `R–OH + HBr → R–Br + H₂O` (SN1 for tertiary alcohols with carbocation), or `ROH + SOCl₂ → R–Cl + SO₂ + HCl` for primary alcohols.

Short example(s)

2-Methyl-2-propanol reacts with HBr to give 2-bromo-2-methylpropane because the tertiary carbocation forms readily.

27.3 Ethers: Structure, Properties, and Nomenclature

Key ideas

  • Ethers (`R–O–R'`) have lower boiling points than alcohols because they cannot hydrogen bond as donors; they are good polar aprotic solvents.
  • Names list the alkyl groups alphabetically followed by `ether` (common) or `alkoxy-alkane` (systematic); cyclic ethers like THF or 1,4-dioxane use heterocyclic nomenclature.
  • Crown ethers (e.g., 18-crown-6) and macrocyclic compounds (nonactin) encapsulate metal ions and serve in supramolecular chemistry.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Ether bond angles: The O in R–O–R′ uses sp³ orbitals to form sigma bonds; lone pairs maintain bond angles ~110°.

Short example(s)

Diethyl ether (`CH₃CH₂OCH₂CH₃`) boils at 35 °C and is more volatile than butanol; 18-crown-6 solubilizes K⁺ in organic solution.

27.4 Reactions of Alcohols

Key ideas

  • Alcohols deprotonate to form alkoxides (`RO⁻`), which are strong bases/nucleophiles used in substitution or elimination.
  • Acid protonates OH to form oxonium ions; dehydration or substitution occurs via carbocations.
  • Functional group interconversion (alcohol → alkene, haloalkane, ether, carbonyl) stems from manipulating the OH site.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alkoxide formation: `2 ROH + 2 Na → 2 RONa + H₂`.
  • Carbocation precursor: `R–OH + H⁺ → R–OH₂⁺ → R⁺ + H₂O` (oxygen leaves as water, making carbocation).

Short example(s)

Sodium metal reacts with ethanol to give sodium ethoxide and H₂; 2-methyl-2-propanol forms a tertiary carbocation that can eliminate to 2-methylpropene or substitute to give 2-bromo-2-methylpropane.

27.5 Nucleophilic Substitution of Haloalkanes

Key ideas

  • SN1 reactions proceed via carbocations (`Rate ~ [R–X]`); SN2 involves concerted attack/inversion (`Rate ~ [R–X][Nu⁻]`).
  • Strong nucleophiles and polar aprotic solvents favor SN2; tertiary substrates often follow SN1/E1 due to stable carbocations.
  • Halide leaving groups order: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻; better leaving groups speed substitution.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • SN2 inversion: `R–Br + CN⁻ → R–CN + Br⁻` occurs with backside attack, flipping stereochemistry.

Short example(s)

Secondary bromides can undergo SN1 under acidic heat or SN2 with strong nucleophiles; tertiary halides favor SN1 due to carbocations.

27.6 β-Elimination to Alkenes

Key ideas

  • β-Elimination (E2/E1) removes HX from adjacent carbons; bases abstract β-H, forming alkenes.
  • E2 requires anti-periplanar H and halogen; strong bases favor E2, while tertiary substrates with weak base/nucleophile may go via E1.
  • E/Z geometry arises because of restricted rotation around the C=C bond formed.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • E2 rate law: `Rate = k[R–X][Base]`. Concerted transition state removes β-H as leaving group departs.

Short example(s)

2-bromobutane with t-BuOK (bulky base) yields trans-but-2-ene (E2) rather than substitution.

27.7 Substitution versus Elimination

Key ideas

  • Temperature, base strength, solvent, and substrate control whether substitution or elimination dominates.
  • Bulky bases and high temperature favor elimination (E2); good nucleophiles in polar aprotic solvents favor SN2.
  • Tertiary haloalkanes with weak nucleophiles favor SN1/E1 due to stable carbocations, while primary ones prefer SN2.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Pathway trends: `strong bulky base → E2`, `strong nucleophile weak base → SN2`, `weak nucleophile/heat → SN1/E1`.

Short example(s)

Heating 2-bromobutane with t-BuOK yields but-2-ene (elim), while NaN₃ in DMSO gives substitution to 2-azidobutane.
