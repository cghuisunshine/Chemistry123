4.1 General Properties of Aqueous Solutions
Solutions, electrolytes, solvation, and strong vs weak electrolytes.
Roots: What happens when substances dissolve?

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. The component present in the greatest amount is the solvent, and the others are solutes. In aqueous solutions, water is the solvent.

When a small amount of sodium chloride (NaCl) dissolves in a large amount of water, water is the solvent and NaCl is the solute. The dissolved particles are dispersed uniformly throughout the solution.

Water is an excellent solvent for many ionic compounds because it is polar. The oxygen atom has a partial negative charge (Œ¥‚àí), and the hydrogen atoms have partial positive charges (Œ¥+). The negative end of the water molecule attracts cations, and the positive end attracts anions.

As an ionic compound dissolves, its ions become solvated (surrounded) by water molecules and are written with the state symbol (aq), such as Na‚Å∫(aq) and Cl‚Åª(aq). Solvation stabilizes ions and prevents them from recombining.

A substance whose aqueous solution contains ions is called an electrolyte. A substance that dissolves but does not form ions in solution is a nonelectrolyte. For example, NaCl(aq) is an electrolyte, while a sucrose solution C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇH‚ÇÇ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÅ‚ÇÅ(aq) is a nonelectrolyte.

Strong electrolytes are solutes that exist in solution almost entirely as ions. Most water-soluble ionic compounds (like NaCl) and a few molecular compounds (like HCl) fall into this category. Weak electrolytes exist mostly as neutral molecules with only a small fraction as ions.

When a weak electrolyte ionizes, the process is reversible and reaches a chemical equilibrium, where the relative numbers of ions and molecules remain constant over time. We represent this with half-arrows in both directions (‚áå).

It is important not to confuse solubility with strength as an electrolyte. A compound can be very soluble but a weak electrolyte (like acetic acid), or only slightly soluble but a strong electrolyte for the amount that does dissolve (like Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ).

Weak electrolyte example (acetic acid):

[4.1] CH‚ÇÉCOOH(aq) ‚áå CH‚ÇÉCOO‚Åª(aq) + H‚Å∫(aq)

Here, acetic acid is a weak electrolyte: only a small fraction of molecules donate H‚Å∫ to water and form acetate ions. The double half-arrow (‚áå) shows that ionization and recombination both occur and reach equilibrium.

Strong electrolyte example (hydrochloric acid):

[4.2] HCl(aq) ‚Üí H‚Å∫(aq) + Cl‚Åª(aq)

HCl is a strong acid and strong electrolyte. The single arrow (‚Üí) indicates essentially complete ionization into H‚Å∫ and Cl‚Åª in water.

Rule-of-thumb patterns:

  • Water-soluble ionic compounds (containing metals and nonmetals or NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫) are generally strong electrolytes.
  • Most molecular compounds are nonelectrolytes, except acids and NH‚ÇÉ, which can form ions.
  • Weak acids and weak bases are weak electrolytes and always shown with ‚áå in ionization equations.

Example: electrolyte vs nonelectrolyte. If you test three liquids‚Äîpure water, sugar solution, and salt solution‚Äîbetween electrodes:

  • Pure water and sugar solution produce almost no light from a connected bulb: they contain virtually no ions ‚Üí nonelectrolytes.
  • NaCl solution lights the bulb: Na‚Å∫ and Cl‚Åª ions conduct electricity ‚Üí electrolyte.

Example: strong vs weak electrolyte at the same molarity. A 0.10 M NaCl solution has ‚âà0.10 M Na‚Å∫ and 0.10 M Cl‚Åª, giving high conductivity. A 0.10 M acetic acid solution has mostly CH‚ÇÉCOOH molecules and a tiny fraction of ions, so it conducts poorly.

Tip: Strength as an electrolyte depends on how completely a substance forms ions, not on how many grams dissolve. A very soluble weak acid can still be a weak electrolyte.

Common pitfall: Do not automatically assume every substance with H at the front of its formula is a strong acid. Many (like HF or H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ) are weak acids and therefore weak electrolytes.

4.2 Precipitation Reactions
Solubility rules, insoluble salts, exchange reactions, and ionic equations.
Trunk: Which ions stay in solution, which form solids?

A precipitation reaction occurs when two solutions are mixed and an insoluble solid, called a precipitate, forms. The precipitate appears when certain cation‚Äìanion combinations have such strong attractions that they form a solid rather than staying dissolved.

The solubility of a substance is the amount that can be dissolved in a given quantity of solvent at a certain temperature. In this chapter, any ionic compound with solubility below about 0.01 mol¬∑L‚Åª¬π is treated as insoluble.

Ionic compounds vary widely in solubility. There is no single simple physical rule to predict solubility from charges alone, but empirical solubility guidelines give reliable patterns for many common ions.

Precipitation reactions typically follow an exchange (metathesis) pattern, where the cations trade anions. However, a precipitate forms only if one of the new combinations is insoluble in water.

In aqueous reaction equations, we distinguish between different levels of detail:

  • Molecular equation: shows complete formulas of reactants and products.
  • Complete ionic equation: shows all strong electrolytes as ions.
  • Net ionic equation: drops spectator ions and shows only species that change.

Example precipitation: lead(II) iodide formation.

[4.3] Pb(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ(aq) + 2 KI(aq) ‚Üí PbI‚ÇÇ(s) + 2 KNO‚ÇÉ(aq)

Here, Pb¬≤‚Å∫ and I‚Åª combine to form the insoluble yellow solid PbI‚ÇÇ. K‚Å∫ and NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª remain in solution.

Example precipitation: magnesium hydroxide from Mg(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ and NaOH.

[4.4] Mg(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ(aq) + 2 NaOH(aq) ‚Üí Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ(s) + 2 NaNO‚ÇÉ(aq)

General metathesis pattern:

[4.5] AX + BY ‚Üí AY + BX

Solubility guidelines (summary):

  • Always soluble: All NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª and CH‚ÇÉCOO‚Åª salts; all Group 1 and NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ salts.
  • Usually soluble, but with important exceptions:
    • Cl‚Åª, Br‚Åª, I‚Åª: insoluble with Ag‚Å∫, Hg‚ÇÇ¬≤‚Å∫, Pb¬≤‚Å∫.
    • SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª: insoluble with Sr¬≤‚Å∫, Ba¬≤‚Å∫, Hg‚ÇÇ¬≤‚Å∫, Pb¬≤‚Å∫.
  • Usually insoluble:
    • S¬≤‚Åª, CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª, PO‚ÇÑ¬≥‚Åª: soluble only with Group 1 and NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫.
    • OH‚Åª: soluble with Group 1, NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫, and moderately with Ca¬≤‚Å∫, Sr¬≤‚Å∫, Ba¬≤‚Å∫.

Molecular, complete ionic, and net ionic equations for Pb¬≤‚Å∫/I‚Åª precipitation:

Molecular:

Pb(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ(aq) + 2 KI(aq) ‚Üí PbI‚ÇÇ(s) + 2 KNO‚ÇÉ(aq)

Complete ionic:

[4.6] Pb¬≤‚Å∫(aq) + 2 NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª(aq) + 2 K‚Å∫(aq) + 2 I‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí PbI‚ÇÇ(s) + 2 K‚Å∫(aq) + 2 NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª(aq)

Net ionic:

[4.7] Pb¬≤‚Å∫(aq) + 2 I‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí PbI‚ÇÇ(s)

In the complete ionic equation, K‚Å∫ and NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª appear unchanged on both sides and are therefore spectator ions. They are omitted from the net ionic equation.

Example: will a precipitate form?

Mix solutions of Mg(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ and NaOH. First, list ions: Mg¬≤‚Å∫, NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, Na‚Å∫, OH‚Åª. Possible products are Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ and NaNO‚ÇÉ.

  • NaNO‚ÇÉ: nitrate compounds are always soluble.
  • Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ: hydroxides are generally insoluble, and Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ is not one of the special soluble hydroxides.

Therefore, Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ(s) precipitates. The net ionic equation is:

Mg¬≤‚Å∫(aq) + 2 OH‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ(s)

Tip for exams: For precipitation questions, always:

  1. Write all ions present.
  2. Form all possible new cation‚Äìanion pairs.
  3. Check each combination with solubility rules.
  4. If no insoluble product appears, write ‚Äúno reaction‚Äù.

Common pitfall: Forgetting that Group 1 and NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ salts are always soluble. If they appear as possible precipitates, that combination stays in solution and cannot be the precipitate.

4.3 Acids, Bases, and Neutralization Reactions
Proton donors and acceptors, strong vs weak, and gas-forming neutralization.
Roots: H‚Å∫, OH‚Åª, and what ‚Äúneutralization‚Äù really means.

Acids are substances that ionize in aqueous solution to form H‚Å∫(aq). They are often called proton donors because a hydrogen ion is essentially a bare proton.

Bases are substances that accept H‚Å∫ or produce OH‚Åª(aq) when they dissolve in water. Ionic hydroxides like NaOH and KOH produce OH‚Åª directly, whereas some molecular bases (like NH‚ÇÉ) react with water to make OH‚Åª.

Acids can be classified by the number of protons they can donate:

  • Monoprotic acids (HCl, HNO‚ÇÉ) donate one H‚Å∫ per molecule.
  • Diprotic acids (H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ) can donate two protons in steps, forming HSO‚ÇÑ‚Åª and then SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª.

Strong acids and bases are strong electrolytes: they are essentially 100% ionized or dissociated in water. Weak acids and bases are weak electrolytes: they are only partially ionized and establish an equilibrium.

Neutralization reactions occur when a solution of an acid is mixed with a solution of a base. For strong acid‚Äìstrong base reactions, the essential process is H‚Å∫(aq) reacting with OH‚Åª(aq) to form water.

The term salt refers to an ionic compound generated in a neutralization reaction whose cation comes from the base and anion from the acid (for example, Na‚Å∫ from NaOH and Cl‚Åª from HCl forming NaCl).

Some neutralization reactions lead to the formation of gases such as H‚ÇÇS(g) and CO‚ÇÇ(g) when acids react with sulfides or carbonates/hydrogen carbonates.

Diprotic acid ionization (sulfuric acid):

[4.8] H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ(aq) ‚Üí H‚Å∫(aq) + HSO‚ÇÑ‚Åª(aq)
[4.9] HSO‚ÇÑ‚Åª(aq) ‚áå H‚Å∫(aq) + SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª(aq)

The first ionization is essentially complete (strong acid behavior). The second ionization is only partial and is written with an equilibrium arrow.

Ammonia acting as a base:

[4.10] H‚ÇÇO(l) + NH‚ÇÉ(aq) ‚áå OH‚Åª(aq) + NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫(aq)

Only a small fraction of NH‚ÇÉ molecules react with water, so NH‚ÇÉ is a weak base and weak electrolyte.

Neutralization of strong acid with strong base:

[4.11] HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO(l) + NaCl(aq)

Complete ionic form:

[4.12] H‚Å∫(aq) + Cl‚Åª(aq) + Na‚Å∫(aq) + OH‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO(l) + Na‚Å∫(aq) + Cl‚Åª(aq)

Net ionic form:

[4.13] H‚Å∫(aq) + OH‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO(l)

Equation [4.13] summarizes the essential feature of strong acid‚Äìstrong base neutralization: H‚Å∫ and OH‚Åª forming water.

Neutralization with a slightly soluble base (Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ):

[4.14] Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ(s) + 2 HCl(aq) ‚Üí MgCl‚ÇÇ(aq) + 2 H‚ÇÇO(l)

Net ionic:

[4.15] Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ(s) + 2 H‚Å∫(aq) ‚Üí Mg¬≤‚Å∫(aq) + 2 H‚ÇÇO(l)

Gas-forming neutralization reactions:

Acid + sulfide:

[4.16] 2 HCl(aq) + Na‚ÇÇS(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇS(g) + 2 NaCl(aq)
[4.17] 2 H‚Å∫(aq) + S¬≤‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇS(g)

Acid + hydrogen carbonate:

[4.18] HCl(aq) + NaHCO‚ÇÉ(aq) ‚Üí NaCl(aq) + H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ(aq)
[4.19] H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO(l) + CO‚ÇÇ(g)

Overall:

[4.20] HCl(aq) + NaHCO‚ÇÉ(aq) ‚Üí NaCl(aq) + H‚ÇÇO(l) + CO‚ÇÇ(g)

Net ionic:

[4.21] H‚Å∫(aq) + HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO(l) + CO‚ÇÇ(g)

Example: neutralizing stomach acid with NaHCO‚ÇÉ. Stomach acid contains HCl. When you swallow a tablet containing NaHCO‚ÇÉ (baking soda), the HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª ions react with H‚Å∫ in your stomach:

H‚Å∫(aq) + HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª(aq) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO(l) + CO‚ÇÇ(g)

The CO‚ÇÇ gas produced causes fizzing or belching, and the removal of H‚Å∫ relieves excess acidity.

Example: strong vs weak acid at equal concentration. A 0.10 M solution of HCl and a 0.10 M solution of acetic acid both contain the same total acid concentration, but:

  • 0.10 M HCl is almost completely ionized: [H‚Å∫] ‚âà 0.10 M, strong electrolyte, high conductivity.
  • 0.10 M CH‚ÇÉCOOH is only partly ionized: [H‚Å∫] ‚â™ 0.10 M, weak electrolyte, lower conductivity.

Tip: Remember the list of strong acids and strong bases. Any acid not on the ‚Äústrong‚Äù list should be treated as a weak acid in this course. NH‚ÇÉ is the key molecular weak base you need to recognize.

Common pitfall: Confusing sour/bitter taste and pH alone with strength. Strength is about the extent of ionization in water, not about how dangerous or concentrated the solution feels.

4.4 Oxidation‚ÄìReduction Reactions
Electron transfer, oxidation numbers, displacement reactions, and activity series.
Roots: Who loses electrons, who gains them?

Many reactions involve the transfer of electrons between species. These are called oxidation‚Äìreduction (redox) reactions.

Oxidation is the loss of electrons. The oxidation number of the atom increases (becomes more positive). Reduction is the gain of electrons, and the oxidation number decreases (becomes more negative).

Oxidation and reduction always occur together: whenever one species loses electrons, another must gain them. You can think of electrons as being ‚Äúpassed‚Äù from the substance being oxidized to the substance being reduced.

To track electrons in reactions, chemists assign each atom an oxidation number (also called oxidation state). In monatomic ions, this equals the ionic charge. In neutral molecules and polyatomic ions, oxidation numbers are assigned according to systematic rules and may be thought of as hypothetical charges.

Many redox reactions in solution are displacement reactions, where a metal reacts with an acid or a solution of another metal‚Äôs salt, producing a different metal or hydrogen gas and a new salt.

A list of metals arranged in order of decreasing ease of oxidation is called an activity series. Metals at the top (such as alkali and alkaline earth metals) are easily oxidized and react readily. Metals at the bottom (Cu, Ag, Hg, Pt, Au) are called noble metals because they resist oxidation.

Oxidation by oxygen: formation of CaO.

[4.22] 2 Ca(s) + O‚ÇÇ(g) ‚Üí 2 CaO(s)

Oxidation states: Ca goes from 0 to +2 (oxidized), and O goes from 0 to ‚àí2 (reduced). Oxygen gains electrons from calcium.

Rules for assigning oxidation numbers:

  • Atoms in their elemental form (H‚ÇÇ, O‚ÇÇ, P‚ÇÑ, S‚Çà) have oxidation number 0.
  • For a monatomic ion, the oxidation number equals the ionic charge (Na‚Å∫ is +1, S¬≤‚Åª is ‚àí2).
  • In compounds:
    • Group 1 metals: +1; Group 2 metals: +2; Al: +3.
    • Oxygen is usually ‚àí2, except in peroxides (O‚ÇÇ¬≤‚Åª) where it is ‚àí1.
    • Hydrogen is +1 when bonded to nonmetals, ‚àí1 when bonded to metals.
    • Fluorine is always ‚àí1; other halogens are usually ‚àí1 unless combined with oxygen.
  • In a neutral compound, the sum of oxidation numbers is 0.
  • In a polyatomic ion, the sum equals the net charge of the ion.

General displacement redox reaction:

[4.23] A + BX ‚Üí AX + B

Here, A is oxidized (becomes part of AX), and B is reduced (released as an element).

Metal with acid: hydrogen gas formation.

[4.24] Mg(s) + 2 HCl(aq) ‚Üí MgCl‚ÇÇ(aq) + H‚ÇÇ(g)

Net ionic:

[4.25] Mg(s) + 2 H‚Å∫(aq) ‚Üí Mg¬≤‚Å∫(aq) + H‚ÇÇ(g)

Mg is oxidized from 0 to +2; H‚Å∫ is reduced from +1 to 0.

Metal with another metal‚Äôs ion (Fe and Ni¬≤‚Å∫):

Fe(s) + Ni¬≤‚Å∫(aq) ‚Üí Fe¬≤‚Å∫(aq) + Ni(s)

Iron is oxidized; nickel ion is reduced. This reaction occurs because Fe is above Ni in the activity series.

Activity series concept: Metals higher in the series can displace metals (or hydrogen) below them from compounds. For example, Zn can displace Cu¬≤‚Å∫ from CuSO‚ÇÑ, but Ag cannot.

Example: oxidation number of S in Na‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÉ.

We know Na is +1 (Group 1) and O is ‚àí2. Let the oxidation number of S be x.

Sum must be zero: 2(+1) + x + 3(‚àí2) = 0 ‚Üí 2 + x ‚àí 6 = 0 ‚Üí x = +4. So sulfur is in the +4 oxidation state in Na‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÉ.

Example: will Zn react with HCl? Zinc is above hydrogen in the activity series. Therefore, Zn(s) will be oxidized by H‚Å∫, and H‚ÇÇ gas will be produced:

Zn(s) + 2 H‚Å∫(aq) ‚Üí Zn¬≤‚Å∫(aq) + H‚ÇÇ(g)

By contrast, Cu(s) will not react with HCl because Cu is below H in the activity series.

Tip: When identifying what is oxidized and what is reduced in a reaction:

  1. Assign oxidation numbers to each element on both sides.
  2. Look for atoms whose oxidation number increases (oxidation) or decreases (reduction).
  3. Check that the total increase in oxidation numbers equals the total decrease; this corresponds to conservation of electrons.

Common pitfall: Assuming every reaction involving oxygen is a redox reaction. Many are, but the key is change in oxidation number, not the presence of oxygen alone.

4.5 Concentrations of Solutions
Molarity, ion concentrations, and dilution of stock solutions.
Trunk: M, moles, liters, and how they connect.

Concentration tells us how much solute is present in a given amount of solvent or solution. Many biological and chemical processes depend critically on solution concentrations.

The most common concentration unit in chemistry is molarity (M), which expresses the number of moles of solute per liter of solution.

When an ionic compound dissolves, we are often interested in the concentrations of individual ions, which depend on the compound‚Äôs formula. For instance, a solution of Ca(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ contains both Ca¬≤‚Å∫ and NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª ions in specific proportions.

Solutions of known molarity can be prepared by weighing a known amount of solute, dissolving it in a small amount of solvent, and then diluting to a precise final volume in a volumetric flask.

Another common method is dilution of a stock solution. A small volume of a more concentrated solution is measured out and then diluted with solvent to increase the total volume and obtain a lower molarity.

Definition of molarity:

[4.31] M = (moles of solute) / (volume of solution in liters)

This relationship can be rearranged to find moles from molarity and volume: n = M √ó V, or volume from moles and molarity: V = n / M.

Ion concentrations from formula units: For an ionic solute, the concentration of each ion is found by multiplying the solution‚Äôs molarity by the subscript of that ion in the formula. For example, in Ca(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ(aq):

  • Each formula unit gives 1 Ca¬≤‚Å∫ and 2 NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª ions.
  • If [Ca(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ] = 0.025 M, then [Ca¬≤‚Å∫] = 0.025 M and [NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª] = 0.050 M.

Dilution formula: When a more concentrated solution is diluted with pure solvent, the number of moles of solute stays the same, so:

[4.33] Mconc √ó Vconc = Mdil √ó Vdil

Here, Mconc and Vconc are the molarity and volume of the concentrated solution, and Mdil and Vdil are those of the diluted solution.

Example: molarity from mass and volume. A solution is made by dissolving 23.4 g of Na‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ in enough water to form 125 mL of solution.

  1. Convert mass to moles: molar mass Na‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ ‚âà 142.1 g¬∑mol‚Åª¬π. n = 23.4 g √ó (1 mol / 142.1 g) ‚âà 0.165 mol.
  2. Convert volume to liters: 125 mL = 0.125 L.
  3. Compute molarity: M = 0.165 mol / 0.125 L ‚âà 1.32 M.

Ion concentrations: [Na‚Å∫] = 2 √ó 1.32 M = 2.64 M; [SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª] = 1.32 M.

Example: preparing a dilute solution from a stock. How much 3.0 M H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ is needed to make 450 mL of 0.10 M H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ?

Using MconcVconc = MdilVdil:

3.0 √ó Vconc = 0.10 √ó 0.450 ‚Üí Vconc = (0.10 √ó 0.450) / 3.0 = 0.015 L = 15 mL.

You measure 15 mL of 3.0 M H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ into a volumetric flask and add water until the total volume is 450 mL.

Tip: When comparing different solutions, pay attention to both:

  • Molarity of the solute.
  • Number of ions produced per formula unit (for ionic solutes).
A 1.0 M Na‚ÇÉPO‚ÇÑ solution has more total ions per liter than a 1.0 M NaCl solution.

4.6 Solution Stoichiometry and Chemical Analysis
Using molarity in stoichiometric calculations and titrations.
Leaves: Putting it all together with moles, volumes, and reactions.

In Chapter 3, you used stoichiometry to relate masses of reactants and products using balanced chemical equations. In solutions, the same idea applies, but now molarity links solution volume to moles of solute.

The key relationships are:

  • For pure substances: grams ‚Üî moles via molar mass.
  • For solutions: volume ‚Üî moles via molarity, n = M √ó V.
  • Balanced equation coefficients relate moles of one substance to moles of another.

A common way to determine unknown concentrations is through a titration. A solution of known concentration (the standard solution) is slowly added to a solution of unknown concentration until the reaction reaches the equivalence point, where stoichiometrically equivalent amounts have reacted.

In acid‚Äìbase titrations, a suitable indicator changes color near the equivalence point, signaling that the reaction is complete.

General stoichiometry pathway with a solution and a solid:

  • Given mass of solid A ‚Üí convert to moles A using molar mass.
  • Use coefficients in the balanced equation to find moles of solute B that react with A.
  • Use B‚Äôs molarity to find the volume of B required (V = n / M), or vice versa.

Example neutralization equation (Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ and HNO‚ÇÉ):

2 HNO‚ÇÉ(aq) + Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ(s) ‚Üí 2 H‚ÇÇO(l) + Ca(NO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ(aq)

This balanced equation shows that 2 moles of HNO‚ÇÉ react with 1 mole of Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ.

Titration relationships: At the equivalence point, the moles of reactants have reacted exactly according to the stoichiometric coefficients in the balanced equation. For a 1:1 acid‚Äìbase reaction like HCl with NaOH, at equivalence:

n(HCl) = n(NaOH) = M √ó V

If the concentration of one solution is known and volumes of both are measured, you can solve for the unknown molarity.

Example: mass of base needed to neutralize an acid. How many grams of Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ are needed to neutralize 25.0 mL of 0.100 M HNO‚ÇÉ?

  1. Convert acid volume to moles using molarity: V = 25.0 mL = 0.0250 L; n(HNO‚ÇÉ) = 0.100 mol¬∑L‚Åª¬π √ó 0.0250 L = 2.50 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ mol.
  2. Use the stoichiometric ratio (2 HNO‚ÇÉ : 1 Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ): n[Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ] = (1/2) √ó n(HNO‚ÇÉ) = 1.25 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ mol.
  3. Convert moles of Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ to grams: molar mass ‚âà 74.1 g¬∑mol‚Åª¬π; m = 1.25 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ mol √ó 74.1 g¬∑mol‚Åª¬π ‚âà 0.0926 g.

So, about 0.093 g of Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ is required.

Example: finding an unknown acid concentration from a titration. Suppose 25.0 mL of 0.100 M NaOH is required to neutralize 20.0 mL of HCl solution. The reaction is:

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) ‚Üí NaCl(aq) + H‚ÇÇO(l)

At equivalence, n(HCl) = n(NaOH).

  • n(NaOH) = 0.100 M √ó 0.0250 L = 2.50 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ mol.
  • Thus n(HCl) = 2.50 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ mol.
  • Volume of HCl = 20.0 mL = 0.0200 L.
  • M(HCl) = n / V = 2.50 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ mol / 0.0200 L = 0.125 M.

Tip: For multistep solution stoichiometry:

  1. Start with what you know (mass, volume, or molarity).
  2. Convert to moles using molar mass or M √ó V.
  3. Use the balanced equation to move between substances.
  4. Finish by converting moles back to the desired unit (grams, volume, or molarity).

Common pitfall: Forgetting to convert mL to L before using M = n / V. Always convert mL to L by dividing by 1000.
