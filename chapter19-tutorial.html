Chapter 19 Tutorial — Chemical Thermodynamics

19.1 Spontaneous Processes

Key ideas

  • Spontaneous processes proceed without external help and are directional; their reverse paths are nonspontaneous.
  • Spontaneity depends on conditions such as temperature and pressure; e.g., water melts above 0 °C but freezes below.
  • Real spontaneous processes are irreversible and do not imply speed.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Reversible process: Requires infinitesimally small changes and can be inverted without altering surroundings; otherwise the process is irreversible.

Short example(s)

An egg falling and breaking is spontaneous but reassembling is impossible; rusting iron is spontaneous yet slow, showing spontaneity ≠ rate.

19.2 Entropy and the Second Law

Key ideas

  • The second law states `ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr ≥ 0`; spontaneous processes increase total entropy.
  • Entropy is tied to dispersal of energy; heat flow into surroundings raises their entropy.
  • Processes may be exothermic or endothermic, but the net entropy change determines spontaneity.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Entropy change of surroundings: `ΔS_surr = -ΔH_sys/T` (since heat leaving system enters surroundings).
  • Total entropy: `ΔS_univ ≥ 0` indicates spontaneity.

Short example(s)

Melting ice at 298 K absorbs heat (endothermic) so `ΔS_sys > 0`; surroundings lose heat so `ΔS_surr < 0`, but net `ΔS_univ > 0` if T > 0 °C.

19.3 Molecular Interpretation & Third Law

Key ideas

  • Entropy relates to microstates: `S = k ln W`; more accessible configurations mean greater entropy.
  • Heating increases molecular motion and accessible states, raising entropy.
  • The third law states that perfect crystals at 0 K have zero entropy (only one microstate).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Boltzmann’s equation: `S = k ln W` links microscopic arrangements to macroscopic entropy.

Short example(s)

As temperature rises, vibrational amplitudes grow and new rotational/vibrational states become available, increasing S.

19.4 Entropy Changes in Reactions

Key ideas

  • Reaction entropy `ΔS°_rxn = Σ n_p S°_p − Σ n_r S°_r` quantifies disorder change.
  • Gases contribute large positive entropy terms; solids/liquids smaller ones.
  • Entropy changes are often predicted by counting “freedom”: more moles of gas or more dispersed particles usually means larger ΔS.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Entropy change: Use tabulated standard molar entropies to compute `ΔS°_rxn`.
  • Units: Standard molar entropies S° and ΔS° are typically in J·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹, so keep temperatures in Kelvin.

Short example(s)

A decomposition that produces more gas molecules increases entropy (positive `ΔS`).

19.5 Gibbs Free Energy

Key ideas

  • Gibbs free energy G = H − TS determines spontaneity at constant T and P; ΔG < 0 means spontaneous.
  • ΔG combines the “heat” tendency (ΔH) and the “dispersal” tendency (ΔS) into one criterion.
  • ΔG is a state function, so only initial and final states matter (not the path).
  • Second-law connection: At constant T and P, the Gibbs criterion is just the second law rewritten: ΔG = −TΔS_univ, so ΔG < 0 is equivalent to ΔS_univ > 0.
  • For a step-by-step derivation and intuition (including temperature’s entropy meaning), see gibbs.html.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Gibbs equation: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. Use T in Kelvin; ΔH is typically in kJ/mol and ΔS in J·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹, so convert units before combining.
  • Sign test: If ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0, then ΔG is negative at all temperatures (always spontaneous under constant T,P conditions).
  • Derived from the Second Law (constant T, P): Start with ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr and use ΔS_surr = −ΔH_sys/T. Then ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys − ΔH_sys/T. Multiply by −T to get −TΔS_univ = ΔH_sys − TΔS_sys, which is ΔG.

Short example(s)

An endothermic dissolution may become spontaneous at high temperature if `TΔS > ΔH`.

19.6 Free Energy and Temperature

Key ideas

  • The temperature dependence of `ΔG = ΔH − TΔS` allows prediction of temperatures where a reaction switches from nonspontaneous to spontaneous.
  • When ΔH and ΔS are approximately constant over a temperature range, ΔG varies roughly linearly with T.
  • The “crossover temperature” where spontaneity changes is found by setting ΔG = 0.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • The slope of `ΔG` vs `T` is `−ΔS`, while the intercept at `T=0` is `ΔH`.
  • Crossover temperature (when applicable): If ΔH and ΔS are both nonzero, ΔG=0 occurs near T = ΔH/ΔS (use consistent units).

Short example(s)

For a reaction with positive `ΔH` but larger `ΔS`, `ΔG` becomes negative above `T = ΔH/ΔS`.

19.7 Free Energy and Equilibrium Constants

Key ideas

  • `ΔG° = −RT ln K` links standard free energy to equilibrium constants, so `ΔG° < 0` implies `K > 1`.
  • Nonstandard conditions use `ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q` to determine spontaneity before equilibrium.
  • At equilibrium, ΔG = 0 and Q = K, which ties “direction” (spontaneity) to “position” (equilibrium composition).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Equilibrium form: `K = e^{−ΔG°/(RT)}`.
  • Reaction quotient: `ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q`, so if `Q < K`, `ΔG < 0` and reaction proceeds forward.
  • Units note: In thermodynamics, K is treated as dimensionless (built from activities relative to standard states), which is why it can appear inside ln.

Short example(s)

For `ΔG° = −40 kJ/mol` at 298 K, `K ≈ e^{+16} ≈ 9 × 10⁶`, so products dominate at equilibrium.
