Acid-Base Review

16.1 Acid–Base Equilibria

  • Brønsted-Lowry Theory: Acids donate H⁺, bases accept H⁺
  • Amphoterism: Water acts as both acid and base
  • Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs: Every acid-base reaction involves equilibrium between protonated and deprotonated forms
  • Ka and Kb: Acid/base dissociation constants quantify strength

16.2 Autoionization of Water

  • Kw = [H₃O⁺][OH⁻] ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25 °C
  • Pure water: [H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M

16.3 The pH Scale

  • pH = -log[H₃O⁺]
  • pH + pOH = 14 at 25 °C
  • Each pH unit represents a tenfold difference in [H₃O⁺]

16.4-16.6 Strong and Weak Acids/Bases

  • Strong acids/bases dissociate completely (K very large)
  • Weak acids/bases require ICE tables: Ka = [H₃O⁺][A⁻]/[HA]
  • Approximation: [H₃O⁺] ≈ √(Ka·[HA]₀) when Ka is small
Week 2

Intro to Thermodynamics, State Variables, Systems, Surroundings, Ideal Gases, First Law, Internal Energy, Paths and Processes, Pistons

Ch 5: Thermochemistry Ch 5 PDF Ch 10: Gases Ch 10 PDF

Intro to Thermodynamics

  • Spontaneous Processes: Proceed without outside intervention. Spontaneity depends on temperature and pressure.
  • Reversible vs. Irreversible: Reversible processes can be reversed by an infinitesimal change; irreversible cannot.

State Variables

  • State functions: E, H, S, G depend only on current state, not the path.

Systems/Surroundings

We define a specific amount of matter as the system; everything outside is the surroundings. A closed system can exchange energy, but not matter, with the surroundings.

Ideal Gases

  • Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant n, T)
  • Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant n, P)
  • Avogadro's Law: V ∝ n (constant P, T)
  • Ideal Gas Equation: PV = nRT
  • Combined Gas Law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
  • Kinetic-Molecular Theory: Pressure stems from collisions; Average KE = ½ m u_rms²; u_rms ∝ √T.

First Law / Internal Energy

  • First Law: Energy is neither created nor destroyed.
  • Equation: ΔE = q + w (Internal energy change equals heat plus work)
  • Internal Energy (E): Sum of all kinetic and potential energies of components.

Paths and Processes

  • Path functions: Heat (q) and work (w) depend on the specific path taken.

Pistons

When a gas is produced or consumed in a piston (constant pressure), the system performs pressure–volume (P–V) work: w = −P_ext ΔV. Expansion (ΔV > 0) means work is done by the system (w is negative).

Week 3

Measuring Heat and Work, Heat Capacities, Calorimetry, Intro to Enthalpy, Standard States, Hess’s Law, Entropy, Second and Third Laws of Thermo

Ch 5: Thermochemistry Ch 5 PDF Ch 19: Thermodynamics Ch 19 PDF

Measuring Heat and Work / Heat Capacities / Calorimetry

  • Heat capacity: q = C·ΔT
  • Specific heat: q = m·c·ΔT
  • Calorimetry: Measuring heat flow in reactions
  • Constant-pressure calorimetry: qp = ΔH
  • Bomb calorimetry: qv = ΔE

Intro to Enthalpy / Standard States / Hess’s Law

  • Enthalpy (H): H = E + PV (State function)
  • ΔH = qp at constant pressure (Heat of reaction)
  • Standard states: 1 atm, 25 °C, pure substances. ΔHf° is standard enthalpy of formation.
  • Hess’s Law: ΔH_rxn = Σ ΔH_products − Σ ΔH_reactants

Entropy and Laws of Thermo

  • Second Law: ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr ≥ 0 for spontaneous processes
  • Entropy (S): State function measuring energy/matter dispersal (S = k ln W)
  • Third Law: Perfect crystals at 0 K have S = 0
  • Reaction Entropy: ΔS°_rxn = Σ n_p S°_p − Σ n_r S°_r (Gas formation increases S)
Week 4

Spontaneity, Gibbs Free Energy, Equilibrium, van’t Hoff plots, Intro to Kinetics, Rate Laws, Rate Constants, Reaction Orders, Method of Initial Rates

Ch 19: Thermodynamics Ch 19 PDF Ch 14: Kinetics Ch 14 PDF

Spontaneity & Gibbs Free Energy

  • ΔG = ΔH − TΔS: Determines spontaneity at constant T and P
  • ΔG < 0: Spontaneous
  • ΔG = 0: Equilibrium
  • ΔG > 0: Nonspontaneous
  • Temperature dependence: ΔG vs T plots are linear with slope −ΔS

Equilibrium & van’t Hoff

  • ΔG° = −RT ln K: Links standard free energy to K
  • Conditions: K > 1 (Products favored, ΔG° < 0); K < 1 (Reactants favored, ΔG° > 0)
  • Nonstandard: ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q
  • van't Hoff equation: ln K = −ΔH°/RT + ΔS°/R
  • Dynamic equilibrium: Forward and reverse rates equal

Intro to Kinetics / Rate Laws

  • Reaction rate: Change in concentration per unit time (Rate = −Δ[A]/Δt)
  • Rate law: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
  • Method of initial rates: Compare experiments to find orders (m, n) which are determined experimentally.
  • Rate Constant (k): specific to reaction and temperature.

Integrated Rate Laws and Graphical Analysis, Half-life, Overall Reactions, Mechanisms, Elementary Reactions, Reaction Coordinate Diagrams, Intermediates and Catalysts, Transition States

Ch 14: Kinetics Ch 14 PDF

Integrated Rate Laws and Graphical Analysis

  • Zero-order: [A]_t = [A]₀ − kt (Plot [A] vs t, slope = −k)
  • First-order: ln[A]_t = ln[A]₀ − kt (Plot ln[A] vs t, slope = −k)
  • Second-order: 1/[A]_t = 1/[A]₀ + kt (Plot 1/[A] vs t, slope = k)
  • Half-life (First-order): t₁/₂ = 0.693/k (independent of concentration)

Mechanisms & Elementary Reactions

  • Mechanism: Sequence of elementary steps (Unimolecular, Bimolecular)
  • Intermediates: Produced then consumed (do not appear in overall eq)
  • Catalysts: Consumed then produced (lower Ea, not consumed overall)

Energy Diagrams & Transition States

  • Reaction coordinate diagrams: Plot Energy vs. reaction progress
  • Activation energy (Ea): Energy barrier to reaction
  • Transition states: High-energy configurations at peaks
Week 6

Multi-Step Reaction Mechanisms, Rate Determining Step, Rate Laws from Mechanisms, Steady-State Approximation, Michaelis-Menten Enzyme Kinetics, Temperature-dependence of k (Arrhenius Equation)

Ch 14: Kinetics Ch 14 PDF

Advanced Mechanisms

  • Rate Determining Step (RDS): Slowest step limits overall rate.
  • Steady-State Approximation: Assumes d[intermediate]/dt ≈ 0.
  • Pre-equilibrium: Fast equilibrium step before RDS affects rate law.

Michaelis-Menten Enzyme Kinetics

  • Mechanism: E + S ⇌ ES → E + P
  • Equation: v = V_max[S]/(K_M + [S])
  • Saturation: Rate approaches V_max at high [S].

Arrhenius Equation (Temperature dependence)

  • Equation: k = A e^(−Ea/(RT))
  • Rate constant increases exponentially with temperature.
  • Arrhenius plot: ln k vs 1/T gives straight line with slope −Ea/R.
Week 7

Intro to Organic, Representing Organic Molecules, Nomenclature/Functional Groups, Electronic Structure

Ch 24: Organic Compounds Ch 24 PDF

Intro to Organic & Hydrocarbons

  • Carbon characteristics: Forms 4 bonds; sp³ (tetrahedral), sp² (trigonal), sp (linear).
  • Hydrocarbons: Alkanes (sat, single bonds), Alkenes (C=C), Alkynes (C≡C), Aromatic (benzene rings).
  • Homologous series: Smooth trends in physical properties.

Representing Organic Molecules & Electronic Structure

  • Structure: Line-angle formulas commonly used.
  • Hybridization: sp³ (109.5°, tetrahedral), Sigma bonds (head-on overlap).
  • Isomers: Constitutional isomers have same formula but different connectivity.

Nomenclature & Functional Groups

  • Nomenclature: Longest chain parent + numbered substituents + alphabetized names.
  • Functional Groups: R-OH (Alcohol), R-O-R (Ether), R-NH₂ (Amine), R-X (Halide), R-COOH (Acid), R-CHO (Aldehyde), R-CO-R (Ketone).
Week 8

Bonding and Resonance, Curved Arrow Notation, Organic view of Acid-Base Chemistry

Ch 8: Bonding Ch 8 PDF Ch 26: Alkenes Ch 26 PDF

Bonding and Resonance

  • Lewis structures: Show bonding and lone pairs. FC = V - N - B/2.
  • Resonance: Multiple valid Lewis structures contribute to resonance hybrid.
  • Stabilization: Delocalized electrons lower energy.

Curved Arrow Notation

  • Usage: Shows electron pair movement from source (nucleophile/bond) to destination (electrophile/atom).
  • Types: Double-headed (pair), Single-headed (radical).

Organic Acid-Base Chemistry

  • Factors affecting acidity (pKa): Electronegativity, Resonance (stabilizes conjugate base), Inductive effects, Hybridization (sp > sp² > sp³).
  • Trends: Acids (R-COOH ~4-5), Alcohols (~16), Amines (act as bases).
Week 9

Conformational Analysis, Cyclic Alkanes (cyclohexane)

Ch 24: Organic Compounds Ch 24 PDF

Conformational Analysis

  • Conformers: Different arrangements from bond rotation (Newman projections).
  • Ethane: Staggered (lower E) vs Eclipsed (higher E).
  • Butane: Anti (180°, most stable), Gauche (60°, steric strain), Eclipsed.

Cyclic Alkanes (cyclohexane)

  • Chair conformation: Most stable, no angle/torsional strain.
  • Positions: Axial (up/down) vs Equatorial (around equator). Ring flip converts them.
  • Substituents: Prefer equatorial to avoid 1,3-diaxial interactions (Steric strain).
  • Disubstituted: Cis (same face) vs Trans (opposite faces).
Week 10

Alkenes (E and Z), CIP Rules, Stereochemistry, Chirality, (R and S), Relationships between Compounds (Enantiomers, Diastereomers, etc.)

Ch 25: Stereochemistry Ch 25 PDF Ch 26: Alkenes Ch 26 PDF

Alkene Stereochemistry (E/Z) & CIP Rules

  • Geometric Isomers: Restricted rotation. Cis/Trans or E/Z.
  • CIP Rules: Prioritize by atomic number (then mass/outward).
  • Z (zusammen): High priority on same side.
  • E (entgegen): High priority on opposite sides.

Chirality & R/S Configuration

  • Chiral center: C with 4 different groups. Non-superimposable mirror images.
  • R/S Assignment: 1. Priority (1-4). 2. Lowest away. 3. Trace 1→2→3. 4. Clockwise=R, Counter=S.
  • Enantiomers: Opposite configuration, optical isomers.

Relationships between Compounds

  • Enantiomers: Mirror images, opposite R/S.
  • Diastereomers: Stereoisomers not mirror images. Different physical properties.
  • Constitutional Isomers: Different connectivity.
Week 11

Relationships between Compounds (cont.), Meso Compounds, Properties of Chiral Compounds, Optical Rotation, Fischer Projections

Ch 25: Stereochemistry Ch 25 PDF

Relationships / Meso Compounds

  • Multiple Stereocenters: Max 2^n isomers.
  • Meso Compound: Achiral despite having stereocenters (Plane of symmetry). Superimposable on mirror image.

Optical Rotation

  • Optical Activity: Chiral molecules rotate plane-polarized light.
  • Specific Rotation [α]: Physical constant. Dextro (+) or Levo (-).
  • Racemic Mixture: 50:50 enantiomers (0° rotation).
  • Enantiomeric Excess (ee): Measure of purity.

Fischer Projections

  • Convention: Horizontal=wedges, Vertical=dashes. Intersection=Carbon.
  • Rules: Rotate 180° (ok), 90° (inverse). Swap 2 groups (inverse).
  • Used for carbs and amino acids.

Fischer Projections (cont.), Substitution Reactions: SN2 and SN1, Review

Ch 27: Alcohols/Halides Ch 27 PDF

Fischer Projections (Advanced)

  • D/L System: Based on bottom stereocenter (-OH right=D, left=L).
  • Interconversion with Newman and Chair forms.

Substitution Reactions (SN2 vs SN1)

  • SN2: Concerted, Backside attack, Inversion, Rate=k[RX][Nu], 1° > 2° >> 3°, Polar aprotic solvent.
  • SN1: Stepwise (Carbocation), Racemization, Rate=k[RX], 3° > 2° >> 1°, Polar protic solvent, Rearrangements possible.
