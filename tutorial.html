Acid–Base Review (Chapter 16)

Acid–base chemistry is mostly equilibrium + bookkeeping: decide what reacts, write the equilibrium expression, then connect concentration to pH.

16.1 Acid–Base Equilibria: definitions you must be fluent with

  • Brønsted–Lowry acid donates a proton (H+); base accepts a proton.
  • Conjugate acid–base pair: species that differ by exactly one H+ (e.g., HA/A− or BH+/B).
  • Amphoteric: can act as acid or base (water is the classic example).
How to identify conjugate pairs fast

Remove one H+ from the acid → you get its conjugate base. Add one H+ to the base → you get its conjugate acid. Charges change by ±1 each time you add/remove H+.

16.2 Autoionization of water (what Kw actually means)

  • Reaction: 2 H2O ⇌ H3O+ + OH−
  • Water ion product: Kw = [H3O+][OH−] = 1.0×10−14 at 25 °C
  • Units: concentrations are in mol/L (M), so Kw has units M2.
Interpretation

At a fixed temperature, if [H3O+] goes up, [OH−] must go down so that their product stays equal to Kw.

16.3 pH and pOH (log scale = “powers of ten”)

  • pH = −log10([H3O+]) and pOH = −log10([OH−])
  • At 25 °C: pH + pOH = 14.00 because pKw = 14.00
  • Each 1-unit change in pH is a 10× change in [H3O+].
Quick example

If [H3O+]=1.0×10−3 M, then pH=3.00. If pH drops to 2.00, [H3O+] becomes 1.0×10−2 M (10× larger).

16.4–16.6 Strong vs weak acids/bases (when to use ICE)

Strong acids/bases are treated as “complete” in water.

  • Strong acid: [H3O+] ≈ Cacid (after dilution/mixing).
  • Strong base: [OH−] ≈ Cbase.
  • Then use Kw or pH/pOH relations.

Weak acids/bases establish an equilibrium and need ICE logic.

  • Weak acid: Ka = ([H3O+][A−])/[HA]
  • Weak base: Kb = ([BH+][OH−])/[B]
  • Common approximation for weak acids: [H3O+] ≈ √(Ka·C0) when dissociation is small.
Worked example (weak acid pH, “square‑root method”)

Acetic acid: C0=0.10 M, Ka=1.8×10−5. Estimate [H3O+]≈√(Ka·C0)=√(1.8×10−6)=1.34×10−3 M, so pH≈2.87.

Common pitfalls

Mixing up Ka vs Kb, using natural log instead of log base 10 for pH, and forgetting that “strong” means strong in water (leveling effect).

Physical chemistry cross-check (pc): equilibrium viewpoint

  • Acid–base problems are equilibrium problems: Ka and Kb are equilibrium constants.
  • General driving-force link: ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q; at equilibrium ΔG = 0 and Q = K.
  • Interpretation: “strong” acids/bases push equilibrium far to products; “weak” ones don’t, so ICE tables matter.

Big picture: what thermodynamics answers (and what it doesn’t)

  • Thermodynamics predicts direction/limits: “Can this happen?” and “How much energy changes?”
  • Kinetics (later) predicts speed: “How fast does it happen?”
  • Spontaneous means it proceeds without continual outside pushing (not “fast”).

Systems, surroundings, and state variables

Always start by declaring the system (what you track) and the surroundings (everything else).

  • State functions: depend only on the state (e.g., E, H, S, G).
  • Path functions: depend on the process (e.g., heat q and work w).
  • Closed vs open: closed systems exchange energy but not matter; open systems exchange both.
Why state functions matter

If a quantity is a state function, you can compute its change using any convenient path (this is the logic behind Hess’s law next week).

Gas pressure (Chapter 10)

Pressure is the macroscopic “impact rate” of gas molecules hitting a surface.

  • P = F/A (force per area). More collisions or harder collisions → higher pressure.
  • Common units: 1 atm = 760 torr = 101.325 kPa.
  • Fluid columns (manometers/barometers): P = ρgh connects pressure to height difference.
Quick unit habit

Always check that your R matches units: if you use atm and liters, use R=0.08206 L·atm·mol−1·K−1. If you use kPa and liters, use R=8.314 kPa·L·mol−1·K−1.

Ideal gases as a model (Chapter 10)

The ideal gas model works best at low pressure and high temperature, where intermolecular forces and molecular volume matter less.

  • PV = nRT connects pressure, volume, temperature, and moles.
  • Use Kelvin for temperature in gas laws.
  • R depends on units (common: 0.08206 L·atm·mol−1·K−1).
Quick example (ideal gas)

n=0.50 mol, T=298 K, P=1.00 atm → V=nRT/P≈(0.50)(0.08206)(298)=12.2 L.

Gas mixtures and partial pressures (Chapter 10)

  • Dalton’s law: Ptotal = ΣPi.
  • Mole fraction: Xi = ni/ntotal and Pi = Xi·Ptotal.
  • This works especially well for ideal gases because gases behave independently in a mixture.
Quick example (partial pressure)

A tank contains 0.80 mol N2 and 0.20 mol O2 at Ptotal=2.0 atm. XO2=0.20 so PO2=0.40 atm.

Kinetic-molecular theory (what the gas laws “mean”)

  • Temperature tracks average kinetic energy: hotter gas = faster molecules = higher pressure at fixed volume.
  • Root-mean-square speed: urms = √(3RT/M) (M in kg/mol). Lighter gases move faster at the same T.
  • Graham’s law (effusion): r1/r2 = √(M2/M1).
Interpretation

Gas laws aren’t magic: they emerge from collision frequency (volume), collision energy (temperature), and number of particles (moles).

The First Law (energy bookkeeping)

  • ΔE = q + w where ΔE is the system’s internal energy change.
  • Sign convention: q>0 heat flows into system; w>0 work is done on system.
  • P–V work (expansion/compression): w = −PextΔV.
Units

q, w, and ΔE are energies (J or kJ). For gases, 1 L·atm = 101.325 J.

Reversible vs irreversible (why the word matters)

  • Reversible: an idealized limit where the system is always infinitesimally close to equilibrium; it gives the maximum possible work.
  • Irreversible: real-world processes (finite gradients, friction) that produce less work than reversible paths.
Study tip

If a question asks for “maximum work,” you’re usually being steered toward a reversible model (e.g., for an ideal gas isothermal expansion: wrev = −nRT ln(Vf/Vi)).

Pistons and work intuition

Expansion work is “energy spent pushing back the surroundings.” If the system expands (ΔV>0), it does work on the surroundings, so w is negative.

Quick example (P–V work)

A gas expands from 2.0 L to 5.0 L against Pext=1.0 atm: w=−(1.0)(3.0)=−3.0 L·atm≈−304 J.

Real gases (when ideal breaks)

  • At high pressure/low temperature, attractions and molecular size matter, so PV ≠ nRT.
  • A quick diagnostic is the compressibility factor Z = PV/(nRT) = pVm/(RT): ideal ≈ 1; <1 suggests attractions; >1 suggests excluded volume effects.
  • Big picture: you’re upgrading the “equation of state” beyond the ideal-gas model when you need higher accuracy.

What to practice this week

  • Be able to choose system/surroundings and apply sign conventions correctly.
  • Convert between L·atm and J.
  • Solve basic ideal gas law problems and interpret “model assumptions.”
  • Do at least 3 partial pressure problems and 2 manometer/unit-conversion problems.

Heat, temperature, and calorimetry (Chapter 5)

Temperature tells you “how hot,” but heat q is the energy transferred because of a temperature difference.

  • Heat capacity: q = CΔT (C is for the whole object).
  • Specific heat: q = mcΔT (c is per gram).
  • Coffee-cup calorimetry (constant pressure): qp = ΔH.
  • Bomb calorimetry (constant volume): qv = ΔE.
Quick example (coffee‑cup heat)

m=50.0 g water warms by ΔT=5.0 °C, c=4.184 J·g−1·°C−1 → q=(50.0)(4.184)(5.0)=1.05×103 J. That heat came from the reaction/system.

Calorimetry problem pattern (always the same steps)

  • Write qsolution = m·c·ΔT (or use total Ccal if given).
  • Use energy conservation: qrxn + qsolution + qcal = 0 (signs matter).
  • Convert to per-mole enthalpy if required: ΔH = qrxn/nlimiting at constant pressure.
Common pitfall

If the solution warms (ΔT>0), then qsolution>0, so the reaction released heat: qrxn<0.

Enthalpy, standard states, and Hess’s law

  • Enthalpy: H = E + PV. At constant pressure, ΔH tracks heat flow.
  • Standard state (typical): 1 bar (often approximated as 1 atm), 25 °C, solutes at 1 M.
  • Formation enthalpy ΔHf°: enthalpy change to form 1 mol of compound from elements in their standard states.
  • Hess’s law: because H is a state function, you can add/subtract reactions and add/subtract their ΔH.
Pattern (formation enthalpies)

ΔHrxn° = ΣνΔHf°(products) − ΣνΔHf°(reactants). Multiply by stoichiometric coefficients ν.

Short worked example (formation enthalpies)

For CH4 + 2 O2 → CO2 + 2 H2O(l): ΔHrxn° = [ΔHf°(CO2)+2ΔHf°(H2O)] − [ΔHf°(CH4)+0]. Elements in standard state (O2) have ΔHf°=0.

Entropy and the 2nd/3rd laws (Chapter 19)

Entropy is best read as how spread out energy and matter are among available microstates.

  • Second law: ΔSuniv = ΔSsys + ΔSsurr ≥ 0 for spontaneous change.
  • Thermo definition: dS = δqrev/T (entropy change is computed along a reversible path).
  • Boltzmann: S = k ln W (W = number of microstates).
  • Third law: a perfect crystal at 0 K has S=0.
  • Reaction entropy: ΔSrxn° = ΣνS°(products) − ΣνS°(reactants).
Sign intuition

Entropy usually increases when you make more gas particles, go from liquid → gas, or increase “freedom of motion.” It often decreases when gas moles decrease or when ions form ordered solids.

PC-style quick example (ideal gas expansion)

For an ideal gas expanding isothermally: ΔS = nR ln(Vf/Vi). Doubling volume makes ΔS positive because there are more accessible microstates.

Connecting system and surroundings (a high-yield relation)

  • At constant pressure and temperature: ΔSsurr = −ΔHsys/T.
  • This lets you evaluate ΔSuniv using only system data (very common in thermodynamics problems).
Quick example

If a reaction releases ΔHsys = −25 kJ·mol−1 at 298 K, then ΔSsurr = +25000/298 = +84 J·mol−1·K−1 (surroundings gain entropy).

What to practice this week

  • Calorimetry setups: identify what “gets warmer” vs “gets cooler,” and use qsys = −qsurr.
  • Use Hess’s law by reversing/multiplying reactions correctly.
  • Predict the sign of ΔS and connect it to “more microstates.”
  • Do one full problem that combines ΔH and ΔS to decide spontaneity via ΔSuniv.

Gibbs free energy: the “spontaneity meter” at constant T and P

  • ΔG = ΔH − TΔS combines enthalpy and entropy into one criterion.
  • ΔG<0 spontaneous, ΔG=0 equilibrium, ΔG>0 nonspontaneous.
  • Temperature matters because the entropy term is multiplied by T.
Interpretation

ΔH is the “heat/energy” tendency; ΔS is the “dispersal” tendency. At higher T, dispersal effects dominate more strongly.

Equilibrium links: ΔG°, K, and Q

  • Standard link: ΔG° = −RT ln K.
  • Nonstandard conditions: ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q.
  • At equilibrium: Q=K and ΔG=0.
Quick example (K from ΔG°)

If ΔG° = −5.7 kJ·mol−1 at 298 K, then ln K = −ΔG°/(RT) ≈ 5700/(8.314×298)=2.30 so K≈10.

van’t Hoff idea (how K changes with temperature)

  • ln K = −(ΔH°/R)(1/T) + (ΔS°/R) gives a straight line for ln K vs 1/T.
  • Slope = −ΔH°/R, so exothermic reactions (ΔH°<0) behave differently with temperature than endothermic ones.

Equilibrium constants (Chapter 15 essentials)

  • For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]c[D]d/([A]a[B]b).
  • Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from K expressions (their activities are ~1).
  • For gases: Kp uses partial pressures, and Kp = Kc(RT)Δn where Δn is moles(gas) products − reactants.
  • Rules: reverse reaction → 1/K; multiply coefficients by n → Kn; add reactions → multiply K’s.
Quick example (Q vs K reasoning)

If Q < K, the reaction proceeds forward (to make more products). If Q > K, it proceeds backward (to make more reactants).

ICE tables (the core equilibrium workflow)

  • I: write initial concentrations/pressures.
  • C: apply stoichiometric changes using ±x (or ±ax, etc.).
  • E: write equilibrium expressions in terms of x and plug into K.
  • Small‑x approximations can simplify, but always sanity-check (often <5% change).

Le Châtelier’s principle (predicting shifts)

  • Adding reactant / removing product shifts forward; adding product / removing reactant shifts backward.
  • For gases, increasing pressure (decreasing volume) shifts toward fewer moles of gas.
  • Temperature changes change K (treat heat as a reactant/product depending on endo/exo).

Physical chemistry framing (pc): chemical potential and extent of reaction

  • PC often tracks progress using the extent of reaction ξ (in mol), with dni = νidξ.
  • Chemical potential μ is “molar Gibbs energy”; equilibrium corresponds to a minimum in total G at constant T, P.
  • This leads to the same practical rules you use: ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q, and equilibrium when Q = K.

Intro to kinetics: rates and rate laws (Chapter 14)

  • Rate is a slope: rate = −Δ[A]/Δt for a reactant.
  • Rate law: rate = k[A]m[B]n where m, n come from experiment.
  • k depends strongly on temperature (Arrhenius comes in Week 6).
Method of initial rates (pattern)

If doubling [A] makes the rate 4× bigger (with other concentrations held constant), then the order in A is 2 because 2m=4 → m=2.

What to practice this week

  • Compute ΔG from ΔH and ΔS (watch units: J vs kJ).
  • Decide direction using Q vs K and interpret what “large K” means.
  • Set up at least 2 ICE tables (one with a small‑x approximation, one requiring a quadratic).
  • Use initial rates to determine reaction orders from data tables.

Integrated rate laws (what the graphs mean)

“Integrated” means we’ve solved the rate law so concentration is an explicit function of time.

  • Zero-order: [A]t = [A]0 − kt → plot [A] vs t is linear (slope −k).
  • First-order: ln[A]t = ln[A]0 − kt → plot ln[A] vs t is linear (slope −k).
  • Second-order: 1/[A]t = 1/[A]0 + kt → plot 1/[A] vs t is linear (slope +k).
Quick example (first‑order half‑life)

If k=0.231 min−1, then t1/2 = 0.693/k = 3.00 min. After 3.00 min, any initial amount drops to half.

Half-life patterns

  • First-order: half-life is constant (independent of [A]0).
  • Second-order: half-life grows as concentration drops (t1/2 = 1/(k[A]0)).

Graphical analysis: how to identify order from a dataset

  • Try three plots and look for the straight line: [A] vs t (0th), ln[A] vs t (1st), 1/[A] vs t (2nd).
  • The slope gives k (pay attention to sign and units of k).
Worked example (first-order from two points)

If [A]0=0.80 M and [A]t=0.20 M after t=40 s, then ln([A]t/[A]0)=−kt → ln(0.25)=−k(40) so k=0.0347 s−1.

Physical chemistry cross-check (pc): rate as a derivative

  • Instantaneous rate is a derivative: for aA → products, rate = −(1/a)d[A]/dt (keeps “rate” positive by definition).
  • Order is empirical (from data), but mechanisms explain why a certain rate law appears.
  • Use units as a sanity-check: k has units of s−1 (1st order), M−1s−1 (2nd order), etc.

Mechanisms: how reactions actually happen

  • Elementary step: one “collision event.” Its rate law follows its molecularity.
  • Mechanism: sequence of steps; adding them gives the overall reaction.
  • Intermediate: produced then consumed (does not appear in overall equation).
  • Catalyst: consumed then regenerated (appears in steps but cancels overall).
Fast check

If a species is in the overall reaction, it’s not an intermediate. If it appears on both sides after summing steps, it cancels (intermediate/catalyst).

Reaction coordinate diagrams (how to read them)

  • Peaks are transition states (highest-energy “bottlenecks”).
  • Valleys between peaks are intermediates for multi-step mechanisms.
  • Activation energy Ea controls speed; catalysts lower Ea but don’t change ΔE between reactants/products.
Common pitfalls

Don’t confuse thermodynamic favorability (ΔG) with speed (activation barriers). A reaction can be “downhill” but very slow.

Catalysis (Chapter 14.7)

  • Catalysts provide an alternate pathway with lower Ea, increasing rate without being consumed.
  • Homogeneous: same phase as reactants; heterogeneous: different phase (often surface catalysis).
  • Enzymes are highly specific catalysts that can show saturation kinetics (previewed Week 6).

What to practice this week

  • Identify reaction order from graphs and compute k from slope.
  • Use half-life patterns to recognize 1st vs 2nd order quickly.
  • Given a proposed mechanism, identify intermediates/catalysts and check if the summed steps match the net reaction.

Deriving rate laws from mechanisms

The goal is to rewrite the observed rate in terms of measurable reactant concentrations (not intermediates).

  • Rate-determining step (RDS): slowest step controls the overall rate expression.
  • Pre-equilibrium: a fast equilibrium before the slow step lets you solve for an intermediate.
  • Steady-state approximation: set d[intermediate]/dt ≈ 0 (formation rate ≈ consumption rate).
Mini worked pattern (pre‑equilibrium)

If A + B ⇌ I is fast and I + C → P is slow, then rate = k2[I][C] and [I] = (k1/k−1)[A][B], so rate = k[A][B][C].

Steady-state approximation (the core idea)

  • Assume the intermediate concentration stays low and roughly constant: d[I]/dt ≈ 0.
  • Set “rate of formation of I” = “rate of consumption of I” and solve for [I] in terms of reactants.
  • Substitute into the slow-step rate law to remove intermediates.
Common pitfall

Don’t set [I]=0. You set the change in [I] to approximately 0, not the concentration itself.

Michaelis–Menten kinetics (enzyme saturation)

  • Mechanism: E + S ⇌ ES → E + P.
  • Rate law form: v = Vmax[S]/(KM + [S]).
  • Meaning: when [S] ≪ KM, v increases ~linearly with [S]; when [S] ≫ KM, v → Vmax (enzyme saturated).
Quick example

If Vmax=1.2 mM·s−1, KM=2.0 mM, and [S]=2.0 mM, then v=Vmax/2=0.60 mM·s−1.

Temperature dependence (Arrhenius equation)

  • k = A e(−Ea/RT) where Ea is the activation energy barrier.
  • Arrhenius plot: plot ln k vs 1/T; slope is −Ea/R.
  • Two-temperature form: ln(k2/k1) = −(Ea/R)(1/T2 − 1/T1).
Worked example (solve Ea from two k values)

If k1=2.5×10−3 s−1 at 298 K and k2=1.0×10−2 s−1 at 308 K, then Ea≈106 kJ·mol−1.

Physical chemistry add-on (pc): barrier language

  • Both Arrhenius and transition-state ideas treat reactions as crossing an energy barrier.
  • Helpful interpretation: catalysts change the pathway (lower barrier) but don’t change the overall thermodynamic “before vs after” (ΔG°).

What to practice this week

  • Derive a rate law from a mechanism using pre-equilibrium or steady-state.
  • Use Arrhenius plots (ln k vs 1/T) to extract Ea.
  • Interpret Michaelis–Menten curves and explain what KM and Vmax mean physically.

Intro to Organic, Representing Organic Molecules, Nomenclature/Functional Groups, Electronic Structure

Organic chemistry mindset (structure → properties → reactivity)

  • Carbon forms four bonds (neutral carbon); bonding geometry comes from hybridization.
  • Hydrocarbons: alkanes (single bonds), alkenes (C=C), alkynes (C≡C), aromatics (benzene-like π systems).
  • Functional groups are the “reactive handles” that drive chemistry.
Why representation matters

Most organic mistakes are drawing/reading errors: missing hydrogens, wrong connectivity, or incorrect 3D orientation. Train your eye early.

Representations + electronic structure basics

  • Line-angle drawings: each vertex/end is a carbon; hydrogens on carbon are implied to satisfy 4 bonds.
  • σ vs π bonds: single = 1 σ; double = 1 σ + 1 π; triple = 1 σ + 2 π.
  • Hybridization: sp3 (tetrahedral, ~109.5°), sp2 (trigonal planar, ~120°), sp (linear, 180°).
  • Isomers: constitutional (connectivity differs) vs stereoisomers (3D differs).
Quick example (hybridization)

In an alkene carbon (C=C), each double-bond carbon is usually sp2. In an alkyne (C≡C), each triple-bond carbon is usually sp.

Naming (IUPAC basics) and functional groups

Alkane naming pattern

  • Choose the longest chain (parent name).
  • Number to give substituents the lowest set of locants.
  • List substituents alphabetically; use di-, tri-, tetra- for repeats.
Quick example (name)

A 5‑carbon chain with a methyl on carbon 3 is 3-methylpentane.

Common functional groups (recognize + predict properties)

  • Alcohol R–OH (H‑bonding, polar).
  • Ether R–O–R (polar, no O–H H‑bond donor).
  • Alkyl halide R–X (good leaving groups when X = Cl/Br/I).
  • Amine R–NH2 (basic lone pair).
  • Carbonyls: aldehyde R–CHO, ketone R–CO–R (polar C=O).
  • Carboxylic acid R–COOH (acidic, strong H‑bonding).

Hydrocarbon trends (from Chapter 24)

  • Alkanes: CnH2n+2 (saturated). Chain length ↑ → boiling point ↑ (stronger dispersion).
  • Cycloalkanes: CnH2n (a ring removes two H).
  • Isomer idea: same formula can mean different structures → different properties (especially boiling point).

Reactions of alkanes (Chapter 24.7 overview)

  • Combustion: hydrocarbons + O2 → CO2 + H2O (exothermic; “energy release” comes from making strong C=O and O–H bonds).
  • Radical halogenation: substitution under hv or heat (initiation, propagation, termination).
Radical halogenation pattern

Initiation: Cl2 → 2 Cl·. Propagation: Cl· + R–H → HCl + R·, then R· + Cl2 → R–Cl + Cl·. Termination: radicals combine.

What to practice this week

  • Convert between condensed, line-angle, and 3D wedge-dash drawings.
  • Name 10 alkanes (include branching) and draw from names.
  • Identify functional groups quickly and predict which parts of a molecule are polar/reactive.
Week 8

Bonding and Resonance, Curved Arrow Notation, Organic view of Acid-Base Chemistry

Ch 8: Bonding Ch 8 PDF Ch 24: Organic Compounds Ch 24 PDF Ch 26: Alkenes Ch 26 PDF Ch 16: Acid–Base Equilibria Ch 16 PDF PC Ch 8: Quantum theory (intro) PC Ch 8 PDF PC Ch 11: Molecular structure PC Ch 11 PDF PC Ch 18: Molecular interactions PC Ch 18 PDF

Lewis structures, formal charge, and resonance

  • Formal charge: FC = V − N − (B/2) (V valence e−, N nonbonding e−, B bonding e−).
  • Resonance: multiple valid electron placements; the real structure is a resonance hybrid.
  • Resonance stabilizes by delocalizing charge/electrons.
Resonance rules

Only move electrons (π bonds/lone pairs), not atoms. Keep overall charge the same. Favor structures with full octets and minimized charge separation when possible.

Nucleophiles and electrophiles (language of mechanisms)

  • Nucleophile: electron-rich (lone pairs/π bonds/negative charge) → donates electrons.
  • Electrophile: electron-poor (positive charge/δ+) → accepts electrons.
  • In most first-semester mechanisms, “nucleophile attacks electrophile” is the core story.
Rule of thumb

If an atom has a lone pair or a negative charge, it’s often nucleophilic. If it’s positively charged or attached to electronegative atoms (δ+ carbon), it’s often electrophilic.

Curved-arrow notation (electron flow)

  • Arrow tail starts at an electron source (lone pair or bond).
  • Arrow head points to an electron sink (an atom or bond being formed).
  • Double-headed arrows move an electron pair; fishhook arrows move one electron (radicals).
Quick proton-transfer pattern

Base lone pair → H. The H–A bond → A. That’s the entire “acid–base step” in arrow pushing.

Organic acid–base ideas (pKa as a shortcut)

  • Lower pKa = stronger acid (more stable conjugate base).
  • Stabilization factors: electronegativity, resonance, inductive effects, and hybridization (sp > sp2 > sp3 acidity).
How to predict direction using pKa

Acid–base equilibria favor the side with the weaker acid (higher pKa). Compare the acids on each side; equilibrium leans toward higher pKa.

Mini pKa ladder (for fast decisions)

  • Carboxylic acids ~4–5 (stronger acids).
  • Ammonium ions ~9–11 (conjugate acids of amines).
  • Alcohols ~16; water 15.7.
  • Terminal alkynes ~25; alkenes/alkanes ~44–50 (very weak acids).
How to use it

When choosing a base to deprotonate an acid, pick a base whose conjugate acid has a higher pKa than the acid you’re deprotonating.

What to practice this week

  • Draw resonance structures and identify the resonance-stabilized conjugate base.
  • Use arrows correctly (tail from electrons; head to where electrons go).
  • Rank acidity/basicity using structure, not memorization alone.
Week 9

Conformational Analysis, Cyclic Alkanes (cyclohexane)

Ch 24: Organic Compounds Ch 24 PDF PC Ch 11: Molecular structure PC Ch 11 PDF PC Ch 18: Molecular interactions PC Ch 18 PDF

Conformational analysis (Newman projections)

  • Conformers interconvert by rotation about single bonds (no bonds broken).
  • Torsional strain: eclipsing interactions raise energy.
  • Steric strain: large groups crowding increases energy (e.g., gauche butane).
Quick example (butane)

Most stable is anti (CH3 groups 180° apart). Gauche is less stable due to steric interaction; eclipsed is highest due to torsional + steric strain.

Energy language: what “strain” actually means

  • Torsional strain: eclipsing bonds (repulsion between bonding electron clouds).
  • Steric strain: atoms/groups get too close (Pauli repulsion).
  • Angle strain: bond angles forced away from ideal hybridization angles.

Cyclohexane chair conformations

  • Chair is most stable (minimizes torsional + angle strain).
  • Axial vs equatorial: ring flip swaps them, but “up/down” orientation stays the same.
  • 1,3-diaxial interactions make axial substituents less favorable; bulky groups strongly prefer equatorial.
  • Cis/trans on rings describes same-face vs opposite-face substitution (not necessarily axial/equatorial).
Quick example (equatorial preference)

If a substituent has ~7.6 kJ·mol−1 preference for equatorial, then at 298 K the ratio is roughly e(ΔG/RT) ≈ e(7600/2477) ≈ 22, so equatorial is ~22× more common.

What to practice this week

  • Convert line structures ↔ Newman projections for simple chains.
  • Draw chair conformations and label axial/equatorial positions.
  • Identify the most stable chair for monosubstituted and disubstituted cyclohexanes.
Week 10

Alkenes (E and Z), CIP Rules, Stereochemistry, Chirality, (R and S), Relationships between Compounds (Enantiomers, Diastereomers, etc.)

Ch 25: Stereochemistry Ch 25 PDF Ch 26: Alkenes Ch 26 PDF PC Ch 11: Molecular structure PC Ch 11 PDF PC Ch 12: Molecular symmetry PC Ch 12 PDF

Alkene stereochemistry (E/Z) using CIP rules

  • Rotation about a C=C is restricted, so “which side” groups are on matters.
  • Cis/trans works only in simple symmetric cases; E/Z works generally.
  • CIP priority: higher atomic number wins; if tied, compare outward atom-by-atom.
  • Z = higher-priority groups on same side; E = opposite sides.
Common CIP pitfalls

Multiple bonds are treated as if atoms are duplicated. Don’t compare “big groups” by size—compare atomic numbers at the first point of difference.

Worked E/Z example (step-by-step)

Workflow

Pick one alkene carbon → rank its two substituents by CIP. Do the same on the other alkene carbon. If the two “#1 priorities” are on the same side, it’s Z; opposite sides, it’s E.

  • Remember: treat multiple bonds as “duplicated” atoms when ranking.

Chirality and R/S configuration

  • Chiral molecule: not superimposable on its mirror image.
  • Stereocenter: often (but not always) a carbon with four different substituents.
  • R/S steps: assign priorities 1–4, orient 4 away, trace 1→2→3 (clockwise R, counterclockwise S).
Orientation rule

If the lowest priority group (4) points toward you, the observed 1→2→3 direction is reversed (clockwise becomes S, counterclockwise becomes R).

Worked R/S example (step-by-step)

Workflow

Assign priorities 1–4 by atomic number. Put 4 pointing away. Trace 1→2→3: clockwise = R, counterclockwise = S. If 4 points toward you, flip the result.

Classifying relationships (a high-yield checklist)

  • Constitutional isomers: different connectivity (different bonds).
  • Enantiomers: mirror images, opposite configuration at all stereocenters.
  • Diastereomers: stereoisomers that aren’t mirror images (different at some, not all stereocenters).
  • Identical: same molecule in disguise (often via rotation or conformational change).

What to practice this week

  • Assign E/Z and R/S quickly and correctly.
  • Distinguish “configuration” (R/S, E/Z) from “conformation” (rotations, chair flips).
  • Classify pairs as identical / enantiomers / diastereomers / constitutional isomers.
Week 11

Relationships between Compounds (cont.), Meso Compounds, Properties of Chiral Compounds, Optical Rotation, Fischer Projections

Ch 25: Stereochemistry Ch 25 PDF Ch 28: Carbohydrates Ch 28 PDF PC Ch 12: Molecular symmetry PC Ch 12 PDF PC Ch 15: Magnetic resonance PC Ch 15 PDF

Meso compounds and “how many stereoisomers?”

  • With n stereocenters, the maximum is 2n stereoisomers.
  • Meso: has stereocenters but is achiral due to an internal plane of symmetry, reducing the count.
Interpretation

Meso compounds are “self-mirror” molecules. They can have R/S centers but still be achiral because the whole molecule has symmetry.

Properties: enantiomers vs diastereomers

  • Enantiomers: same mp/bp and most spectra in achiral environments, but opposite optical rotation.
  • Diastereomers: different physical properties and often different reactivity.
  • In a chiral environment (enzymes, chiral catalysts), enantiomers can behave differently.

Optical rotation, specific rotation, and enantiomeric excess

  • Optical activity: chiral molecules rotate plane‑polarized light.
  • Specific rotation: [α] = αobs/(l·c) where αobs is degrees, l is path length (dm), c is concentration (g/mL).
  • Racemic mixture (50:50 enantiomers) gives αobs = 0.
  • Enantiomeric excess: ee = |(%R − %S)|.
Quick example

αobs=+2.5°, l=1.0 dm, c=0.50 g/mL → [α]=2.5/(1.0×0.50)=+5.0. If a mixture is 70% R and 30% S, ee=40%.

Fischer projections (a powerful 2D shortcut)

  • Horizontal bonds come toward you; vertical bonds go away.
  • Rotating a Fischer by 180° keeps the same molecule; 90° changes configuration.
  • Swapping two groups in a Fischer inverts configuration; swapping twice returns original.
Practice focus

Be able to convert between wedge‑dash and Fischer and assign R/S correctly from either representation.

What to practice this week

  • Count stereoisomers for molecules with 2–3 stereocenters and identify when meso reduces the count.
  • Compute [α] and ee from simple data.
  • Convert wedge‑dash ↔ Fischer and assign R/S from both representations.
Week 12

Fischer Projections (cont.), Substitution Reactions: SN2 and SN1, Review

Ch 25: Stereochemistry Ch 25 PDF Ch 27: Alcohols/Halides Ch 27 PDF Ch 28: Carbohydrates Ch 28 PDF PC Ch 22: The rates of chemical reactions PC Ch 22 PDF PC Ch 23: The kinetics of complex reactions PC Ch 23 PDF

Fischer projections (advanced) and D/L notation

  • D/L is a relative system (not the same as R/S). For sugars, it’s set by the OH on the bottom stereocenter in the Fischer.
  • D-sugars: OH on the right; L-sugars: OH on the left (at the reference stereocenter).

D/L vs R/S (don’t mix these up)

  • D/L is a relative comparison to glyceraldehyde conventions (common in sugars/amino acids).
  • R/S is absolute configuration based on CIP rules.
  • A D-sugar can have an R or S stereocenter at other positions; they are different naming systems.

Substitution reactions (SN2 vs SN1): choose the mechanism

SN2 (one-step, backside attack)

  • Rate: rate = k[RX][Nu] (bimolecular).
  • Stereochemistry: inversion at the stereocenter.
  • Best substrates: methyl/1° > 2° ≫ 3° (sterics control).
  • Solvent: polar aprotic (keeps nucleophile “naked”).

SN1 (two-step via carbocation)

  • Rate: rate = k[RX] (unimolecular RDS = leaving).
  • Stereochemistry: racemization (planar carbocation).
  • Best substrates: 3° > 2° ≫ 1° (carbocation stability).
  • Solvent: polar protic (stabilizes ions).
  • Rearrangements: possible (hydride/alkyl shifts).
Quick decision examples

tert‑Butyl bromide + methanol → SN1 likely (3° substrate, protic solvent). 1‑bromopropane + CN− in DMSO → SN2 likely (1° substrate, strong nucleophile, aprotic solvent).

Extra SN1/SN2 decision cues

  • Leaving groups: better leaving group = faster (I− > Br− > Cl− ≫ F−; tosylate/mesylate are excellent).
  • SN1 needs a stable carbocation: 3° > 2° ≫ 1°; allylic/benzylic are especially favorable.
  • SN2 hates sterics: methyl/1° are best; bulky bases/nucleophiles can push elimination instead.

High-yield review map

  • Thermo: ΔE, ΔH, ΔS, ΔG, and how signs connect to spontaneity.
  • Equilibrium: K vs Q, ΔG° = −RT ln K, Le Châtelier reasoning.
  • Kinetics: rate laws, integrated forms, mechanism logic, Arrhenius temperature effect.
  • Organic: functional groups, stereochemistry, and “predict the mechanism from conditions.”

What to practice this week

  • Given substrate + nucleophile + solvent, choose SN1 vs SN2 and justify with 2–3 cues.
  • Predict stereochemical outcomes: inversion (SN2) vs racemization (SN1).
  • Do mixed review sets: one thermo (ΔG/K), one kinetics (integrated law), one equilibrium (ICE), one org mechanism (SN1/SN2).
