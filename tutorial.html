Week 1

Admin, Acid-Base Review

Ch 16: Acid–Base Equivalia Ch 16 PDF

Acid-Base Concepts: Acids and bases were first recognized by the properties of their aqueous solutions. The Brønsted–Lowry concept is more general than the Arrhenius concept and emphasizes the transfer of a proton (H⁺) from an acid to a base. A Brønsted–Lowry acid is a substance that donates a proton to another substance; a Brønsted–Lowry base is a substance that accepts a proton.

Conjugate Pairs: The conjugate base of a Brønsted–Lowry acid is the species that remains when a proton is removed from the acid. Together, an acid and its conjugate base are called a conjugate acid–base pair. The stronger an acid, the weaker is its conjugate base. Water is an amphiprotic substance, one that can function as either an acid or base.

Autoionization & pH: Water ionizes to a slight degree, forming H⁺ and OH⁻. The extent of this autoionization is expressed by the ion-product constant Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ (at 25 °C). The concentration of H⁺ is expressed in terms of pH: pH = −log[H⁺].

Strengths: Strong acids (e.g., HCl, HNO₃) are strong electrolytes, ionizing completely in solution. Weak acids (e.g., HA) only partially ionize, described by the acid-dissociation constant Ka. The concentration of a weak acid and its Ka value can be used to calculate the pH of a solution.

Week 2

Intro to Thermodynamics, State Variables, Systems, Surroundings, Ideal Gases, First Law, Internal Energy, Paths and Processes, Pistons

Ch 5: Thermochemistry Ch 5 PDF Ch 10: Gases Ch 10 PDF

System & Surroundings: Thermodynamics is the study of energy and its transformations. We define a specific amount of matter as the system; everything outside is the surroundings. A closed system can exchange energy, but not matter, with the surroundings.

Internal Energy & First Law: The internal energy (E) of a system is the sum of all kinetic and potential energies of its component parts. According to the First Law of Thermodynamics, the change in internal energy (ΔE) is the sum of heat (q) transferred and work (w) done: ΔE = q + w. Energy is neither created nor destroyed.

State Variables: The internal energy, E, is a state function; its value depends only on the state of the system and not on the path taken. Heat (q) and work (w) are path functions; their values depend on the specific way the system changes.

Ideal Gases & Pistons: To describe the state of a gas, we specify pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and quantity (n); related by the Ideal Gas Equation: PV = nRT. When a gas is produced or consumed in a piston (constant pressure), the system performs pressure–volume (P–V) work: w = −P_ext ΔV. Expansion (ΔV > 0) means work is done by the system (w is negative).

Week 3

Measuring Heat and Work, Heat Capacities, Calorimetry, Intro to Enthalpy, Standard States, Hess’s Law, Entropy, Second and Third Laws of Thermo

Ch 5: Thermochemistry Ch 5 PDF Ch 19: Thermodynamics Ch 19 PDF

Enthalpy & Calorimetry: We define a new state function called enthalpy (H). At constant pressure, the change in enthalpy (ΔH) equals the heat gained or lost by the system (qp). For an endothermic process, ΔH > 0; for exothermic, ΔH < 0. Calorimetry measures heat flow. The heat absorbed is related to heat capacity (C) and temperature change: q = m · c · ΔT.

Hess’s Law: Because H is a state function, if a reaction is carried out in a series of steps, ΔH for the overall reaction is the sum of the enthalpy changes for the steps. Standard enthalpies of formation (ΔHf°) are tabulated for substances in their standard states (pure form at 1 atm and 25°C).

Entropy & Laws: Entropy (S) is a measure of randomness or disorder, related to the number of microstates (W) by S = k ln W. The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that for any spontaneous (irreversible) process, the entropy of the universe increases (ΔSuniv > 0). The Third Law states that the entropy of a pure crystalline solid at 0 K is zero.

Week 4

Spontaneity, Gibbs Free Energy, Equilibrium, van’t Hoff plots, Intro to Kinetics, Rate Laws, Rate Constants, Reaction Orders, Method of Initial Rates

Ch 19: Thermodynamics Ch 19 PDF Ch 14: Kinetics Ch 14 PDF

Gibbs Free Energy: Gibbs free energy (G) combines enthalpy and entropy: G = H − TS. For a constant T process: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. When ΔG is negative, the process is spontaneous. At equilibrium, ΔG = 0.

Equilibrium & van't Hoff: The standard free-energy change is related to the equilibrium constant (K) by ΔG° = −RT ln K. Changes in temperature affect K; this dependence is described by the van't Hoff equation, where a plot of ln K vs 1/T yields ΔH°.

Rate Laws: Chemical kinetics studies reaction rates (change in concentration per time). The quantitative relationship is the rate law: Rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ. The constant k is the rate constant; exponents m and n are reaction orders, determined experimentally (e.g., by the method of initial rates).

Week 5

Integrated Rate Laws and Graphical Analysis, Half-life, Overall Reactions, Mechanisms, Elementary Reactions, Reaction Coordinate Diagrams, Intermediates and Catalysts, Transition States

Ch 14: Kinetics Ch 14 PDF

Integrated Rate Laws: Rate laws can be integrated to find concentration at any time. For a first-order reaction (Rate = k[A]), ln[A] vs time is a straight line. The half-life (t1/2) is the time for concentration to drop by half; for first-order, t1/2 = 0.693/k.

Mechanisms: A reaction mechanism details the individual steps (elementary reactions). An intermediate is produced in one step and consumed in another. A catalyst increases rate without being consumed.

Energy Diagrams: The collision model explains rates. The minimum energy for reaction is the activation energy (Ea). The highest energy arrangement is the activated complex or transition state. A catalyst provides a different mechanism with lower Ea.

Week 6

Multi-Step Reaction Mechanisms, Rate Determining Step, Rate Laws from Mechanisms, Steady-State Approximation, Michaelis-Menten Enzyme Kinetics, Temperature-dependence of k (Arrhenius Equation)

Ch 14: Kinetics Ch 14 PDF

Rate Determining Step: In a multi-step mechanism, the overall rate is limited by the slowest step, called the rate-determining step (RDS). If a fast equilibrium precedes the RDS, it affects the rate law.

Steady-State & Enzymes: When no clear RDS exists, the steady-state approximation assumes intermediate concentrations are constant (d[I]/dt ≈ 0). Michaelis-Menten kinetics applies this to enzyme catalysis, describing saturation behavior.

Arrhenius Equation: Rate constants increase with temperature. k = Ae^(−Ea/RT). A plot of ln k versus 1/T yields a straight line with slope −Ea/R, allowing determination of activation energy.

Week 7

Intro to Organic, Representing Organic Molecules, Nomenclature/Functional Groups, Electronic Structure

Ch 24: Organic Compounds Ch 24 PDF

Organic Chemistry: The study of carbon compounds. Carbon forms four bonds. Hydrocarbons contain only C and H. Alkanes (single bonds) are aliphatic and relatively unreactive. Nomenclature is based on the longest continuous chain (meth-, eth-, prop-...) with numbered substituents.

Structure: Hybridization models bonding geometry: sp³ (109.5° tetrahedral). Structures are often drawn as line-angle formulas.

Functional Groups: The chemistry is dominated by functional groups. Examples: Alcohols (R-OH), Alkenes (C=C), Aldehydes (R-CHO), Amines (R-NH2). Heteroatoms like Oxygen play a major role.

Week 8

Bonding and Resonance, Curved Arrow Notation, Organic view of Acid-Base Chemistry

Ch 8: Bonding Ch 8 PDF Ch 26: Alkenes Ch 26 PDF

Bonding: Ionic bonding results from electrostatic attraction. Covalent bonding results from sharing electrons. Lewis structures depict these electron arrangements.

Resonance & Arrows: Organic chemists use curved arrow notation to describe the redistribution of valence electrons. This notation describes resonance structures (delocalization of electrons) or reaction mechanisms (movement from nucleophile to electrophile).

Week 9

Conformational Analysis, Cyclic Alkanes (cyclohexane)

Ch 24: Organic Compounds Ch 24 PDF

Conformations: Rotation about C—C single bonds gives rise to different conformations (e.g., staggered vs eclipsed). Staggered forms dominate due to lower steric repulsion. Newman projections visualize these conformers.

Cyclohexane: Cyclohexane has two main conformations: chair and boat. The chair form is more stable. It has two positions: axial (vertical) and equatorial (outward). Substituents prefer the equatorial position to minimize unfavorable 1,3-diaxial interactions.

Week 10

Alkenes (E and Z), CIP Rules, Stereochemistry, Chirality, (R and S), Relationships between Compounds (Enantiomers, Diastereomers, etc.)

Ch 25: Stereochemistry Ch 25 PDF Ch 26: Alkenes Ch 26 PDF

Alkenes (E/Z): Names are based on the longest chain containing the double bond. The E,Z system names geometric isomers using priority rules (Cahn-Ingold-Prelog). "E" = Entgegen (opposite), "Z" = Zusammen (together).

Chirality & R/S: Optical isomers (enantiomers) are non-superimposable mirror images containing a stereocenter. They are chiral. The absolute configuration is described by R,S notation using sequence priority rules to assign priority to each group around the center.

Week 11

Relationships between Compounds (cont.), Meso Compounds, Properties of Chiral Compounds, Optical Rotation, Fischer Projections

Ch 25: Stereochemistry Ch 25 PDF

Relationships: Molecules with 2+ chiral centers can form enantiomers or diastereomers (stereoisomers not mirror images). An achiral compound with chiral centers is a meso compound.

Optical Rotation: Enantiomers can be distinguished by their interaction with plane-polarized light. One rotates right (+), the other left (-). This property is called optical activity. A 50:50 mixture is a racemic mixture.

Fischer Projections: A 2D representation where horizontal lines are bonds coming out (wedges) and vertical lines go back (dashes).

Week 12

Fischer Projections (cont.), Substitution Reactions: SN2 and SN1, Review

Ch 27: Alcohols/Halides Ch 27 PDF

Nucleophilic Substitution: Reaction where a nucleophile replaces a leaving group (halogen) on an alkyl halide.

SN2 Mechanism: A bimolecular process. Attack of nucleophile occurs simultaneously with leaving group departure. Proceeds with inversion of configuration. Favored by primary halides.

SN1 Mechanism: A unimolecular process. Rate-determining step is formation of a carbocation intermediate (LG leaves first). Then nucleophile attacks. Carbocations are planar, leading to racemization. Favored by tertiary halides.
