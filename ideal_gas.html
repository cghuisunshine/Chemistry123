Newton’s Laws → Temperature (Entropy) → Ideal Gas Law

A physics-style derivation that explicitly starts from Newton’s 2nd law (impulse–momentum), builds a pressure formula from molecular collisions, then introduces the thermodynamic definition of temperature 1/T = (∂S/∂E)V,N, and finishes at PV = nRT.

Mechanics: impulse & momentum
Statistics: isotropy averages
Thermo: temperature from entropy slope
Result: PV = NkT = nRT
0

The model (what we assume and what we “model”)

Ideal-gas kinetic theory setup

We model a gas as N identical particles in a container of volume V. The goal is to compute pressure from mechanics, then connect it to temperature using entropy.

  • Particles have mass m, positions r, velocities v.
  • Between collisions, particles move freely (no forces in the bulk).
  • Collisions with walls are elastic.
  • Velocities are random and isotropic (no preferred direction).
  • (Ideal gas) Intermolecular forces are negligible.
Key idea: Pressure is not “mystical”—it is the average force from momentum transfer to the walls.
1

Newton’s 2nd law → impulse–momentum

Start from F = dp/dt

Newton’s second law:

F = d p / dt

Integrate over the short collision time:

∫F dt = Δp (impulse–momentum theorem)

This is the mechanical foundation: collisions cause momentum changes, and momentum change per time is force.

2

Single molecule collision with a wall

Compute Δp for an elastic bounce

Take a wall perpendicular to the x-axis. For one molecule:

Before: px = m vx
After (elastic): p′x = −m vx
Δpx = p′x − px = −2 m vx
Breaking it down: The wall reverses the x-momentum, so the magnitude of momentum transfer per hit is 2mvx.
3

Average force from repeated collisions

Force = momentum transfer rate

In a cube of side length L, time between hits on the same wall:

Δt = 2L / |vx|

Average force on the wall from this one particle:

F1 = (2m vx) / (2L / vx) = m vx2 / L

This is Newton’s law in action: same momentum per hit, hits per second depends on speed and box size.

4

Pressure from one particle → many particles

P = F/A and isotropic averaging

Wall area A = L2, volume V = L3.

P1 = F1/A = (m vx2/L) / L2 = m vx2 / V

Sum over N particles:

P = (m/V) Σ vx,i2

Isotropy gives ⟨vx2⟩ = ⟨vy2⟩ = ⟨vz2⟩ = (1/3)⟨v2⟩.

P = (1/3) (N m ⟨v2⟩) / V
PV = (1/3) N m ⟨v2⟩
5

Rewrite using average kinetic energy

Mechanics result before introducing temperature

Define average translational kinetic energy per particle:

⟨KE⟩ = (1/2) m ⟨v2⟩

Substitute into the pressure result:

PV = (2/3) N ⟨KE⟩
Important: Up to here we have not used temperature. This is purely Newton + averaging.
6

Thermodynamic definition of temperature

Temperature as an entropy slope
1/T ≡ (∂S/∂E)V,N

Meaning: temperature measures how fast entropy increases as you add energy (at fixed V and N).

Key point: In fundamental thermodynamics, T is defined by entropy, not by kinetic energy. The KE–T relation will be derived next for an ideal gas.
7

Ideal-gas entropy → KE–T relation

Stat mech input + differentiate

For a monatomic ideal gas, entropy has the form (constants omitted):

S(E,V,N) = Nk ln[ (V/N) (E/N)3/2 ] + const

Differentiate at fixed V, N:

(∂S/∂E)V,N = 3Nk / (2E)

Use the definition 1/T = ∂S/∂E:

1/T = 3Nk / (2E) ⇒ E = (3/2)NkT

Since E = N⟨KE⟩ for a monatomic ideal gas:

⟨KE⟩ = E/N = (3/2)kT
8

Finish: combine with the Newton-derived pressure equation

PV = (2/3)N⟨KE⟩ → PV = NkT → PV = nRT

Start from the mechanics result:

PV = (2/3)N⟨KE⟩

Insert ⟨KE⟩ = (3/2)kT:

PV = NkT

Convert particle number to moles:

N = nNA and R = NAk
PV = nRT

Takeaway: The ideal gas law is the macroscopic shadow of Newton’s laws plus the entropy-based definition of temperature.

Notes:
• This derivation is cleanest for a monatomic ideal gas (only translational KE).
• Real gases deviate at high pressure / low temperature due to finite molecular volume and intermolecular forces.
• Kelvin is required because the entropy definition produces an absolute temperature scale.
