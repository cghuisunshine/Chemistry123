Chapter 32: Solving Molecular Structure

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Use molecular formula and degree of unsaturation to constrain possible structures.
  • Interpret simple spectroscopic data (conceptually) such as IR and basic NMR features.
  • Combine multiple pieces of evidence to propose consistent structures.

Key Vocabulary

  • Index of hydrogen deficiency (IHD): counts π bonds and rings relative to a saturated formula.
  • Infrared (IR) spectroscopy: probes bond vibrations; characteristic peaks for functional groups.
  • NMR spectroscopy: probes magnetic environments of nuclei (often 1H or 13C).
  • Chemical shift: position of an NMR signal relative to a reference.
  • Integration: relative area under NMR signals, related to number of nuclei.

1. Formula and Unsaturation (Conceptual)

  • Given C, H, and heteroatom counts, determine IHD to see how many rings + π bonds are present.
  • Use IHD together with functional‑group clues to narrow possibilities (for example, IHD = 4 suggests benzene ring or multiple π bonds).

2. Spectroscopic Clues (Qualitative)

  • IR: broad O–H stretch, sharp C=O peak, etc., signal presence of key functional groups.
  • Basic 1H NMR: number of distinct signals tells how many unique proton environments; splitting patterns relate to neighboring protons.

3. Putting It All Together

  • Start from formula and IHD → list functional group possibilities.
  • Add spectroscopic clues to refine; check consistency with all data.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Describe in words how you would use IHD to distinguish between a saturated acyclic compound and one containing a ring or double bond.
  2. Give examples of IR features that suggest the presence of (a) an O–H group, (b) a C=O group.
  3. Outline a general strategy (steps) for going from molecular formula + IR + simple NMR data to a plausible structure.
