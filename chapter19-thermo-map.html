19.1 Spontaneous Processes
Directionality, reversibility & isothermal changes
spontaneous vs nonspontaneous
reversible vs irreversible

Spontaneous processes proceed in one preferred direction under given conditions, while their reverses are nonspontaneous. Reversible paths are ideal limits used to define entropy changes and contrast with real irreversible changes.

Key ideas
  • A spontaneous process occurs in a given direction without continuous outside input (brick falling, iron rusting, ice melting at T > 0 Â°C).
  • A nonspontaneous process does not occur in the stated direction; the reverse of a spontaneous process is always nonspontaneous under the same conditions.
  • Spontaneity says nothing about rate: a process can be spontaneous but very slow (like rusting), or spontaneous and very fast (like acidâ€“base neutralization).
  • A reversible process is an ideal one that can be reversed so that both system and surroundings return to exactly their original states, with no net change anywhere. It proceeds through infinitesimal steps close to equilibrium.
  • A irreversible process is any real process; if you return the system to its starting point, the surroundings are left changed. All spontaneous processes in nature are irreversible.
  • An isothermal process happens at constant temperature; in thermodynamics we often analyze isothermal expansions and phase changes using entropy.

19.3 Molecular Interpretation & Third Law

Entropy counts the number of accessible microstates and ways to store energy. The third law defines an entropy zero point at absolute zero for a perfect crystal, anchoring absolute values of S.

Key ideas
  • A microstate is a particular microscopic arrangement of positions and energies of particles consistent with the macroscopic state (same T, P, V, n).
  • Entropy S increases as the number of possible microstates W increases, reflecting more ways to distribute energy and particles.
  • Molecules can store energy in translational, rotational, and vibrational motion. More complicated molecules generally have more modes and higher entropy.
  • Gases have far more accessible microstates than liquids, which in turn have more than solids; hence S(g) > S(l) > S(s) for the same substance.
  • Increasing temperature increases molecular motion and the number of accessible microstates, so entropy rises with T.
  • The third law of thermodynamics: S(0 K) = 0 for a pure, perfectly crystalline substance. At absolute zero, only one microstate (perfect order) is available.
