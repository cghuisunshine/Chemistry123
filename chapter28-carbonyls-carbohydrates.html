Chapter 28: Aldehydes, Ketones, and Carbohydrates

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Recognize aldehydes and ketones from structures and names.
  • Describe the polarity of the carbonyl group and its basic reactivity pattern (nucleophilic addition, conceptually).
  • Relate aldehydes and ketones to simple carbohydrate structures qualitatively.

Key Vocabulary

  • Carbonyl group: C=O functional group.
  • Aldehyde: carbonyl carbon bonded to at least one hydrogen (–CHO).
  • Ketone: carbonyl carbon bonded to two carbons.
  • Monosaccharide: simple sugar (for example, glucose, fructose).
  • Aldose: monosaccharide with an aldehyde group.
  • Ketose: monosaccharide with a ketone group.

1. Structure and Polarity

  • Carbonyl carbon is electrophilic; oxygen is relatively electron‑rich, making the C=O bond polar.
  • Aldehydes are generally more reactive than ketones toward nucleophilic addition because the carbonyl carbon is less hindered.

2. Connection to Carbohydrates (Conceptual)

  • Glucose is an aldose (contains an aldehyde group in its open‑chain form); fructose is a ketose (contains a ketone group).
  • In solution, many sugars exist in cyclic forms where the carbonyl reacts intramolecularly with an OH group to form hemiacetals or hemiketals.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Identify the carbonyl carbon in a simple aldehyde (CH3CHO) and a simple ketone (CH3COCH3).
  2. Explain qualitatively why nucleophiles are attracted to the carbonyl carbon rather than the carbonyl oxygen.
  3. In words or sketches, distinguish between an aldose and a ketose carbohydrate.
