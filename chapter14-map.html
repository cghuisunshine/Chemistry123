Overview: What this chapter is about

Chemical kinetics describes how fast reactions occur and what controls their rates. This chapter connects particle‚Äëlevel ideas (collisions and energy barriers) with macroscopic tools (rate laws, graphs, and Arrhenius plots) to interpret and predict reaction speeds.

  • Describe how physical state, concentration, temperature, and catalysts affect reaction rate.
  • Write and interpret rate laws, determine reaction order, and connect stoichiometric coefficients to relative rates of species.
  • Use integrated rate laws and half‚Äëlife expressions to relate concentration and time for zero‚Äë, first‚Äë, and second‚Äëorder reactions.
  • Apply the Arrhenius equation to relate rate constants to activation energy and temperature.
  • Explain how mechanisms and catalysts provide molecular‚Äëlevel pictures of how reactions proceed.

14.1 Factors That Affect Reaction Rates

How collisions, surface area, concentration, temperature, and catalysts control speed.

physical state & surface area concentration temperature catalysts

Chemical kinetics is the study of how fast reactions occur and which factors control their speeds. It complements thermodynamics: thermodynamics tells us whether a reaction is possible; kinetics tells us how quickly it actually happens.

On the particle level, reactions occur when molecules experience effective collisions ‚Äî collisions with enough energy and the right orientation to break old bonds and form new ones. Anything that changes how often effective collisions occur will change the rate.

  • Physical state and surface area: A reaction between a solid and a gas or liquid happens at the solid‚Äôs surface. Grinding a solid into a powder increases its surface area, giving more contact points for collisions and a faster rate.
  • Concentration: Higher concentration means more particles per volume, increasing collision frequency and usually increasing rate.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature means particles move faster and more of them have enough energy to overcome the activation barrier.
  • Catalysts: Catalysts offer an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy, so a larger fraction of collisions are effective at the same temperature.

Conceptually, reaction rate follows the same pattern as any ‚Äúper time‚Äù quantity:

rate ‚àù frequency of effective collisions

Here, ‚Äúeffective collisions‚Äù means collisions that both:

  • Have energy ‚â• activation energy (the minimum required to get over the barrier).
  • Have a geometry that aligns reacting bonds appropriately.

Each of the four main factors affects one of these pieces:

  • Surface area and mixing: control how often particles actually meet.
  • Concentration: changes collision frequency in a given volume.
  • Temperature: shifts the energy distribution of molecules, changing the fraction above the barrier.
  • Catalysts: change the height of the barrier and sometimes the orientation requirements.

Surface area: Powdered medicine dissolves and acts more quickly in the body than the same dose in a large tablet because more surface is in contact with stomach fluid at once.

Concentration: Steel wool burns slowly in air (about 20% O‚ÇÇ) but can flare intensely in nearly pure O‚ÇÇ, where the concentration of oxygen molecules ‚Äî and thus collisions with iron ‚Äî is much higher.

Temperature: Food spoils faster on a warm countertop than in a refrigerator because the biochemical reactions responsible run faster at higher temperature.

Catalysts: Catalytic converters in car exhaust systems use solid catalysts (like Pt, Pd) to make harmful gases react more quickly to safer products at the temperature of the exhaust, without being consumed in the process.

14.2 Reaction Rates

Defining rate in terms of concentration and connecting different species through stoichiometry.

For a reactant A in a reaction, we define its rate of consumption using a concentration‚Äìtime ratio:

This connection is important for converting an experimentally measured rate (say, disappearance of a colored reactant) into the rate of appearance of some product.

14.3 Concentration and Rate Laws

How rate depends on concentration and how to determine reaction orders.

rate = k [A]^m [B]^n reaction order initial rates

A rate law expresses how the rate of a reaction depends on reactant concentrations. For a reaction:

aA + bB ‚Üí products

the rate law often takes the empirical form:

rate = k [A]^m [B]^n
  • k is the rate constant (depends on temperature and mechanism).
  • m is the order with respect to A; n is the order with respect to B.
  • The overall order is m + n.
  • In general, m and n are not the same as the stoichiometric coefficients a and b, except for simple elementary steps.

Order tells you how sensitive the rate is to concentration. For example, if a reaction is second order in A, doubling [A] increases the rate by a factor of four (if other conditions are unchanged).

The units of k depend on the overall order so that the rate has units of M¬∑s‚Åª¬π. For a first‚Äëorder reaction, k has units s‚Åª¬π; for a second‚Äëorder reaction in a single reactant, k has units M‚Åª¬π¬∑s‚Åª¬π.

To determine m and n, we often use the method of initial rates:

  • Run several experiments at the same temperature but with different initial concentrations.
  • Measure the initial rate in each experiment (before concentrations change much).
  • Compare how the rate changes when one reactant‚Äôs concentration is changed while the others are held constant.

For example, if doubling [A] while keeping [B] constant doubles the rate, the reaction is first order in A. If doubling [A] quadruples the rate, the reaction is second order in A.

Once the exponents are known, you can plug any one set of initial data into the rate law to solve for k.

Consider A + B ‚Üí products. At a fixed temperature, you measure:

  • Exp. 1: [A] = 0.10 M, [B] = 0.10 M, rate = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ M¬∑s‚Åª¬π
  • Exp. 2: [A] = 0.20 M, [B] = 0.10 M, rate = 4.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ M¬∑s‚Åª¬π
  • Exp. 3: [A] = 0.10 M, [B] = 0.20 M, rate = 2.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ M¬∑s‚Åª¬π

Assume the rate law has the form rate = k [A]^m [B]^n.

  1. Compare exp. 1 and 2 (B constant at 0.10 M): [A] doubles, rate goes up by a factor of 4. That implies 2^m = 4 ‚Üí m = 2. The reaction is second order in A.
  2. Compare exp. 1 and 3 (A constant at 0.10 M): [B] doubles, rate doubles. That implies 2^n = 2 ‚Üí n = 1. The reaction is first order in B.
  3. The overall rate law is rate = k [A]^2 [B], overall order 3.

This pattern of reasoning is central to connecting experimental data to a quantitative rate law.

14.4 Change of Concentration with Time

Integrated rate laws, graphical tests, and half-life expressions for different orders.

[A] vs t ln[A] vs t 1/[A] vs t half-life

Integrated rate laws relate the concentration of a reactant to time. For a reaction A ‚Üí products, the form of the integrated rate law depends on reaction order and lets you:

  • Predict how [A] changes with time.
  • Determine the reaction order by seeing which plot is linear.
  • Use half-life to describe how quickly A is consumed.

Each order gives a characteristic ‚Äústraight-line‚Äù test:

  • Zero order: plot [A] vs. t ‚Üí straight line with slope ‚àík.
  • First order: plot ln[A] vs. t ‚Üí straight line with slope ‚àík.
  • Second order: plot 1/[A] vs. t ‚Üí straight line with slope +k.

For A ‚Üí products, with [A] in M, t in seconds:

  • Zero order (rate = k):
[A]t = [A]0 ‚àí k t

Here k has units M¬∑s‚Åª¬π. Concentration decreases linearly, and half-life depends on [A]0.

  • First order (rate = k[A]):
ln([A]t / [A]0) = ‚àík t
t1/2 = 0.693 / k

Here k has units s‚Åª¬π. Concentration decays exponentially, and the half-life is constant, independent of [A]0.

  • Second order (rate = k[A]¬≤):

Here k has units M‚Åª¬π¬∑s‚Åª¬π. As the reaction proceeds and [A] falls, the half-life gets longer because fewer reactant molecules collide.

  1. Use the integrated first‚Äëorder law: ln([A]t / [A]0) = ‚àík t.
  2. Plug in values: ln([A]t / 0.100) = ‚àí(0.0500 s‚Åª¬π)(60.0 s) = ‚àí3.00.
  3. Exponentiate: [A]t / 0.100 = e^(‚àí3.00) ‚âà 0.0498 ‚Üí [A]t ‚âà 4.98 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ M.
  4. Interpretation: only about 5% of the original reactant remains after 60 s.

You could also view this in terms of half-lives: t1/2 = 0.693 / 0.0500 ‚âà 13.9 s. After about four half-lives (~56 s), you‚Äôd expect about (1/2)‚Å¥ ‚âà 6.25% remaining, consistent with the calculation.

14.5 Temperature and Rate

Arrhenius equation, activation energy, and how temperature changes rate constants.

k = A e^(‚àíE‚Çê/RT) ln k vs 1/T activation energy

Activation energy E‚Çê is the energy barrier between reactants and products. On an energy diagram, it‚Äôs the difference in energy between the reactants and the highest point along the reaction pathway (the transition state).

At higher temperature, molecules move faster, so a larger fraction have enough energy to exceed E‚Çê. That‚Äôs why many reactions go much faster when warmed, even though the overall enthalpy change ŒîH may not change much.

Kinetics focuses on how many molecules per second get over the barrier, rather than the energy difference between reactants and products.

The Arrhenius equation relates the rate constant k to temperature:

k = A e^(‚àíE‚Çê / (RT))
  • k: rate constant (units depend on reaction order).
  • A: frequency factor, related to collision frequency and orientation.
  • E‚Çê: activation energy (J¬∑mol‚Åª¬π or kJ¬∑mol‚Åª¬π).
  • R: gas constant, usually 8.314 J¬∑mol‚Åª¬π¬∑K‚Åª¬π.
  • T: temperature in kelvin (K).

Taking natural logs gives a linear relationship:

ln k = ln A ‚àí (E‚Çê / R)(1/T)

A plot of ln k vs 1/T is a straight line with slope ‚àíE‚Çê / R. From its slope, you can solve for E‚Çê.

To compare two temperatures without computing A explicitly, use the two‚Äëpoint form:

ln(k‚ÇÇ / k‚ÇÅ) = ‚àí(E‚Çê / R)(1/T‚ÇÇ ‚àí 1/T‚ÇÅ)

This form lets you estimate E‚Çê from two measured rate constants, or predict how k changes when T changes.

Suppose a reaction has k‚ÇÅ = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ s‚Åª¬π at T‚ÇÅ = 300 K and k‚ÇÇ = 2.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ s‚Åª¬π at T‚ÇÇ = 310 K. To estimate E‚Çê:

  1. Compute the ratio of rate constants: k‚ÇÇ / k‚ÇÅ = 2.0 ‚Üí ln(k‚ÇÇ / k‚ÇÅ) = ln 2.0 ‚âà 0.693.
  2. Compute 1/T‚ÇÇ ‚àí 1/T‚ÇÅ: 1/310 ‚àí 1/300 ‚âà ‚àí1.08 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å¥ K‚Åª¬π.
  3. Plug into the two‚Äëpoint Arrhenius equation: 0.693 = ‚àí(E‚Çê / 8.314)(‚àí1.08 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å¥).
  4. Solve for E‚Çê: E‚Çê ‚âà 5.4 √ó 10‚Å¥ J¬∑mol‚Åª¬π ‚âà 54 kJ¬∑mol‚Åª¬π.

This shows that even a moderate activation energy can cause the rate to roughly double with a 10 K increase in temperature, especially in the room‚Äëtemperature range.

14.6 Reaction Mechanisms

Elementary steps, molecularity, intermediates, and the rate-determining step.

elementary steps rate-determining step intermediates

A reaction mechanism is a sequence of elementary steps whose sum gives the overall balanced equation. Each step represents a single, simple molecular event.

The molecularity of an elementary step is the number of molecules colliding in that step:

  • Unimolecular: a single molecule changes form, such as isomerization.
  • Bimolecular: two molecules collide and react.
  • Termolecular: three molecules collide (rare, because three-body collisions are unlikely).

For an elementary step, the rate law can be written directly from its molecularity: for example, for A + B ‚Üí C (bimolecular), the step‚Äôs rate law is rate = k[A][B]. This is different from the overall reaction, where the stoichiometric equation usually does not directly give the rate law.

In many mechanisms, one step is significantly slower than the others. This rate-determining step (RDS) acts like a bottleneck: the overall rate cannot be faster than this slowest step.

If the first step is slow and the others are fast, the overall rate law often matches the rate law of that slow step. For example, consider:

  • Slow: A + B ‚Üí X
  • Fast: X ‚Üí products

The observed rate is then approximately:

rate ‚âà k‚ÇÅ [A][B]

Here X is an intermediate: it‚Äôs produced in one step and consumed in another, and it does not appear in the overall balanced equation.

More complex mechanisms might use pre‚Äëequilibrium or steady‚Äëstate approximations to eliminate intermediates from the final rate law, but the key point is that any proposed mechanism must be consistent with the experimentally determined rate law.

Experimentally, the reaction:

2 NO‚ÇÇ(g) ‚Üí 2 NO(g) + O‚ÇÇ(g)

is found to have rate law rate = k [NO‚ÇÇ]¬≤.

Consider the mechanism:

  1. Slow: NO‚ÇÇ + NO‚ÇÇ ‚Üí NO‚ÇÉ + NO
  2. Fast: NO‚ÇÉ + NO‚ÇÇ ‚Üí NO + O‚ÇÇ + NO‚ÇÇ

Adding the steps cancels the intermediate NO‚ÇÉ and one NO‚ÇÇ, giving the overall reaction 2 NO‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 2 NO + O‚ÇÇ.

The slow step is bimolecular in NO‚ÇÇ, so its rate law is rate = k‚ÇÅ[NO‚ÇÇ]¬≤. Because it is rate‚Äëdetermining, the observed overall rate law matches this form, which is consistent with experiment.

Although this does not prove the mechanism uniquely, it shows how kinetics and mechanisms must agree. Mechanistic proposals that produce the wrong rate law must be rejected.

14.7 Catalysis

How catalysts speed up reactions without being consumed and why they matter.

lower E‚Çê, faster rate homogeneous vs heterogeneous enzymes

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a reaction but is not consumed overall. It participates in one or more steps but is regenerated by the end of the mechanism.

Catalysts work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. In terms of the Arrhenius equation:

  • The catalyzed pathway has a smaller E‚Çê.
  • At the same temperature, a smaller E‚Çê means a larger rate constant k.

Important: catalysts do not change the overall enthalpy change ŒîH or the equilibrium constant K. They speed up both the forward and reverse reactions equally, helping the system reach equilibrium faster but not changing the equilibrium composition itself.

Catalysts can be classified as:

  • Homogeneous: same phase as reactants (for example, all species in aqueous solution).
  • Heterogeneous: different phase, often a solid surface with gas or liquid reactants adsorbed.
  • Enzymes: biological catalysts, usually proteins, with specific active sites that bind substrates and dramatically lower E‚Çê.

We can compare uncatalyzed and catalyzed pathways using Arrhenius‚Äëtype expressions:

kuncat = A e^(‚àíE‚Çê,uncat / (RT))
kcat = A' e^(‚àíE‚Çê,cat / (RT))

Because E‚Çê,cat < E‚Çê,uncat, the exponential term for the catalyzed pathway is larger at the same T, so kcat > kuncat.

On an energy diagram, the catalyzed reaction has a lower ‚Äúhill‚Äù (activation barrier) between reactants and products but the same overall difference in energy between them.

Decomposition of hydrogen peroxide:

The reaction 2 H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ(aq) ‚Üí 2 H‚ÇÇO(l) + O‚ÇÇ(g) occurs slowly on its own at room temperature. In a sealed bottle of hydrogen peroxide, you see only gradual bubble formation over months.

Adding a catalyst such as iodide ion (I‚Åª) or the enzyme catalase (present in your cells) makes the reaction much faster: O‚ÇÇ bubbles appear vigorously. The overall balanced equation is unchanged, but the mechanism includes catalyst‚Äëdependent steps with lower activation energies.

Enzymes in biology:

Enzymes are highly specific catalysts that speed up particular biochemical reactions, sometimes by factors of 10‚Å∂ or more compared to the uncatalyzed rate. They bind reactants (substrates) at an active site, stabilize the transition state, and then release products while regenerating the catalyst.

These examples underscore that catalysts impact how fast equilibrium is reached, not what the final equilibrium composition is.
