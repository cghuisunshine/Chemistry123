Gibbs Free Energy as the Second Law (Constant T, P)

A step-by-step derivation showing why the criterion ΔG < 0 is just the Second Law rewritten for common chemistry conditions.

Key result: ΔG = −TΔSuniv
Conditions: constant T, constant P, only PV work
Spontaneous ⇔ ΔG < 0

Big picture

The Second Law is the fundamental “direction” law: nature proceeds in the direction that increases the entropy of the universe. Gibbs free energy is not a separate law—it's a convenient quantity that packages the Second Law into something you can compute using only system properties under constant temperature and pressure.

Second Law ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr ≥ 0

The difficulty: calculating ΔS_univ directly is inconvenient because it includes the surroundings. So we rewrite ΔS_surr in terms of measurable energy changes of the system.

Step 1 — Make the surroundings computable (thermal reservoir)

Key idea

Assume the surroundings behave like a thermal reservoir (huge heat capacity) at constant temperature T. For a reservoir, temperature is effectively constant during the heat exchange.

Important formulas & their meaning

Entropy definition (state function) ΔS = ∫ (δq_rev / T)

Because entropy is a state function, we can compute ΔS using a reversible path even if the real process is irreversible. For a reservoir with constant T:

Reservoir entropy change ΔS_surr = (1/T) ∫ δq_rev,surr = q_surr / T

Heat conservation between system and surroundings:

Heat exchanged q_surr = − q_sys

So we get the working expression:

Key bridge ΔS_surr = − q_sys / T
Interpretation: If the system releases heat (q_sys < 0), the surroundings gain heat (q_surr > 0) and the surroundings entropy increases.

Step 2 — Replace heat with enthalpy at constant pressure (qp = ΔH)

Key idea

Most chemistry is done at ~constant pressure (open to the atmosphere) and typically only PV work is involved. Under these conditions, the heat flow at constant pressure equals the enthalpy change.

Important formulas & their meaning

First Law ΔU = q + w

With only PV work:

PV work w = −P_ext ΔV

At constant external pressure (P_ext = P):

Constant-pressure heat ΔU = q_p − PΔV ⇒ q_p = ΔU + PΔV

Define enthalpy (this is a definition, not a law):

Enthalpy H ≡ U + PV

Take changes at constant pressure:

At constant P ΔH = ΔU + Δ(PV) = ΔU + PΔV

Compare the last two equations:

Result q_p = ΔH
Interpretation: At constant pressure, heat flow includes not only changing the system’s internal energy but also the energy needed to “push back” the atmosphere as volume changes. Enthalpy packages that together.

Step 3 — Substitute into the Second Law

Key idea

Combine Step 1 and Step 2 to eliminate the surroundings and express the Second Law using system quantities.

Important formulas & their meaning

From Step 1:

From reservoir ΔS_surr = − q_sys / T

From Step 2 (constant pressure, only PV work):

Replace heat q_sys = q_p = ΔH_sys

So:

Surroundings entropy (system enthalpy) ΔS_surr = − ΔH_sys / T

Put into the Second Law:

Second Law rewritten ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr = ΔS_sys − (ΔH_sys / T)

Multiply both sides by −T (with T > 0):

Multiply by −T −TΔS_univ = ΔH_sys − TΔS_sys

How temperature T is related to entropy S

Temperature is fundamentally defined through entropy, not through thermometers. In thermodynamics, temperature measures how energy changes with entropy.

Fundamental definition 1 / T ≡ (∂S / ∂E)V,N T ≡ (∂E / ∂S)V,N

This means temperature tells us how much energy must be added to produce a given increase in entropy.

Low T: adding energy greatly increases entropy
High T: adding energy barely increases entropy

From this definition, reversible heat transfer satisfies:

Reversible energy–entropy relation dS = dE / T

This directly explains why heat flows from higher temperature to lower temperature: doing so increases total entropy, satisfying the Second Law.

Thermal equilibrium TA = TB ⇔ (∂S/∂E)A = (∂S/∂E)B

This entropy-based definition of temperature is why the product TS appears naturally in Gibbs free energy.

Step 4 — Gibbs free energy emerges

Key idea

Define a new thermodynamic potential that matches the right-hand side:

Definition G ≡ H − TS

At constant temperature T, its finite change is:

At constant T ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

Compare with the result from Step 3:

Core identity (constant T,P) ΔG = −TΔS_univ
What this proves: Under constant T and P (and only PV work), Gibbs free energy is exactly the Second Law expressed as a system-only quantity.

Spontaneity criterion (why chemists love ΔG)

The Second Law says ΔS_univ ≥ 0. With ΔG = −TΔS_univ and T > 0:

Spontaneity table ΔS_univ > 0 ⇔ ΔG < 0 (spontaneous) ΔS_univ = 0 ⇔ ΔG = 0 (equilibrium) ΔS_univ < 0 ⇔ ΔG > 0 (non-spontaneous)

This is why it’s fair to say “Gibbs is the Second Law” — but only under the stated conditions.

When the shortcut needs care (boundaries of the derivation)

  • Not constant pressure: you can’t replace q by ΔH so simply.
  • Non-PV work present (electrical/shaft/etc.): energy bookkeeping changes; the neat “only ΔH” step needs modification.
  • Surroundings not a reservoir / T not constant: ΔS_surr = q_surr/T with a single T may fail.
Still true: The Second Law ΔS_univ ≥ 0 is always the fundamental rule.
Derived convenience: Gibbs free energy is the “constant T,P” package of that rule.

One-line exam summary

Memorize this At constant T and P (PV work only): ΔG = −TΔS_univ ⇒ ΔG < 0 means spontaneous.
