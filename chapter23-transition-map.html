23.1 The Transition Metals

Transition metals occupy groups 3‚Äì12, where d orbitals are being filled. Their partially filled d subshells give them characteristic radii, multiple oxidation states, colored compounds, and a rich set of magnetic behaviors that show up in everything from structural alloys to blood pigments.

Key ideas
  • Transition metals occupy the d-block (groups 3‚Äì12). Their valence shells contain (n‚àí1)d and ns electrons, and the (n‚àí1)d subshell is in the process of being filled across each series.
  • In nature they are found as minerals (Fe2O3, CuFeS2, etc.). Metallurgy involves mining, concentrating, reducing, and refining these ores and often alloying the resulting metals.
  • Metallic radii decrease from left to right at first due to increasing effective nuclear charge and stronger metallic bonding, then level off or increase; the lanthanide contraction causes 4d and 5d metals in a group to have similar radii and chemistry.
  • When transition metals are oxidized, ns electrons are lost before (n‚àí1)d electrons; most cations retain partially filled d subshells.
  • Partially filled d subshells lead to variable oxidation states, colored compounds, and magnetic behavior (paramagnetism, ferromagnetism, antiferromagnetism, ferrimagnetism).
  • Diamagnetic substances (all electrons paired) are weakly repelled by a magnetic field, whereas paramagnetic substances (one or more unpaired electrons) are attracted.

23.4 Nomenclature and Isomerism in Coordination Chemistry
How to name complexes & recognize their isomers

cis/trans & optical

Coordination compounds are named by listing ligands in alphabetical order followed by the metal with its oxidation state, and they can exhibit structural (linkage and coordination-sphere) and stereoisomerism (geometric and optical). Correct naming and isomer classification rely on careful attention to ligand identity, charge, and connectivity.

Key ideas
  • In salts, name the cation first, then the anion. Within a complex ion or neutral complex, name ligands in alphabetical order (ignoring numeric prefixes), then the metal.
  • Anionic ligands end in -ido (chloro, bromo, cyano, hydroxido, oxalato, carbonato). Neutral ligands often retain their molecular names but use special names like aqua (H‚ÇÇO), ammine (NH‚ÇÉ), carbonyl (CO).
  • Use di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa- for simple ligands; use bis-, tris-, tetrakis- for polydentate or already-prefixed ligands, writing their names in parentheses.
  • If the complex ion is an anion, the metal name ends in -ate, sometimes using Latin forms: ferrate (Fe), cuprate (Cu), nickelate (Ni), molybdate (Mo).
  • The metal oxidation state is given in Roman numerals in parentheses after the metal name.

23.5 Color and Magnetism in Coordination Chemistry

The vivid colors and magnetic properties of many transition-metal complexes originate in d‚Äìd electronic transitions between split d orbitals and in the number of unpaired d electrons. The wavelength of light absorbed is related to the energy gap E = hc/Œª, and the observed color is usually the complement of the absorbed color.

Key ideas
  • In many complexes, the metal d orbitals are split into two energy levels by the ligand field; electronic transitions between these levels absorb photons in the visible range (d‚Äìd transitions), producing color.
  • A complex appears colored because it absorbs certain wavelengths of visible light while transmitting or reflecting others. The perceived color is the mixture of unabsorbed light.
  • Complementary colors are opposite each other on a color wheel: if a complex absorbs orange, it appears blue; if it absorbs red, it appears green, and so on.
  • An absorption spectrum plots how strongly a sample absorbs each wavelength. Peaks in the visible region correspond to electronic transitions in the complex.
  • The number of unpaired d electrons in a complex determines whether it is paramagnetic (one or more unpaired electrons) or diamagnetic (all paired); this can be measured and compared with crystal- field predictions.

Crystal-field theory treats ligands as point charges or dipoles that split the energies of metal d orbitals. The resulting crystal-field splitting energy Œî controls both the color of a complex and whether it adopts a high-spin or low-spin electron configuration.
