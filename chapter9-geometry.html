Chapter 9: Molecular Geometry and Bonding Theories

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Use VSEPR theory to predict molecular shapes from Lewis structures.
  • Relate electron‑domain geometry to bond angles and lone‑pair effects.
  • Determine whether a molecule is polar or nonpolar.
  • Explain basic ideas of hybridization (sp, sp2, sp3) and σ/π bonding.

Key Vocabulary

  • VSEPR theory: Valence‑Shell Electron‑Pair Repulsion model; electron domains repel and arrange to minimize repulsions.
  • Electron‑domain geometry: arrangement of all electron domains (bonding + lone pairs) around a central atom.
  • Molecular geometry: arrangement of only the atoms.
  • Bond dipole: separation of charge across a bond due to electronegativity difference.
  • Hybridization: mixing of atomic orbitals on an atom to form new hybrid orbitals.
  • σ (sigma) bond: head‑on overlap of orbitals along the internuclear axis.
  • π (pi) bond: side‑by‑side overlap above and below the internuclear axis.

1. VSEPR and Common Shapes

  • Steps: draw the Lewis structure → count electron domains on the central atom → assign electron‑domain geometry → deduce molecular geometry.
  • Basic electron‑domain geometries (no lone pairs): linear (2), trigonal planar (3), tetrahedral (4), trigonal bipyramidal (5), octahedral (6).
  • Lone pairs compress bond angles and change molecular geometry (for example, tetrahedral domains: tetrahedral, trigonal pyramidal, bent).

2. Molecular Polarity

  • A molecule is polar if it has bond dipoles that do not cancel.
  • Symmetry matters: CO2 is nonpolar (linear, dipoles cancel) while H2O is polar (bent).

3. Hybridization and Multiple Bonds

  • sp: 2 domains → linear (for example, in CO2 and alkynes).
  • sp2: 3 domains → trigonal planar (for example, in alkenes).
  • sp3: 4 domains → tetrahedral (for example, in CH4).
  • Single bonds are σ; double bonds = 1 σ + 1 π; triple bonds = 1 σ + 2 π.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Use VSEPR to predict the molecular geometry and approximate bond angles of: CO2, BF3, CH4, NH3, and H2O.
  2. Decide whether each molecule is polar or nonpolar: CO2, SO2, CF4, CHCl3.
  3. Assign the hybridization of the central atom in: BeCl2, BF3, CH4, C2H2, and C2H4.
  4. For ethene (C2H4), identify which bonds are σ and which are π, and describe the geometry around each carbon.
  5. Explain why lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs and how that affects bond angles in NH3 and H2O compared to CH4.
