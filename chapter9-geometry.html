Chapter 9: Molecular Geometry and Bonding Theories

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Use VSEPR theory to predict molecular geometry from a Lewis structure.
  • Relate electron‑domain geometry, molecular shape, and approximate bond angles.
  • Determine whether a molecule is polar or nonpolar from shape and bond dipoles.
  • Explain basic hybridization ideas (sp, sp2, sp3) and σ/π bonding.

Key Vocabulary

  • VSEPR theory: Valence‑Shell Electron‑Pair Repulsion model; electron domains around a central atom arrange to minimize repulsions.
  • Electron‑domain geometry: arrangement of all electron domains (bonding + lone pairs) around the central atom.
  • Molecular geometry: arrangement of the atoms (ignores lone pairs).
  • Bond dipole: separation of charge across a bond due to electronegativity differences.
  • Hybridization: mixing of atomic orbitals on a central atom to form new hybrid orbitals.
  • σ (sigma) bond: head‑on overlap along the internuclear axis.
  • π (pi) bond: side‑by‑side overlap above and below the internuclear axis.

1. VSEPR and Common Shapes

  1. Draw a correct Lewis structure for the molecule or ion.
  2. Count electron domains (bonding pairs + lone pairs) around the central atom.
  3. Assign electron‑domain geometry based on the domain count.
  4. Determine molecular geometry by looking at positions of atoms only.

Electron‑Domain Geometries (No Lone Pairs)

  • 2 domains: linear, 180°.
  • 3 domains: trigonal planar, 120°.
  • 4 domains: tetrahedral, 109.5°.
  • 5 domains: trigonal bipyramidal, 90° & 120°.
  • 6 domains: octahedral, 90°.

Effect of Lone Pairs

  • Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs and compress bond angles slightly.
  • Example: In tetrahedral domains: CH4 (109.5°), NH3 (~107°), H2O (~104.5°).

Worked Example 1 – Shapes and Angles

Predict shapes and approximate bond angles for: CO2, BF3, CH4, NH3, H2O.

  1. CO2: 2 domains (O–C–O) → linear, 180°.
  2. BF3: 3 domains → trigonal planar, 120°.
  3. CH4: 4 domains, no lone pairs → tetrahedral, 109.5°.
  4. NH3: 4 domains, 1 lone pair → trigonal pyramidal, ~107°.
  5. H2O: 4 domains, 2 lone pairs → bent, ~104.5°.

2. Molecular Polarity

  • A molecule is polar if it has bond dipoles that do not cancel, giving an overall molecular dipole.
  • Geometry matters: symmetric shapes with identical substituents (like CO2, CF4) are often nonpolar even if individual bonds are polar.

Worked Example 2 – Polarity

Decide whether each molecule is polar or nonpolar: CO2, SO2, CF4, CHCl3.

  1. CO2: linear with equal and opposite C=O dipoles → nonpolar.
  2. SO2: bent; dipoles do not cancel → polar.
  3. CF4: tetrahedral with four identical C–F bonds → nonpolar overall.
  4. CHCl3: tetrahedral but not symmetric (three Cl and one H) → polar.

3. Hybridization and Multiple Bonds

  • sp: 2 electron domains → linear geometry (for example, in CO2, alkynes).
  • sp2: 3 electron domains → trigonal planar (for example, in alkenes, carbonyls).
  • sp3: 4 electron domains → tetrahedral (for example, in alkanes, water, ammonia).
  • Single bonds are σ; double bonds consist of 1 σ + 1 π; triple bonds are 1 σ + 2 π.

Worked Example 3 – Hybridization in Organic Molecules

Assign hybridizations to the carbon atoms in: ethane (C2H6), ethene (C2H4), ethyne (C2H2).

  1. Ethane: C–C single bond and four C–H single bonds → each C has 4 domains → sp3.
  2. Ethene: each C double‑bonded to the other and to two H atoms → 3 domains → sp2.
  3. Ethyne: each C triple‑bonded to the other and bonded to one H → 2 domains → sp.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Use VSEPR to predict the molecular geometry and approximate bond angles of: SO2, PCl3, SF6, XeF2.
  2. Decide whether each molecule is polar or nonpolar and briefly justify: CO2, NH3, CCl4, PF3.
  3. Assign hybridization to the central atom in: BeCl2, BF3, CH4, C2H2, and C2H4.
  4. For ethene (C2H4), identify which bonds are σ and which are π, and describe the geometry around each carbon atom.
  5. Explain why lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs and how this affects bond angles in NH3 and H2O compared to CH4.
