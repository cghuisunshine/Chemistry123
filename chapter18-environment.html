Chapter 18: Chemistry of the Environment

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Describe key chemical processes in the atmosphere (for example, ozone formation and depletion).
  • Explain the greenhouse effect and distinguish major greenhouse gases.
  • Outline basic chemistry of acid rain and water pollution.

Key Vocabulary

  • Troposphere: lowest layer of the atmosphere, where weather occurs.
  • Stratosphere: layer above the troposphere, containing the ozone layer.
  • Ozone (O3): allotrope of oxygen that absorbs UV radiation.
  • Greenhouse effect: warming of Earth due to gases that absorb IR radiation.
  • Greenhouse gas: gas that absorbs IR (for example, CO2, H2O vapor, CH4, N2O).
  • Acid rain: precipitation with pH < 5.6 due to dissolved acidic oxides (SO2, NOx).

1. Atmospheric Chemistry and Ozone

  • Ozone forms and decomposes via photochemical reactions; balance protects life from high‑energy UV.
  • Certain halogenated compounds (for example, chlorofluorocarbons) can catalytically destroy ozone.

2. Greenhouse Effect and Climate

  • Sunlight (short‑wavelength) passes through the atmosphere; Earth radiates IR which greenhouse gases absorb and re‑emit.
  • Increased greenhouse gas concentrations enhance this effect and shift energy balance.

3. Acid Rain and Water Chemistry

  • Combustion of sulfur‑containing fuels forms SO2, which can be oxidized and hydrated to form H2SO4.
  • NO and NO2 from high‑temperature combustion lead to nitric acid.
  • Acidic deposition can damage ecosystems, corrode materials, and affect water quality.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Explain qualitatively why ozone in the stratosphere is beneficial but ozone near Earth’s surface can be a pollutant.
  2. Name three major greenhouse gases and describe one source for each.
  3. Write a set of symbolic steps showing how SO2 from coal burning can contribute to acid rain.
  4. Describe one strategy for reducing emissions of (a) SO2, (b) NOx, or (c) CO2.
