Chapter 23: Transition Metals and Coordination Chemistry

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Recognize common oxidation states of selected transition metals.
  • Describe coordination complexes, ligands, and coordination number qualitatively.
  • Name simple coordination compounds using standard nomenclature rules.
  • Relate crystal‑field splitting to color and magnetism at a qualitative level.

Key Vocabulary

  • Transition metal: element with partially filled d subshell in elemental form or common ions.
  • Complex ion: central metal ion bonded to ligands.
  • Ligand: molecule or ion that donates a lone pair to the metal (for example, H2O, NH3, Cl−, CN−).
  • Coordination number: number of ligand donor atoms bonded to the metal.
  • Crystal‑field splitting: energy difference between sets of d orbitals in a ligand field.
  • High‑spin/low‑spin: describes number of unpaired electrons when crystal‑field splitting competes with pairing energy.

1. Structures and Nomenclature

  • Complexes often written in brackets: [M(L)x] with counterions outside.
  • Names list ligands (alphabetical) then metal with oxidation state in Roman numerals.
  • Neutral ligand names: aqua (H2O), ammine (NH3), carbonyl (CO); anionic ligands often end in ‑o (chloro, cyano).

Worked Example 1 – Oxidation States

Assign oxidation states to the metal in [Fe(CN)6]3−, [Cu(H2O)4]SO4, and [Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2.

  1. [Fe(CN)6]3−: each CN− is −1 → total ligands −6; complex is −3 → Fe must be +3.
  2. [Cu(H2O)4]SO4: SO42− means complex cation is 2+; water is neutral → Cu is +2.
  3. [Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2: overall neutral; 2 Cl− outside → complex cation is 2+; NH3 neutral, Cl inside is −1 → Co is +3.

2. Color and Magnetism (Qualitative)

  • d–d transitions between split d orbitals lead to characteristic colors in many transition‑metal complexes.
  • Stronger‑field ligands (for example, CN−, CO) cause larger splitting; can produce low‑spin complexes with fewer unpaired electrons.
  • Weak‑field ligands (for example, H2O, F−) give smaller splitting; often high‑spin complexes with more unpaired electrons.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Name each complex: [Cr(H2O)6]Cl3, K3[Fe(CN)6], [Cu(NH3)4]SO4.
  2. Assign oxidation states and coordination numbers for the metal in each complex in problem 1.
  3. Explain qualitatively why many transition‑metal complexes are colored while s‑block metal salts are often colorless.
  4. Describe how you could infer whether a complex is high‑spin or low‑spin from magnetic measurements (number of unpaired electrons).
