Chapter 23: Transition Metals and Coordination Chemistry

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Recognize common oxidation states of transition metals.
  • Describe coordination complexes, ligands, and coordination number.
  • Name simple coordination compounds using standard nomenclature.
  • Relate crystal‑field splitting to color and magnetism qualitatively.

Key Vocabulary

  • Transition metal: element with partially filled d subshell in elemental form or common ions.
  • Complex ion: central metal ion bonded to ligands.
  • Ligand: molecule or ion that donates a lone pair to the metal (for example, H2O, NH3, Cl−).
  • Coordination number: number of ligand donor atoms bonded to the metal.
  • Crystal‑field splitting: energy difference between sets of d orbitals in a ligand field.
  • High‑spin / low‑spin: relates to number of unpaired electrons depending on splitting and pairing energy.

1. Structures and Nomenclature

  • Complexes often written in brackets: [M(L)x] with counterions outside.
  • Names list ligands (alphabetical) then metal with oxidation state in Roman numerals.
  • Neutral ligand names: aqua (H2O), ammine (NH3), carbonyl (CO); anionic ligands often end in ‑o (chloro, cyano).

2. Color and Magnetism (Qualitative)

  • d–d transitions between split d orbitals lead to characteristic colors.
  • Stronger‑field ligands (for example, CN−, CO) cause larger splitting; can produce low‑spin complexes.
  • Number of unpaired electrons determines paramagnetism vs diamagnetism.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Assign oxidation states to the metal in [Fe(CN)6]3−, [Cu(H2O)4]SO4, and [Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2.
  2. Name each complex: [Cr(H2O)6]Cl3, K3[Fe(CN)6], [Cu(NH3)4]SO4.
  3. Explain qualitatively why some transition‑metal complexes are colored while others are nearly colorless.
  4. Describe how you could infer whether a complex is high‑spin or low‑spin from magnetic measurements.
