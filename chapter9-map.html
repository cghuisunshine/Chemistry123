9.1
Molecular Shapes
Geometry basics
R
Roots ‚Äì From Lewis to 3D
AB‚Çô patterns and characteristic bond angles.
‚ñ∂
  • Lewis structures are flat; **molecular shapes** are defined by 3D bond angles and lengths.
  • For molecules of the form **AB‚Çô**:
    • AB‚ÇÇ: linear (180¬∞) or bent (B‚ÄìA‚ÄìB < 180¬∞).
    • AB‚ÇÉ: trigonal planar (120¬∞), trigonal pyramidal, or T-shaped.
    • AB‚ÇÑ: tetrahedral; derivatives include trigonal pyramidal (3 B + 1 lone pair) and bent (2 B + 2 lone pairs).
    • AB‚ÇÖ: trigonal bipyramidal; derivatives include seesaw, T-shaped, and linear.
    • AB‚ÇÜ: octahedral; derivatives include square pyramidal and square planar.
  • Five symmetric arrangements of electron domains around A are the ‚Äúparents‚Äù of most shapes: linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, trigonal bipyramidal, and octahedral.
  • Removing one or more outer atoms from these arrangements gives the common derived shapes seen in real molecules (for example, bent, trigonal pyramidal, square planar).

Common ideal bond angles that correspond to parent shapes:

  • Linear (AB‚ÇÇ): 180¬∞.
  • Trigonal planar (AB‚ÇÉ): 120¬∞.
  • Tetrahedral (AB‚ÇÑ): 109.5¬∞.
  • Trigonal bipyramidal (AB‚ÇÖ):
    • Equatorial‚Äìequatorial: 120¬∞.
    • Axial‚Äìequatorial: 90¬∞.
  • Octahedral (AB‚ÇÜ): 90¬∞ between all neighboring positions.

Real molecules deviate slightly from these angles due to lone pairs and multiple bonds, but these values are the geometric ‚Äútargets‚Äù to compare to.

9.2
The VSEPR Model
Electron-domain repulsions
R
Roots ‚Äì Electron domains & repulsions
From domains to shapes.
‚ñ∂
  • Each **electron domain** is a region of high electron density around a central atom:
    • Lone pair (nonbonding pair).
    • Single bond.
    • Double or triple bond (counts as one domain for shape).
  • VSEPR principle: electron domains repel each other and arrange to minimize repulsions, like inflated balloons tied to a central point.
  • Two geometries:
    • Electron-domain geometry: arrangement of all domains (bonds + lone pairs).
    • Molecular geometry: arrangement of only the atoms (bonding domains) around the central atom.
  • When all domains are bonds, electron-domain and molecular geometries are the same; with lone pairs, they differ.
T
Trunk ‚Äì VSEPR algorithm & effects of lone pairs
Stepwise prediction & angle distortions.
‚ñ∂

Three-step VSEPR process

  • 1. Draw a correct Lewis structure; count electron domains around the central atom.
  • 2. Use the number of domains to assign the **electron-domain geometry** (Table: 2 ‚Üí linear, 3 ‚Üí trigonal planar, 4 ‚Üí tetrahedral, 5 ‚Üí trigonal bipyramidal, 6 ‚Üí octahedral).
  • 3. Use how many domains are lone pairs vs bonds to name the **molecular geometry**.

Relative ‚Äúsize‚Äù of domains

  • Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs, pushing bonds closer together (reducing bond angles).
  • Multiple bonds behave like ‚Äúfatter‚Äù domains and also compress adjacent bond angles slightly.
  • Typical example: CH‚ÇÑ (109.5¬∞) ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ (~107¬∞) ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO (~104.5¬∞) as lone pairs increase from 0 to 1 to 2.

Trigonal bipyramidal and octahedral cases

  • Trigonal bipyramidal: two distinct sets of positions:
    • Axial (aligned vertically).
    • Equatorial (form an equilateral triangle in a plane).
    Lone pairs occupy equatorial positions to minimize 90¬∞ repulsions.
  • Octahedral: all positions equivalent; lone pairs go opposite each other for maximum separation.

9.3
Molecular Shape and Molecular Polarity
Dipoles & symmetry
R
Roots ‚Äì Bond dipoles vs molecular dipole
Geometry controls cancellation or reinforcement.
‚ñ∂
  • A **bond dipole** arises when a bond connects atoms of different electronegativities; electrons are pulled toward the more electronegative atom.
  • The **molecular dipole moment** is the vector sum of all bond dipoles in the molecule.
  • Whether a molecule is polar or nonpolar depends on:
    • Bond polarities (differences in electronegativity).
    • 3D arrangement of bonds (molecular geometry).
  • In symmetric shapes with identical surrounding atoms (linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, trigonal bipyramidal, octahedral), bond dipoles can cancel ‚Üí nonpolar molecule.
  • If geometry is unsymmetric (lone pairs, mixed substituents) or dipoles do not cancel, the molecule is polar.
T
Trunk ‚Äì Vector picture of polarity
Adding bond dipoles qualitatively.
‚ñ∂

We can think of each bond dipole as an arrow (vector) pointing from Œ¥‚Å∫ to Œ¥‚Åª. For an AB‚Çô molecule:

  • If the vectors add to zero (perfect head-to-tail cancellation), the molecule has **no net dipole** (nonpolar).
  • If the vectors add to a nonzero resultant, the molecule has a **net dipole** (polar).

There is no simple algebra formula students need to apply; we combine the **geometry from VSEPR** with **electronegativity differences** to reason about vector addition qualitatively.

9.4
Covalent Bonding and Orbital Overlap
Valence-bond viewpoint
R
Roots ‚Äì Valence-bond (VB) picture
Overlap of atomic orbitals forms bonds.
‚ñ∂
  • In **valence-bond theory**, a covalent bond is formed when atomic orbitals on two atoms overlap and share a pair of electrons.
  • The shared electron pair is concentrated in the region of overlap and attracts both nuclei, lowering the energy.
  • The amount and orientation of overlap help determine bond strength, bond length, and molecular shape.
  • Two broad types of overlaps:
    • Head-on (end-to-end) ‚Üí œÉ bond.
    • Side-by-side ‚Üí œÄ bond.
T
Trunk ‚Äì œÉ bonds and orbital types
Where œÉ bonds come from.
‚ñ∂

œÉ bonds arise when orbitals overlap along the internuclear axis:

  • s‚Äìs overlap (e.g., H‚ÇÇ).
  • s‚Äìp overlap (e.g., HCl: H 1s with Cl 3p).
  • p‚Äìp head-on overlap (e.g., in F‚ÇÇ).

In each case, electron density is highest directly between the nuclei, which is an efficient way to hold atoms together. VB theory focuses on these localized overlaps to explain bonding patterns and shapes.

‚ñ∂

Example ‚Äì H‚ÇÇ

Two H atoms each contribute a 1s orbital with one electron. These orbitals overlap head-on to form one œÉ bond with electron density between the nuclei, lowering energy relative to separated atoms.

H 1s overlaps with a Cl 3p orbital that points along the H‚ÄìCl axis. The resulting œÉ bond has electron density skewed toward Cl due to its higher electronegativity, making the bond polar.

Two Cl atoms each have a half-filled 3p orbital oriented along the internuclear axis. These overlap head-on to form a œÉ(3p‚Äì3p) bond, with shared electron density between the Cl nuclei.

9.5
Hybrid Orbitals
VB meets VSEPR
R
Roots ‚Äì Why hybrid orbitals
Matching VB bonding to observed shapes.
‚ñ∂
  • Hybridization is the mixing of atomic orbitals on a central atom (e.g., s and p) to form new, equivalent hybrid orbitals.
  • The number of hybrid orbitals formed equals the number of atomic orbitals mixed.
  • Hybrids point toward directions that match VSEPR electron-domain geometries, allowing equivalent œÉ bonds and consistent shapes.
  • Common hybrid sets in organic/general chemistry:
    • sp: 2 hybrids ‚Üí linear.
    • sp¬≤: 3 hybrids ‚Üí trigonal planar.
    • sp¬≥: 4 hybrids ‚Üí tetrahedral.
  • Hybrid orbitals house œÉ bonds and lone pairs, while unhybridized p orbitals (if any) are available for œÄ bonding.
T
Trunk ‚Äì Mapping domains to hybrid sets
Electron-domain count ‚Üí hybridization.
‚ñ∂

Given a central atom:

  • 2 electron domains ‚Üí linear ‚Üí sp hybridization.
  • 3 electron domains ‚Üí trigonal planar ‚Üí sp¬≤ hybridization.
  • 4 electron domains ‚Üí tetrahedral ‚Üí sp¬≥ hybridization.

These domain counts can include bonds and lone pairs; hybrid orbitals are used for all œÉ bonds and lone pairs on the central atom.

domains 2 ‚Üí sp; 3 ‚Üí sp¬≤; 4 ‚Üí sp¬≥

The hybridization model works especially well for second-row (period 2) atoms with octet-compatible electron-domain geometries.

9.6
Multiple Bonds
œÉ and œÄ, localized & delocalized
R
Roots ‚Äì œÉ vs œÄ content
What single, double, and triple bonds contain.
‚ñ∂
  • A **single bond** is always one œÉ bond.
  • A **double bond** consists of 1 œÉ + 1 œÄ bond.
  • A **triple bond** consists of 1 œÉ + 2 œÄ bonds.
  • œÉ bonds result from overlap of hybrid (or unhybridized) orbitals along the internuclear axis.
  • œÄ bonds result from sideways overlap of unhybridized p orbitals, with electron density above and below (or in front of and behind) the œÉ bond axis.
  • œÄ bonds require unhybridized p orbitals, so they are commonly found with sp or sp¬≤ hybridized atoms.
  • Multiple bonds restrict rotation and often impose planarity in their local region because p orbitals must stay aligned for good overlap.
T
Trunk ‚Äì Localized vs delocalized œÄ systems
Resonance and œÄ-electron spreading.
‚ñ∂

Localized œÄ bond

  • œÄ electrons are shared between just two atoms (like the simple double bond in ethene).

Delocalized œÄ system

  • œÄ electrons are spread over three or more atoms (often associated with resonance structures).
  • Delocalization lowers energy and often makes all related bonds equivalent in length and strength.
  • Typical in benzene (C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÜ) and in ions like CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª and NO‚ÇÇ‚Åª.

When resonance structures show double bonds in different positions, the real molecule often has œÄ electrons delocalized across all those positions rather than any single localized double bond.

Example ‚Äì Ethene (C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÑ)

Each carbon is sp¬≤ hybridized (three domains). Three sp¬≤ hybrids form œÉ bonds (one C‚ÄìC and two C‚ÄìH per carbon). The remaining p orbital on each carbon overlaps sideways to form a œÄ bond. Each carbon is sp¬≤ hybridized, forming a hexagonal ring of œÉ bonds. The remaining six p orbitals overlap to form a delocalized œÄ system with six œÄ electrons spread around the ring. All C‚ÄìC bonds have equal lengths, intermediate between single and double bonds, consistent with delocalization rather than alternating single/double bonds.

9.7
Molecular Orbitals
Bonding as electron waves
R
Roots ‚Äì Bonding & antibonding MOs
Constructive vs destructive combinations.
‚ñ∂
  • Molecular orbitals (MOs) arise from combining atomic orbitals; each MO can hold 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Two atomic orbitals combine to give:
    • One **bonding MO**: lower energy, electron density between nuclei.
    • One **antibonding MO**: higher energy, with a nodal plane between nuclei (region of zero electron density).
  • Constructive combination of wave functions ‚Üí bonding MO; destructive combination ‚Üí antibonding MO.
  • MOs that have electron density along the internuclear axis are **œÉ MOs** (œÉ, œÉ*), while those with density above and below the axis are **œÄ MOs** (œÄ, œÄ*).
  • Electrons fill MOs starting from lowest energy, obeying Pauli exclusion and Hund‚Äôs rule, just like atomic orbitals.
T
Trunk ‚Äì Bond order in MO theory
Net bonding vs antibonding electrons.
‚ñ∂

Bond strength and existence in MO theory are captured by **bond order**, which compares bonding and antibonding electrons:

bond order = (N_bonding ‚àí N_antibonding) / 2
  • Higher bond order ‚Üí stronger, shorter bond.
  • Bond order 0 ‚Üí no net bonding; the species is not stable as a bound molecule.
  • Half-integer bond orders arise naturally when there are unpaired electrons (e.g., bond order 1/2, 3/2, 5/2).

Magnetic behavior from MO theory:

  • Unpaired electrons ‚Üí **paramagnetic** (attracted to external magnetic fields).
  • All electrons paired ‚Üí **diamagnetic** (weakly repelled by magnetic fields).

‚ñ∂

Example ‚Äì H‚ÇÇ vs He‚ÇÇ

H‚ÇÇ: 2 electrons. They both occupy the bonding œÉ(1s) MO; none in œÉ*(1s). Bond order = (2 ‚àí 0)/2 = 1 ‚Üí stable single bond. Diamagnetic (all electrons paired).

He‚ÇÇ: 4 electrons. œÉ(1s) receives 2, œÉ*(1s) receives 2. Bond order = (2 ‚àí 2)/2 = 0 ‚Üí no net bond, so He‚ÇÇ is not a stable molecule.

MO diagram for O‚ÇÇ shows 12 valence electrons filling MOs. Two electrons occupy degenerate œÄ* antibonding orbitals with parallel spins (Hund‚Äôs rule), resulting in two unpaired electrons.

Bond order of O‚ÇÇ = 2, consistent with a double bond; presence of unpaired electrons predicts **paramagnetism**. Experimentally, liquid O‚ÇÇ is strongly attracted into a magnetic field, confirming the MO picture and going beyond what a simple Lewis structure can show.

R
Roots ‚Äì 2s and 2p MOs
Building MO diagrams for B‚ÇÇ‚ÄìNe‚ÇÇ.
‚ñ∂
  • For homonuclear diatomic molecules from B‚ÇÇ to Ne‚ÇÇ, valence MOs are built from **2s and 2p atomic orbitals** on each atom.
  • 2s orbitals form œÉ(2s) bonding and œÉ*(2s) antibonding MOs; 2p orbitals form œÉ(2p), œÄ(2p), œÄ*(2p), and œÉ*(2p) MOs.
  • The relative ordering of œÉ(2p) and œÄ(2p) depends on 2s‚Äì2p mixing:
    • B‚ÇÇ, C‚ÇÇ, N‚ÇÇ: œÄ(2p) is lower in energy than œÉ(2p) (strong mixing).
    • O‚ÇÇ, F‚ÇÇ, Ne‚ÇÇ: œÉ(2p) lies below œÄ(2p) (weaker mixing).
  • Filling these MOs with the correct number of electrons and using the bond-order formula predicts bond strength, bond length trends, and magnetic properties.

Key results for Period 2 diatomics:

  • B‚ÇÇ: bond order = 1; 2 unpaired electrons ‚Üí paramagnetic.
  • C‚ÇÇ: bond order = 2; all electrons paired ‚Üí diamagnetic.
  • N‚ÇÇ: bond order = 3; very short, strong triple bond; diamagnetic; chemically inert.
  • O‚ÇÇ: bond order = 2; 2 unpaired electrons ‚Üí paramagnetic; bond consistent with double bond but with special magnetic properties.
  • F‚ÇÇ: bond order = 1; diamagnetic; relatively weak single bond compared with N‚ÇÇ or O‚ÇÇ.
  • Ne‚ÇÇ: bond order = 0; all bonding and antibonding MOs filled; no stable Ne‚ÇÇ molecule.

As bond order increases, bond length generally decreases and bond enthalpy (strength) increases, though exact values also depend on other factors like nuclear charge and orbital overlap.
