13.1 The Solution Process

Roots ‚Ä¢ Trunk ‚Ä¢ Leaves entropy, enthalpy, intermolecular forces

Solutions and components

  • A solution is a homogeneous mixture; its substances are called components.
  • The solvent is usually the component present in the greatest amount; other components are solutes.
  • In this chapter we mostly consider aqueous solutions, where water is the solvent.

Natural tendency to mix

  • When different substances are allowed to mix, they often do so spontaneously, increasing the system‚Äôs entropy (disorder or energy dispersal).
  • For gases, intermolecular forces are very weak, so mixing is driven largely by entropy: gases spread out to fill the available volume.

Role of intermolecular forces

  • For liquid and solid solutes/solvents, whether a solution forms depends on three interactions: solute‚Äìsolute, solvent‚Äìsolvent, and solute‚Äìsolvent.
  • The key question: are the new solute‚Äìsolvent attractions strong enough to ‚Äúpay for‚Äù breaking solute‚Äìsolute and solvent‚Äìsolvent attractions?
  • This is the molecular basis of the rule ‚Äúlike dissolves like‚Äù:
    • Polar/ionic solutes tend to dissolve in polar solvents (e.g., NaCl in water).
    • Nonpolar solutes tend to dissolve in nonpolar solvents (e.g., wax in hexane).

Dissolution vs chemical reaction

  • Dissolution itself is a physical process: solute particles separate and are surrounded by solvent; the chemical identity stays the same.
  • Sometimes a solute also undergoes a chemical reaction while dissolving (for example, a metal reacting with acid), but that‚Äôs separate from the pure solution process.

Enthalpy of solution

ŒîHsoln = ŒîHsolute + ŒîHsolvent + ŒîHmix

Meaning: the overall enthalpy change for forming a solution is the sum of:

  • ŒîHsolute > 0: energy to separate solute particles (break solute‚Äìsolute forces).
  • ŒîHsolvent > 0: energy to separate solvent molecules (break solvent‚Äìsolvent forces).
  • ŒîHmix < 0: energy released when new solute‚Äìsolvent attractions form.

Student interpretation:

  • If ŒîHmix is very negative (strong solute‚Äìsolvent attractions), the process is often exothermic (solution warms).
  • If ŒîHmix is not very negative, the process may be endothermic (solution cools), but can still be spontaneous because mixing increases entropy.
  • Whether a solution forms depends on the balance of enthalpy (ŒîH) and entropy, not enthalpy alone.

Example: NaCl in water vs NaCl in hexane

  • NaCl in water: ions are strongly hydrated by polar water molecules (ion‚Äìdipole forces). ŒîHmix is large and negative, so NaCl readily dissolves.
  • NaCl in hexane: hexane is nonpolar, cannot stabilize ions; ŒîHmix is too small to offset the cost of breaking ionic bonds ‚Üí NaCl remains undissolved.

Example: hot vs cold packs

  • Solutions that form with large negative ŒîHsoln (e.g., MgSO‚ÇÑ in water) can be used in instant hot packs.
  • Solutions with positive ŒîHsoln (e.g., NH‚ÇÑNO‚ÇÉ in water) absorb heat and are used in cold packs.

Tip: When deciding if a solute will dissolve, always think first about intermolecular forces and ‚Äúlike dissolves like,‚Äù then about energy changes and entropy.

13.2 Saturated Solutions & Solubility

Equilibrium dynamic dissolving & crystallization

Solubility

  • Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a given amount of solvent at a given temperature to form a stable, equilibrium solution.
  • It is often given as ‚Äúg of solute per 100 g (or mL) of water‚Äù or in mol¬∑L‚Åª¬π.
  • Solubility depends strongly on temperature and on the nature of both solute and solvent.

Types of solutions

  • Saturated solution: contains the maximum amount of dissolved solute in equilibrium with undissolved solute.
    • Dissolving and crystallization occur at equal rates.
  • Unsaturated solution: contains less solute than the solubility; more solute can still dissolve.
  • Supersaturated solution: contains more dissolved solute than the solubility predicts at that temperature; it is unstable and can crystallize suddenly.

Seed crystals and crystallization

  • In a supersaturated solution, solute particles must arrange in an orderly lattice to crystallize.
  • Adding a tiny seed crystal provides a template, making crystallization much easier and rapidly driving the system back to a saturated state plus excess solid.

Solubility as an equilibrium quantity

  • The solubility value describes a dynamic equilibrium between dissolved and undissolved solute.
  • At equilibrium:
    • Rate of dissolving (solid ‚Üí dissolved) = rate of crystallization (dissolved ‚Üí solid).
    • Concentrations and solubility remain constant.

Temperature dependence

  • Most ionic solids in water have higher solubility at higher temperatures.
  • Some solids are exceptions and may have decreasing solubility with temperature; solubility must be read from data or graphs.

Example: NaCl at 0 ¬∞C

  • Solubility of NaCl in water at 0 ¬∞C ‚âà 35.7 g per 100 mL of water.
  • 10.0 g NaCl in 100 mL water at 0 ¬∞C ‚Üí unsaturated (more NaCl can dissolve).
  • 35.7 g in 100 mL at 0 ¬∞C ‚Üí saturated.

Example: supersaturated sodium acetate

  • Prepare a hot solution with ‚âà 170 g sodium acetate in 100 mL water at a high temperature and cool it slowly to 20 ¬∞C.
  • At 20 ¬∞C, solubility is about 46 g per 100 mL. If no crystals form, the solution holds much more solute than equilibrium allows ‚Üí supersaturated.
  • Adding a seed crystal rapidly triggers crystallization of excess solute back to the stable saturated level.

Tip: To test if a solution is saturated, unsaturated, or supersaturated at a given T, you can compare how much solute is already dissolved to accepted solubility data, or add a tiny amount of solute and observe whether it dissolves or triggers crystallization.

13.3 Factors Affecting Solubility

Patterns IMFs, pressure, temperature

Solute‚Äìsolvent interactions

  • The stronger the attraction between solute particles and solvent molecules, the greater the solubility tends to be.
  • ‚ÄúLike dissolves like‚Äù:
    • Polar or ionic solutes (e.g., Na‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ, HCl) dissolve well in polar solvents (water).
    • Nonpolar solutes (e.g., C‚ÇáH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÜ, I‚ÇÇ) dissolve better in nonpolar solvents (CCl‚ÇÑ, hexane).
  • Miscible liquids mix in all proportions (e.g., ethanol and water). Immiscible liquids do not mix (e.g., gasoline and water).

Pressure and gas solubility

  • For gases in liquids, solubility increases with the gas‚Äôs partial pressure above the solution.
  • Compressing the gas (higher pressure) ‚Üí more frequent collisions with the liquid surface ‚Üí more gas dissolves until a new equilibrium is reached.

Temperature effects

  • For most ionic solids in water: solubility increases with temperature (added thermal energy helps break crystal lattice and hydrate ions).
  • For gases in water: solubility decreases as temperature increases (gas molecules gain energy and escape more easily).

Henry‚Äôs law

Sg = k Pg
  • Sg: solubility of gas (usually mol¬∑L‚Åª¬π).
  • Pg: partial pressure of the gas (atm, kPa, or Pa).
  • k: Henry‚Äôs law constant, depends on gas, solvent, and temperature.

Interpretation:

  • Solubility ‚àù pressure. Doubling the gas pressure doubles the equilibrium amount dissolved.
  • Different gases have different k values. More polarizable gases (heavier, larger atoms) often have higher solubility in water.

Qualitative rules to remember:

  • Cool, high-pressure conditions favor gases dissolving in liquids.
  • Solids usually dissolve better in hot water; gases dissolve better in cold water.

Example: CO‚ÇÇ in soft drinks

  • Soft drinks are bottled under high CO‚ÇÇ pressure. A higher PCO‚ÇÇ increases CO‚ÇÇ solubility (Henry‚Äôs law).
  • When the bottle is opened, PCO‚ÇÇ above the solution suddenly drops to near atmospheric levels, so CO‚ÇÇ escapes as bubbles until a new equilibrium is reached.
  • Warming the drink further decreases CO‚ÇÇ solubility, causing even more gas to come out of solution.

Example: deep-sea diving

  • At depth, total pressure and partial pressures of gases increase, so more gases dissolve in blood.
  • Rapid ascent ‚Üí pressures drop quickly and dissolved gases come out of solution as bubbles, causing decompression sickness (‚Äúthe bends‚Äù).
  • Using helium (lower solubility) instead of nitrogen in breathing mixtures reduces this risk.

Tip: If a problem mentions gas solubility, always check what happens to both pressure and temperature.

13.4 Expressing Solution Concentration

Qualitative vs quantitative concentration

  • ‚ÄúDilute‚Äù and ‚Äúconcentrated‚Äù are qualitative and relative; they don‚Äôt tell exact amounts.
  • Quantitative description uses concentration units such as mass %, ppm, mole fraction, molarity, and molality.

Why multiple units?

  • Mass-based units (mass %, ppm, ppb) are convenient when mass is measured directly or when dealing with trace components.
  • Molarity (M) is ideal for volumetric solution prep and stoichiometry in reactions.
  • Molality (m) is preferred for colligative properties because it does not change with temperature.

13.5 Colligative Properties

Numbers matter Raoult, ŒîT, Œ†, i

What are colligative properties?

  • Physical properties of solutions that depend only on the number of solute particles, not their identity.
  • Key colligative properties:
    • Vapor-pressure lowering.
    • Boiling-point elevation.
    • Freezing-point depression.
    • Osmotic pressure.

Electrolytes vs nonelectrolytes

  • Nonelectrolyte: does not dissociate into ions (e.g., glucose, urea). Each formula unit is one particle (i ‚âà 1).
  • Strong electrolyte: dissociates almost completely (e.g., NaCl, CaCl‚ÇÇ). Each formula unit ideally produces multiple particles (NaCl ‚Üí 2, CaCl‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 3).
  • Real solutions show ion pairing, so measured i is often slightly less than the ideal integer.

  • Osmosis: net movement of solvent through a semipermeable membrane from lower solute concentration (higher solvent concentration) to higher solute concentration.
  • Osmotic pressure Œ†: the external pressure required to stop osmosis.
  • Isotonic: same Œ†; hypotonic: lower Œ†; hypertonic: higher Œ†.

13.6 Colloids

Particles Tyndall effect, hydrophilic, hydrophobic

What is a colloid?

  • A colloid is a dispersion with particle sizes between those in true solutions and suspensions.
  • Particles are large enough to scatter light but small enough not to settle quickly under gravity.
  • Examples: milk (fat in water), fog (liquid droplets in gas), smoke (solids in gas).

Tyndall effect

  • Colloidal particles scatter light, making a beam visible as it passes through the mixture.
  • This scattering is called the Tyndall effect and explains why some colloids look cloudy.

Hydrophilic vs hydrophobic colloids

  • Hydrophilic (‚Äúwater-loving‚Äù): particles have polar or charged surface groups that interact strongly with water (e.g., proteins, enzymes).
  • Hydrophobic (‚Äúwater-fearing‚Äù): particles have little affinity for water (e.g., many organic droplets) and tend to aggregate unless stabilized.

Adsorption vs absorption

  • Adsorption: molecules or ions adhere to the surface of a particle.
  • Absorption: molecules penetrate into the interior (like water absorbed by a sponge).

Stabilizing hydrophilic colloids

  • Large biomolecules (proteins, enzymes) often fold so that:
    • Hydrophobic parts are tucked inside.
    • Hydrophilic groups (polar, charged) are exposed on the surface.
  • These surface groups hydrogen-bond or ionically interact with water, keeping the particles dispersed.

Stabilizing hydrophobic colloids

  • Adsorbing ions:
    • Ions (like anions) adhere to the particle surface.
    • This gives all particles similar charge, causing electrostatic repulsion that prevents them from clumping.
  • Using amphiphilic molecules (like soaps or detergents):
    • They have a hydrophobic tail and a hydrophilic head.
    • The tail embeds in the hydrophobic droplet; the head sticks out into water, making the droplet surface water-friendly and charged.

Example: Tyndall effect in the lab

  • Shine a laser pointer through a beaker of a clear solution (like salt water): the beam is hardly visible.
  • Shine the same beam through a colloidal dispersion (like diluted milk): the beam path lights up because colloidal particles scatter light.

Example: oil, water, and soap

  • Oil and water are immiscible; oil droplets coalesce and float.
  • Adding soap (e.g., sodium stearate) creates an emulsion:
    • Stearate‚Äôs hydrophobic tail embeds in oil droplets.
    • Its hydrophilic, charged head stays in the water.
    • Oil droplets now carry a charge and hydrophilic shell, so they repel each other and remain dispersed as a stable hydrophobic colloid.

Biological example: proteins in blood

  • Proteins like hemoglobin remain dispersed in blood plasma because their hydrophilic surface groups interact with water.
  • Changes in pH, ionic strength, or protein structure can reduce solubility and cause aggregation or crystallization (as in sickle-cell disease).

Tip: When you see cloudy but non-settling mixtures, think ‚Äúcolloid‚Äù and ask: what is the dispersing medium, what is the dispersed phase, and which stabilization mechanism is likely at work?
