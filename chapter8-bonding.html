Chapter 8: Basic Concepts of Chemical Bonding

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Explain ionic, covalent, and polar covalent bonding in terms of electrons.
  • Use electronegativity to estimate bond polarity and classify bonds.
  • Draw Lewis structures, including resonance forms and formal charges.
  • Relate bond strength and bond length to bond energies.

Key Vocabulary

  • Ionic bond: electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
  • Covalent bond: sharing of electron pairs between atoms.
  • Polar covalent bond: unequal sharing of electrons due to electronegativity differences.
  • Electronegativity: ability of an atom to attract electrons in a bond.
  • Lewis structure: 2D representation showing valence electrons and bonds.
  • Resonance: multiple valid Lewis structures that differ only in electron placement.
  • Formal charge: bookkeeping charge assigned to an atom in a Lewis structure.
  • Bond energy: energy required to break one mole of bonds in the gas phase.

1. Types of Bonds and Electronegativity

  • Large electronegativity difference (metal + nonmetal) → often ionic bonding.
  • Small to moderate difference (nonmetal + nonmetal) → covalent, often polar.
  • Nonpolar bond: electrons shared roughly equally (for example, Cl–Cl).

2. Lewis Structures and Formal Charge

  1. Count total valence electrons.
  2. Connect atoms with single bonds; complete octets (or duet for H).
  3. Use multiple bonds if needed; then assign formal charges:

Formal charge = (valence e−) − (nonbonding e−) − (bonding e−/2)

  • Prefer structures with smaller formal charges and negative charge on more electronegative atoms.

3. Bond Energies and Lengths

  • Multiple bonds are shorter and stronger than single bonds.
  • Average bond energies can be used to estimate ΔH for reactions by “breaking minus making.”

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Classify each bond as mostly ionic, polar covalent, or nonpolar covalent: Na–Cl, C–H, O–H, Cl–Cl.
  2. Draw Lewis structures (with formal charges) for CO2, NO3−, and SO32−. Identify any resonance.
  3. Explain why the O–H bond in water is polar and how this affects the properties of water.
  4. Compare bond length and bond energy for C–C, C=C, and C≡C bonds.
  5. Using average bond energies, estimate ΔH for the combustion of methane: CH4 + 2 O2 → CO2 + 2 H2O.
