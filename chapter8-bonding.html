Chapter 8: Basic Concepts of Chemical Bonding

8.0 Overview — Basic Concepts of Chemical Bonding

Key ideas

  • Valence electrons drive all bonding: electron transfer gives ionic solids, sharing gives covalent molecules, and delocalization gives metallic bonding.
  • Lewis symbols expose which electrons sit in the outer shell, and the octet rule explains why many atoms aim for eight surrounding electrons.

Important formulas & their meaning

Valence electrons Valence electrons = group number (main-group)

Counting valence electrons is always the first step before drawing Lewis symbols or structures; it is unaffected by where the electrons “come from.”

Octet goal Octet = 8 electrons (full s + p)

The octet rule represents the noble-gas configuration that atoms strive for through ionic or covalent bonding.

Short example(s)

Metals paired with nonmetals form ionic lattices; nonmetals sharing electrons form molecular covalent compounds. Recognizing which electrons participate is the first move.

8.1 Lewis Symbols and the Octet Rule

Key ideas

  • Lewis symbols place a dot for each valence electron around the element symbol so you can see how electron pairs might form.
  • Most s- and p-block atoms reach stability by ending up with eight valence electrons; this is the octet rule.
  • Main-group atoms usually have as many valence electrons as their group number; subtract 10 for p-block nonmetals to find the electrons outside the noble-gas core.

Important formulas & their meaning

Valence electron count Valence = group number (main-group); p-block: group number - 10

This tells you how many dots to place when drawing Lewis symbols; e.g., Group 16 gives six dots because 16 - 10 = 6.

Octet guideline Octet = 8 electrons (full s + p)

An octet completes the valence s and p orbitals, which is why atoms often gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve this number.

Short example(s)

Sulfur (Group 16) uses six dots in its Lewis symbol; when it forms SO₂ it shares electron pairs until each atom reaches an octet.

8.2 Ionic Bonding

Key ideas

  • Ionic bonds form when a metal donates electrons to a nonmetal, producing cations and anions that assemble into a lattice.
  • Lattice energy captures the stabilization when gaseous ions come together and offsets the endothermic ionization steps.
  • Born–Haber cycles sum atomization, ionization, electron affinity, and lattice formation to compute lattice energies.

Important formulas & their meaning

Formation of NaCl Na(s) + ½Cl₂(g) → NaCl(s) ΔHf° = -410.9 kJ/mol

The strongly negative enthalpy reflects the large energy release when the ionic lattice forms.

Lattice energy NaCl(s) → Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g) ΔHlattice = +788 kJ/mol

Lattice energy is the energy needed to separate a solid into gaseous ions, so its magnitude equals the stabilization gained when the lattice forms.

Coulombic trend E = k Q₁ Q₂ / d

This shows the electrostatic energy increases with ionic charge magnitudes and decreases with distance, so CaO (2+ & 2−) has a larger lattice energy than NaCl.

Short example(s)

Ordering CsI < NaF < CaO by lattice energy demonstrates how increased ionic charge and smaller radii raise the stabilization of the lattice.

8.3 Covalent Bonding

Key ideas

  • Covalent bonds form when atoms share electron pairs; each shared pair contributes to the octet of both atoms (hydrogen only needs two electrons).
  • Lewis structures show shared pairs as lines and lone pairs as dots, making it easy to track bonding.
  • Group trends predict typical bonds: Group 17 elements usually bond once, Group 16 twice, Group 15 three times, and Group 14 four times.
  • Higher bond order (more shared pairs) pulls atoms closer and strengthens the bond.

Important formulas & their meaning

Bond order Bond order = number of shared electron pairs

A single bond (order 1) shares one pair; increasing to double or triple bonds raises the bond order, making the bond shorter and stronger.

Short example(s)

CO₂ features two C=O double bonds so each atom has an octet; N₂ has a triple bond (bond order 3) giving a short 110 pm length and inertness.

8.4 Bond Polarity and Electronegativity

Key ideas

  • Atoms differ in electronegativity, so shared electrons shift toward the more electronegative atom and create partial charges (δ⁻ on the stronger puller, δ⁺ on the other).
  • Electronegativity generally increases across a period and decreases down a group; fluorine is the most electronegative element.
  • Dipole moments (in Debye) quantify how much charge separation exists in a bond.

Important formulas & their meaning

Electronegativity difference Δχ = |χ_A - χ_B|

Use Δχ to classify bonds: <0.4 is nonpolar covalent, 0.4–2 polar covalent, and >2 behaves mostly as ionic.

Dipole moment μ = Q × r

μ equals the separated charge Q times the distance r between them; a larger μ means greater polarity.

Short example(s)

HF has a large Δχ (~1.9) and strong dipole, HCl (Δχ ≈ 0.9) is moderately polar (μ ≈ 1.08 D), and LiF (Δχ > 3) behaves like an ionic solid with virtually full charge separation.

8.5 Drawing Lewis Structures

Key ideas

  • Count total valence electrons (adjusting for charges), place atoms (central atom usually least electronegative), and connect them with single bonds.
  • Complete octets on surrounding atoms, place remaining electrons on the center, and add multiple bonds if the central atom still lacks eight.
  • Use formal charges to choose the best structure: the lowest absolute charges, with negative values on more electronegative atoms, dominate.

Important formulas & their meaning

Total valence electrons Total = Σ group numbers ± charge

Add one electron per negative charge, subtract one per positive charge; later you will place these electrons in bonds and lone pairs.

Formal charge FC = valence - (nonbonding + ½ bonding)

FC estimates the charge on each atom; the structure with the least total FC magnitude and negative FC on electronegative atoms is best.

Short example(s)

COCl₂’s favored Lewis structure uses a C=O double bond and two C–Cl single bonds because this arrangement yields zero formal charge on every atom.

8.6 Resonance Structures

Key ideas

  • When a molecule cannot be described by a single Lewis structure (e.g., because bonds are equivalent), draw all valid resonance structures.
  • Atom connectivity must stay constant; only electron placements differ. The molecule is a resonance hybrid—an average of the contributors.
  • Dominant contributors have smaller formal charges; equivalent structures contribute equally.

Important formulas & their meaning

Resonance hybrid concept Resonance hybrid ≈ average of dominant contributors

The actual molecule reflects a weighted average of resonance forms, so measurable properties (bond length, energy) match the hybrid.

Short example(s)

Ozone has two resonance structures; both O–O bonds measure ≈127.8 pm. Benzene’s two Kekulé structures average to six identical C–C bonds of 140 pm and give aromatic stability.

8.7 Exceptions to the Octet Rule

Key ideas

  • Some species have an odd number of electrons (NO, ClO₂) so not every atom can have an octet.
  • Electron-deficient molecules (BF₃, BeH₂) are stable with fewer than eight electrons around the central atom.
  • Hypervalent species (PF₅, SF₆, ICl₄⁻) have more than eight electrons around a Period 3 or heavier atom because of their large size.

Important formulas & their meaning

Exception check Total valence = Σ group numbers ± charge

If the total cannot give every atom eight electrons, the molecule may be electron-deficient or needs resonance/multiple bonds.

Hypervalent rule of thumb Central atoms from Period 3 onward can comfortably hold >8 electrons

Large atoms can bond to more partners without extreme repulsion, which explains PF₅ and SF₆’s stability.

Short example(s)

BF₃ remains electron-deficient and accepts lone pairs; NO has an unpaired electron; PF₅ hosts ten electrons because phosphorus is large enough to accommodate five bonds.

8.8 Strengths and Lengths of Covalent Bonds

Key ideas

  • Average bond enthalpies measure the energy required to break a bond; higher bond order increases bond enthalpy.
  • Bond length decreases as bond order increases (single > double > triple) because more shared pairs pull the atoms closer.
  • Estimate reaction enthalpies by adding bond enthalpies for bonds broken and subtracting those formed.

Important formulas & their meaning

Bond order trends C−C: 154 pm, 348 kJ/mol; C=C: 134 pm, 614 kJ/mol; C≡C: 120 pm, 839 kJ/mol

As bond order rises, length shrinks and strength grows. The pattern holds for many atom pairs.

Reaction enthalpy approximation ΔH ≈ ΣD(bonds broken) − ΣD(bonds formed)

Adding the energies for bonds you break and subtracting the energies of bonds you form gives a quick estimate for ΔH.

Short example(s)

Forming COCl₂ from CO and Cl₂ breaks a C≡O and a Cl–Cl bond while forming a C=O and two C–Cl bonds; using averaged D values yields ΔH ≈ −141 kJ/mol, so the reaction is exothermic.
