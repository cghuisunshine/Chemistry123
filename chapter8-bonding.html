Chapter 8: Basic Concepts of Chemical Bonding

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish ionic, covalent, and polar covalent bonding in terms of electron transfer or sharing.
  • Use electronegativity differences to estimate bond polarity and classify bonds.
  • Draw Lewis structures, including resonance forms, and evaluate formal charges.
  • Relate bond strength and bond length to bond order and bond energies.

Key Vocabulary

  • Ionic bond: electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
  • Covalent bond: sharing of electron pairs between atoms.
  • Polar covalent bond: unequal sharing of electrons due to electronegativity differences.
  • Electronegativity: ability of an atom to attract electrons in a bond.
  • Lewis structure: 2D representation showing valence electrons and bonding pairs.
  • Resonance: multiple valid Lewis structures that differ only in electron placement.
  • Formal charge: bookkeeping charge assigned to atoms in a Lewis structure.
  • Bond energy: energy required to break one mole of bonds in the gas phase.

1. Types of Bonds and Electronegativity

  • Large electronegativity difference (metal + nonmetal) → largely ionic bonding.
  • Moderate difference (nonmetal + nonmetal) → polar covalent bonding.
  • Very small difference → nonpolar covalent bonding (for example, Cl–Cl).

Worked Example 1 – Classifying Bonds

Classify each bond as mostly ionic, polar covalent, or nonpolar covalent: Na–Cl, C–H, O–H, Cl–Cl.

  1. Na–Cl: metal + nonmetal, large electronegativity difference → mostly ionic.
  2. C–H: small difference → essentially nonpolar covalent.
  3. O–H: O more electronegative than H → polar covalent (δ− on O, δ+ on H).
  4. Cl–Cl: same element → nonpolar covalent.

2. Lewis Structures and the Octet Rule

  1. Count total valence electrons (sum from periodic table, adjust for charges).
  2. Write skeletal structure; connect atoms with single bonds.
  3. Complete octets on outer atoms, then on central atom if possible.
  4. Use multiple bonds if needed to satisfy octets and match electron count.

Formal Charge

Formal charge = (valence e−) − (nonbonding e−) − (bonding e−/2)

  • Good Lewis structures minimize formal charges and place negative charge on more electronegative atoms.

Worked Example 2 – Lewis Structure and Formal Charge for CO2

Draw a Lewis structure for CO2 and compute formal charges.

  1. Total valence electrons: C (4) + 2 × O (6) = 16 e−.
  2. Skeletal structure: O–C–O.
  3. Try double bonds: O = C = O uses 4 e− in bonds, leaving 12 for lone pairs (3 pairs on each O).
  4. Formal charges: each O: 6 − 4 (lone pairs) − 2 (bonding/2) = 0; C: 4 − 0 − 4 = 0. All zeros → good structure.

3. Resonance and Delocalization

  • When more than one valid Lewis structure exists (same connectivity, different electron placement), the real structure is a resonance hybrid.
  • Electrons are delocalized across the molecule, often stabilizing it.

Worked Example 3 – Resonance in NO3−

NO3− has three equivalent resonance forms with a double bond to each O in turn. Actual N–O bond lengths are equal and intermediate between single and double bonds.

4. Bond Lengths and Energies

  • Single bonds are longer and weaker; double bonds are shorter and stronger; triple bonds shorter and stronger still.
  • Average bond energies can estimate ΔH for a reaction by “bonds broken − bonds formed.”

5. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Classify each bond as mostly ionic, polar covalent, or nonpolar covalent: K–Br, H–F, C–Cl, N≡N.
  2. Draw Lewis structures (with formal charges) for CO2, NO3−, and SO32−. Indicate any resonance.
  3. Explain why the O–H bond in water is polar and how this polarity helps water dissolve ionic compounds.
  4. Compare bond length and bond energy qualitatively for C–C, C=C, and C≡C bonds.
  5. Using average bond energies (from your text), estimate ΔH for the combustion of methane: CH4 + 2 O2 → CO2 + 2 H2O, and comment on why the reaction is exothermic.
