Chapter 24 Tutorial — The Chemistry of Organic Compounds

24.1 General Characteristics of Organic Molecules

Key ideas

  • Carbon forms four bonds (sp³, sp², sp) and creates the hydrocarbon skeleton; hydrogen sits on the periphery because it forms only one bond.
  • Strong, nonpolar C–C and C–H bonds make hydrocarbons inert, so functional groups such as C–O or polar halides provide the reactive sites.
  • Organic molecules mix an inert hydrocarbon framework with functional groups that define reactivity.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alkane general formula: `CnH2n+2`. Every additional methylene (`–CH₂–`) adds two hydrogens while keeping the chain saturated.
  • Functional-group notation: `R–H` (alkanes) vs. `R–OH` (alcohols). The placeholder R represents the carbon skeleton, so the functional group (here –OH) controls reactivity.

Short example(s)

Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) reacts through its polar O–H bond while the CH₃CH₂– portion remains unchanged, illustrating the functional group focus.

24.2 An Introduction to Hydrocarbons

Key ideas

  • Hydrocarbons contain only carbon and hydrogen; the four classes are alkanes (single bonds), alkenes (double), alkynes (triple), and aromatic rings.
  • Alkanes form a homologous series (`CnH2n+2`); each member differs from the next by one CH₂ unit, giving similar chemistry and steadily increasing boiling points.
  • Dispersion forces grow with chain length, so light alkanes are gases, medium ones liquids (gasoline), and longer ones solids (waxes).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alkane formula: `CnH2n+2`. For n = 1, CH₄; n = 5, C₅H₁₂; the series extends indefinitely with predictable increases in molecular weight and physical properties.

Short example(s)

Pentane (C₅H₁₂) is a liquid with a boiling point near 36 °C and is a component of gasoline.

24.3 Structures of Alkanes

Key ideas

  • Carbons in alkanes are sp³ hybridized and tetrahedral (109.5°), forming sigma bonds by head-on overlap of hybrid orbitals.
  • Straight-chain alkanes (n-alkanes) have constitutional isomers when the carbon backbone branches.
  • Rotation about C–C single bonds gives conformational isomers, with staggered arrangements lower in energy than eclipsed ones.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Conformational energy: Staggered < eclipsed because torsional strain arises when adjacent bonds align; molecules adopt staggered conformations to minimize energy.

Short example(s)

Cyclohexane prefers the chair conformer over the boat by ~30 kJ/mol because it minimizes steric and torsional strain.

24.4 Alkane Nomenclature

Key ideas

  • Name alkanes by finding the longest chain, numbering it to give substituents the lowest locants, and listing substituents alphabetically with prefixes (di-, tri-).
  • Alkyl substituents replace the `-ane` ending with `-yl` (methyl, ethyl, propyl, etc.).
  • For multiple identical substituents use `di-`, `tri-`, `tetra-` etc., and separate location numbers with commas.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Naming formula: `number-ligand + parent alkane`. E.g., `3,4-dimethylheptane` indicates two methyls on carbons 3 and 4 of a seven-carbon chain.

Short example(s)

3-ethyl-2-methylpentane is drawn by adding an ethyl at C3 and a methyl at C2 to the pentane backbone (`CH₃CH(CH₃)CH(CH₂CH₃)CH₂CH₃`).

24.5 Cycloalkanes

Key ideas

  • Cycloalkanes are rings of carbons with formula `CnH2n` and arise when an alkane chain closes; short rings (<5) suffer angle strain.
  • Cyclohexane uses chair and boat conformations to approach 109.5° angles; the chair form is more stable because it avoids eclipsing and nonbonded interactions.
  • Substituents prefer equatorial positions to reduce 1,3-diaxial interactions (e.g., methylcyclohexane favors the equatorial methyl group).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Cycloalkane formula: `CnH2n`. Closing the chain removes two hydrogens compared to the corresponding open-chain alkane.

Short example(s)

In cyclohexane, the axial methyl experiences repulsion from axial hydrogens on the same face (1,3-diaxial), so the equatorial conformer is more stable.

24.6 Organic Functional Groups

Key ideas

  • Functional groups are reactive atoms/groups (e.g., –OH, –NH₂, C=O, halides) that define how organic molecules behave.
  • Many functional groups contain heteroatoms such as O and N; functional group chemistries are similar across different molecules once the group is present.
  • The alkyl symbol R represents the carbon framework when discussing reactions focused on the functional group (e.g., R–OH for alcohols).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Functional shorthand: `R–OH` (alcohol), `R–CHO` (aldehyde), `R–COOH` (carboxylic acid), etc.; the functionality dictates reactions while R stabilizes the molecule.

Short example(s)

Morphine, heroin, methadone, and naloxone all share key functional groups (alcohol, amine, ester) that influence solubility and receptor binding.

24.7 Reactions of Alkanes

Key ideas

  • Alkanes resist most reagents due to strong C–C and C–H bonds, but they combust with oxygen and undergo radical halogenation when exposed to heat or light.
  • Chain radical halogenation follows initiation (Cl₂ → 2 Cl·), propagation (Cl· abstracts H → R·; R· reacts with Cl₂ → RCl + Cl·), and termination (radicals recombine) steps.
  • Secondary and tertiary hydrogens are more easily abstracted than primary ones because the resulting radicals are more stable (selectivity ~1°:2°:3° = 1:4:5 for Cl, 1:100:1500 for Br).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Combustion: `CnH2n+2 + (3n+1)/2 O₂ → n CO₂ + (n+1) H₂O`. Example: `2 C₂H₆ + 7 O₂ → 4 CO₂ + 6 H₂O` releases ~2855 kJ.
  • Radical selectivity: `1°:2°:3° ≈ 1:4:5` for Cl and `1:100:1500` for highly selective Br due to differences in C–H bond energies and radical stabilization.

Short example(s)

Propane chlorination yields 1-chloropropane and 2-chloropropane; tertiary hydrogens would give even higher preference in bromination because tertiary radicals are most stabilized.
