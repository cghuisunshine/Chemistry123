18.1 Earth‚Äôs Atmosphere
Layers, composition & photochemistry

ppm & photons

The atmosphere is layered by temperature, composed mainly of N‚ÇÇ and O‚ÇÇ, and shaped by photodissociation and photoionization in the upper regions that shield Earth from high-energy radiation.

Key ideas
  • The atmosphere is divided into four main layers by how temperature changes with altitude: troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, and thermosphere, separated by boundaries called the tropopause, stratopause, and mesopause.
  • Troposphere: extends from the surface to about 10 km; temperature generally decreases with altitude; contains about 75% of atmospheric mass and nearly all weather.
  • Stratosphere: ~10‚Äì50 km; temperature increases with altitude due to ozone absorbing UV radiation; contains the ozone layer.
  • Air pressure decreases rapidly with altitude near the surface and more slowly higher up because the gas is compressible.
  • Dry sea-level air is ~78% N‚ÇÇ, ~21% O‚ÇÇ, ~0.93% Ar, ~0.04% CO‚ÇÇ by volume; the rest is trace gases in the ppm or lower range.
  • For gases, ppm is parts per million by volume or moles; since gas volume is proportional to moles, ppm equals mole fraction √ó 10‚Å∂.
  • In the upper atmosphere, high-energy solar radiation causes photodissociation (bond breaking without ion formation) and photoionization (electron ejection, forming ions), which intercept dangerous UV before it reaches the surface.

18.2 Human Activities and Earth‚Äôs Atmosphere

Human emissions of CFCs, sulfur and nitrogen oxides, hydrocarbons, and CO‚ÇÇ alter atmospheric composition, leading to ozone depletion, photochemical smog, acid rain, and enhanced greenhouse warming.

Key ideas
  • The ozone layer in the stratosphere absorbs much of the Sun‚Äôs UV-B and UV-C radiation. A major thinning would allow more harmful UV to reach Earth‚Äôs surface, increasing risks like skin cancer and ecosystem damage.
  • Ozone forms and is destroyed in a natural cycle involving O‚ÇÇ, O atoms, and a third body M. This cycle converts UV light into heat and maintains a band of elevated O‚ÇÉ concentration in the stratosphere.
  • Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are exceptionally stable in the troposphere and reach the stratosphere, where UV light photodissociates them to produce chlorine atoms that catalytically destroy ozone, leading to the Antarctic ‚Äúozone hole.‚Äù
  • Sulfur oxides (SO‚ÇÇ, SO‚ÇÉ) and nitrogen oxides (NO, NO‚ÇÇ) from combustion are key air pollutants; oxidized and dissolved in water, they produce sulfuric and nitric acids that contribute to acid rain.
  • Photochemical smog is a complex haze formed when sunlight drives reactions between NOx and volatile organic compounds (VOCs), generating ozone and other oxidants in the troposphere.
  • Greenhouse gases such as H‚ÇÇO, CO‚ÇÇ, CH‚ÇÑ, and N‚ÇÇO strongly absorb infrared radiation emitted by Earth‚Äôs surface, trapping heat in the lower atmosphere ‚Äî the greenhouse effect.
  • Fossil fuel combustion has raised atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ from preindustrial levels to about 400 ppm, contributing to global climate change.

18.3 Earth‚Äôs Water
Water cycle, oceans & carbon

salinity & ions
ocean acidification

Most of Earth‚Äôs water is in the salty world ocean and moves through a global cycle of phase changes that also redistributes heat. Dissolved carbon and carbonate equilibria link the ocean to the atmosphere and marine life.

Key ideas
  • Water covers about 72% of Earth‚Äôs surface. Because of hydrogen bonding, it has high boiling and melting points, high heat capacity, and excellent ability to dissolve ionic and polar substances.
  • The global water cycle includes evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration into the ground, and groundwater flow. Phase changes move both water and heat around the planet.
  • The world ocean (a single interconnected saltwater body) contains about 97% of Earth‚Äôs water. Only a tiny fraction exists as freshwater in rivers, lakes, and groundwater.
  • Typical seawater has a salinity of about 35 g of dissolved salts per kilogram of water, dominated by ions such as Cl‚Åª, Na‚Å∫, SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª, Mg¬≤‚Å∫, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, and K‚Å∫.
  • Seawater temperature decreases with depth: warm surface waters overlie a thermocline and cold deep waters around 4 ¬∞C. Light penetrates only the upper few hundred meters.
  • The ocean absorbs a significant fraction of atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ. Dissolved CO‚ÇÇ participates in a carbonic acid / bicarbonate / carbonate buffer system that maintains seawater pH around 8.0‚Äì8.3.
  • Freshwater (low dissolved solids) is scarce and precious. About 20% of it is in underground aquifers, which can store water for long times but may be vulnerable to contamination.

18.4 Human Activities and Water Quality
Pollution, DO & water treatment
DO & BOD
eutrophication
desalination & treatment

Human wastes and nutrients can overload natural waters with oxygen demand and pollutants. Desalination and multi-step municipal treatment aim to provide safe freshwater while limiting ecological damage.

Key ideas
  • Freshwater contains relatively low concentrations of dissolved salts (less than ~500 ppm); it includes rivers, lakes, and shallow groundwater and is essential but limited in supply.
  • Dissolved oxygen (DO) is necessary for fish and other aerobic aquatic organisms; it is usually measured in mg¬∑L‚Åª¬π or ppm. Levels below about 4‚Äì5 ppm can stress or kill many fish species.
  • Substances that can be broken down by aerobic bacteria are called biodegradable. Their oxidation consumes DO; such substances are called oxygen-demanding wastes.
  • If oxygen-demanding wastes are too abundant, DO can be depleted, causing fish kills and foul odors. The severity is often quantified by biochemical oxygen demand (BOD).
  • Nutrients (combined nitrogen and phosphorus) can overstimulate algal and plant growth. When these organisms die and decompose, they become additional oxygen-demanding waste, leading to eutrophication.
  • Desalination removes dissolved salts from seawater or brackish water, using either distillation or reverse osmosis, to yield water suitable for human use.
  • Municipal water treatment typically includes screening, sedimentation, sand filtration, aeration, and disinfection. Disinfectants like chlorine can form volatile by-products (trihalomethanes) with natural organic matter.

Important relationships & processes

1. Osmotic pressure and reverse osmosis

Osmotic pressure: œÄ = M R T

Here œÄ is osmotic pressure, M is the molarity of dissolved particles, R is the gas constant, and T is temperature. In reverse osmosis, a pressure greater than œÄ is applied to the saline side of a semi-permeable membrane to force water to flow toward the pure-water side, leaving salts behind.

2. Oxygen-demanding wastes

Biodegradable organic matter acts as fuel for aerobic bacteria:

Oxidation: organic matter + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO (+ other products)

The amount of O‚ÇÇ that would be consumed if this oxidation went to completion is the biochemical oxygen demand. High BOD means high potential to deplete DO in receiving waters.

3. Typical steps in water treatment

  • Coarse screening removes large debris.
  • Sedimentation allows heavier particles to settle.
  • Sand filtration removes fine suspended matter.
  • Aeration helps remove dissolved gases and can improve taste and odor.
  • Disinfection (e.g., chlorination or ozonation) kills pathogens; excess disinfectant or by-products are often controlled to meet safety standards.
Short examples & tips
Example ‚Äì Sewage and DO depletion

When untreated sewage enters a river, aerobic bacteria oxidize the organic matter, consuming dissolved oxygen. If the oxygen demand exceeds the rate at which O‚ÇÇ is replenished from the air and by photosynthesis, DO drops sharply. Fish and other aerobic organisms may die, and the water can develop strong odors until dilution and reoxygenation occur.

Example ‚Äì Why cleaner source water matters

If a water source contains less organic matter, less disinfectant is required to achieve the same level of pathogen removal. This reduces the formation of by-products like trihalomethanes, which arise when chlorine reacts with natural organics. Controlling pollution at the source thus makes downstream treatment both safer and cheaper.

  • Strategy: For any water-quality question, track both the mass of pollutant added and the system‚Äôs capacity to dilute, degrade, or treat it.
  • Common pitfall: Ignoring groundwater. Contamination of aquifers can persist for years or centuries because flow and mixing are slow compared to surface waters.
18.5 Green Chemistry
Designing safer, cleaner processes
12 principles
safer syntheses
atom economy

Green chemistry promotes chemical products and processes that minimize waste, hazards, and energy use while meeting society‚Äôs needs, guided by twelve widely adopted principles.

Key ideas
  • Earth is effectively a closed system for matter: most atoms remain on the planet, so chemical activities must be compatible with long-term environmental health if human societies are to thrive.
  • Green chemistry is the design and application of chemical products and processes that reduce or eliminate the use and generation of hazardous substances and preserve the environment.
  • The 12 principles of green chemistry cover prevention, atom economy, safer synthesis, safer products, solvents and auxiliaries, energy efficiency, renewable feedstocks, fewer derivatizations, catalysis, design for degradation, real-time analysis, and inherently safer processes.
  • Applying these principles can improve chemical technology across the board, from industrial synthesis to consumer products, without sacrificing performance.
prevention atom economy safer solvents catalysis renewable feedstocks
Important principles & their meaning

1. Prevention and atom economy

  • Prevention: It is better to prevent waste than to treat or clean it up after it has been created.
  • Atom economy: Synthetic methods should maximize the incorporation of all atoms from starting materials into the final product, minimizing by-products and waste.

2. Safer syntheses and safer chemicals

  • Wherever possible, synthetic methods should use and generate substances with little or no toxicity to humans and the environment.
  • Chemical products should be designed to perform their function while minimizing toxicity and to break down into harmless substances at the end of their life.

3. Solvents, energy, and catalysis

  • Use safer solvents and auxiliaries (or none at all) whenever possible, favoring substances like water or supercritical CO‚ÇÇ over volatile, toxic solvents.
  • Reactions should be conducted at near-ambient temperature and pressure when feasible to reduce energy consumption.
  • Catalytic reagents are preferred to stoichiometric reagents because they can improve yields and rates with lower energy input and fewer by-products.

4. Renewable feedstocks and design for degradation

  • Renewable feedstocks (for example, biomass-derived chemicals) should be used when they are technically and economically practical, rather than depleting finite resources.
  • Products should be designed to degrade after use into innocuous substances that do not persist in the environment or accumulate in organisms.
Short example & reflections
Example ‚Äì Greener production of styrene

Traditional styrene manufacture uses a two-step process: benzene + ethene ‚Üí ethylbenzene, then ethylbenzene + high-temperature steam ‚Üí styrene + H‚ÇÇ. Benzene is a carcinogen, both feedstocks are fossil-derived, and the process uses much energy. A greener route reacts toluene with methanol in a one-step catalytic process at moderate temperature. Methanol can be made from biomass-derived CO, and the new route improves atom economy, energy efficiency, and safety while maintaining product performance.

  • Strategy: When evaluating a process, ask how each green-chemistry principle applies: Where is waste generated? Could a catalyst replace a stoichiometric reagent? Can a fossil feedstock be replaced with a renewable one?
  • Perspective: Green chemistry is proactive: it aims to design problems out of a process up front rather than managing pollution at the end of the pipe.
