24.2 An Introduction to Hydrocarbons

A hydrocarbon contains only carbon and hydrogen. Aliphatics (alkanes/alkenes/alkynes) are contrasted with aromatic rings like benzene, and each can be saturated (single bonds) or unsaturated (double/triple).

A homologous series is a chain of molecules differing by ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚Äì units; neighbors have similar chemical behavior but gradual changes in boiling/melting points.

24.3 Structures of Alkanes

Conformation: a snapshot shape of a molecule created by rotating around a single C‚ÄìC bond; the same connectivity, different arrangement.

Constitutional isomer: molecules with the same molecular formula but different bonding sequences, so their properties (boiling point, melting point) vary.

24.4 Alkane Nomenclature

Every accepted IUPAC name follows prefix‚Äìparent‚Äìsuffix. Prefixes describe substituents (locations by number plus name), the parent gives the longest carbon chain, and the suffix (always ‚Äìane for alkanes) flags saturation.

Alkyl groups are alkanes missing one hydrogen, so they can attach as substituents; they end in ‚Äìyl (methyl, ethyl, propyl).

  • Select the longest continuous carbon chain as the parent.
  • Number the chain from the end closest to any substituent so the numbers are as low as possible.
  • Name each substituent, use di/tri/tetra when duplicates appear, and list them alphabetically (ignore multipliers when alphabetizing).

The structure `CH‚ÇÉCH(CH‚ÇÉ)CH(CH‚ÇÇCH‚ÇÉ)CH‚ÇÇCH‚ÇÇCH‚ÇÉ` is named 3-ethyl-2-methylpentane: pentane is the longest chain, with methyl on C-2 and ethyl on C-3.

Numbering from the end that gives the lower set of locants (e.g., 3,4-dimethylheptane instead of 4,5-dimethylheptane) avoids ambiguity and maintains the lowest possible numbers.

24.5 Cycloalkanes

Cycloalkanes are ringed alkanes whose general formula is CnH2n ; closing the ring removes two hydrogens relative to the chain.

Small rings (cyclopropane/cyclobutane) bend bond angles far below 109.5¬∞, increasing strain and reactivity.

Cyclohexane adopts a chair conformation where axial hydrogens point perpendicular to the average ring plane and equatorial hydrogens lie roughly in-plane.

Axial substituents experience 1,3-diaxial repulsions (~7.3‚ÄØkJ/mol for a methyl), so bulky groups prefer equatorial positions; boat conformations are ~30‚ÄØkJ/mol higher because of eclipsing and steric crowding.

Methylcyclohexane lives mostly in the equatorial chair because the equatorial methyl avoids 1,3-diaxial clashes.

Cyclopropane‚Äîwith its 60¬∞ angles versus the ideal 109.5¬∞‚Äîis strain-rich, so it reacts under conditions where an equivalent alkane would stay inert.
