Chapter 26 Tutorial — Chemistry of Alkenes and Alkynes

26.1 The Structure of Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

Key ideas

  • Alkenes (CnH2n) and alkynes (CnH2n−2) contain C=C or C≡C p bonds that are weaker than sigma bonds and prevent free rotation, making them more reactive than alkanes.
  • Sideways overlap of p orbitals above/below the internuclear axis forms the p bond; the electron density is absent on the axis.
  • Unsaturated small molecules (e.g., ethene, acetylene, isoprene) serve as monomers for polymers and appear in natural products such as terpenes.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alkene formula: `CnH2n`. Each CH₂ unit adds two hydrogens while maintaining a double bond.
  • Alkyne formula: `CnH2n−2`. Triple bonds further remove two hydrogens compared to the corresponding alkene.

Short example(s)

Ethene drives plant responses and polymerization; acetylene burns in oxyacetylene torches at ~3200 K.

26.2 Isomerism and Nomenclature

Key ideas

  • Geometric (E/Z) isomers arise because p bonds lock the substituents relative to each other; cis/trans descriptors depend on substituent priority (Cahn-Ingold-Prelog).
  • Alkene names indicate the position of the double bond; cycloalkenes share the formula CnH2n with alkynes but differ in connectivity.
  • Terpenes (e.g., limonene, menthol, carotene) are built from linked isoprene units, showing biological use of unsaturation.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Naming rule: Number the longest chain containing the unsaturation so the double/triple bond gets the lowest locant, and end with `-ene` or `-yne` (e.g., pent-1-ene, pent-2-yne).

Short example(s)

2-ethyl-3-methylpent-1-ene places the double bond at carbon 1 and uses substituent prefixes to indicate ethyl at C3 and methyl at C2.

26.3 Arrow Notation and Resonance Structures

Key ideas

  • Curved arrows show electron flow in mechanisms: from bonds/lone pairs to atoms or bonds; fishhook arrows illustrate single-electron movement (radicals).
  • Resonance structures (e.g., nitromethane, formate) illustrate electron delocalization; the actual molecule is a hybrid, stabilizing charge.
  • Nucleophiles donate electron pairs, electrophiles accept them; arrow notation maps these steps in additions or substitutions.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • General electrophilic addition: `Nu:` + `E⁺` → `Nu–E`. A nucleophile attacks an electrophile to form a new bond, often via a carbocation.

Short example(s)

In nitromethane/resonance, a curved arrow moves a lone pair on one oxygen to form an N=O bond while shifting the other N=O electrons into a lone pair.

26.4 Electrophilic Addition Reactions

Key ideas

  • Alkenes undergo electrophilic addition: halogenation, hydrohalogenation, hydration, and halohydrin formation all proceed via carbocation intermediates.
  • Carbocation stability (3° > 2° > 1°) and Markovnikov’s rule dictate regioselectivity (electrophile to least substituted carbon).
  • Bromination proceeds via bromonium ions, giving anti addition; halohydrins form when Br₂ and H₂O add across the double bond with OH replacing one halogen.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Markovnikov’s rule: `HX` addition: `H` attaches to the carbon with more hydrogens; the halogen ends up on the more substituted carbon because that carbocation is more stable.
  • Rate law: `Rate = k[alkene][HX]` because the slow step involves both reagents forming the carbocation.

Short example(s)

1-methylcyclohexene reacting with HCl gives 1-chloro-1-methylcyclohexane via a tertiary carbocation; bromination of cyclohexene yields trans-dibromocyclohexane due to the bromonium intermediate.

26.5 Catalytic Hydrogenation

Key ideas

  • H₂ adds across C=C or C≡C bonds with metal catalysts (Pt, Pd, Ni) in heterogeneous hydrogenation; surface adsorption lowers activation energy.
  • The catalyst adsorbs H₂ and alkene, breaks the H–H bond, and delivers atoms sequentially to the carbons before releasing the saturated alkane.
  • Hydrogenation turns liquid vegetable oils into solid fats (margarine) by saturating double bonds, raising melting points because straight chains pack more tightly.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Hydrogenation example: `C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆` (ΔH ≈ −137 kJ/mol) with Pt lowering the activation barrier by adsorbing reactants.

Short example(s)

3-methyl-1-pentene hydrogenates to 3-methylpentane; Pd/C yields cis-1,2-dimethylcyclohexane from the corresponding cyclohexene.

26.6 Addition Polymerization

Key ideas

  • Addition (chain-growth) polymerization links alkene monomers via radicals (initiation, propagation, termination) to form high-molecular-weight polymers such as polyethylene.
  • Initiators (e.g., peroxides) generate radicals; propagation adds monomers, and termination occurs when two radicals combine.
  • Polymer properties (density, stiffness) depend on molecular weight, branching (LDPE vs. HDPE), crystallinity, plasticizers, and cross-linking (vulcanized rubber, Teflon® discovery).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Polymer repeat unit: `n CH₂=CH₂ → (−CH₂−CH₂−)ₙ`. Each monomer adds via its double bond, forming saturates chains.
  • Polymer stages: Initiation (radical formation), propagation (radical adds monomers), termination (radical combination). Radical stability follows `3° > 2° > 1°`.

Short example(s)

Polyethylene forms as LDPE (branched, low crystallinity) or HDPE (linear, high crystallinity) depending on conditions; ethene polymerization via Ziegler–Natta catalysts gives HDPE used in bottles.
