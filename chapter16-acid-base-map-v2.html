16.1 Acid‚ÄìBase Equilibria
Arrhenius vs Br√∏nsted‚ÄìLowry, conjugate pairs, and amphiprotic species.
Roots: Proton donors, acceptors, and their partners.

Historically, Arrhenius acids were defined as substances that increase [H‚Å∫] in water, while Arrhenius bases increase [OH‚Åª]. This is useful but limited to aqueous solutions and to bases that contain OH‚Åª.

The more general Br√∏nsted‚ÄìLowry definition focuses on proton transfer:

  • Acid: proton (H‚Å∫) donor.
  • Base: proton (H‚Å∫) acceptor.

Every proton-transfer reaction involves both an acid and a base at once: one species donates H‚Å∫ while another accepts it.

In water:

  • HCl donates H‚Å∫ to H‚ÇÇO ‚Üí HCl is a Br√∏nsted acid; H‚ÇÇO is a Br√∏nsted base.
  • NH‚ÇÉ accepts H‚Å∫ from H‚ÇÇO ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ is a Br√∏nsted base; H‚ÇÇO is a Br√∏nsted acid.

Amphiprotic substances, such as H‚ÇÇO, HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, and H‚ÇÇPO‚ÇÑ‚Åª, can act as either acids or bases depending on the reaction partner.

For a generic reaction of an acid HA with water:

HA(aq) + H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚áå A‚Åª(aq) + H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫(aq)
  • HA / A‚Åª is one conjugate acid‚Äìbase pair.
  • H‚ÇÇO / H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫ is another conjugate acid‚Äìbase pair.
  • The conjugate base A‚Åª differs from HA by the absence of one proton.
  • The conjugate acid H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫ differs from H‚ÇÇO by the addition of one proton.

In any acid‚Äìbase equilibrium:

  • The stronger the acid, the weaker its conjugate base.
  • The stronger the base, the weaker its conjugate acid.
  • Equilibrium favors formation of the weaker acid and weaker base (proton transfer goes from stronger acid/stronger base to weaker acid/weaker base).

Example: HCl in water.

  • HCl(g) + H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚Üí H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫(aq) + Cl‚Åª(aq).
  • HCl is a very strong acid, so the equilibrium lies essentially completely to the right; Cl‚Åª is a very weak base.

Example: NH‚ÇÉ in water.

  • NH‚ÇÉ(aq) + H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚áå NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫(aq) + OH‚Åª(aq).
  • Only a small fraction of NH‚ÇÉ is converted to NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ is a weak base, and NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ is a weak acid.

Example: amphiprotic water.

  • With HCl, H‚ÇÇO acts as a base and becomes H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫.
  • With NH‚ÇÉ, H‚ÇÇO acts as an acid and becomes OH‚Åª.

Tip: To identify conjugate pairs, look for species that differ by exactly one H‚Å∫. The one with the extra H‚Å∫ is the conjugate acid; the one without it is the conjugate base.

16.2 The Autoionization of Water
Kw, neutral water, and the link between [H‚Å∫] and [OH‚Åª].
Trunk: Kw ties acids and bases together.

Water is amphiprotic and can react with itself in a process called autoionization:

H‚ÇÇO(l) + H‚ÇÇO(l) ‚áå H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫(aq) + OH‚Åª(aq)

We often simplify H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫(aq) to H‚Å∫(aq) for convenience.

Because water is the solvent and present in huge excess, its concentration is treated as constant and omitted from the equilibrium expression. This leads to the ion-product constant for water.

This relationship holds for pure water and for any dilute aqueous solution at 25 ¬∞C.

  • Neutral solution: [H‚Å∫] = [OH‚Åª] = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∑ M.
  • Acidic solution: [H‚Å∫] > [OH‚Åª].
  • Basic solution: [H‚Å∫] < [OH‚Åª].

Given any one of [H‚Å∫] or [OH‚Åª], you can calculate the other:

[H‚Å∫] = Kw / [OH‚Åª],   [OH‚Åª] = Kw / [H‚Å∫]

This relationship underlies the pH and pOH scales and is the basis for classifying solutions as acidic, neutral, or basic.

Example: pure water at 25 ¬∞C.

  • Neutral means [H‚Å∫] = [OH‚Åª] = x.
  • Using Kw: x¬≤ = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬π‚Å¥ ‚Üí x = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∑ M.

Example: basic solution with [OH‚Åª] = 0.010 M.

  • [H‚Å∫] = (1.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬π‚Å¥) / 0.010 = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬π¬≤ M.
  • [H‚Å∫] (10‚Åª¬π¬≤ M) is much smaller than neutral 10‚Åª‚Å∑ M ‚Üí strongly basic.

Tip: In strongly acidic or basic solutions, [H‚Å∫] or [OH‚Åª] from water autoionization is often negligible compared to the acid or base added. But autoionization always dictates the product [H‚Å∫][OH‚Åª].

16.3 The pH Scale
Logarithmic measure of acidity and basicity, and how we measure it.
Trunk: pH, pOH, and their relationship to [H‚Å∫] and [OH‚Åª].

The pH scale provides a convenient way to express [H‚Å∫] over many orders of magnitude:

pH pH = ‚àílog[H‚Å∫]

Similarly:

pOH = ‚àílog[OH‚Åª]

Because Kw = [H‚Å∫][OH‚Åª] = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬π‚Å¥ at 25 ¬∞C,

At 25 ¬∞C:

  • Neutral: pH = 7.00.
  • Acidic: pH < 7.00.
  • Basic: pH > 7.00.

A change of 1.0 in pH corresponds to a factor of 10 change in [H‚Å∫].

pH meter:

  • Uses a special electrode pair immersed in the solution.
  • Measures a voltage that depends on pH and displays pH directly.

Indicators:

  • Colored weak acids/bases that have different colors in their acid and base forms.
  • Each indicator has a characteristic pH transition range (~2 pH units).
  • Examples: methyl red, bromothymol blue, phenolphthalein.

Indicators can give approximate pH (for example, ‚Äúacidic,‚Äù ‚Äúnear neutral,‚Äù or ‚Äúbasic‚Äù), while pH meters provide precise numerical values.

Example: pH from [H‚Å∫].

  • If [H‚Å∫] = 1.0 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ M, then pH = 3.00 (acidic).

Example: [H‚Å∫] from pH.

  • [H‚Å∫] = 10‚Åª¬≥¬∑‚Å∑‚Å∂ ‚âà 1.7 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å¥ M.

Indicator-based reasoning: If a colorless solution turns pink with phenolphthalein (which turns pink above roughly pH 8‚Äì9), the solution is basic (pH > about 8).

16.4 Strong Acids and Bases
Complete ionization and straightforward pH calculations.
Roots: When the acid/base is the only source of H‚Å∫ or OH‚Åª.

Strong acids and strong bases are strong electrolytes: they are essentially 100% ionized in water.

Common strong acids:

  • HCl, HBr, HI (strong binary acids of the heavier halogens).
  • HNO‚ÇÉ, HClO‚ÇÉ, HClO‚ÇÑ (strong oxyacids of nitrogen and chlorine).
  • H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ (strong in its first ionization step).

Common strong bases:

  • Group 1 hydroxides: LiOH, NaOH, KOH, etc.
  • Heavy Group 2 hydroxides: Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ, Sr(OH)‚ÇÇ, Ba(OH)‚ÇÇ.

In a solution of a strong monoprotic acid, [H‚Å∫] ‚âà cacid. In a solution of a strong base MOH, [OH‚Åª] ‚âà cbase (or 2cbase for M(OH)‚ÇÇ).

For a strong monoprotic acid HA:

HA ‚Üí H‚Å∫ + A‚Åª,   [H‚Å∫] ‚âà cHA

[OH‚Åª] ‚âà cMOH  or  [OH‚Åª] ‚âà 2cM(OH)‚ÇÇ

Once you know [H‚Å∫] or [OH‚Åª], use the pH and pOH relations:

pH = ‚àílog[H‚Å∫],  pOH = ‚àílog[OH‚Åª],  pH + pOH = 14.00

  • HClO‚ÇÑ is a strong acid; [H‚Å∫] ‚âà 0.040 M.
  • pH = ‚àílog(0.040) ‚âà 1.40.

Example: 0.010 M NaOH.

  • [OH‚Åª] = 0.010 M ‚Üí pOH = 2.00.
  • pH = 14.00 ‚àí 2.00 = 12.00 (strongly basic).

Comparing strong acids of different concentrations:

  • 0.20 M HClO‚ÇÉ (pH ‚âà 0.70), 0.0030 M HNO‚ÇÉ (pH ‚âà 2.52), 1.50 M HCl (pH ‚âà ‚àí0.18).
  • Lower pH ‚Üí higher [H‚Å∫]. In this set, 1.50 M HCl is the most acidic.

Tip: For typical concentrations (‚â• 10‚Åª‚Åµ M), the [H‚Å∫] or [OH‚Åª] from water (~10‚Åª‚Å∑ M) is negligible. At very low acid/base concentrations, you may need to consider water‚Äôs contribution more carefully.

16.5 Weak Acids

Roots: Partial ionization and equilibrium constants.

Weak acids ionize only partially in water. For a generic weak acid HA:

HA(aq) ‚áå H‚Å∫(aq) + A‚Åª(aq)

The acid-dissociation constant Ka measures the tendency of HA to donate H‚Å∫:

Ka Ka = [H‚Å∫][A‚Åª] / [HA]

  • Small Ka ‚Üí weaker weak acid.

Percent ionization links Ka and concentration:

% ionization = ( [H‚Å∫]eq / [HA]initial ) √ó 100%

For a given acid, percent ionization decreases as initial concentration increases.

To calculate the pH of a weak acid solution:

  1. Write the ionization equilibrium (for example, CH‚ÇÉCOOH ‚áå H‚Å∫ + CH‚ÇÉCOO‚Åª).
  2. Set up an ICE table with initial concentrations, changes (in terms of x), and equilibrium values.
  3. Write the Ka expression and substitute equilibrium concentrations.
  4. Solve for x (often using the approximation that x ‚â™ initial concentration).
  5. Use x = [H‚Å∫] to determine pH and percent ionization.

For many weak acids, the approximation

Ka ‚âà x¬≤ / C

is valid, where C is the initial acid concentration. Always check that x is small compared with C (for example, < 5%) to justify the approximation.

Polyprotic acids (H‚ÇÇA, H‚ÇÉA, etc.) ionize in steps:

  • Each step has its own Ka: Ka1 > Ka2 > Ka3‚Ä¶
  • The first dissociation usually dominates the [H‚Å∫] at moderate concentrations.

  • CH‚ÇÉCOOH ‚áå H‚Å∫ + CH‚ÇÉCOO‚Åª.

Example: HF (Ka = 6.8 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å¥) at 0.10 M.

Tip: If solving for x gives x ‚â• ~5% of the initial acid concentration, redo the calculation using the full quadratic equation. Also, for typical weak acid concentrations, the additional [H‚Å∫] from water autoionization (~10‚Åª‚Å∑ M) is negligible compared to [H‚Å∫] from the acid.

16.6 Weak Bases

Weak bases partially accept protons from water:

The base-dissociation constant Kb is:

Kb Kb = [BH‚Å∫][OH‚Åª] / [B]

Main categories of weak bases:

    • NH‚ÇÉ, CH‚ÇÉNH‚ÇÇ, C‚ÇÖH‚ÇÖN (pyridine), HONH‚ÇÇ, etc.
  • Anions that are conjugate bases of weak acids:

  1. Write B + H‚ÇÇO ‚áå BH‚Å∫ + OH‚Åª and its Kb expression.
  2. Set up an ICE table with initial base concentration C and change x.
  3. Approximate [B] ‚âà C ‚àí x ‚âà C if x ‚â™ C.
  4. Use Kb ‚âà x¬≤/C to solve for x ‚âà [OH‚Åª].

Tip: In problems involving salts that contain weak bases (like NaClO or NaHCO‚ÇÉ), identify which ion is acting as the weak base, write its Kb equilibrium, and proceed as with NH‚ÇÉ.

16.7 Acid‚ÄìBase Properties of Salt Solutions
How ions from salts control the pH of a solution.
Roots: Cations, anions, and amphiprotic behavior.

Salts dissolve in water to give cations and anions. Each ion may be acidic, basic, or neutral. The net pH reflects the combination of their effects.

Cations:

  • Group 1 (Li‚Å∫, Na‚Å∫, K‚Å∫) and heavy Group 2 (Ca¬≤‚Å∫, Sr¬≤‚Å∫, Ba¬≤‚Å∫) cations are essentially neutral.
  • NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ and protonated amines (RNH‚ÇÉ‚Å∫) are acidic (conjugate acids of weak bases).
  • Small, highly charged metal cations (Al¬≥‚Å∫, Fe¬≥‚Å∫) are acidic because they strongly polarize surrounding water, leading to H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫ formation.

Anions:

  • Conjugate bases of strong acids (Cl‚Åª, Br‚Åª, NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, ClO‚ÇÑ‚Åª) are neutral.
  • Conjugate bases of weak acids (F‚Åª, HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, CH‚ÇÉCOO‚Åª, ClO‚Åª, CN‚Åª, CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª) are basic.
  • Amphiprotic anions, like HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, H‚ÇÇPO‚ÇÑ‚Åª, HPO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª, HSO‚ÇÑ‚Åª, can act as either acids or bases.

To decide if a salt solution is acidic, basic, or neutral, analyze each ion and see which behavior dominates.

For an amphiprotic anion like HPO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª, we must consider its behavior as both acid and base:

  • As acid: HPO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª ‚áå H‚Å∫ + PO‚ÇÑ¬≥‚Åª (Ka = Ka‚ÇÉ for H‚ÇÉPO‚ÇÑ).
  • As base: HPO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª + H‚ÇÇO ‚áå H‚ÇÇPO‚ÇÑ‚Åª + OH‚Åª (Kb = Kw/Ka‚ÇÇ).

Compare Ka (for losing H‚Å∫) with Kb (for gaining H‚Å∫ via forming OH‚Åª). The larger constant indicates whether the ion behaves predominantly as an acid or a base in water.

For Na‚ÇÇHPO‚ÇÑ:

  • Na‚Å∫ is neutral.
  • HPO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª has Kb ‚â´ Ka, so the solution is basic overall.

In general, Ka and Kb are related via:

Kb(A‚Åª) = Kw / Ka(HA)

This helps determine whether salts of amphiprotic ions are acidic or basic.

Example: NH‚ÇÑCl.

  • NH‚ÇÑCl ‚Üí NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ + Cl‚Åª.
  • NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ is acidic, Cl‚Åª is neutral ‚Üí solution is acidic.

Example: NaClO.

  • NaClO ‚Üí Na‚Å∫ + ClO‚Åª.
  • Na‚Å∫ is neutral, ClO‚Åª is basic ‚Üí solution is basic.

Example: KBr.

  • K‚Å∫ neutral; Br‚Åª neutral (conjugate base of strong acid HBr) ‚Üí solution is neutral.

Example: Na‚ÇÇHPO‚ÇÑ.

  • Na‚Å∫ neutral; HPO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª is amphiprotic.
  • Ka (acid reaction) is tiny, Kb (base reaction) is much bigger ‚Üí solution is basic.

Tip: For an unknown salt, first identify the acid and base that would form it by neutralization. If the original acid or base was strong, its conjugate partner in the salt is neutral; if it was weak, its conjugate partner will be acidic or basic.

16.8 Acid‚ÄìBase Behavior and Chemical Structure
Binary acids, oxyacids, carboxylic acids, and Lewis acids/bases.
Roots: How structure makes an acid strong or weak.

Acid strength depends on how easy it is to lose H‚Å∫ from HA. Three key factors:

  • H‚ÄìA bond polarity: H must be partially positive (Œ¥‚Å∫) to be donated as H‚Å∫.
  • H‚ÄìA bond strength: weaker bonds break more easily ‚Üí stronger acids.
  • Conjugate base stability: more stable A‚Åª ‚Üí stronger acid HA.

Binary acids (HX, H‚ÇÇY):

  • Down a group: bond length increases, bond strength decreases ‚Üí acidity increases (HF < HCl < HBr < HI).
  • Across a period: increasing electronegativity of X ‚Üí more polar H‚ÄìX, more stable X‚Åª ‚Üí acidity increases (CH‚ÇÑ ‚â™ NH‚ÇÉ < H‚ÇÇO < HF).

Oxyacids (Y‚ÄìO‚ÄìH):

  • Y is often a nonmetal or a strongly positive metal.
  • As electronegativity of Y increases, more electron density is pulled from the O‚ÄìH bond, making H‚Å∫ easier to lose and A‚Åª more stable ‚Üí stronger acid.
  • More oxygens attached to Y ‚Üí stronger acid (HClO < HClO‚ÇÇ < HClO‚ÇÉ < HClO‚ÇÑ).

Carboxylic acids (R‚ÄìCOOH) are common weak acids whose strength comes from:

  • Resonance stabilization of the conjugate base R‚ÄìCOO‚Åª (charge delocalized over two oxygen atoms).
  • Inductive effects: electron-withdrawing groups on R increase acidity by stabilizing the conjugate base.

Lewis acids and bases expand the acid‚Äìbase concept:

  • Lewis acid: electron pair acceptor.
  • Lewis base: electron pair donor.

This definition includes, but is broader than, the Br√∏nsted‚ÄìLowry definition. Many metal cations and electron-deficient molecules are Lewis acids; any species with a lone pair can be a Lewis base.

Binary acid trends:

  • Group 17: HF (weak acid), HCl, HBr, HI (all strong acids in water).
  • HF has a very strong H‚ÄìF bond (high bond energy), which makes it harder for H‚Å∫ to leave despite high bond polarity.

Oxyacid trends:

  • Hypohalous acids Y‚ÄìO‚ÄìH (Y = Cl, Br, I): Ka increases with electronegativity of Y ‚Üí HClO (strongest) > HBrO > HIO.
  • With a fixed central atom Y, more O atoms (more electron-withdrawing groups) lead to stronger acids (for example, HNO‚ÇÇ < HNO‚ÇÉ).

Carboxylic vs alcohol acids:

  • Acetic acid (CH‚ÇÉCOOH) is much more acidic than ethanol (CH‚ÇÉCH‚ÇÇOH).
  • Acetate (CH‚ÇÉCOO‚Åª) is resonance-stabilized; ethoxide (CH‚ÇÉCH‚ÇÇO‚Åª) is not.

Lewis acid‚Äìbase examples:

  • BF‚ÇÉ + NH‚ÇÉ ‚Üí F‚ÇÉB‚ÜêNH‚ÇÉ:
    • BF‚ÇÉ (electron-deficient boron) is a Lewis acid.
    • NH‚ÇÉ (lone pair on N) is a Lewis base.
  • Fe¬≥‚Å∫ + 6H‚ÇÇO ‚Üí [Fe(H‚ÇÇO)‚ÇÜ]¬≥‚Å∫: Fe¬≥‚Å∫ is a Lewis acid; H‚ÇÇO is a Lewis base.

Tip: For structure‚Äìacidity questions, look for:

  • How easy it is to break H‚ÄìA (bond length and strength).
  • How polar the bond is (electronegativity differences).
  • How well the resulting conjugate base can spread out or stabilize the negative charge (resonance, inductive effects).
