Chapter 10: Gases

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • State and apply the gas laws (Boyle’s, Charles’s, Avogadro’s, and combined gas law).
  • Use the ideal‑gas equation PV = nRT in stoichiometry and density problems.
  • Work with partial pressures and mole fractions (Dalton’s law).
  • Describe deviations from ideal behavior qualitatively.

Key Vocabulary

  • Pressure (P): force per unit area (SI unit: Pa; common lab unit: atm).
  • Boyle’s law: at constant T and n, P ∝ 1/V.
  • Charles’s law: at constant P and n, V ∝ T (T in K).
  • Avogadro’s law: at constant T and P, V ∝ n.
  • Ideal‑gas equation: PV = nRT.
  • Dalton’s law: total pressure is sum of partial pressures.
  • Mole fraction: χ_i = n_i / n_total.

1. Ideal‑Gas Relationships

  • Combined gas law: P_1V_1/T_1 = P_2V_2/T_2 (n constant).
  • Standard temperature and pressure (STP): 1 atm and 273.15 K; 1 mol ideal gas occupies ~22.4 L.

2. Gas Stoichiometry and Density

  • Use PV = nRT to find moles from P, V, T, then use stoichiometry for reactions involving gases.
  • Gas density: d = m/V = (MP)/(RT), where M is molar mass.

3. Partial Pressures

  • For a gas mixture: P_total = ΣP_i, and P_i = χ_i P_total.
  • Useful for collecting gases over water and for gas‑phase reactions with mixtures.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. A 2.00 L sample of gas at 1.00 atm and 298 K is compressed to 1.00 L at constant temperature. What is the new pressure?
  2. How many moles of gas are contained in a 5.00 L flask at 27.0 °C and 0.850 atm?
  3. What is the density of O2(g) at 1.00 atm and 25.0 °C?
  4. A mixture contains 0.50 mol N2 and 0.25 mol O2 in a 10.0 L container at 300 K. Calculate the partial pressure of each gas and the total pressure.
  5. Qualitatively, why do real gases deviate from ideal behavior at high pressure and low temperature?
