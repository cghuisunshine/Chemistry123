Chapter 10: Gases

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Define pressure and use common pressure units (atm, mmHg, kPa, torr).
  • State and apply Boyle’s, Charles’s, Avogadro’s, and the combined gas law.
  • Use the ideal‑gas equation PV = nRT to solve for any variable, including in stoichiometry and density problems.
  • Work with gas mixtures using Dalton’s law of partial pressures and mole fractions.
  • Describe deviations from ideal behavior qualitatively (high pressure, low temperature, strong attractions).

Key Vocabulary

  • Pressure (P): force per unit area; SI unit Pa, but atm and mmHg are widely used.
  • Boyle’s law: at constant T and n, P ∝ 1/V or P_1V_1 = P_2V_2.
  • Charles’s law: at constant P and n, V ∝ T (T in kelvin) or V_1/T_1 = V_2/T_2.
  • Avogadro’s law: at constant T and P, V ∝ n or V_1/n_1 = V_2/n_2.
  • Ideal‑gas equation: PV = nRT.
  • Dalton’s law: total pressure equals sum of partial pressures of components.
  • Mole fraction: χ_i = n_i / n_{total}; P_i = χ_i P_{total}.

1. Single‑Gas Laws and Units

  • Standard temperature and pressure (STP): 1 atm and 273.15 K; 1 mol ideal gas occupies ≈ 22.4 L at STP.
  • Always convert T to kelvin in gas calculations: K = °C + 273.15.

Worked Example 1 – Boyle’s Law

A 2.00 L gas sample at 1.00 atm is compressed at constant temperature to 0.500 L. What is the new pressure?

  1. Use P_1V_1 = P_2V_2.
  2. 1.00\,\text{atm} × 2.00\,\text{L} = P_2 × 0.500\,\text{L}.
  3. P_2 = (1.00 × 2.00)/0.500 = 4.00\,\text{atm}.

2. The Ideal‑Gas Equation

The combined gas law plus Avogadro’s law lead to:

PV = nRT

  • R is the gas constant; common value: 0.08206 L·atm·mol−1·K−1.
  • Use consistent units: P in atm, V in L, T in K, n in mol.

Worked Example 2 – Moles of Gas

How many moles of gas are contained in a 5.00 L flask at 27.0 °C and 0.850 atm?

  1. Convert T to K: 27.0 °C → 300.2 K (approx 300 K if using 3 sig figs).
  2. Use n = PV/RT.
  3. n = (0.850\,\text{atm})(5.00\,\text{L}) / [(0.08206)(300\,\text{K})] ≈ 0.173\,\text{mol} (check calculator and sig figs).

3. Gas Density and Molar Mass

  • Rearrange ideal‑gas equation to get density d = m/V in terms of molar mass M:

d = (MP)/(RT)

  • You can also solve for M if you know d, P, T: M = dRT/P.

Worked Example 3 – Density of O2

What is the density of O2(g) at 1.00 atm and 25.0 °C?

  1. T = 298 K; M(O2) ≈ 32.0 g·mol−1.
  2. d = MP/RT = (32.0\,\text{g mol}^{−1})(1.00\,\text{atm}) / [(0.08206)(298\,\text{K})] ≈ 1.31\,\text{g L}^{−1}.

4. Gas Mixtures and Partial Pressures

For a mixture of gases in a container:

  • P_{total} = P_1 + P_2 + ....
  • P_i = χ_i P_{total}, where χ_i is the mole fraction.

Worked Example 4 – Partial Pressures

A mixture contains 0.50 mol N2 and 0.25 mol O2 in a 10.0 L container at 300 K. Find the partial pressure of each gas and the total pressure.

  1. Total moles: ntotal = 0.50 + 0.25 = 0.75 mol.
  2. Mole fractions: χ(N2) = 0.50/0.75 = 0.667; χ(O2) = 0.25/0.75 = 0.333.
  3. Use ideal‑gas equation for total: P_{total} = nRT/V = (0.75)(0.08206)(300)/10.0 ≈ 1.84\,\text{atm}.
  4. Partial pressures: P(N_2) = 0.667 × 1.84 ≈ 1.23\,\text{atm}; P(O_2) = 0.333 × 1.84 ≈ 0.61\,\text{atm}.

5. Real Gases (Qualitative)

  • Ideal‑gas model assumes point particles with no volume and no intermolecular attractions.
  • At high pressure: gas particles occupy a significant fraction of the volume → measured P is higher than ideal predicts.
  • At low temperature: attractions cause particles to stick together more → measured P is lower than ideal predicts.
  • Real‑gas corrections (van der Waals equation) adjust for volume and attractions; you usually just need qualitative understanding in Chem 11.

6. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. A 3.00 L sample of gas at 1.20 atm and 20.0 °C is heated to 40.0 °C at constant pressure. What is the new volume?
  2. How many grams of N2 gas are in a 7.50 L tank at 35.0 °C and 2.00 atm?
  3. What is the density of CO2(g) at 0.750 atm and 20.0 °C?
  4. A mixture contains 0.10 mol He, 0.20 mol Ne, and 0.30 mol Ar in a 5.00 L container at 298 K. (a) What is the total pressure? (b) What is the partial pressure of each gas?
  5. Explain qualitatively why real gases deviate from ideal behavior at high pressures and low temperatures and why some gases show larger deviations than others.
