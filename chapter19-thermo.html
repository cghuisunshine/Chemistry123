Chapter 19: Chemical Thermodynamics

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Explain what makes a process spontaneous and how this relates to the second law of thermodynamics.
  • Define entropy and calculate ΔS for simple processes.
  • Use standard molar entropies to estimate ΔS° for reactions.
  • Define Gibbs free energy and use ΔG = ΔH - T ΔS to predict spontaneity.
  • Relate ΔG, ΔG°, Q, and K using ΔG° = -RT ln K.

Key Vocabulary

  • Spontaneous process: occurs without ongoing outside intervention (may be fast or slow).
  • Nonspontaneous process: requires continuous external influence to occur.
  • Second law of thermodynamics: for any spontaneous process, the total entropy of the universe increases (ΔSuniv > 0).
  • Entropy (S): measure of energy dispersal or randomness (J·K−1).
  • Microstate: a specific way of arranging the energy among particles.
  • Third law of thermodynamics: entropy of a perfect crystal at 0 K is zero.
  • Standard molar entropy (S°): entropy of 1 mol of a substance at 1 bar, usually 298 K.
  • Gibbs free energy (G): defined by G = H - TS.
  • ΔG: change in free energy; predicts spontaneity at constant T and P.
  • Reaction quotient (Q): ratio of product to reactant activities at any moment.
  • Equilibrium constant (K): value of Q at equilibrium.

1. Spontaneity and the Second Law

  • Spontaneity is about direction, not speed.
  • Examples of spontaneous processes: ice melting above 0 °C, gas expansion into a vacuum, iron rusting in moist air.

Second law (qualitative):

For a spontaneous process:

ΔS_{univ} = ΔS_{sys} + ΔS_{surr} > 0

At equilibrium: ΔS_{univ} = 0.

Nonspontaneous forward: ΔS_{univ} < 0 for the forward direction (reverse is spontaneous).

2. Entropy: Definition and Ideas

Statistical View

Entropy is related to the number of accessible microstates (W):

S = k ln W, where k is the Boltzmann constant.

More ways to arrange energy → higher W → higher S.

Trends in Entropy

  • Phase changes: S(s) < S(l) < S(g).
  • More particles (more moles of gas) usually means higher entropy.
  • Larger, more complex molecules tend to have higher S°.
  • Higher temperature → higher entropy (more accessible energy levels).

3. Entropy Changes for the System (ΔSsys)

Reversible Heat Transfer at Constant Temperature

For a reversible process at constant temperature:

ΔS = q_rev / T

Phase Changes at the Transition Temperature

For melting, vaporization, etc. at the transition temperature:

ΔS = ΔH_transition / T_transition

Standard Reaction Entropy

For a reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD

ΔS°rxn = [Σ n S°(products)] − [Σ n S°(reactants)]

(Multiply each S° by its stoichiometric coefficient, then subtract.)

Symbolic Example

For:

2 SO2(g) + O2(g) → 2 SO3(g)

ΔS°rxn = [2 S°(SO3)] − [2 S°(SO2) + S°(O2)]

4. Entropy Change of the Surroundings (ΔSsurr)

At constant pressure, the surroundings gain or lose heat equal to the negative of the system’s heat:

ΔS_surr = -q_sys / T ≈ -ΔH_sys / T

Then:

ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr

Use ΔSuniv to assess spontaneity.

5. Gibbs Free Energy and Spontaneity

Definition

Gibbs free energy is defined as:

G = H - TS

At constant temperature and pressure:

ΔG = ΔH - T ΔS

Spontaneity Criterion (Constant T, P)

  • ΔG < 0 → process is spontaneous.
  • ΔG > 0 → process is nonspontaneous (reverse is spontaneous).
  • ΔG = 0 → system is at equilibrium.

Signs and Interpretation

  • ΔH < 0, ΔS > 0 → ΔG < 0 at all T (always spontaneous).
  • ΔH > 0, ΔS < 0 → ΔG > 0 at all T (never spontaneous in forward direction).
  • ΔH < 0, ΔS < 0 → spontaneous at low T (enthalpy-driven).
  • ΔH > 0, ΔS > 0 → spontaneous at high T (entropy-driven).

6. Standard Free Energy Change (ΔG°) and Temperature

At standard conditions (1 bar, usually 298 K):

ΔG° = ΔH° - T ΔS°

Standard Reaction Free Energy

From standard free energies of formation:

ΔG°rxn = [Σ n ΔG°f(products)] − [Σ n ΔG°f(reactants)]

As with enthalpy, reversing a reaction changes the sign, and scaling the reaction scales ΔG°.

7. ΔG, Equilibrium, and K

Nonstandard Conditions

The reaction quotient Q measures the current composition:

ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q

where R = 8.314 J·mol−1·K−1 and T is in K.

At Equilibrium

At equilibrium, ΔG = 0 and Q = K:

0 = ΔG° + RT ln K

so:

ΔG° = -RT ln K

Interpretation of ΔG° and K

  • ΔG° < 0 → K > 1 → products favored at equilibrium.
  • ΔG° > 0 → K < 1 → reactants favored.
  • ΔG° ≈ 0 → K ≈ 1 → significant amounts of both.

8. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

Try these problems. You can check your work with a teacher or tutor, or ask for detailed solutions.

  1. Conceptual – Spontaneity and Entropy
    (a) Is every spontaneous process exothermic? Explain briefly.
    (b) For the evaporation of water at 25 °C and 1 atm, what is the sign of ΔSsys? Of ΔSsurr? Of ΔSuniv?
  2. ΔS° for a Reaction
    Given S° values (in J·mol−1·K−1), calculate ΔS° for:
    N2(g) + 3 H2(g) → 2 NH3(g)
    Use: S°(N2) = 192, S°(H2) = 131, S°(NH3) = 193.
  3. Using ΔH and ΔS to Find ΔG°
    A reaction has ΔH° = −125 kJ·mol−1 and ΔS° = −150 J·mol−1·K−1 at 298 K.
    (a) Calculate ΔG° at 298 K.
    (b) Is the reaction spontaneous at 298 K?
    (c) Predict whether raising the temperature makes the reaction more or less favorable.
  4. Temperature at Which ΔG Changes Sign
    A process has ΔH° = +85.0 kJ·mol−1 and ΔS° = +220 J·mol−1·K−1.
    (a) At what temperature (in K) is ΔG° = 0?
    (b) For T above this value, is the process spontaneous or nonspontaneous?
  5. ΔG° and K
    For a reaction at 298 K, ΔG° = −14.2 kJ·mol−1.
    (a) Compute K (use R = 8.314 J·mol−1·K−1).
    (b) Is K > 1 or < 1? What does this say about the equilibrium composition?
  6. ΔG Under Nonstandard Conditions
    Consider: A ↔ B, with ΔG° = +5.0 kJ·mol−1 at 298 K.
    (a) Write the expression relating ΔG, ΔG°, and Q.
    (b) If at some moment [B]/[A] = 0.010, calculate ΔG (in kJ·mol−1).
    (c) Based on your answer, does the system shift toward A or B?
  7. Entropy and Phase Change
    The molar enthalpy of fusion of ice at 0 °C is ΔHfus = 6.01 kJ·mol−1.
    (a) Calculate ΔS for melting 1.00 mol of ice at 0 °C.
    (b) Comment on why this ΔS has that sign.
