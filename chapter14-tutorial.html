Chapter 14 Tutorial — Chemical Kinetics

14.1 Factors That Affect Reaction Rates

Key ideas

  • Reaction rate measures how fast product forms or reactant disappears; it increases when molecules collide more often and with sufficient energy.
  • Physical state, concentration, temperature, and catalysts are the main levers chemists use to control rates.
  • More surface area or higher concentration increases collision frequency; higher temperature raises kinetic energy; catalysts provide lower-energy pathways.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Rate ∝ collisions: Raising temperature/concentration increases the number of effective collisions, so rates rise.
  • Catalyst effect: Catalysts lower the activation energy E_a, increasing the rate constant k without being consumed.

Short example(s)

Steel wool burns sluggishly in air (20% O₂) but vigorously in pure O₂ because higher oxygen concentration increases collision frequency.

14.2 Reaction Rates

Key ideas

  • Reaction rate is the change in molar concentration per unit time, e.g., rate = −Δ[A]/Δt or +Δ[B]/Δt.
  • Rates may be averaged over an interval or taken instantaneously via tangents on concentration vs time plots.
  • Stoichiometry lets you convert rates between species in a balanced equation.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Rate definition: rate = Δ[product]/Δt (Equation 14.1) or with reactants include a negative sign because their concentration decreases.

Short example(s)

If [A] drops from 1.00 M to 0.54 M in 20 s, the average rate is 0.023 M/s.

14.3 Concentration and Rate Laws

Key ideas

  • Rate laws connect the rate to reactant concentrations: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n, where exponents are determined experimentally.
  • The overall reaction order is the sum of the individual orders and reveals how sensitive rate is to concentration changes.
  • The rate constant k depends on temperature and activation energy.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Rate law: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n; if doubling [A] quadruples the rate, m = 2.

Short example(s)

For `rate = k[NO]^2[Cl₂]`, doubling [NO] quadruples rate while doubling [Cl₂] doubles it.

14.4 The Change of Concentration with Time

Key ideas

  • Integrated rate laws describe concentration profiles: zero-order decreases linearly, first-order exponentially, second-order hyperbolically.
  • Plotting appropriate functions (e.g., ln[A] vs t for first-order) tests the order and yields k.
  • Half-life `t_½ = 0.693/k` for first-order reactions is constant and independent of initial concentration.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • First-order: [A]_t = [A]_0 e^{−kt}. The slope of `ln[A]` vs `t` is `−k`.
  • Half-life: t_½ = 0.693/k for first-order, making it easy to compare rates from experimental half-lives.

Short example(s)

If `[A]_0 = 1.00 M` and `k = 0.0496 s⁻¹`, the concentration after 28 s is 0.25 M.

14.5 Temperature and Rate

Key ideas

  • Temperature affects the rate constant `k` via the Arrhenius equation; raising T accelerates reactions by populating higher-energy collisions.
  • The Arrhenius plot (`ln k` vs `1/T`) is linear, and the slope yields the activation energy `E_a`.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Arrhenius equation: k = A e^{−E_a/(RT)} (Equation 14.2). Higher `E_a` values make `k` more sensitive to temperature.
  • Two-temperature form: ln(k₂/k₁) = −E_a/R · (1/T₂ − 1/T₁), allowing calculation of activation energy from rate constants at two temperatures.

Short example(s)

If heating doubles the rate constant, solving the Arrhenius relationship gives `E_a ≈ 50 kJ/mol`.

14.6 Reaction Mechanisms

Key ideas

  • A mechanism is a sequence of elementary steps whose sum matches the overall stoichiometry.
  • The slowest step is rate-determining and establishes the observed rate law; intermediates appear in steps but not in the overall equation.
  • Pre-equilibrium and steady-state approaches allow elimination of intermediates when deriving rate laws.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Rate law from mechanism: Derived from the rate-determining step; e.g., if it’s `NO + NO₃ → products`, `rate ∝ [NO][NO₃]`.
  • Pre-equilibrium substitution: When `NO₂ + NO₂ ⇌ NO₃ + NO` is fast, use the equilibrium expression for `[NO₃]` in the slow step’s rate law.

Short example(s)

A mechanism with a fast equilibrium followed by a slow step yields a rate law involving the reactants only once the intermediate concentration is replaced.

14.7 Catalysis

Key ideas

  • Catalysts provide alternate low-activation-energy pathways and are unchanged after the reaction;
  • Homogeneous catalysts share phase with reactants, while heterogeneous catalysts offer surface sites, and enzymes are biological catalysts with highly specific active sites.
  • Inhibitors and poisons block catalysts by occupying active sites or altering their structure.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Arrhenius effect: Lower `E_a` increases `k` exponentially (`k = A e^{−E_a/(RT)}`).
  • Turnover number (qualitative): The number of catalytic cycles per unit time; large numbers indicate efficient catalysis.

Short example(s)

Platinum catalyzes hydrogen dissociation on fuel-cell surfaces, and enzyme sucrase dramatically lowers the activation energy for sucrose hydrolysis in the gut.
