Chapter 13 Tutorial — Properties of Solutions

13.1 The Solution Process

Key ideas

  • Solutions form when solute particles disperse uniformly in a solvent, driven by entropy yet moderated by intermolecular forces.
  • Three energetic steps determine the process: breaking solute–solute and solvent–solvent interactions (endothermic) and making solute–solvent attractions (exothermic).
  • Spontaneity requires the favorable solute–solvent enthalpy to counterbalance the separation costs, explaining why some salts dissolve only in polar solvents.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Enthalpy of solution: ΔH_soln = ΔH_solute + ΔH_solvent + ΔH_mix. If strong solute–solvent interactions (ΔH_mix negative) compensate for the energetic costs, the salt dissolves.
  • Ion–dipole solvation: Polar water aligns its partial charges toward ions (oxygen toward Na⁺, hydrogens toward Cl⁻), releasing hydration energy that overcomes lattice enthalpy.

Short example(s)

NaCl dissolves endothermically by breaking ionic lattice and water hydrogen bonds while releasing ion–dipole hydration energy (~+3.9 kJ/mol overall).

13.2 Saturated Solutions and Solubility

Key ideas

  • Saturated solutions contain the maximum dissolved solute at given conditions; any extra solute remains undissolved and a dynamic equilibrium prevails.
  • Solubility curves plot solute amount vs. temperature; solids typically become more soluble with heat, while gases become less soluble.
  • Gaseous solubility also depends on pressure via Henry’s law.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Solubility expression: Moles or grams of solute per 100 g solvent (or per liter) provide a numerical value `s` for the curve.
  • Henry’s law: P_gas = k_H · [gas]; increasing pressure raises the amount dissolved in liquids proportionally.

Short example(s)

Carbonated beverages remain fizzy because CO₂ solubility is maintained under high pressure; once opened, pressure drops and CO₂ effuses until equilibrium is reestablished.

13.3 Factors Affecting Solubility

Key ideas

  • “Like dissolves like”: polar solvents dissolve polar solutes via dipole interactions, while nonpolar solvents rely on dispersion forces.
  • Temperature raises the solubility of solids (endothermic dissolution) but lowers that of gases.
  • Pressure strongly affects gases (Henry’s law) but not solids or liquids.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • **Qualitative rule:** Stronger solute–solvent interactions vs. solute–solute/solvent–solvent interactions determine whether a solution forms.
  • **Le Chatelier:** For endothermic ΔH_soln > 0, raising temperature increases solubility; exothermic dissolutions show the opposite trend.

Short example(s)

Sugar dissolves faster in hot tea because heat helps disrupt sugar–sugar hydrogen bonds; oxygen becomes less soluble in warm water, which is why warm lakes hold less dissolved O₂.

13.4 Expressing Solution Concentration

Key ideas

  • Concentrations can be reported as mass percent, molarity, molality, mole fraction, or ppm/ppb depending on the application.
  • Molarity depends on solution volume and thus varies with temperature, while molality depends on solvent mass and stays constant with heating.
  • Mole fractions describe composition in mole-based terms and are useful for gas mixtures and Raoult’s law.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Molarity: M = n_solute / V_solution (mol/L).
  • Molality: m = n_solute / m_solvent (mol/kg), ideal for colligative properties.
  • Mole fraction: X_i = n_i / Σn_j, used in Raoult’s law and Dalton’s law.

Short example(s)

0.5 mol NaCl dissolved to give 0.25 L solution yields 2.0 M, whereas the same solute in 0.2 kg water gives 2.5 molal; heating changes only the molarity.

13.5 Colligative Properties

Key ideas

  • Colligative properties depend on solute particle count, not identity: vapor-pressure lowering, boiling-point elevation, freezing-point depression, and osmotic pressure.
  • Nonvolatile solutes reduce the solvent’s vapor pressure according to their mole fraction.
  • Osmotic pressure is the hydrostatic pressure needed to stop solvent flow through a semipermeable membrane.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Raoult’s law: P_solution = X_solvent · P°_solvent; each solute molecule reduces solvent vapor pressure proportionally.
  • Boiling point elevation: ΔT_b = K_b · m · i; Freezing point depression: ΔT_f = K_f · m · i, where `m` is molality and `i` is van ’t Hoff factor.
  • Osmotic pressure: Π = M · R · T for dilute solutions (M molarity), measuring the pressure needed to stop osmosis.

Short example(s)

Adding 1 mol NaCl (i ≈ 2) to water increases the boiling point by `ΔT_b = K_b · m · 2`, making salted water boil above 100 °C and lowering the freezing point for de-icing roads.

13.6 Colloids

Key ideas

  • Colloids contain particles 1 nm–1 μm; they scatter light (Tyndall effect) and do not settle readily.
  • Unlike true solutions, colloidal particles are large enough to experience Brownian motion but too small to precipitate.
  • Charged surfaces, surfactants, or steric stabilization prevent particles from aggregating and maintain dispersion.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Tyndall scattering: Intensity ∝ particle size², explaining why colloids appear cloudy while solutions stay clear.
  • Zeta potential: A surface charge measure; high zeta potential (positive or negative) keeps particles repelling each other and stable.

Short example(s)

Milk is a colloid of fat droplets in water; emulsifiers coat the fat droplets to keep them from coalescing, and the Tyndall effect makes milk opaque.
