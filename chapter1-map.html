Overview: What this chapter is about

This chapter introduces how chemists describe and measure the physical world: how we classify matter, how we distinguish physical and chemical change, and how we make and interpret measurements with the right units and significant figures.

  • Understand how matter is classified by state and composition.
  • Differentiate physical vs. chemical properties and changes.
  • Use SI units, metric prefixes, and temperature scales correctly.
  • Apply significant-figure rules and dimensional analysis, especially with density.

1. Classification of Matter

States of matter and how we sort elements, compounds, and mixtures.

matter elements & compounds homogeneous vs. heterogeneous classification rules

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. Every object you can weigh and that takes up room in a container counts as matter.

Matter can be described by its state:

  • Solid: particles are packed closely; solids have a fixed shape and volume and are not easily compressed.
  • Liquid: particles are close but able to move past each other; liquids have a fixed volume but take the shape of their container.
  • Gas: particles are far apart and move freely; gases have neither fixed shape nor fixed volume and are highly compressible.

We also classify matter by composition:

  • Pure substances have a constant composition.
    • Element: a substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means (e.g., O‚ÇÇ, Cu).
    • Compound: a substance made from two or more elements in a fixed ratio (e.g., H‚ÇÇO, NaCl).
  • Mixtures have variable composition.
    • Homogeneous mixture (solution): uniform throughout, with no visible boundaries (e.g., saltwater, air).
    • Heterogeneous mixture: non‚Äëuniform, with visible pieces or phases (e.g., oil and water, salad).

The ‚Äúrules‚Äù for classifying matter act like a decision tree:

  • Pure substance vs. mixture:
    • If the composition is the same everywhere and the components cannot be separated by physical methods like filtration, you are likely dealing with a pure substance.
    • If you can separate components physically or samples vary in composition, you have a mixture.
  • Element vs. compound:
    • A substance made of just one type of atom is an element.
    • A substance that can be decomposed into simpler substances by chemical means and contains two or more elements in a fixed ratio is a compound.
  • Homogeneous vs. heterogeneous mixture:
    • If a mixture looks uniform and the same throughout, it is homogeneous.
    • If you can see different parts or phases, it is heterogeneous.

Students should interpret these as practical classification patterns: observe the sample, think about uniformity, and consider whether physical separation is possible.

Example ‚Äì Classifying common substances

  1. Sugar (sucrose) has a fixed composition of C, H, and O; it can only be broken down chemically. ‚Üí Compound.
  2. Brass is a uniform solid solution of copper and zinc; it looks the same throughout. ‚Üí Homogeneous mixture.
  3. Orange juice with pulp has visible pulp pieces suspended in liquid. ‚Üí Heterogeneous mixture.
  4. Oxygen gas, O‚ÇÇ, consists of one type of atom in diatomic molecules. ‚Üí Element.

Tip: when in doubt, imagine zooming in under a microscope. If every tiny region looks identical, think ‚Äúpure substance‚Äù or ‚Äúhomogeneous mixture‚Äù; if not, it is likely heterogeneous.

2. Properties, Changes, and Energy

Physical vs. chemical change and how energy fits in.

physical vs. chemical potential & kinetic energy system & surroundings

Physical properties are characteristics that can be observed without changing a substance‚Äôs composition (e.g., color, density, melting point, boiling point).

Chemical properties describe how a substance behaves in chemical reactions and forms new substances (e.g., flammability, ability to rust, reactivity with acids).

Physical changes leave the substance itself unchanged‚Äîonly its form or appearance changes (melting ice, boiling water, dissolving sugar in water).

Chemical changes produce new substances with different compositions and properties (burning, rusting, digestion, gas formation in reactions).

Energy can appear as:

  • Potential energy: stored due to position or composition (e.g., energy stored in chemical bonds).
  • Kinetic energy: energy of motion (e.g., moving particles in a gas).

Chemical processes almost always involve energy transfer between the reacting system and its surroundings, often in the form of heat being absorbed or released.

Although this section has no new numeric formulas, it emphasizes recurring patterns:

  • Physical change pattern: same particles, new arrangement. Ice melting, water freezing, and water boiling all involve H‚ÇÇO molecules, just in different arrangements and distances.
  • Chemical change pattern: particles themselves change identity. For example, when iron rusts, Fe atoms combine with O atoms to form new compounds (iron oxides).
  • Energy in change: chemical changes usually involve changes in potential energy stored in bonds. When these changes happen, energy is transferred as heat or work to or from the surroundings.

Students should always ask: ‚ÄúAre the particles themselves changing into new substances, or are they just moving or rearranging?‚Äù This question is central to deciding whether a change is physical or chemical.

Examples ‚Äì Physical vs. chemical change

  • Melting ice: solid water turns into liquid water; the molecules are still H‚ÇÇO. ‚Üí Physical change.
  • Boiling water: liquid water becomes water vapor; again, still H‚ÇÇO. ‚Üí Physical change.
  • Burning paper: cellulose reacts with oxygen to form CO‚ÇÇ, H‚ÇÇO, and ash‚Äînew substances. ‚Üí Chemical change.
  • Rusting iron: iron reacts with oxygen and water to form iron oxides (rust). ‚Üí Chemical change.

Tip: look for clues of chemical change such as unexpected color changes, gas formation (bubbles not caused by boiling), formation of a solid (precipitate), or large heat effects.

3. Measurement, SI Units, and Prefixes

Chemistry uses the SI (International System of Units). Key base units include:

4. Significant Figures and Scientific Notation

How measurement precision controls the digits you are allowed to keep.

sig fig rules addition & multiplication scientific notation

Significant figures (sig figs) are the digits in a measurement that are known with certainty plus one final estimated digit.

Accuracy describes how close a measurement is to the true value, while precision describes how close repeated measurements are to each other.

Counting significant figures follows standard rules:

  • All nonzero digits are significant.
  • Zeros between nonzero digits are significant (e.g., 101 has three sig figs).
  • Leading zeros (before the first nonzero digit) are not significant (e.g., 0.0032 has two sig figs).
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant (e.g., 2.300 has four sig figs).

We use significant figures to make sure our reported results reflect the precision of the measurements we started with.

For addition and subtraction:

The result must have the same number of decimal places as the measurement with the fewest decimal places.

For multiplication and division:

The result must have the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest significant figures.

Scientific notation writes numbers as

a √ó 10‚Åø

where 1 ‚â§ |a| < 10 and n is an integer. The coefficient a carries the significant figures, and the power of ten shifts the decimal point.

5. Dimensional Analysis and Density

Using units as a roadmap, and linking mass to volume with density.

Dimensional analysis (factor‚Äëlabel method) is a systematic way to solve problems by treating units like algebraic quantities that can cancel.

The general strategy is:

  1. Write down the starting quantity with its units.
  2. Multiply by one or more conversion factors so that units cancel step by step.
  3. Stop when you reach the desired units.
  4. Apply significant‚Äëfigure rules and check whether the answer is reasonable.
