Chapter 12 Tutorial — Solids and Modern Materials

12.1 Classification of Solids

Key ideas

  • Solids are classified by predominant bonding: metallic, ionic, covalent-network, molecular, plus polymers and nanomaterials.
  • Crystalline solids have repeating unit cells and lattice scaffolds; amorphous solids lack long-range order.
  • Seven primitive 3D lattices (triclinic, monoclinic, orthorhombic, tetragonal, rhombohedral, hexagonal, cubic) plus centered variants describe how unit cells tile space.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Density formula: ρ = (n·M)/(N_A·V_cell) links density to the number of formula units per cell, molar mass, Avogadro’s number, and cell volume.
  • Bragg’s law: nλ = 2d sin θ (qualitative). X-ray diffraction gives lattice spacings d, so constructive interference identifies crystal structure.

Short example(s)

NaCl fills an FCC lattice; plugging its `n=4`, `M=58.44 g/mol`, and lattice parameter into the density formula yields the experimental density ≈ 2.16 g/cm³.

12.2 Metallic Solids

Key ideas

  • Metallic bonding consists of atoms embedded in a sea of delocalized valence electrons, producing conductivity and ductility.
  • Close-packed structures (FCC, HCP) maximize atom packing efficiency (~74%) and are common in metals; BCC is also prevalent (e.g., iron).
  • Band theory explains easy electron flow: partially filled bands allow electrons to drift under an electric field.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Packing efficiency: FCC and HCP pack spheres at ~74%, so 3/4 of the volume is occupied by atoms, giving high density.
  • Conductivity relation: σ ∝ n_e·μ. Higher density of delocalized electrons and mobility leads to better conductivity.

Short example(s)

Copper adopts an FCC lattice with atoms at corners and faces, enabling electrons to move freely and making copper a top electrical conductor.

12.3 Ionic Solids

Key ideas

  • Ionic solids consist of alternating cations and anions held by strong electrostatic attraction, yielding hardness, brittleness, and high melting points.
  • Coordination numbers depend on ionic size ratios; common structures include NaCl (6:6) and CsCl (8:8).
  • Lattice energy quantifies the electrostatic stabilization and explains thermal stability.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Lattice energy (Coulombic): U ∝ (|q⁺q⁻|)/(r⁺ + r⁻); higher charges and smaller ions lead to larger negative lattice energies (stronger binding).
  • Density via unit cell: Same formula as Section 12.1; e.g., NaCl’s FCC cell has four formula units, giving a density consistent with experimental data.

Short example(s)

NaCl’s 3D structure places Na⁺ and Cl⁻ in an FCC lattice; the strong ionic lattice energy (~-787 kJ/mol) explains its 801 °C melting point and brittleness.

12.4 Covalent Solids

Key ideas

  • Covalent-network solids are built from atoms connected by directional covalent bonds throughout, making them extremely hard (e.g., diamond, quartz).
  • Bonding rigidity leads to high melting points and low electrical conductivity unless doped (as in semiconductors like silicon).
  • The motif may not sit on lattice points; diamond’s basis has two tetrahedrally bonded carbons per primitive cubic cell.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Bond energy: Breaking covalent bonds (~400 kJ/mol) costs far more energy than intermolecular attractions, yielding high heats of fusion/vaporization.
  • Coordination number: Each carbon in diamond bonds to four neighbors (tetrahedral), giving a rigid network responsible for its hardness and melting point near 3500 °C.

Short example(s)

Diamond’s cubic lattice has each carbon covalently bonded in a tetrahedral motif, producing a transparent, superhard material used in cutting tools.

12.5 Polymers

Key ideas

  • Polymers are macromolecules composed of covalently bonded repeating units; intermolecular forces between chains govern bulk properties.
  • Chain entanglement and cross-linking raise viscosity, tensile strength, and melting temperature.
  • Thermoplastics soften with heat (chains slide past each other), while thermosets (cross-linked) cannot be remelted.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Degree of polymerization: n = M_polymer / M_monomer; larger `n` means longer chains, higher molecular weight, and stronger mechanical properties.
  • Glass transition: Temperature `T_g` marks where amorphous regions gain mobility; below `T_g` polymers are glassy and brittle, above `T_g` they become rubbery.

Short example(s)

Polyethylene (−CH₂−CH₂−)ₙ with high `n` is used for durable containers; lowering `n` yields waxy, low-melt materials.

12.6 Nanomaterials

Key ideas

  • Nanomaterials have at least one dimension in the 1–100 nm range, so a large fraction of atoms lie at surfaces, altering melting points, reactivity, and optical/electronic properties.
  • Quantum confinement raises bandgap energies as size decreases, enabling tunable optical properties (e.g., quantum dots).
  • Surface-area-to-volume ratio increases inversely with size, enhancing catalysis and interactions with environments.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Surface-area-to-volume: For spheres, SA/V ∝ 1/r; halving radius doubles surface atoms, improving catalytic activity.
  • Quantum confinement: In a particle-in-a-box model E ∝ h²/(2m r²), so smaller radius `r` increases energy gaps, shifting absorption/emission to higher energies (bluer light).

Short example(s)

TiO₂ nanoparticles remain transparent while surfacing more UV-active due to the high surface area, explaining their use in sunscreens and photocatalysis.
