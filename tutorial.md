# Chemistry 123 Weekly Tutorial Guide

This tutorial guide maps the 12-week course schedule to detailed content from *Chemistry: The Central Science*. Each week includes links to relevant chapters, key concepts, and learning objectives.

---

## Week 1 - Admin, Acid-Base Review

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 16: Acid–Base Equilibria](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter16-tutorial.html)
- [Chapter 4: Reactions in Aqueous Solution](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter4-reaq.html)

### Key Topics

#### 16.1 Acid–Base Equilibria
- **Brønsted-Lowry Theory**: Acids donate H⁺, bases accept H⁺
- **Amphoterism**: Water acts as both acid and base
- **Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs**: Every acid-base reaction involves equilibrium between protonated and deprotonated forms
- **Ka and Kb**: Acid/base dissociation constants quantify strength

#### 16.2 Autoionization of Water
- **Kw = [H₃O⁺][OH⁻] ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴** at 25 °C
- Pure water: [H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M

#### 16.3 The pH Scale
- **pH = -log[H₃O⁺]**
- **pH + pOH = 14** at 25 °C
- Each pH unit represents a tenfold difference in [H₃O⁺]

#### 16.4-16.6 Strong and Weak Acids/Bases
- Strong acids/bases dissociate completely (K very large)
- Weak acids/bases require ICE tables: Ka = [H₃O⁺][A⁻]/[HA]
- Approximation: [H₃O⁺] ≈ √(Ka·[HA]₀) when Ka is small

## Week 2 - Intro to Thermodynamics, State Variables, Systems, Surroundings, Ideal Gases, First Law, Internal Energy, Paths and Processes, Pistons

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 19: Chemical Thermodynamics](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter19-tutorial.html)
- [Chapter 5: Thermochemistry](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter5-thermo.html)
- [Chapter 10: Gases](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter10-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### 19.1 Spontaneous Processes
- Spontaneous changes proceed without outside intervention
- Spontaneity depends on temperature and pressure
- Reversible vs. irreversible processes

#### 10.3-10.4 Ideal Gas Laws
- **Boyle's Law**: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant n, T)
- **Charles's Law**: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant n, P)
- **Avogadro's Law**: V ∝ n (constant P, T)
- **Ideal Gas Equation**: PV = nRT
- **Combined Gas Law**: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

#### 10.6 Kinetic-Molecular Theory
- Pressure stems from molecular collisions
- Average kinetic energy: KE_avg = ½ m u_rms²
- u_rms ∝ √T

#### First Law of Thermodynamics (Chapter 5)
- **ΔE = q + w**: Internal energy change equals heat plus work
- **State functions**: E, H, S, G depend only on current state
- **Path functions**: q and w depend on the process path

## Week 3 - Measuring Heat and Work, Heat Capacities, Calorimetry, Intro to Enthalpy, Standard States, Hess's Law, Entropy, Second and Third Laws of Thermo

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 5: Thermochemistry](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter5-thermo.html)
- [Chapter 19: Chemical Thermodynamics](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter19-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### Heat Capacity and Calorimetry (Chapter 5)
- **Heat capacity**: q = C·ΔT
- **Specific heat**: q = m·c·ΔT
- **Calorimetry**: Measuring heat flow in reactions
- **Constant-pressure calorimetry**: qp = ΔH
- **Bomb calorimetry**: qv = ΔE

#### Enthalpy (Chapter 5)
- **Enthalpy**: H = E + PV
- **ΔH = qp** at constant pressure
- **Standard states**: 1 atm, 25 °C, pure substances
- **Hess's Law**: ΔH_rxn = Σ ΔH_products − Σ ΔH_reactants
- **Formation reactions**: ΔH°f for standard enthalpy of formation

#### 19.2 Entropy and Second Law
- **Second Law**: ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr ≥ 0 for spontaneous processes
- **Entropy**: State function measuring energy/matter dispersal
- **ΔS = q_rev/T** for reversible processes
- **ΔS_surr = -ΔH_sys/T**

#### 19.3 Third Law and Molecular Interpretation
- **Boltzmann's equation**: S = k ln W
- **Third Law**: Perfect crystals at 0 K have S = 0
- Heating increases accessible microstates, raising entropy

#### 19.4 Entropy Changes in Reactions
- **ΔS°_rxn = Σ n_p S°_p − Σ n_r S°_r**
- Entropy increases with gas formation, particle increase, or disorder

## Week 4 - Spontaneity, Gibbs Free Energy, Equilibrium, van't Hoff plots, Intro to Kinetics, Rate Laws, Rate Constants, Reaction Orders, Method of Initial Rates

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 19: Chemical Thermodynamics](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter19-tutorial.html)
- [Chapter 15: Chemical Equilibrium](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter15-tutorial.html)
- [Chapter 14: Chemical Kinetics](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter14-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### 19.5 Gibbs Free Energy
- **ΔG = ΔH − TΔS**: Determines spontaneity at constant T and P
- **ΔG < 0**: Spontaneous
- **ΔG = 0**: Equilibrium
- **ΔG > 0**: Nonspontaneous

#### 19.6 Free Energy and Temperature
- **ΔG vs T plots**: Linear with slope −ΔS
- Transition temperature: T = ΔH/ΔS when ΔG = 0

#### 19.7 Free Energy and Equilibrium
- **ΔG° = −RT ln K**: Links standard free energy to equilibrium constant
- **K > 1**: Products favored (ΔG° < 0)
- **K < 1**: Reactants favored (ΔG° > 0)
- **ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q**: Nonstandard conditions
- **van't Hoff equation**: ln K = −ΔH°/RT + ΔS°/R

#### 15.1-15.2 Chemical Equilibrium
- **Dynamic equilibrium**: Forward and reverse rates equal
- **Equilibrium constant**: Kc = [products]^coeff/[reactants]^coeff
- **Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn** for gas-phase reactions

#### 14.1-14.3 Introduction to Kinetics
- **Reaction rate**: Change in concentration per unit time
- **Rate = −Δ[A]/Δt** for reactants, **+Δ[B]/Δt** for products
- **Rate law**: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
- Orders (m, n) determined experimentally, not from coefficients
- **Method of initial rates**: Compare experiments to find orders

## Week 5 - Integrated Rate Laws and Graphical Analysis, Half-life, Overall Reactions, Mechanisms, Elementary Reactions, Reaction Coordinate Diagrams, Intermediates and Catalysts, Transition States

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 14: Chemical Kinetics](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter14-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### 14.4 Integrated Rate Laws
- **Zero-order**: [A]_t = [A]₀ − kt
- **First-order**: [A]_t = [A]₀ e^(−kt) or ln[A]_t = ln[A]₀ − kt
- **Second-order**: 1/[A]_t = 1/[A]₀ + kt

#### Half-life
- **First-order**: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k (independent of [A]₀)
- **Second-order**: t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀) (depends on [A]₀)

#### Graphical Analysis
- Plot ln[A] vs t for first-order (slope = −k)
- Plot 1/[A] vs t for second-order (slope = k)
- Plot [A] vs t for zero-order (slope = −k)

#### 14.6 Reaction Mechanisms
- **Mechanism**: Sequence of elementary steps
- **Elementary reactions**: Unimolecular (A → products), bimolecular (A + B → products)
- **Intermediates**: Appear in mechanism but not overall equation
- **Rate-determining step**: Slowest step controls observed rate law
- **Reaction coordinate diagrams**: Energy vs. reaction progress
- **Transition states**: High-energy configurations at peaks
- **Activation energy (Ea)**: Energy barrier to reaction

#### 14.7 Catalysis
- **Catalysts**: Lower Ea without being consumed
- Appear in mechanism but cancel out overall
- **Homogeneous catalysts**: Same phase as reactants
- **Heterogeneous catalysts**: Different phase (usually solid surfaces)
- **Enzymes**: Biological catalysts with high specificity

## Week 6 - Multi-Step Reaction Mechanisms, Rate Determining Step, Rate Laws from Mechanisms, Steady-State Approximation, Michaelis-Menten Enzyme Kinetics, Temperature-dependence of k (Arrhenius Equation)

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 14: Chemical Kinetics](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter14-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### 14.6 Advanced Mechanisms
- **Rate law from mechanism**: Derived from rate-determining step
- **Pre-equilibrium**: Fast equilibrium before slow step
- **Steady-state approximation**: d[intermediate]/dt ≈ 0
- Substitute [intermediate] from equilibrium or steady-state into rate law

#### Michaelis-Menten Kinetics
- **Enzyme-substrate complex**: E + S ⇌ ES → E + P
- **Michaelis-Menten equation**: v = V_max[S]/(K_M + [S])
- **K_M**: Michaelis constant (substrate concentration at half V_max)
- **V_max**: Maximum reaction rate when enzyme is saturated

#### 14.5 Temperature and Rate (Arrhenius Equation)
- **k = A e^(−Ea/(RT))**: Rate constant depends exponentially on temperature
- **A**: Pre-exponential factor (frequency factor)
- **Ea**: Activation energy
- **Two-temperature form**: ln(k₂/k₁) = −Ea/R · (1/T₂ − 1/T₁)
- **Arrhenius plot**: ln k vs 1/T gives straight line with slope −Ea/R
- Higher Ea makes rate more sensitive to temperature

## Week 7 - Intro to Organic, Representing Organic Molecules, Nomenclature/Functional Groups, Electronic Structure

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 24: The Chemistry of Organic Compounds](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter24-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### 24.1 General Characteristics
- **Carbon**: Four valence electrons, forms four covalent bonds
- **Hybridization**: sp³ (tetrahedral), sp² (trigonal planar), sp (linear)
- **C–C and C–H bonds**: Strong, nonpolar, relatively inert
- **Functional groups**: Reactive centers (C–O, O–H, halides, amines, etc.)

#### 24.2 Introduction to Hydrocarbons
- **Alkanes**: CnH2n+2 (saturated, only C–C single bonds)
- **Alkenes**: Contain C=C double bonds
- **Alkynes**: Contain C≡C triple bonds
- **Aromatic**: Benzene rings with delocalized π electrons
- **Homologous series**: Smooth trends in physical properties with chain length

#### 24.3 Structures of Alkanes
- **sp³ hybridization**: Tetrahedral geometry (109.5° bond angles)
- **Sigma bonds**: Head-on orbital overlap
- **Constitutional isomers**: Same formula, different connectivity
- **Conformations**: Rotation about C–C single bonds
- **Staggered vs. eclipsed**: Staggered lower in energy (less torsional strain)

#### 24.4 Alkane Nomenclature
- Identify longest carbon chain (parent)
- Number to give substituents lowest locants
- Name substituents (methyl, ethyl, propyl, etc.)
- Alphabetize substituents
- Use prefixes: di-, tri-, tetra- for multiples

#### 24.5 Cycloalkanes
- **Formula**: CnH2n (two fewer H than alkanes)
- **Angle strain**: Small rings (3-4 carbons)
- **Cyclohexane chair**: Most stable conformation
- **Axial vs. equatorial**: Equatorial positions minimize steric strain

#### 24.6 Functional Groups
- **R–OH**: Alcohols
- **R–O–R**: Ethers
- **R–NH₂**: Amines
- **R–X**: Halides (X = F, Cl, Br, I)
- **R–COOH**: Carboxylic acids
- **R–CHO**: Aldehydes
- **R–CO–R**: Ketones

## Week 8 - Bonding and Resonance, Curved Arrow Notation, Organic view of Acid-Base Chemistry

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 8: Basic Concepts of Chemical Bonding](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter8-bonding.html)
- [Chapter 24: The Chemistry of Organic Compounds](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter24-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### Bonding in Organic Molecules (Chapter 8)
- **Lewis structures**: Show bonding and lone pairs
- **Formal charge**: FC = V − N − B/2 (V = valence, N = nonbonding, B = bonding electrons)
- **Octet rule**: Atoms tend toward 8 valence electrons
- **Resonance**: Multiple valid Lewis structures
- **Resonance hybrid**: Actual structure is blend of resonance forms
- **Resonance stabilization**: Delocalized electrons lower energy

#### Curved Arrow Notation
- **Electron movement**: Arrows show electron pair flow
- **Arrow from electron source to destination**
- **Double-headed arrows**: Resonance structures
- **Single-headed arrows**: Radical mechanisms
- **Nucleophile**: Electron-rich species (donates electrons)
- **Electrophile**: Electron-poor species (accepts electrons)

#### Organic Acid-Base Chemistry
- **Brønsted-Lowry in organic context**: Proton transfer reactions
- **pKa values**: Lower pKa = stronger acid
- **Structural effects on acidity**:
  - **Electronegativity**: More electronegative atoms stabilize conjugate base
  - **Resonance**: Delocalization stabilizes conjugate base
  - **Inductive effects**: Electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity
  - **Hybridization**: sp > sp² > sp³ (more s-character = more acidic)
- **Carboxylic acids**: pKa ≈ 4-5 (resonance-stabilized conjugate base)
- **Alcohols**: pKa ≈ 15-16
- **Amines as bases**: Lone pair accepts H⁺

## Week 9 - Conformational Analysis, Cyclic Alkanes (cyclohexane)

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 24: The Chemistry of Organic Compounds](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter24-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### 24.3 Conformational Analysis
- **Conformers**: Different spatial arrangements from bond rotation
- **Newman projections**: View along C–C bond
- **Ethane conformations**:
  - **Staggered**: Lower energy (0° dihedral angle)
  - **Eclipsed**: Higher energy (60° dihedral angle)
  - **Energy difference**: ~12 kJ/mol
- **Butane conformations**:
  - **Anti**: Most stable (180° dihedral)
  - **Gauche**: Less stable (60° dihedral, steric interaction)
  - **Eclipsed**: Highest energy

#### 24.5 Cyclohexane Conformations
- **Chair conformation**: Most stable
  - **Axial positions**: Perpendicular to ring plane (6 positions)
  - **Equatorial positions**: Around ring equator (6 positions)
  - **Ring flip**: Converts axial ↔ equatorial
- **Boat conformation**: Higher energy (~30 kJ/mol above chair)
  - **Flagpole interactions**: H atoms at bow/stern
  - **Eclipsing strain**: Along sides
- **Twist-boat**: Slightly more stable than boat

#### Substituted Cyclohexanes
- **Monosubstituted**: Substituent prefers equatorial position
- **1,3-diaxial interactions**: Steric clash when substituent is axial
- **Energy difference**: Larger groups favor equatorial more strongly
  - **Methyl**: ~7.6 kJ/mol preference for equatorial
  - **tert-butyl**: ~23 kJ/mol preference (locks ring in one chair)
- **Disubstituted cyclohexanes**:
  - **Cis**: Both substituents on same face
  - **Trans**: Substituents on opposite faces
  - **Cis-1,2**: One axial, one equatorial
  - **Trans-1,2**: Both axial or both equatorial

## Week 10 - Alkenes (E and Z), CIP Rules, Stereochemistry, Chirality, (R and S), Relationships between Compounds (Enantiomers, Diastereomers, etc.)

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 25: Stereochemistry of Organic Compounds](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter25-tutorial.html)
- [Chapter 26: Chemistry of Alkenes and Alkynes](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter26-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### Alkene Stereochemistry (E/Z)
- **Geometric isomers**: Restricted rotation around C=C
- **Cis/trans**: Simple cases with two identical groups
- **E/Z nomenclature**: Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) priority rules
  - **Z (zusammen)**: Higher priority groups on same side
  - **E (entgegen)**: Higher priority groups on opposite sides

#### Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) Priority Rules
1. **Atomic number**: Higher atomic number = higher priority
2. **Isotopes**: Higher mass number = higher priority
3. **Multiple bonds**: Count as separate atoms
4. **Work outward**: Compare at first point of difference

#### 25.3 Chirality
- **Chiral center (stereocenter)**: Carbon with four different substituents
- **Chiral molecules**: Not superimposable on mirror image
- **Achiral molecules**: Superimposable on mirror image
- **Plane of symmetry**: Makes molecule achiral

#### 25.5 R/S Configuration
- **R/S assignment**:
  1. Assign CIP priorities (1-4) to substituents
  2. Orient molecule with lowest priority (4) pointing away
  3. Trace path 1→2→3
  4. **Clockwise = R**, **Counterclockwise = S**
- **Enantiomers**: Opposite R/S at all stereocenters

#### Relationships Between Compounds
- **Constitutional isomers**: Same formula, different connectivity
- **Stereoisomers**: Same connectivity, different 3D arrangement
  - **Enantiomers**: Nonsuperimposable mirror images
    - Opposite configuration at all stereocenters
    - Identical physical properties (except optical rotation)
    - Different behavior in chiral environments
  - **Diastereomers**: Stereoisomers that are NOT mirror images
    - Different configuration at some (not all) stereocenters
    - Different physical properties
    - Can be separated by standard techniques

## Week 11 - Relationships between Compounds (cont.), Meso Compounds, Properties of Chiral Compounds, Optical Rotation, Fischer Projections

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 25: Stereochemistry of Organic Compounds](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter25-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### 25.2 Cis-Trans Isomerism in Cycloalkanes
- **Cycloalkane stereochemistry**: Restricted rotation creates cis/trans isomers
- **Cis**: Substituents on same face of ring
- **Trans**: Substituents on opposite faces
- **Relationship**: Cis and trans are diastereomers

#### 25.3 Meso Compounds
- **Definition**: Molecule with stereocenters but achiral due to internal symmetry
- **Plane of symmetry**: Divides molecule into mirror-image halves
- **Example**: meso-tartaric acid (R,S configuration with internal mirror plane)
- **Stereoisomer count**: Reduces from 2^n when meso forms exist

#### 25.6 Molecules with Multiple Stereocenters
- **Maximum stereoisomers**: 2^n for n stereocenters
- **Actual count**: May be less due to meso compounds
- **Example**: 2,3-dibromobutane
  - Two stereocenters (n=2)
  - Expected: 2² = 4 stereoisomers
  - Actual: 3 (two enantiomers + one meso)

#### 25.4 Optical Activity
- **Plane-polarized light**: Light waves oscillating in single plane
- **Optical rotation**: Chiral molecules rotate plane of polarized light
- **Dextrorotatory (d or +)**: Rotates clockwise (to the right)
- **Levorotatory (l or −)**: Rotates counterclockwise (to the left)
- **Racemic mixture**: 50:50 mixture of enantiomers, no net rotation
- **Specific rotation**: [α]ᵀλ = α_obs / (l·c)
  - α_obs: observed rotation (degrees)
  - l: path length (dm)
  - c: concentration (g/mL)
  - T: temperature
  - λ: wavelength (usually D line, 589 nm)
- **Enantiomeric excess (ee)**: Measures optical purity
  - ee = |([R] − [S])/([R] + [S])| × 100%

#### Fischer Projections
- **Convention**: 2D representation of 3D molecules
- **Horizontal lines**: Bonds coming out of page (wedges)
- **Vertical lines**: Bonds going into page (dashes)
- **Intersection**: Chiral center
- **Rules**:
  - Rotate 180° in plane: Same compound
  - Rotate 90° in plane: Enantiomer
  - Swap any two groups: Enantiomer
  - Swap twice: Back to original
- **R/S assignment**: Apply CIP rules to Fischer projection
- **Common in carbohydrate chemistry**

## Week 12 - Fischer Projections (cont.), Substitution Reactions: SN2 and SN1, Review

### Chapter References
- [Chapter 25: Stereochemistry of Organic Compounds](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter25-tutorial.html)
- [Chapter 27: Alcohols, Haloalkanes, and Ethers](https://cghuisunshine.github.io/Chemistry123/chapter27-tutorial.html)

### Key Topics

#### Fischer Projections (Advanced)
- **Multiple stereocenters**: Stack vertically
- **Carbohydrate notation**: D/L system
  - **D-sugars**: OH on right at bottom stereocenter
  - **L-sugars**: OH on left at bottom stereocenter
- **Interconversion with other representations**:
  - Fischer ↔ Newman projections
  - Fischer ↔ Wedge-dash structures
  - Fischer ↔ Chair conformations (for cyclic forms)

#### Substitution Reactions (Chapter 27)

##### SN2 (Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution)
- **Mechanism**: One-step, concerted
- **Nucleophile attacks from backside** (opposite leaving group)
- **Transition state**: Pentacoordinate carbon
- **Stereochemistry**: **Inversion of configuration** (Walden inversion)
- **Rate law**: rate = k[RX][Nu⁻] (second-order)
- **Kinetics**: Bimolecular (both substrate and nucleophile in rate-determining step)
- **Substrate preference**: 1° > 2° >> 3° (steric effects)
- **Solvent**: Polar aprotic favors SN2 (doesn't solvate nucleophile strongly)
- **Leaving group**: Better leaving groups = faster reaction
- **Nucleophile strength**: Stronger nucleophiles = faster reaction

##### SN1 (Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution)
- **Mechanism**: Two-step
  1. Slow: Leaving group departs → carbocation
  2. Fast: Nucleophile attacks carbocation
- **Stereochemistry**: **Racemization** (carbocation is planar, attack from either face)
  - May show partial inversion if nucleophile attacks before full carbocation formation
- **Rate law**: rate = k[RX] (first-order, independent of nucleophile)
- **Kinetics**: Unimolecular (only substrate in rate-determining step)
- **Substrate preference**: 3° > 2° >> 1° (carbocation stability)
- **Carbocation stability**: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl
  - **Hyperconjugation**: Alkyl groups stabilize positive charge
  - **Resonance**: Allylic/benzylic carbocations especially stable
- **Solvent**: Polar protic favors SN1 (stabilizes carbocation and leaving group)
- **Rearrangements**: Possible via hydride or alkyl shifts to form more stable carbocation

##### Comparison: SN1 vs SN2
| Feature | SN2 | SN1 |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Mechanism** | One-step, concerted | Two-step, carbocation intermediate |
| **Rate law** | rate = k[RX][Nu⁻] | rate = k[RX] |
| **Stereochemistry** | Inversion | Racemization |
| **Substrate** | 1° > 2° >> 3° | 3° > 2° >> 1° |
| **Nucleophile** | Strong needed | Weak acceptable |
| **Solvent** | Polar aprotic | Polar protic |
| **Rearrangements** | No | Yes (possible) |
