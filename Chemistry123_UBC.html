Week 1 – Foundations & Stoichiometry

Build the language of chemistry: units, significant figures, dimensional analysis, classification of matter, and the mole/stoichiometry bridge.

Textbook chapters: 1 – Introduction, Matter, Energy, and Measurement, 2 – Atoms, Molecules, and Ions, 3 – Chemical Reactions and Stoichiometry

SI units & conversions significant figures mole concept balancing equations limiting reactant

Key ideas

  • Classify matter by state (solid, liquid, gas) and composition (element, compound, mixture).
  • Use SI units and prefixes; treat units as algebraic quantities.
  • Define the mole and relate moles, mass, and number of particles.
  • Balance chemical equations and interpret coefficients as mole ratios.

Core formulas & patterns

  • Density: ρ = m/V
  • Moles: n = m / Mr, particles: N = n NA
  • Percent yield: % yield = (actual / theoretical) × 100%
  • Dimensional analysis: chain conversion where units cancel step by step.

Typical problems

  • Convert multi-step units (for example, km → cm, g·mL−1 → kg·L−1).
  • Given masses of reactants, identify the limiting reactant and compute theoretical yield.
  • Use a balanced equation to go mass → moles → moles → mass between reactant and product.

Week 2 – Thermochemistry & Calorimetry

Introduce energy, work, heat, and enthalpy; start connecting chemical change with energy change.

Textbook chapters: 5 – Thermochemistry, plus relevant review from 10 – Gases

q, w, ΔE enthalpy ΔH calorimetry Hess’s law

Key ideas

  • Distinguish system vs surroundings; heat vs work.
  • First law: energy is conserved, ΔE = q + w.
  • Enthalpy H as heat at constant pressure; exothermic vs endothermic processes.
  • Use coffee‑cup calorimetry data to estimate ΔH.
  • Apply Hess’s law and standard enthalpies of formation to compute reaction enthalpies.

Core formulas

  • q (heat) for a sample: q = m c ΔT
  • PV work at constant external pressure: w = −PΔV
  • Calorimetry: qreaction = −qsolution
  • Hess’s law and enthalpies of formation: ΔH°rxn = ΣnΔH°f(products) − ΣnΔH°f(reactants)

Typical problems

  • From temperature rise in a solution, calculate q and ΔH for a reaction per mole of reactant.
  • Use Hess’s law to build a target equation from given steps and sum their enthalpies.
  • Use standard enthalpies of formation to estimate heat of reaction at 298 K.

Week 3 – Entropy & the Second/Third Laws

Move from “how much heat?” to “will it happen?” by introducing entropy, microstates, and the second and third laws.

Textbook chapters: 19 – Chemical Thermodynamics

entropy S microstates ΔSsys, ΔSsurr, ΔSuniv second & third laws

Key ideas

  • Entropy as a measure of energy dispersal and number of microstates.
  • Second law: spontaneous processes increase the entropy of the universe.
  • Third law: a perfect crystal at 0 K has S = 0; standard molar entropies are measured relative to this.
  • Qualitative entropy trends: phase, number of particles, complexity, and temperature.

Core formulas

  • Microstate definition: S = k ln W
  • System entropy at constant T (reversible): ΔS = qrev/T
  • Reaction entropy from tables: ΔS°rxn = ΣnS°(products) − ΣnS°(reactants)
  • Surroundings entropy (approx): ΔSsurr ≈ −ΔHsys/T

Typical problems

  • Predict the sign of ΔS for phase changes, dissolution, and gas‑forming reactions.
  • Compute ΔS° for a reaction using tabulated S° values and interpret whether entropy increases or decreases.
  • Combine ΔSsys and ΔSsurr to decide if a process is spontaneous at a given temperature.

Week 4 – Free Energy & Equilibrium

Combine enthalpy and entropy into Gibbs free energy, link ΔG to equilibrium, and interpret van’t Hoff plots qualitatively.

Textbook chapters: 19 – Chemical Thermodynamics, 15 – Chemical Equilibrium

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS ΔG° and K reaction quotient Q Le Châtelier (thermo view)

Key ideas

  • Gibbs free energy as a “score” combining enthalpy and entropy at a given temperature.
  • Sign of ΔG predicts spontaneity at specified T and composition.
  • At equilibrium, ΔG = 0 and Q = K; ΔG° relates to K through the exponential dependence.

Core formulas

  • ΔG = ΔH − TΔS (nonstandard conditions, but using system ΔH, ΔS).
  • ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q for any composition at temperature T.
  • ΔG° = −RT ln K linking standard free energy change and equilibrium constant.
  • Qualitative van’t Hoff form: ln K ≈ −ΔH°/(R T) + ΔS°/R.

Typical problems

  • Given ΔH° and ΔS°, decide for which temperature ranges a reaction is spontaneous.
  • Relate the magnitude and sign of ΔG° to whether K is > 1, ≈ 1, or < 1.
  • Use ΔG° = −RT ln K to estimate K for thermodynamically favored or disfavored reactions.

Week 5 – Chemical Kinetics

Shift from “will it happen?” to “how fast?” by focusing on rates, rate laws, and activation energy.

Textbook chapters: 14 – Chemical Kinetics

rate laws orders of reaction half-life Arrhenius equation

Key ideas

  • Define rate in terms of disappearance of reactants or appearance of products.
  • Rate laws relate rate to reactant concentrations; orders are found experimentally.
  • Integrated rate laws give [A] as a function of time for zero-, first-, second-order reactions.
  • Activation energy and Arrhenius equation explain temperature dependence of k.

Core formulas

  • General rate law: rate = k[A]n (for a simple A → products).
  • First-order: ln([A]t / [A]0) = −kt, t1/2 = 0.693/k.
  • Second-order: 1/[A]t = 1/[A]0 + kt.
  • Arrhenius: k = A e−Ea/(RT).

Typical problems

  • From initial-rate data, determine the order in each reactant and the rate constant k.
  • Use a straight-line plot (ln[A] vs t, 1/[A] vs t) to identify reaction order and extract k.
  • Use the Arrhenius equation or a plot of ln k vs 1/T to estimate Ea.

Week 6 – Equilibrium & Acid–Base Basics

Introduce equilibrium constants, Q vs K, and the Brønsted–Lowry picture of acids and bases.

Textbook chapters: 15 – Chemical Equilibrium, 16 – Acid–Base Equilibria

Kc, Kp, Q Le Châtelier weak acids/bases pH and pKa

Key ideas

  • Write equilibrium expressions and relate K to the position of equilibrium.
  • Use Q vs K to predict the direction of shift for any composition.
  • Brønsted–Lowry acids donate H+, bases accept H+; conjugate pairs differ by one proton.
  • Weak acids/bases establish equilibria described by Ka or Kb.

Core formulas

  • Equilibrium constant for aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]c[D]d / ([A]a[B]b).
  • Reaction quotient Q has the same form as K but uses current concentrations.
  • Water autoprotolysis: Kw = [H+][OH−] ≈ 1.0 × 10−14 at 25 °C.
  • pH: pH = −log[H+]; for weak acids: Ka = [H+][A−]/[HA].

Typical problems

  • Set up ICE tables to find equilibrium concentrations and K, or to find concentrations given K.
  • Calculate the pH of a weak acid solution using Ka and an ICE approximation.
  • Classify acid–base conjugate pairs and estimate the relative strength of conjugate bases.

Week 7 – Buffers, Titrations & Solubility

Build buffer intuition, sketch qualitative titration curves, and connect Ksp to precipitation.

Textbook chapters: 17 – Additional Aspects of Aqueous Equilibria, 13 – Properties of Solutions

buffer pH Henderson–Hasselbalch titration curves Ksp and Q

Key ideas

  • Buffers contain significant amounts of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and conjugate acid).
  • Henderson–Hasselbalch equation gives pH in terms of pKa and the ratio [base]/[acid].
  • Titration curves show how pH changes as titrant is added; equivalence point and buffer regions stand out.
  • Sparingly soluble salts have Ksp; the common-ion effect reduces solubility.

Core formulas

  • Buffer pH: pH = pKa + log([base]/[acid]).
  • Solubility product for MX(s) ⇌ M+ + X−: Ksp = [M+][X−].
  • For simple 1:1 salts in pure water: if s = molar solubility, Ksp = s2.

Typical problems

  • Calculate buffer pH before and after adding a small amount of strong acid or base.
  • Sketch a qualitative titration curve for a weak acid titrated by a strong base, indicating buffer region and equivalence point.
  • Use Ksp and ionic concentrations to decide whether a precipitate will form (compare Q to Ksp).

Week 8 – Electrochemistry

Translate redox chemistry into electrical work: galvanic cells, cell notation, and E°–ΔG–K links.

Textbook chapters: 20 – Electrochemistry

redox & oxidation states galvanic cells E° and ΔG° qualitative Nernst

Key ideas

  • Assign oxidation numbers and identify oxidizing and reducing agents.
  • Dissect a galvanic cell into half‑reactions at the anode and cathode.
  • Interpret cell notation and direction of electron flow.
  • Relate standard cell potential to ΔG° and to K qualitatively.

Core formulas

  • Standard cell potential: E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode.
  • Free energy: ΔG° = −nF E°cell.
  • Connection to equilibrium (25 °C): E°cell = (0.0592 V / n) log K.

Typical problems

  • Given standard reduction potentials, write the spontaneous cell reaction and compute E°cell.
  • Determine whether a redox reaction is product- or reactant-favoured from the sign of E°cell.
  • Qualitatively predict how changing ion concentrations shifts cell potential using the idea behind the Nernst equation.

Week 9 – Intro to Organic & Structure

Switch gears to organic chemistry: functional groups, line structures, and basic IUPAC naming.

Textbook chapters: 24 – The Chemistry of Organic Compounds, 26 – Chemistry of Alkenes and Alkynes

functional groups line-angle formulas alkanes/alkenes/alkynes basic naming

Key ideas

  • Recognize core functional groups: alkane, alkene, alkyne, alcohol, carbonyl, carboxylic acid, amine.
  • Translate between condensed formulas and line-angle drawings.
  • Apply simple IUPAC naming: choose longest chain, identify substituents, number to minimize locants.

Core patterns

  • Base names: meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, etc., with -ane, -ene, -yne endings.
  • Isomerism: same molecular formula, different connectivity or geometry.
  • Double bonds restrict rotation and can lead to cis/trans (E/Z) isomerism.

Typical problems

  • Draw line-angle structures for simple alkanes and substituted alkanes.
  • Name straight-chain and branched alkanes and simple alkenes.
  • Identify the functional groups present in a given organic structure.

Week 10 – Stereochemistry

Bring 3D thinking into organic: chirality, R/S configuration, and relationships between stereoisomers.

Textbook chapters: 25 – Stereochemistry of Organic Compounds

chiral centers R/S notation enantiomers vs diastereomers meso compounds

Key ideas

  • Chiral centers are typically sp3 carbons with four different substituents.
  • Enantiomers are non-superimposable mirror images; diastereomers are stereoisomers that are not mirror images.
  • Assign R or S using CIP priority rules and a 3D viewing convention.

Core patterns

  • Number of possible stereoisomers ≤ 2n for n stereocenters (adjust for meso).
  • Meso compounds contain stereocenters but have an internal plane of symmetry and are achiral.

Typical problems

  • Identify chiral centers and classify pairs of structures as enantiomers, diastereomers, or identical.
  • Assign R/S configuration to labeled stereocenters on wedge-dash diagrams.
  • Determine whether a drawn structure is meso.

Week 11 – Functional Groups & Reactivity

Connect structure to reactivity for alkenes, alkynes, alcohols, haloalkanes, and ethers.

Textbook chapters: 26 – Chemistry of Alkenes and Alkynes, 27 – Alcohols, Haloalkanes, and Ethers

π-bond reactivity nucleophiles & electrophiles substitution vs elimination (concept) functional group interconversion

Key ideas

  • Alkenes and alkynes are electron-rich π systems that undergo addition reactions.
  • Alcohols, ethers, and haloalkanes differ in polarity and leaving-group ability, influencing substitution and elimination.
  • Conceptual link between structure (primary/secondary/tertiary) and reactivity patterns.

Core patterns

  • π bonds act as nucleophiles; electrophiles add first, then nucleophiles follow.
  • Haloalkanes can undergo nucleophilic substitution (Nu replaces halogen) or elimination to give alkenes.
  • Hydrogen bonding in alcohols raises boiling points relative to ethers and haloalkanes of similar mass.

Typical problems

  • Predict products for simple additions across C=C or C≡C.
  • Classify reagents as nucleophiles or electrophiles and identify likely sites of attack.
  • Compare physical properties and reactivity trends across a set of alcohols, ethers, and haloalkanes.

Week 12 – Carbonyls, Biomolecules & Structure Solving

Tie together carbonyl chemistry, carboxylic acids, nitrogen-containing compounds, and basic spectroscopic structure determination.

Textbook chapters: 28 – Aldehydes, Ketones, and Carbohydrates, 29 – Carboxylic Acids and Their Derivatives, 31 – Nitrogen-Containing Organic Compounds, 32 – Solving Molecular Structure

carbonyl polarity carboxylic acids & derivatives amines & amides IR & NMR (concept)

Key ideas

  • Carbonyl carbon is electrophilic; aldehydes and ketones undergo nucleophilic addition.
  • Carboxylic acids are more acidic than alcohols due to resonance-stabilized conjugate bases.
  • Carboxylic acid derivatives (acid chlorides, esters, amides) differ in reactivity based on leaving groups.
  • Amines are basic due to their lone pair; amides are less basic because of resonance with the carbonyl.

Structure tools (qualitative)

  • Index of hydrogen deficiency (IHD) counts rings and π bonds.
  • IR: broad O–H stretches, sharp C=O peaks, nitrile/alkyne signatures help identify functional groups.
  • Basic 1H NMR: number of signals, integration, and simple splitting patterns reveal the skeleton.

Typical problems

  • Given functional-group information, suggest likely reactivity (addition vs substitution, acid vs base behavior).
  • Use a molecular formula, simple IR notes (for example, “strong C=O, no O–H”), and a few NMR hints to sketch plausible structures.
  • Recognize how carbohydrates, amino acids, and peptide bonds use the carbonyl and amine chemistry developed here.
