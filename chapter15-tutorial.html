Chapter 15 Tutorial — Chemical Equilibrium

15.1 The Concept of Equilibrium

Key ideas

  • Chemical equilibrium occurs when forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, so concentrations of reactants and products become constant while reactions continue dynamically.
  • The equilibrium position is independent of how the system was initiated and requires a closed system.
  • Even though macroscopic concentrations remain steady, molecules continue interconverting—the equilibrium is dynamic.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Rate equality: At equilibrium, rate_forward = rate_reverse, so k_f[A]^a[B]^b = k_r[C]^c[D]^d; the ratio of rate constants becomes the equilibrium constant.

Short example(s)

In the NO₂ ⇌ N₂O₄ equilibrium the solution color stops changing once rates match, even though molecules keep exchanging.

15.2 The Equilibrium Constant

Key ideas

  • For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, K_c = [C]^c[D]^d/[A]^a[B]^b; only species with changing concentrations appear, excluding pure solids/liquids.
  • K_p uses partial pressures: K_p = (P_C)^c(P_D)^d/(P_A)^a(P_B)^b.
  • K_p and K_c are related by K_p = K_c(RT)^Δn, where Δn is gaseous mole change.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • K expression: For ammonia synthesis K_c = [NH₃]^2/[N₂][H₂]^3.
  • Conversion: K_p = K_c(RT)^Δn; Δn = 2 − (1+3) = −2 for the Haber process.

Short example(s)

At 450 °C the Haber `K_c` is small, so high pressure and NH₃ removal (shifting equilibrium) maximize yield.

15.3 Understanding and Working with Equilibrium Constants

Key ideas

  • Manipulating the balanced equation alters K: reversing it gives 1/K; multiplying the equation multiplies the exponent (K^n).
  • K is constant at a given temperature; changing T shifts K depending on the reaction’s enthalpy.
  • ICE tables (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) organize concentrations so you can solve for equilibrium values.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Algebraic rules: If K₁ is known for an equation, reversing it gives K = 1/K₁, adding two equations multiplies their Ks.
  • ICE tables: Write initial concentrations, express changes with `x`, and plug equilibrium expressions into the K equation to solve.

Short example(s)

Combining `K₁ = 2` for `A ⇌ B` with `K₂ = 4` for `B ⇌ C` yields `K_total = 8` for `A ⇌ C`.

15.4 Calculating Equilibrium Constants

Key ideas

  • Plug experimentally determined equilibrium concentrations into the equilibrium expression to compute K.
  • Solve quadratic equations from ICE tables; use approximations when `x` is small compared to initial concentrations.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • ICE method: `Δ` terms show stoichiometric change (e.g., `−x` for reactant, `+x` for product) before substituting into `K`.
  • Small-x approximation: If `K` is small and `x` ≪ initial concentration, drop `x` in the denominator to simplify solving.

Short example(s)

If equilibrium gives `[NH₃] = 0.2 M` while `[N₂] = 0.1 M` and `[H₂] = 0.3 M`, plug into `K = [NH₃]^2/[N₂][H₂]^3` to find `K_c`.

15.5 Le Châtelier’s Principle

Key ideas

  • If equilibrium is disturbed (by concentration, pressure, temperature), the system shifts to counter the change and re-establish equilibrium.
  • Adding reactants shifts toward products; removing products or increasing pressure (if fewer gas moles on product side) shifts toward products.
  • Temperature changes alter `K`: heating an endothermic reaction increases `K`, cooling decreases it.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Le Châtelier’s Principle: A qualitative rule that predicts how equilibrium moves in response to stress.
  • Pressure effect: For gaseous equilibria, increasing total pressure by reducing volume favors the side with fewer moles of gas.

Short example(s)

For `N₂ + 3 H₂ ⇌ 2 NH₃`, raising pressure favors NH₃ because the right side has fewer gas moles; heating shifts toward reactants since the reaction is exothermic.
