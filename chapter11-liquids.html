Chapter 11: Liquids and Intermolecular Forces

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Identify and compare types of intermolecular forces (IMFs): dispersion, dipole–dipole, hydrogen bonding, ion–dipole.
  • Relate strength of IMFs to boiling point, melting point, viscosity, surface tension, and vapor pressure.
  • Interpret heating curves and understand energy changes during phase transitions.
  • Describe qualitatively what a phase diagram shows and locate the triple point and critical point.

Key Vocabulary

  • Intermolecular forces: attractions between separate molecules (not the covalent bonds inside a molecule).
  • Dispersion forces (London): weak attractions present in all molecules, arising from temporary dipoles.
  • Dipole–dipole forces: attractions between permanent molecular dipoles in polar molecules.
  • Hydrogen bonding: strong IMF involving H bonded to N, O, or F and a nearby lone pair on N, O, or F.
  • Viscosity: resistance of a liquid to flow.
  • Surface tension: energy needed to increase a liquid’s surface area.
  • Vapor pressure: pressure of a vapor in equilibrium with its liquid at a given temperature.

1. Types of Intermolecular Forces

  • Dispersion forces: increase with molar mass and surface area; important for nonpolar molecules (for example, noble gases, alkanes).
  • Dipole–dipole forces: present in polar molecules in addition to dispersion (for example, HCl, CH3Cl).
  • Hydrogen bonding: especially strong dipole–dipole attraction (for example, H2O, HF, NH3).
  • Ion–dipole forces: between ions and polar molecules (important for dissolving ionic solids in water).

Worked Example 1 – Comparing Boiling Points

Predict relative boiling points for CH4, CH3Cl, and CH3OH.

  1. CH4: nonpolar → dispersion only.
  2. CH3Cl: polar → dispersion + dipole–dipole.
  3. CH3OH: polar with O–H → dispersion + dipole–dipole + hydrogen bonding.
  4. Boiling point order (lowest → highest): CH4 < CH3Cl < CH3OH.

2. Properties of Liquids

  • Stronger IMFs → higher boiling and melting points, higher viscosity, higher surface tension, lower vapor pressure.
  • Viscosity decreases with increasing temperature; molecules move more easily past one another.
  • Vapor pressure increases with temperature as more molecules escape into the gas phase.

Heating Curves

  • Plot of temperature vs heat added.
  • Sloped segments: temperature rises as heat increases kinetic energy.
  • Flat plateaus: phase changes—added energy breaks IMFs at constant temperature.

3. Phase Diagrams (Qualitative)

  • Show regions of stability for solid, liquid, and gas as a function of pressure and temperature.
  • Triple point: all three phases coexist at equilibrium.
  • Critical point: above this temperature and pressure, no clear distinction between liquid and gas (supercritical fluid).

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Order these by expected boiling point and explain: CH4, CH3OH, CH3CH3NH2.
  2. Explain why water has a much higher boiling point than H2S, even though O and S are in the same group.
  3. Sketch a qualitative heating curve for ice starting well below 0 °C and ending above 100 °C. Label the phase changes.
  4. Describe qualitatively what happens to vapor pressure and boiling point of a liquid as external pressure decreases (for example, at high altitude).
  5. On a conceptual phase diagram for water, mark the triple point and critical point and explain what each represents.
