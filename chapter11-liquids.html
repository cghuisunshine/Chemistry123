Chapter 11: Liquids and Intermolecular Forces

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Identify types of intermolecular forces (IMFs) and relate them to physical properties.
  • Explain trends in boiling point, viscosity, surface tension, and vapor pressure.
  • Interpret heating curves and phase diagrams qualitatively.

Key Vocabulary

  • Intermolecular forces: attractions between molecules (not within).
  • Dispersion forces: weak IMFs present in all molecules, arising from temporary dipoles.
  • Dipole–dipole forces: attractions between permanent molecular dipoles.
  • Hydrogen bonding: strong IMF involving H bonded to N, O, or F and a nearby lone pair.
  • Viscosity: resistance to flow.
  • Surface tension: energy required to increase a liquid’s surface area.
  • Vapor pressure: pressure of vapor in equilibrium with its liquid at a given temperature.

1. Types of Intermolecular Forces

  • Dispersion forces increase with molar mass and surface area.
  • Polar molecules have dipole–dipole forces in addition to dispersion.
  • Hydrogen bonding leads to unusually high boiling points (for example, water, HF, NH3).

2. Properties of Liquids

  • Stronger IMFs → higher boiling point and viscosity, lower vapor pressure.
  • Heating curves show plateaus at phase changes where added energy breaks IMFs at constant temperature.

3. Phase Diagrams (Qualitative)

  • Show regions where solid, liquid, or gas is stable as a function of T and P.
  • Triple point: all three phases coexist in equilibrium.
  • Critical point: above this, no distinct liquid–gas boundary (supercritical fluid).

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Order these substances by expected boiling point: CH4, CH3OH, CH3Cl. Explain using IMFs.
  2. Explain why water has a much higher boiling point than H2S, even though O and S are in the same group.
  3. Sketch a qualitative heating curve for ice starting well below 0 °C and ending above 100 °C. Label phase changes.
  4. Describe qualitatively what happens to vapor pressure and boiling point of a liquid as external pressure decreases.
  5. On a conceptual phase diagram, indicate where the triple point and critical point lie and describe their significance.
