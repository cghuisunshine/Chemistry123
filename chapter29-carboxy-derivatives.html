Chapter 29: Carboxylic Acids and Their Derivatives

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Recognize carboxylic acids and common derivatives (esters, amides, acid chlorides) from structures.
  • Describe qualitatively why carboxylic acids are more acidic than simple alcohols.
  • Relate derivative reactivity conceptually in terms of leaving‑group ability and resonance.

Key Vocabulary

  • Carboxylic acid: compound with –CO2H (–COOH) functional group.
  • Ester: derivative where the OH is replaced by OR′ (–CO2R).
  • Amide: derivative where OH is replaced by NR2.
  • Acid chloride: derivative with –COCl group.
  • Resonance: delocalization of electrons over more than one atom, stabilizing the structure.

1. Structure and Acidity

  • Carboxylic acids are more acidic than alcohols because their conjugate base (carboxylate) is resonance‑stabilized.
  • Electron‑withdrawing substituents near the CO2H group generally increase acidity by stabilizing the conjugate base.

2. Derivative Reactivity (Conceptual)

  • Reactive hierarchy often summarized qualitatively: acid chloride > anhydride > ester ≈ carboxylic acid > amide.
  • More reactive species have better leaving groups attached to the carbonyl carbon (for example, Cl− is a better leaving group than OR−, which is better than NR2−).

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Identify the functional group in each: CH3CO2H, CH3CO2CH3, CH3CONH2, and CH3COCl.
  2. Explain in words why resonance makes carboxylic acids stronger acids than comparable alcohols.
  3. Given a simple structure, decide qualitatively whether it is likely to be more reactive (like an acid chloride) or less reactive (like an amide) toward nucleophilic acyl substitution.
