Chapter 12: Solids and Modern Materials

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish between crystalline and amorphous solids.
  • Compare metallic, ionic, molecular, and network covalent solids.
  • Describe band theory ideas for metals, semiconductors, and insulators.

Key Vocabulary

  • Crystalline solid: atoms or molecules arranged in an ordered, repeating pattern.
  • Amorphous solid: lacks long‑range order (for example, glass).
  • Unit cell: smallest repeating box that builds a crystal lattice.
  • Metallic solid: positive metal ions in a “sea” of delocalized electrons.
  • Ionic solid: ions held by electrostatic attractions.
  • Molecular solid: molecules held together by intermolecular forces.
  • Network covalent solid: extended 3D network of covalent bonds (for example, diamond, SiO2).
  • Band gap: energy gap between valence and conduction bands in a solid.

1. Types of Solids

  • Metallic: good conductors, malleable, ductile.
  • Ionic: hard, brittle, high melting, conduct electricity when molten or dissolved.
  • Molecular: low melting points; properties depend on strength of IMFs.
  • Network solids: very high melting points, hard, often poor conductors (unless doped).

2. Band Theory and Modern Materials

  • Metals: overlapping valence and conduction bands → high conductivity.
  • Insulators: large band gap → few thermally excited charge carriers.
  • Semiconductors: small band gap; conductivity increases with T and doping.
  • Doping introduces extra energy levels that modify conductivity.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Classify each as metallic, ionic, molecular, or network covalent: NaCl(s), diamond, solid CO2, copper metal.
  2. Explain why ionic solids are often brittle, while metals are malleable.
  3. Compare the band diagrams of a metal, an insulator, and a semiconductor.
  4. Describe qualitatively how n‑type and p‑type doping change the conductivity of silicon.
