Chapter 12: Solids and Modern Materials

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish crystalline vs amorphous solids.
  • Compare metallic, ionic, molecular, and network covalent solids in terms of structure and properties.
  • Describe the basics of band theory for metals, semiconductors, and insulators.
  • Explain qualitatively how doping changes the conductivity of semiconductors.

Key Vocabulary

  • Crystalline solid: atoms or molecules arranged in an ordered, repeating pattern.
  • Amorphous solid: lacks long‑range order (for example, glass, some polymers).
  • Unit cell: smallest repeating box that builds the crystal lattice.
  • Metallic solid: positive metal ions in a “sea” of delocalized electrons.
  • Ionic solid: ions held together by electrostatic attractions.
  • Molecular solid: molecules held together by intermolecular forces.
  • Network covalent solid: extended 3D network of covalent bonds (for example, diamond, SiO2).
  • Band gap: energy gap between valence and conduction bands in a solid.

1. Types of Solids and Properties

  • Metallic solids: good electrical and thermal conductors; malleable and ductile; shiny; bond strength and melting point vary widely.
  • Ionic solids: hard and brittle; high melting points; conduct electricity when molten or in solution, but not as solids.
  • Molecular solids: low to moderate melting points; properties depend strongly on strength of IMFs (for example, dry ice vs sugar).
  • Network covalent solids: very high melting points; hard; often poor conductors (unless doped or with delocalized electrons).

2. Band Theory (Qualitative)

  • In solids, atomic orbitals combine into bands of closely spaced energy levels.
  • Valence band: largely filled with electrons.
  • Conduction band: higher‑energy, typically empty or partially filled.
  • Metals: valence and conduction bands overlap or valence band is half‑filled → many mobile electrons → high conductivity.
  • Insulators: large band gap between valence and conduction bands → few thermally excited carriers → low conductivity.
  • Semiconductors: small band gap; conductivity increases with temperature.

3. Doping and Modern Materials

  • n‑type doping: add atoms with more valence electrons than the host (for example, P in Si) → extra electrons in conduction band.
  • p‑type doping: add atoms with fewer valence electrons (for example, B in Si) → “holes” in valence band act as positive charge carriers.
  • By controlling doping and structure, we engineer materials for diodes, transistors, solar cells, etc.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Classify each as metallic, ionic, molecular, or network covalent and justify briefly: NaCl(s), diamond, solid CO2, copper metal.
  2. Explain why ionic solids are often brittle, while metals are malleable and ductile.
  3. Compare the band diagrams qualitatively for a metal, an insulator, and a semiconductor.
  4. Describe qualitatively how n‑type and p‑type doping change the conductivity of silicon.
