Chapter 27: Alcohols, Haloalkanes, and Ethers

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Identify alcohols, haloalkanes (alkyl halides), and ethers from structures and names.
  • Describe how polarity and hydrogen bonding affect boiling points and solubility of these functional groups.
  • Outline basic substitution and elimination ideas qualitatively for haloalkanes.

Key Vocabulary

  • Alcohol: organic compound with an OH group bonded to sp3 carbon.
  • Haloalkane (alkyl halide): alkane where one or more H atoms are replaced by halogen (F, Cl, Br, I).
  • Ether: compound with C–O–C linkage.
  • Nucleophile: electron‑rich species that donates a pair of electrons.
  • Leaving group: group that departs with an electron pair in a substitution or elimination.

1. Structure and Properties

  • Alcohols: O–H bond allows hydrogen bonding → higher boiling points than similar‑mass hydrocarbons or ethers.
  • Ethers: C–O–C; polar but no O–H bond → moderate boiling points; good solvents in some cases.
  • Haloalkanes: more polarizable; heavier halides generally have higher boiling points; often less polar than alcohols.

2. Conceptual Reactivity

  • Haloalkanes undergo nucleophilic substitution (Nu replaces halogen) and elimination (forming alkenes) depending on conditions.
  • Alcohols can be protonated to form better leaving groups and can be oxidized to carbonyl compounds (links to aldehydes/ketones).

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Identify which of the following are alcohols, ether, or haloalkanes: CH3OCH3, CH3CH2Cl, CH3CH2OH, CH3CH2OCH3.
  2. Predict which has the higher boiling point and briefly justify: CH3CH2OH vs CH3OCH3.
  3. Explain in simple terms what is meant by a nucleophile and a leaving group in substitution reactions of haloalkanes.
