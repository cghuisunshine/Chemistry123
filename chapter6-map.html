6.0 Overview — Electronic Structure of Atoms

Why we need quantum theory to describe electrons

Classical physics could not explain several key observations about atoms and light:

  • Blackbody radiation: hot objects emitting light with characteristic spectra.
  • Photoelectric effect: light ejecting electrons from metal surfaces with a threshold frequency.
  • Atomic line spectra: atoms emitting only specific wavelengths of light.

This led to the development of quantum theory, which introduced:

  • Energy quantization (discrete energy levels rather than a continuum).
  • Photons (light as particles) and wave‚Äìparticle duality.
  • Orbitals and quantum numbers as the language for electron energies and probabilities.

6.4 The Wave Behavior of Matter

De Broglie waves & Heisenberg uncertainty

De Broglie hypothesis: particles like electrons can exhibit wave-like behavior, described by a wavelength that depends on their momentum.

These matter waves are especially important for very small, light particles (like electrons) moving not too slowly, because their wavelengths are comparable to atomic sizes.

Heisenberg uncertainty principle: there is a fundamental limit to how precisely we can simultaneously know both the position and momentum of a particle. For electrons in atoms, this limit is large enough that we must abandon the idea of precise orbits.

6.7 Many-Electron Atoms

Subshell energies, degeneracy, and spin

For hydrogen, the energy of an orbital depends only on n. In a many-electron atom, the energy also depends on l because of electron‚Äìelectron repulsions and shielding effects.

Within a shell of given n, the energy of subshells increases with l:

ns < np < nd < nf.

Orbitals within the same subshell (same n and l but different m‚Çó) are still degenerate (same energy).

Electron spin introduces the spin quantum number m‚Çõ and is essential for understanding the Pauli exclusion principle and the filling of orbitals.

Energy ordering for filling orbitals:

  • 1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f < 5d < 6p < 7s < 5f < 6d < 7p.

Spin & Pauli (restated):

  • Each orbital can hold 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Spin quantum number m‚Çõ = +¬Ω or ‚àí¬Ω.

Combined with quantum numbers (n, l, m‚Çó), spin ensures that no two electrons have identical sets of four quantum numbers.

Hydrogen vs multi-electron atoms.

  • Hydrogen: 2s and 2p are degenerate (same energy).
  • Many-electron atoms: 2s is lower in energy than 2p due to penetration and shielding differences.
  • Likewise, 4s is lower than 3d in the neutral atoms near K, Ca, Sc.

Degeneracy within subshells:

  • All three 2p orbitals are equal in energy (degenerate) but differ in orientation (m‚Çó = ‚àí1, 0, +1).
  • The same holds for sets of five d or seven f orbitals.

The periodic table layout is effectively this energy-order list bent into a rectangle; reading across rows and blocks shows which subshell is being filled.
