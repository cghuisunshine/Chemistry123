Chapter 25: Stereochemistry of Organic Compounds

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Define chirality and identify chiral centers in simple organic molecules.
  • Distinguish enantiomers and diastereomers conceptually.
  • Assign R/S configuration using the Cahn–Ingold–Prelog priority rules (qualitatively described).

Key Vocabulary

  • Chiral: object not superimposable on its mirror image.
  • Achiral: object superimposable on its mirror image.
  • Chiral center: typically a tetrahedral carbon bonded to four different groups.
  • Enantiomers: non‑superimposable mirror‑image isomers.
  • Diastereomers: stereoisomers that are not mirror images.
  • R/S configuration: labels for absolute configuration at a chiral center, determined by CIP priority rules.
  • Meso compound: stereoisomer with chiral centers but an internal plane of symmetry; overall achiral.

1. Identifying Chirality

  • Look for tetrahedral carbons (sp3) with four different substituents.
  • Check for internal symmetry; an internal mirror plane often indicates achirality even if chiral centers are present (meso).

2. Assigning R/S Configuration (Conceptual)

  • Assign priorities (1 = highest) to the four substituents based on atomic number (higher Z → higher priority).
  • If the first atoms are the same, move outward along the chain until a difference is found.
  • Orient the molecule so that the lowest‑priority group points away from you.
  • Trace the path from priority 1 → 2 → 3: clockwise = R, counterclockwise = S.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. For CHBrClF, identify the chiral center and sketch (or imagine) both enantiomers.
  2. Explain how you would determine if two drawn structures are enantiomers, diastereomers, or identical.
  3. Given simple wedge‑dash drawings (from your notes or text), practice assigning R or S to each chiral center.
  4. Define a meso compound and give an example in words (such as meso‑tartaric acid), noting its internal plane of symmetry.
