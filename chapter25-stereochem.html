Chapter 25: Stereochemistry of Organic Compounds

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Define chirality and identify chiral centers.
  • Distinguish enantiomers and diastereomers.
  • Assign R/S configuration using CIP priority rules.

Key Vocabulary

  • Chiral: object not superimposable on its mirror image.
  • Achiral: object superimposable on its mirror image.
  • Chiral center: typically a tetrahedral carbon with four different substituents.
  • Enantiomers: non‑superimposable mirror images.
  • Diastereomers: stereoisomers that are not mirror images.
  • R/S configuration: absolute configuration assigned by Cahn–Ingold–Prelog rules.
  • Meso compound: stereoisomer with chiral centers but an internal plane of symmetry, overall achiral.

1. Identifying Chirality

  • Look for tetrahedral carbons with four different groups; check for internal symmetry.
  • Enantiomers have identical physical properties except for optical rotation and chiral environments.

2. Assigning R/S Configuration (Conceptual)

  • Assign priorities to substituents based on atomic number (higher Z → higher priority).
  • Orient molecule so lowest‑priority group points away; trace 1 → 2 → 3: clockwise = R, counterclockwise = S.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. For a simple substituted carbon (for example, CHBrClF), identify whether the carbon is chiral and sketch both enantiomers.
  2. Explain how you would determine if two drawn structures are enantiomers, diastereomers, or identical.
  3. Given appropriately drawn molecules (from your notes or text), practice assigning R or S to each chiral center.
  4. Define a meso compound and give an example (such as meso‑tartaric acid) in words or sketches.
