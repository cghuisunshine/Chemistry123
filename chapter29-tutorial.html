Chapter 29 Tutorial — Carboxylic Acids and Their Derivatives

29.1 Carboxylic Acids

Key ideas

  • The carboxyl group (COOH) merges a carbonyl and hydroxyl attached to an sp2 carbon, giving both resonance-stabilized conjugate bases and strong hydrogen bonding at the OH that raises boiling points and water solubility for small acids.
  • The acidity of RCOOH (pKa ~4-5 for acetic acid) stems from the stable carboxylate anion; resonance over two equivalent O atoms spreads negative charge, making the conjugate base far less reactive than the alkoxide from the corresponding alcohol (pKa ~16).
  • Inductive effects tune acidity: electron-withdrawing substituents (e.g., Cl) near the COOH weaken the O-H bond (lowering pKa), and reacting with bases or amines makes soluble salts that aid drug delivery or formulation.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Acid dissociation: RCOOH + H2O -> RCOO- + H3O+ (Equation 29.1). The carboxyl carbon pulls electron density toward both oxygens; when the O-H bond breaks, the negative charge spreads over both O atoms, which is why carboxylic acids are weak acids whose pKa values sit near 4-5.
  • Carboxylate resonance: [R-C(=O)-O-] <-> [R-CO(-1)-O=]. This resonance explains why the conjugate base is stabilized and why molecules like acetic acid are much stronger acids than ethanol.
  • Base neutralization: RCOOH + NaOH -> RCOONa + H2O (Equation 29.2). Salt formation increases aqueous solubility, so pharmaceuticals are often delivered as carboxylate or ammonium salts (for example, acetate + ethylamine -> ethylammonium acetate, Equation 29.4).
  • Bicarbonate reaction: RCOOH + NaHCO3 -> RCOONa + CO2 + H2O (Equation 29.3). The evolution of CO2 is a convenient test for acids (e.g., in effervescent tablets) and shows carbonate salts release gas when acidified.

Short example(s)

  • Acetic acid (CH3COOH) dissolves in water and donates a proton to water to make acetate; adding NaOH gives sodium acetate, which is more soluble and used in buffers.
  • Mixing acetic acid with ethanol in the presence of acid makes ethyl acetate (Fischer esterification, Section 29.3) while reacting with NaHCO3 effervesces CO2, illustrating acidity and salt behavior.

29.2 Preparation of Carboxylic Acids

Key ideas

  • Oxidizing primary alcohols (or aldehydes) with strong oxidants (K2Cr2O7/H2SO4, KMnO4) converts them to carboxylic acids; aldehydes that form along the way are quickly over-oxidized, so stopping at the acid requires careful control.
  • Industrial acetic acid comes from carbonylation of methanol (CH3OH + CO -> CH3COOH, Equation 29.5) with a rhodium catalyst, while benzoic acid forms by oxidizing toluene or via phenylmagnesium bromide plus CO2.
  • Functional-group interconversion routes (hydrolyzing esters, amides, nitriles) and Grignard addition to dry ice are reliable ways to extend carbon chains to acids.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alcohol oxidation: RCH2OH + [O] -> RCOOH + H2O. Strong oxidizing agents attack the primary carbon, eventually forming the carboxyl group; this is why ethanol exposed to air or acid turns into acetic acid (vinegar).
  • Carbonylation: CH3OH + CO -> CH3COOH (Equation 29.5). The CO inserts between the methyl and hydroxyl; the process is highly efficient for making acetic acid at scale.
  • Grignard + CO2: R-MgX + CO2 -> RCOO-MgX -> H3O+ -> RCOOH (Equation 29.10). Carbon dioxide acts as an electrophile, so nucleophilic carbanions add to it, and protonation delivers the carboxylic acid with one extra carbon.
  • Hydrolysis sources: esters, amides and nitriles hydrolyze under acidic or basic conditions (Equations 29.11–29.13) to regenerate RCOOH, which chemists use to cleave protecting groups or produce acids from simpler precursors.

Short example(s)

  • Oxidation of pentan-1-ol with acidic dichromate produces pentanoic acid, giving a longer-chain acid starting from the alcohol (Equation 29.6).
  • Treating 1-bromobutane with Mg/ether, then CO2, and finally acid yields pentanoic acid (Sample Exercise 29.2), showing how Grignard chemistry extends carbon chains.

29.3 Esters and Esterification

Key ideas

  • Esters RCOOR' form when a carboxylic acid condenses with an alcohol under acid catalysis (Fischer esterification); the alcohol portion gives the “alkyl” name and the acid the “-oate” suffix.
  • The reaction is reversible; adding water or using dilute acid shifts equilibrium back to acids and alcohols, making esters useful as temporary protecting groups.
  • Basic hydrolysis (saponification) cleaves esters to carboxylate salts and alcohols, underpinning soap manufacture and flavor chemistry.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Esterification: RCOOH + R'OH <-> RCOOR' + H2O (Equation 29.14). The acid catalyst protonates the carbonyl, allowing the alcohol to attack; removing water or using excess alcohol drives the equilibrium to ester formation.
  • Acidic hydrolysis: RCOOR' + H2O -> RCOOH + R'OH. Reintroducing water shifts the equilibrium back, regenerating the acid and alcohol; this reversibility is the basis for protecting carboxyl groups.
  • Saponification (base hydrolysis): RCOOR' + OH- -> RCOO- + R'OH (Equation 29.16). The base produces a soluble carboxylate salt, and upon acidification the free acid returns; soaps exploit the amphiphilic salt to emulsify grease.

Short example(s)

  • Ethyl acetate forms from acetic acid and ethanol, giving a sweet-smelling ester used in nail polish remover (Figure 29.5 and Equation 29.14).
  • Saponifying methyl propionate with NaOH yields sodium propionate and methanol (Equation 29.16), a reaction analogous to how soap producers cleave fats.

29.4 Fats, Oils, and Waxes

Key ideas

  • Lipids encompass hydrophobic esters such as waxes (fatty acid + long alcohol) and triglycerides (glycerol + three fatty acids); their nonpolar alkyl chains dominate their solubility and melting behavior.
  • Saturated fatty acids pack tightly and give solid fats (e.g., stearic acid); unsaturated chains introduce kinks, lower melting points (oils), and allow hydrogenation (hardening) to make margarines.
  • Saponification of triglycerides with strong base creates soap (carboxylate salts), and detergents replace carboxylate heads with sulfonate groups to avoid scum formation in hard water.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Triglyceride formation: glycerol + 3 RCOOH -> glycerol triester + 3 H2O. Each ester link removes water, building a triester whose long chains give the characteristic lipid texture.
  • Saponification: triglyceride + 3 NaOH -> glycerol + 3 RCOONa (Equation 29.17). The carboxylate salts act as soaps—micelles form with hydrophobic tails inside and hydrophilic heads outside to emulsify oils (Figure 29.8).
  • Hydrogenation (hardening): C=C + H2 -> C-C across the unsaturated fatty chains. Partial hydrogenation increases saturation, raising melting points to turn oils into fats while controlling trans isomer formation.
  • Soap scum formation: 2 RCOO- + Ca2+ -> (RCOO)2Ca (Equation 29.18); detergents avoid that by using sulfonate heads whose calcium salts remain soluble.

Short example(s)

  • Vegetable oils (rich in oleic/linoleic acids) are hydrogenated to margarine by adding hydrogen across double bonds, making the triglyceride solid at room temperature.
  • Classic soap-making boils animal fat with NaOH: the triglyceride saponifies to glycerol plus sodium stearate, which forms micelles to clean greasy grime.

29.5 Acid Chlorides, Anhydrides, and Nucleophilic Acyl Substitution

Key ideas

  • Reactive acyl derivatives (acid chlorides and anhydrides) form from carboxylic acids (e.g., RCOOH + SOCl2 -> RCOCl + SO2 + HCl) and allow selective transformation without affecting other functional groups, a strategy used for protecting groups in complex synthesis.
  • Nucleophilic acyl substitution occurs when Nu- adds to the carbonyl of RCOY (Y = Cl, OR, OCOR), forming a tetrahedral intermediate that collapses to expel Y- and give esters, amides, or acids depending on the nucleophile.
  • Esters react further with strong nucleophiles (e.g., Grignard reagents or hydride) because the intermediate regenerates an electrophilic ketone, so two equivalents are used to reach stable tertiary or primary alcohols.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Acid chloride formation: RCOOH + SOCl2 -> RCOCl + SO2 + HCl (Equation 29.20). Thionyl chloride replaces the hydroxyl with Cl, making a much more electrophilic acyl halide useful for downstream substitutions.
  • Nucleophilic acyl substitution: RCOY + Nu- -> RCONu + Y- (Equation 29.23). The tetrahedral intermediate ejects Y- (a good leaving group) and regenerates a carbonyl with the new nucleophile attached, enabling conversions to amides, esters, anhydrides, etc.
  • Grignard addition to esters: RCOOR' + 2 RMgX -> tertiary alkoxide -> H3O+ -> tertiary alcohol (Equations 29.24–29.25). The first equivalent makes a ketone, which reacts again because it is reactive, so two equivalents are required to avoid mixtures.
  • Hydride reduction: RCOOR' + LiAlH4 -> RCH2OH (via aldehyde intermediate, Equations 29.26–29.27). Controlled hydride delivery yields primary alcohols; esters act as precursors to both aldehydes and alcohols.

Short example(s)

  • Butanoic acid treated with SOCl2 gives butanoyl chloride, which then reacts with ethanol to form ethyl butanoate without affecting other groups (Equations 29.20 and 29.21).
  • Adding excess phenylmagnesium bromide to ethyl benzoate yields a tertiary alcohol after acidic workup (Sample Exercise 29.3), showing how nucleophilic acyl substitution follows through to alcohols.

29.6 Condensation Polymerization

Key ideas

  • Condensation (step-growth) polymerization links difunctional monomers, one bond at a time, releasing small molecules (e.g., water); polyesters come from diacids/diols and polyamides from diacids/diamines.
  • PET (polyethylene terephthalate, Dacron) is a polyester widely used in textiles and medical devices; nylon 6,6 arises from adipic acid and hexamethylenediamine, while nylon 6 derives from caprolactam ring opening and condensation.
  • Copolymers alternate monomers (A-B-A-B) for nylon 6,6, whereas nylon 6 has repeating single monomer units (A-A-A) from the same monomer.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Polyester formation: n dimethyl terephthalate + n ethylene glycol -> PET + 2n H2O (Equation 29.28). Ester links connect repeating units, and removing water drives the polymerization.
  • Polyamide formation (nylon 6,6): n hexamethylenediamine + n adipic acid -> nylon 6,6 + 2n H2O (Equation 29.29). Amide bonds (CONH) join the chains, giving strong, heat-resistant fibers.
  • Ring-opening (nylon 6): e-caprolactam + heat + H2O -> nylon 6. One monomer repeatedly forms amide linkages; the single-digit designation (6) reflects the six-carbon monomer.

Short example(s)

  • Dacron (PET) fibers for heart-valve rings arise from ethylene glycol and dimethyl terephthalate heated to condense water (Equation 29.28), showcasing the biocompatibility of polar oxygen linkages.
  • Nylon 6,6 is formed from adipic acid and hexamethylene diamine (Equation 29.29), giving the 6,6 naming and providing materials for apparel, carpets, and ropes.
