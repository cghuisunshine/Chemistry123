Chapter 23 Tutorial — Transition Metals and Coordination Chemistry

23.1 The Transition Metals

Key ideas

  • Partially filled d orbitals give transition metals variable oxidation states, colorful salts, and catalytic behavior.
  • Period 5 and 6 transition metals mirror one another because of the lanthanide contraction.
  • Paramagnetic, ferromagnetic, ferrimagnetic, and antiferromagnetic behaviors depend on how unpaired d-electron spins align; above TC or TN they become paramagnetic.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Magnetism and unpaired electrons: Fewer unpaired d electrons produce diamagnetism while more lead to paramagnetism, so measuring magnetism reveals the number of unpaired electrons and thus the metal’s electron configuration.
  • Ordering temperatures: TC (ferro/ferrimagnetic) and TN (antiferromagnetic) are the thresholds where magnetic order collapses because thermal energy overcomes spin alignment.

Short example(s)

Iron, cobalt, and nickel are the only pure transition metals that are ferromagnetic at room temperature, so they can be magnetized strongly until the Curie temperature is exceeded.

23.2 Transition-Metal Complexes

Key ideas

  • Coordination compounds contain a metal ion surrounded by ligands (Lewis bases) that donate electron pairs.
  • Coordination numbers (often 4 or 6) dictate tetrahedral, square-planar, or octahedral geometries.
  • Complex formation alters color and redox behavior because coordinating ligands stabilize particular oxidation states.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Complex charge: `q_complex = q_metal + Σ q_ligand`. In [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺ the neutral ammines leave Cu’s +2 charge as the complex charge.
  • Formation constant: `K_f = [complex]/([metal][ligand]^n)`. The much larger K_f for Ni(en)₃²⁺ versus Ni(NH₃)₆²⁺ arises from the chelate effect—chelation releases more solvent and binds tighter.

Short example(s)

[Rh(NH₃)₅Cl](NO₃)₂ contains Rh³⁺ because the neutral ammines add no charge, the coordinated chloride is −1, and the two nitrate counterions contribute −2, so the cation must be 2⁺ to balance.

23.3 Common Ligands in Coordination Chemistry

Key ideas

  • Ligands bond via donor atoms; monodentate ligands donate one pair while bidentate/polydentate (chelating) ligands donate multiple pairs simultaneously.
  • The chelate effect stabilizes metal complexes much more than similar monodentate ligands because entropy increases when fewer chelate ligands form multiple bonds.
  • Natural chelates such as porphyrins hold Fe(II) in hemoglobin and Mg(II) in chlorophyll, enabling oxygen transport and photosynthesis.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Chelate effect data: K_f(Ni(en)₃²⁺) ≈ 6.8 × 10¹⁷ vs. K_f(Ni(NH₃)₆²⁺) ≈ 1.2 × 10⁹ shows that chelating ethylenediamine forms an enormously more stable complex than six ammines.

Short example(s)

Na₂Ca(EDTA) treats lead poisoning because the EDTA chelate is strong enough to solubilize Pb²⁺ so it can be excreted.

23.4 Nomenclature and Isomerism in Coordination Chemistry

Key ideas

  • Names list ligands alphabetically, the metal with its oxidation state, and add `-ate` for anionic complexes, e.g., potassium hexacyanidoferrate(II).
  • Constitutional isomers include linkage isomers (NO₂⁻ vs. ONO⁻) and coordination-sphere isomers where different species occupy the inner sphere.
  • Stereoisomers—geometric (cis/trans) and optical (enantiomers)—have the same bonds but different spatial arrangements with different physical and biological consequences.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Naming rule: `ligands → metal (oxidation state) → counterions`. Example: [Co(NH₃)₅Cl]Cl₂ is pentaamminechloridocobalt(III) chloride; the Cl⁻ anion is named last.

Short example(s)

cis-[Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂] (cisplatin) is an effective anticancer drug because the cis arrangement lets platinum chelate adjacent DNA nitrogens, whereas trans-[Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂] cannot.

23.5 Color and Magnetism in Coordination Chemistry

Key ideas

  • The observed color is the complement of the absorbed wavelength; absorption spectra reveal which visible photons excite d electrons.
  • The number of unpaired d electrons determines whether a complex is paramagnetic or diamagnetic.
  • Changing ligands alters the d-orbital splitting and so shifts both color (λ_max) and magnetism.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Photon energy: `E = hc/λ`. Absorption at 680 nm (red) means the complex appears green because blue/green light is transmitted.

Short example(s)

trans-[Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]²⁺ absorbs red light (~680 nm) so it looks green, while the cis isomer absorbs yellow light and appears violet—different geometries give different spectra.

23.6 Crystal-Field Theory

Key ideas

  • Ligand fields split the metal d orbitals (t₂ and e for octahedral, reversed for tetrahedral) because some orbitals point toward ligands and experience stronger repulsion.
  • Δ (crystal-field splitting energy) competes with spin-pairing energy; when Δ > pairing energy a low-spin configuration results, while Δ < pairing energy gives high-spin.
  • The spectrochemical series orders ligands by increasing Δ (Cl⁻ < H₂O < NH₃ < en < CN⁻), so replacing water with cyanide shortens the absorption λ_max and often converts a paramagnetic complex to a diamagnetic one.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Spin pairing vs. Δ: When Δ > spin-pairing energy, electrons pair in t₂ orbitals → low-spin (e.g., [Co(CN)₆]³⁻); when Δ < pairing energy, they remain unpaired in the e orbitals → high-spin (e.g., [CoF₆]³⁻).
  • Δ from spectroscopy: `Δ = hc/λ_max`. Ti(H₂O)₆³⁺ absorbs 500 nm light, so its Δ matches that photon energy, explaining its purple color.

Short example(s)

[Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ is square planar and diamagnetic because CN⁻ is strong-field, while tetrahedral [NiCl₄]²⁻ is paramagnetic with two unpaired electrons due to weak-field chlorides.
