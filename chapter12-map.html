12.1 Classification of Solids

Bonding types, crystalline vs amorphous, and how X-ray diffraction reveals structure.

metallic, ionic, molecular, network crystalline vs amorphous unit cells & lattices Bragg‚Äôs law

Solids are grouped by how their particles are bonded together, because bonding controls hardness, melting point, conductivity, and mechanical behavior.

  • Metallic solids: metal atoms in a lattice, with valence electrons delocalized in a shared ‚Äúsea.‚Äù This explains high electrical and thermal conductivity, metallic luster, and malleability.
  • Ionic solids: cations and anions held by strong electrostatic attractions. They are hard, have high melting points, and conduct only when molten or dissolved (ions must move).
  • Covalent-network solids: atoms linked in an extended 2D or 3D network of covalent bonds, as in diamond or quartz. These solids are typically very hard and have very high melting points.
  • Molecular solids: discrete molecules held together by intermolecular forces (dispersion, dipole‚Äìdipole, hydrogen bonding). They tend to have lower melting points and are often softer.
  • Polymers: long covalent chains (usually carbon-based) with weaker forces between chains. They are generally tougher and more flexible than small‚Äëmolecule molecular solids.
  • Nanomaterials: conventional materials reduced to particle sizes of about 1‚Äì100 nm. At this scale, surface and quantum effects change properties.

Solids also differ in the arrangement of their particles:

  • Crystalline solids: atoms, ions, or molecules arranged in a repeating pattern in all three dimensions. This regularity leads to flat faces and definite angles, as seen in quartz or salt crystals.
  • Amorphous solids: lack long‚Äërange order. Their local structure resembles a frozen liquid, but particles are locked in place. Glass and many polymers are amorphous.

In crystalline solids, the repeating pattern is captured in a unit cell (the smallest repeating ‚Äúbox‚Äù). Stacking unit cells (like identical bricks) builds up the entire crystal lattice, which is a 3D grid of lattice points. There are only five possible 2D lattice shapes and seven 3D primitive lattices that can tile space without gaps.

Because atomic layers in crystals are spaced about 0.1‚Äì1 nm apart (similar to X-ray wavelengths), crystals diffract X-rays much like a diffraction grating diffracts light. This lets us ‚Äúsee‚Äù inside a crystal by measuring how X-rays scatter.

The key relationship is Bragg‚Äôs law:

nŒª = 2d sinŒ∏
  • n is the diffraction order (1, 2, 3, ‚Ä¶).
  • Œª is the X-ray wavelength.
  • d is the spacing between parallel planes of atoms.
  • Œ∏ is the angle between the X-ray beam and the atomic planes.

Constructive interference (bright spots on the detector) occurs only for angles that satisfy Bragg‚Äôs law. By measuring Œ∏ at known Œª, we can calculate d. From many such distances and intensities, we reconstruct the full arrangement of atoms in the unit cell.

This technique, X-ray crystallography, underlies our knowledge of structures ranging from metals and ceramics to complex biological molecules like proteins and DNA.

Classifying by bonding:

  • NaCl(s): ions in a 3D lattice, high melting point, brittle, conducts when molten ‚Üí ionic solid.
  • Diamond: each C covalently bonded to 4 others in a 3D network, extremely hard ‚Üí covalent-network solid.
  • Solid CO‚ÇÇ (dry ice): discrete CO‚ÇÇ molecules held by dispersion forces, sublimates at low temperatures ‚Üí molecular solid.
  • Copper metal: excellent conductor, malleable, ductile ‚Üí metallic solid.

Crystalline vs amorphous:

  • Quartz: shows flat faces and characteristic angles; X-ray diffraction gives sharp spots ‚Üí crystalline.
  • Glass: lacks sharp faces; X-ray pattern is diffuse ‚Üí amorphous.

Bragg‚Äôs law picture: In an X-ray experiment, a monochromatic beam shines on a single crystal. The outgoing spots on the detector form a pattern that encodes the distances between atomic planes (via nŒª = 2d sinŒ∏) and the symmetry of the crystal. By analyzing many such patterns at different orientations, you can determine the unit-cell size and where the atoms sit inside it.

12.2 Metallic Solids

Metallic bonding, cubic metallic structures, close packing, and density from the unit cell.

‚Äúsea‚Äù of electrons SC, BCC, FCC density from unit cell alloys

In metallic solids, atoms are arranged in a regular lattice and share a ‚Äúsea‚Äù of delocalized valence electrons. The positively charged metal ion cores sit at lattice points, and the electrons move relatively freely throughout the solid.

This bonding picture explains key properties of metals:

  • Electrical conductivity: mobile electrons carry current easily when an electric field is applied.
  • Thermal conductivity: electrons and lattice vibrations both carry heat, so metals feel ‚Äúcold‚Äù to the touch as they rapidly conduct heat away from your hand.
  • Malleability and ductility: layers of metal atoms can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond, allowing metals to be hammered into sheets or drawn into wires.
  • Luster: the sea of electrons can absorb and re-emit photons, giving metals their shiny appearance.

Metals often adopt simple 3D arrangements (cubic lattices), especially simple cubic (rare), body-centered cubic (BCC), and face-centered cubic (FCC). These maximize packing efficiency and help stabilize the metallic bonding.

  • BCC: 8 corners √ó 1/8 + 1 body‚Äëcenter atom = 2 atoms per unit cell.
  • FCC: 8 corners √ó 1/8 + 6 faces √ó 1/2 = 4 atoms per unit cell.

12.3 Ionic Solids

Cation‚Äìanion packing, coordination numbers, common structures, and density calculations.

electrostatic attractions coordination number NaCl, CsCl, ZnS, CaF‚ÇÇ œÅ from ions per unit cell

Ionic solids consist of positively charged cations and negatively charged anions. The arrangement of ions is chosen to maximize attractive forces and minimize repulsive forces while obeying overall charge neutrality.

Key structural ideas:

  • Coordination number (CN): the number of nearest neighbors of opposite charge around an ion. For example, in NaCl each Na‚Å∫ has six Cl‚Åª neighbors (CN = 6), and each Cl‚Åª has six Na‚Å∫ neighbors (also CN = 6).
  • Common coordination patterns include 4:4 (tetrahedral), 6:6 (octahedral), and 8:8 (cubic).
  • The relative sizes of cations and anions (radius ratios) influence which coordination pattern is stable.

Typical properties of ionic solids:

  • High melting points due to strong electrostatic attractions.
  • Hard and brittle; when layers shift so like charges line up, the crystal breaks rather than deforming.
  • Poor electrical conductors as solids, but good conductors when melted or dissolved, because ions are free to move.

Structure types like NaCl, CsCl, ZnS, and CaF‚ÇÇ show different ways cations and anions pack into 3D arrays, all designed to balance charge and optimize attraction.

Counting ions in the unit cell: Just as in metals, ions on corners, edges, and faces are shared among neighboring cells. You sum their contributions to get the total number of cations and anions per unit cell and then check that charge balance is satisfied.

12.4 Covalent Solids

Network covalent structures, graphite vs diamond, and band theory for semiconductors.

network covalent bonding diamond, graphite, Si, SiC band gap n- and p-type doping

Covalent-network solids are built from atoms joined in continuous 2D or 3D covalent-bond networks. Unlike molecular solids, there are no discrete molecules; breaking the solid means breaking covalent bonds.

Important examples:

  • Diamond (C): each carbon is sp¬≥‚Äëhybridized and bonded tetrahedrally to 4 other carbons in 3D. This structure gives diamond extreme hardness and a very high melting point.
  • Quartz (SiO‚ÇÇ): Si atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by O, and each O bridges between two Si atoms. This forms a 3D network responsible for quartz‚Äôs hardness and thermal stability.
  • Silicon (Si): same structure as diamond but with Si atoms. It is a key semiconductor for electronics.
  • Silicon carbide (SiC), boron nitride (BN): network covalent solids with very high thermal stability and hardness.
  • Graphite: composed of layers of hexagonally arranged carbon atoms. Each carbon forms three bonds in a plane (sp¬≤). Strong C‚ÄìC bonds within sheets, weak forces between sheets make graphite soft and a good lubricant.

These solids are often insulators or semiconductors because electrons are localized in covalent bonds or require energy to move into conduction bands.

In solids, individual atomic orbitals combine into bands with many closely spaced energy levels. For valence electrons, two bands are crucial:

  • Valence band: highest occupied (or nearly occupied) band at low temperature.
  • Conduction band: higher‚Äëenergy band where electrons can move more freely and conduct.

The band gap is:

Eg = Econduction bottom ‚àí Evalence top
  • If Eg is very small or bands overlap, electrons can move easily ‚Üí metal.
  • If Eg is large, few electrons can be promoted at moderate temperature ‚Üí insulator.
  • If Eg is modest (~1 eV), some electrons can be thermally excited ‚Üí semiconductor.

For semiconductors, conductivity increases with temperature because more electrons gain enough energy to cross the band gap. A simplified relationship is:

œÉ ‚àù e^(‚àíEg / (2kBT))

Here œÉ is conductivity, kB is Boltzmann‚Äôs constant, and T is temperature.

Doping modifies band structure to create extra charge carriers:

  • n-type: dope with an element having more valence electrons (e.g., P in Si). Extra electrons go into states near the conduction band edge, increasing negative charge carriers.
  • p-type: dope with fewer valence electrons (e.g., B in Si). This creates holes (empty states) in the valence band that act like positive charge carriers.

By combining n‚Äë and p‚Äëtype regions in one crystal, we build diodes, transistors, and other semiconductor devices central to modern electronics.

Diamond vs graphite:

  • In diamond, each C is bonded tetrahedrally to 4 neighbors by strong œÉ bonds. Breaking the crystal requires breaking many strong bonds simultaneously ‚Üí extreme hardness, electrical insulation.
  • In graphite, each C forms 3 bonds in a plane, leaving a delocalized electron system across each sheet. Sheets are held together only by weak forces, so they slide easily ‚Üí soft and flaky. The delocalized electrons along sheets provide electrical conductivity within planes.

n‚Äë and p‚Äëtype silicon:

  • Pure Si has a small band gap. Doping with P (5 valence electrons) introduces extra electrons that can occupy states near the conduction band, making the solid n‚Äëtype.
  • Doping with B (3 valence electrons) leaves ‚Äúholes‚Äù in the valence band. Electrons can hop between these empty states, so the holes act as positive carriers ‚Üí p‚Äëtype.
  • These doped covalent-network solids are the basis of integrated circuits, solar cells, and LEDs.

12.5 Polymers

Monomers, addition vs condensation polymerization, and structure‚Äìproperty relationships.

Polymers are long-chain molecules built from many repeating units called monomers. Typically, the backbone is a carbon chain, with side groups and cross-links controlling properties.

Two major polymerization types:

  • Addition (chain-growth) polymerization: monomers with double bonds (like alkenes) add to one another without loss of atoms. The double bond opens and links monomers end‚Äëto‚Äëend.
  • Condensation (step-growth) polymerization: monomers with two or more functional groups react and small molecules (like H‚ÇÇO or HCl) are eliminated when each new bond forms. Many polyesters and polyamides (nylons) form this way.

Polymer properties depend strongly on chain length, branching, cross-linking, and the nature of the side groups. This is why plastics can range from stretchy and soft to rigid and strong, even when made from similar elements.

  • Polymer: polyethylene, represented by a repeating ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚Äì unit.
  • n is the number of repeating units, often thousands or more.

Nylon 6,6 forms from a diamine and a diacid (hexamethylenediamine and adipic acid). Each linkage creates an amide bond and eliminates water. The repeating unit contains ‚ÄìNH‚Äì(CH‚ÇÇ)‚ÇÜ‚ÄìNH‚Äì and ‚ÄìCO‚Äì(CH‚ÇÇ)‚ÇÑ‚ÄìCO‚Äì segments connected by amide linkages.

  • Branching: branched chains pack less efficiently and form fewer crystalline regions ‚Üí lower density, softer materials (LDPE).
  • Linear chains: pack closely, forming more crystalline regions ‚Üí higher density and stiffness (HDPE).
  • Cross-linking: covalent links between chains restrict motion, making materials harder and more rigid (e.g., vulcanized rubber).

  • Both start from ethene monomers, but LDPE has more branching. The branches prevent chains from aligning and packing tightly, giving a material with density < 0.94 g¬∑cm‚Åª¬≥ that is flexible and used for films and bags.
  • HDPE chains are mostly linear and align to form crystalline regions. This tighter packing yields densities ‚â• 0.94 g¬∑cm‚Åª¬≥ and materials that are stronger and more rigid, suitable for bottles and piping.

These examples illustrate how choice of monomer and polymerization pathway, plus chain architecture, determine the mechanical and chemical behavior of polymer materials.

12.6 Nanomaterials

How shrinking to the nanoscale changes surface area, electronic structure, and properties.

1‚Äì100 nm surface area ‚Üë quantum size effects nanoparticles & quantum dots

Nanomaterials are materials in which at least one dimension of the structural units (particles, grains, or features) lies roughly between 1 and 100 nm (1 nm = 1 √ó 10‚Åª‚Åπ m). At this scale, materials can behave differently from bulk samples of the same composition.

Two main reasons for new behavior:

  • High surface area-to-volume ratio: as particle size decreases, the fraction of atoms at the surface increases dramatically. Surface atoms experience different bonding than atoms in the interior, which affects reactivity, melting point, and mechanical properties.
  • Quantum confinement: for very small particles, electron motion is confined to a tiny region, leading to discrete energy levels and size-dependent band gaps. This is particularly important in semiconductor nanocrystals (quantum dots).

Nanomaterials can be made from metals, semiconductors, oxides, polymers, and more, and are used in fields ranging from medicine to electronics and catalysis.

Surface area scaling: Consider a large cube of material with edge length L, subdivided into smaller cubes with edge length d.

  • Total volume stays L¬≥ regardless of particle size.
  • Number of small cubes: (L/d)¬≥.
  • Surface area of each small cube: 6d¬≤, so total surface area = (L/d)¬≥ ¬∑ 6d¬≤ = 6L¬≥/d.

This shows that as d decreases (smaller particles), total surface area increases like 1/d. Nanoparticles (very small d) thus have enormous surface area per gram, which explains why they are often more reactive and better catalysts.

Quantum size effects in semiconductors: In bulk semiconductors, the band gap Eg is fixed. But in very small crystals (2‚Äì10 nm), electron and hole motion becomes confined, raising the effective band gap. Qualitatively:

smaller particle size ‚Üí larger effective Eg ‚Üí higher-energy (bluer) light emission

This explains why quantum dots of the same material can emit different colors depending solely on their size.

Gold nanoparticles:

  • Bulk gold is yellow and opaque. But gold nanoparticles suspended in a liquid can appear red, purple, or other vivid colors depending on size.
  • This arises from surface plasmon resonances (collective oscillations of conduction electrons at the surface) and quantum effects that shift how the particles absorb and scatter light.

Quantum dot LEDs (QLEDs):

  • CdSe or InP quantum dots are semiconductor nanocrystals that emit light when excited.
  • By controlling particle size, manufacturers tune the emission color: smaller dots emit blue light (larger band gap), whereas larger dots emit red light (smaller band gap).
  • All dots can have the same composition; size alone controls the color, a clear demonstration of quantum confinement in nanomaterials.

These examples show how shrinking materials to the nanoscale opens up new optical and electronic behaviors that bulk materials do not exhibit.
