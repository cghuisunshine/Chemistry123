Chapter 22: Chemistry of the Nonmetals

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Recognize common nonmetallic elements and their typical oxidation states.
  • Describe key oxides, acids, and other compounds of representative nonmetals (for example, C, N, O, S, halogens).
  • Relate nonmetal reactivity to periodic trends and bonding.

Key Vocabulary

  • Allotrope: different structural forms of the same element (for example, O2 vs O3).
  • Oxidizing agent: species that causes oxidation in another and is reduced itself.
  • Nonmetal oxide: often acidic, forming oxyacids with water (for example, SO3 + H2O → H2SO4).

1. Representative Nonmetals

  • Carbon: forms CO, CO2, carbonates, and organic compounds; can be in oxidation states from −4 to +4.
  • Nitrogen: N2 (very stable), NH3, NO, NO2, nitrates; key in fertilizers and environmental chemistry.
  • Oxygen and sulfur: form oxides and oxyacids; important in redox and acid–base chemistry.
  • Halogens: strong oxidizers; form halides with metals and covalent molecules with nonmetals.

2. General Reactivity Patterns

  • Nonmetals often gain electrons to form anions in ionic compounds or share electrons in covalent bonds.
  • Reactivity is linked to electronegativity and ability to stabilize different oxidation states.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. List common oxidation states for C, N, O, S, and Cl and give an example compound for each element.
  2. Write a balanced equation for the reaction of SO3(g) with water and describe the acid formed.
  3. Explain why halogens are strong oxidizing agents and why their oxidizing strength generally decreases down the group.
  4. Compare the bonding and properties of O2 and O3 as allotropes of oxygen.
