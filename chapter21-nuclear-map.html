21.2 Patterns of Nuclear Stability

Stable nuclides cluster in a belt of stability where neutron-to-proton ratios are optimal. Nuclei outside this belt adjust their n/p ratio by Œ≤‚Åª, Œ≤‚Å∫, electron capture, or Œ± decay, and very heavy nuclides often decay in chains until they reach a stable lead isotope.

  • All nuclei with Z ‚â• 84 are radioactive; many decay via Œ± emission to move diagonally toward the belt, reducing both Z and A.
  • Magic numbers of protons (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82) or neutrons (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) correspond to extra stability, analogous to filled electron shells.
  • Nuclei with even numbers of protons and/or neutrons are more likely to be stable than those with both odd, reflecting pairing effects.

21.5 Energy Changes in Nuclear Reactions
Mass‚Äìenergy, binding, fission & fusion

fission & fusion

Nuclear reactions alter nuclear masses, and via E = mc¬≤ even tiny mass defects translate into enormous energy changes. Both fission of heavy nuclei and fusion of light nuclei can release energy due to changes in binding energy per nucleon.

Key ideas
  • The mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of the masses of its separate protons and neutrons; this mass defect corresponds to the nuclear binding energy.
  • The higher the binding energy per nucleon, the more stable the nucleus; a plot of binding energy per nucleon vs mass number peaks near Fe/Ni.
  • Fission of heavy nuclei (e.g., U-235, Pu-239) into intermediate-mass fragments increases binding energy per nucleon and releases energy.
  • Fusion of light nuclei (e.g., H ‚Üí He) also increases binding energy per nucleon and releases energy; this powers stars.
  • Fission can proceed in self-sustaining chain reactions if enough fissile material is present, forming the basis of nuclear reactors and weapons.

21.6 Radiation in the Environment and Living Systems
Ionizing radiation, dose, and risk

Ionizing radiation can ionize water in living tissue, creating reactive radicals that damage biomolecules. Radiation doses, expressed in rad/gray and rem/sievert, quantify absorbed energy and biological impact, helping us gauge risks from medical procedures and environmental exposure.

Key ideas

  • In the body, water absorbs most radiation energy; ionization of water produces highly reactive free radicals like ‚Ä¢OH that attack DNA, proteins, and membranes.
  • Tissues with rapidly dividing cells (bone marrow, lymph nodes) are especially sensitive; radiation can cause cancer by damaging cell growth regulation.
  • Œ±, Œ≤, and Œ≥ rays differ in penetrating power: Œ≥ penetrates deeply and is dangerous from external sources; Œ± is very damaging when inside the body despite low external penetration.
  • Biological effect depends on dose, dose rate, radiation type, and whether the source is external or internal.
