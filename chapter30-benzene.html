Chapter 30: Benzene and its Derivatives

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Recognize benzene rings and simple aromatic compounds.
  • Explain the idea of aromaticity qualitatively (cyclic, planar, conjugated, 4n+2 π electrons).
  • Identify common benzene‑based functional groups (for example, phenols, anilines, toluenes) in names and structures.

Key Vocabulary

  • Aromatic compound: cyclic, planar, conjugated system that follows Hückel’s rule (4n+2 π electrons).
  • Benzene: C6H6, prototypical aromatic ring.
  • Phenyl group: benzene ring minus one H (–C6H5).
  • Phenol: benzene ring with an OH substituent.

1. Structure and Aromaticity

  • Benzene has a ring of sp2 carbons with delocalized π system above and below the ring.
  • Equal bond lengths and resonance structures reflect delocalization.

2. Substituted Benzenes (Conceptual)

  • Common names: toluene (methylbenzene), aniline (aminobenzene), phenol.
  • Disubstituted benzenes can be described as ortho‑, meta‑, or para‑ depending on relative positions (1,2‑; 1,3‑; 1,4‑).

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Sketch benzene using line‑angle notation and indicate where the π electrons are conceptually located.
  2. Explain in words the four criteria for aromaticity and give one example other than benzene.
  3. Given names such as p‑nitrophenol or m‑xylene, describe or sketch the relative positions of substituents on the ring.
