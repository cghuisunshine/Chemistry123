Chapter 30 Tutorial — Benzene and Its Derivatives

30.1 The Structure of Benzene

Key ideas

  • Each carbon in benzene is sp2 hybridized, forming six C-C sigma bonds and six C-H sigma bonds in the plane while leaving one perpendicular 2p orbital per carbon to host the pi electrons.
  • The six unhybridized 2p orbitals overlap to make a continuous pi-cloud; drawing two Kekule structures and the circled hexagon emphasizes that the pi-electrons are delocalized over the ring.
  • Benzene resists reactions that would break its aromaticity (addition) and instead prefers substitutions that retain the delocalized pi-system.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • pi-electron count: Each carbon contributes one electron to the pi system (6 pi-electrons = 4n + 2 with n = 1), showing that benzene satisfies Huckel's rule for aromaticity and is unusually stable.
  • Bond uniformity: The C-C bond length in benzene (~139 pm) is intermediate between single and double bonds, so students can infer that all six bonds share the same strength because the pi-electrons are spread evenly.

Short example(s)

  • The circle-in-a-hexagon drawing replaces alternating double bonds in benzene to stress delocalization, unlike cyclohexene where the double bond is localized and reacts readily with bromine.
  • Hydrogenation of benzene does not proceed like three separate alkene additions, which would release ~348 kJ/mol, but instead only releases −208 kJ/mol, pointing to the extra stability of the aromatic ring.

30.2 Isomerism and Nomenclature in Aromatic Compounds

Key ideas

  • Benzene derivatives are named either with the benzene ring as the parent (e.g., nitrobenzene) or as a substituent phenyl/benzyl group attached to another functional group (e.g., phenylamine or benzyl chloride).
  • Disubstituted benzenes are labeled ortho (1,2-), meta (1,3-), or para (1,4-) when two positions are occupied; higher substitution uses numeric locants with the lowest set of numbers while respecting functional-group precedence.
  • The aromatic core is often used as a building block in polymers (e.g., polystyrene from styrene) and pharmaceuticals (e.g., aspirin and paracetamol contain a benzene core with specific substitution patterns).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Numbering rule: Assign the principal functional group at carbon 1, then number around the ring to give the smallest possible set of locants (e.g., 2-chloro-6-methylphenol), so students understand the systematic approach to aromatic nomenclature.
  • Substituent handles: phenyl- indicates a benzene ring attached to a parent chain, while benzyl- indicates the substituent with a CH2 spacer (C6H5CH2-), which influences reactivity and naming.

Short example(s)

  • Toluene (methylbenzene) can lose a hydrogen to give a phenyl or benzyl substituent; this explains why benzyl chloride (C6H5CH2Cl) is named differently from chlorobenzene (C6H5Cl).
  • The o/m/p notation simplifies the naming of disubstituted benzenes like o-xylene (1,2-dimethylbenzene) while still allowing numeric locants when more than two substituents exist.

30.3 Aromaticity

Key ideas

  • A compound is aromatic if it is cyclic, planar, fully conjugated (each atom provides a p orbital), and contains 4n + 2 pi-electrons; benzene meets all these criteria with n = 1.
  • Aromaticity leads to resonance energy that stabilizes benzene more than a nonaromatic analogue, so the ring avoids addition reactions that would disrupt the pi-cloud.
  • Comparing heats of hydrogenation quantifies aromatic stabilization (benzene releases −208 kJ/mol while three isolated double bonds would release ~−348 kJ/mol), showing students how stability is an experimentally measurable concept.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Huckel's rule: 4n + 2 pi-electrons (for benzene n = 1 gives 6 pi-electrons). This rule predicts when cyclic conjugated systems gain aromatic stabilization and explains why cyclobutadiene (4 pi-electrons) is unstable.
  • Hydrogenation comparison: Cyclohexene hydrogenation gives −120 kJ/mol, 1,4-cyclohexadiene ~−232 kJ/mol, but benzene hydrogenation is only −208 kJ/mol despite appearing to have three double bonds; the 140 kJ/mol difference is the aromatic stabilization energy.

Short example(s)

  • Cyclohexene reacts quickly with bromine, while benzene does not under the same conditions, reflecting that benzene avoids addition to maintain aromaticity.
  • The benzene molecule has equivalent C-C bonds and a circular pi-cloud, so the addition of H2 would break aromaticity and is therefore thermodynamically disfavored compared to hydrogenating isolated alkenes.

30.4 Acidity of Phenols

Key ideas

  • Phenols are more acidic than alcohols because the phenolate ion formed upon deprotonation is resonance stabilized over the aromatic ring, spreading the negative charge.
  • The pKa of phenol (~10) versus cyclohexanol (~16) shows that aromatic conjugate bases are stronger acids; electron-withdrawing substituents further stabilize the phenolate ion and lower the pKa.
  • Phenols can be deprotonated to phenolate salts (e.g., sodium phenoxide) that are soluble in water and useful in synthesis or as intermediates for separation based on acidity differences.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Acidity vs resonance: PhOH + OH- -> PhO- + H2O; the phenolate ion distributes the negative charge across the aromatic ring via resonance contributors, while aliphatic alkoxides cannot delocalize charge and are less stable.
  • pKa comparison: pKa(phenol) ≈ 10, pKa(cyclohexanol) ≈ 16, showing that students can interpret a lower pKa as greater acidity due to stronger conjugate base stabilization.
  • Inductive effects: Electron-withdrawing groups (e.g., NO2) ortho/para to the OH further stabilize the phenolate and lower pKa; electron-donating groups (e.g., OCH3) raise pKa by destabilizing the anion.

Short example(s)

  • Deprotonating phenol with NaOH yields sodium phenoxide, which dissolves in water whereas phenol itself is sparingly soluble; acidifying the solution regenerates phenol.
  • Nitrophenols are stronger acids than phenol, so mixing nitrophenol and cyclohexanol with aqueous NaOH allows selective dissolution of the nitrophenolate salt for separation.

30.5 Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS) Reactions

Key ideas

  • EAS involves two steps: attack of an electrophile to form a Wheland (sigma) complex, then deprotonation to regain aromaticity; the aromatic stabilization is the driving force for substitution instead of addition.
  • Common EAS reactions include halogenation (Cl+, Br+), nitration (NO2+), sulfonation (SO3H+), and Friedel–Crafts alkylation/acylation (R+ or RCO+ with AlCl3), each with typical reagents listed in Table 30.2.
  • Substituent effects govern regioselectivity: electron-donating groups activate and direct to ortho/para, halogens are deactivating yet ortho/para-directing, and electron-withdrawing groups deactivate and direct to the meta position.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • General mechanism: ArH + E+ -> [Ar-E-H]+ (Wheland) -> ArE + H+. The first slow step forms a resonance-stabilized carbocation; the second removes H+ so aromaticity returns, explaining why addition reactions are avoided.
  • Directing effects: EDG (e.g., NH2, OH, OR) -> ortho/para, EWG (e.g., NO2, SO3H, CF3) -> meta, while halogens -> ortho/para but slow due to inductive deactivation. This explains how the substituent's electron-pushing/pulling behavior tunes both rate and site of attack.
  • Reagent examples (Table 30.2): Nitration: HNO3/H2SO4 -> NO2+, Halogenation: Cl2/FeCl3 -> Cl+, Sulfonation: SO3/H2SO4 -> SO3H+, Friedel–Crafts: RCl/AlCl3 -> R+ (or RCOCl/AlCl3 -> RCO+). These formulas help students connect reagents to electrophiles.

Short example(s)

  • Toluene nitrates faster than benzene because the methyl group donates electron density, activating the ring and favoring ortho/para substitution; nitrobenzene nitrates much slower because NO2 withdraws electrons and destabilizes the sigma complex.
  • Halogenating bromobenzene still gives ortho/para products even though bromine is deactivating; its resonance donation overrides the inductive pull in directing the electrophile.
