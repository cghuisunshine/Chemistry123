1.1 The Study of Chemistry
What chemistry is about and why it is central to science.
Roots: Matter, atoms, and the three realms.

Chemistry is described as the ‚Äúcentral science‚Äù because chemical principles underpin biology, medicine, environmental science, materials science, and energy technology.

  • Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. Air, water, metals, and your body are all matter.
  • A property is any characteristic that helps us recognize a type of matter and distinguish it from others (for example, density, boiling point, flammability).
  • An element is a substance that cannot be decomposed into simpler substances by chemical means. Each element is made of one kind of atom.
  • An atom is an almost unimaginably small building block of matter; different elements have different kinds of atoms.
  • A molecule is a collection of two or more atoms held together in a specific arrangement. Composition and shape control properties.

Chemistry constantly connects three realms:

  • Macroscopic: everyday, visible substances and changes (solutions, flames, rust).
  • Microscopic: atoms, ions, and molecules we imagine and model.
  • Symbolic: chemical formulas, equations, and diagrams we use to represent matter and its changes.

Chemists broadly do three things: they make new substances, measure properties, and develop models or theories to explain and predict behavior.

Although there are no algebraic formulas here, precise definitions guide how you interpret problems:

  • Chemistry is the science that seeks to understand the properties and behavior of matter by studying atoms and molecules.
  • All the complex behavior of matter (color, reactivity, toxicity, energy changes) ultimately comes from the types of atoms present and how they are arranged in molecules and materials.
  • Whenever you see a macroscopic change (for example, iron rusting), imagine what must be happening to atoms and molecules at the microscopic level and represent it symbolically with formulas and equations.

Interpreting chemistry problems effectively means moving back and forth between these three levels.

Example: ethanol vs ethylene glycol.

  • Both molecules contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, but they differ in composition and structure.
  • Ethanol is safe to drink in moderate amounts and is used in beverages; ethylene glycol is used as antifreeze and is toxic.
  • Small differences in how atoms are connected can produce enormous differences in physical and biological properties.

Why this matters to you: Chemistry is behind medicines, batteries, fertilizers, foods, cleaning products, plastics, and air and water quality. Understanding basic chemistry helps you interpret scientific claims and make informed choices.

Study tip: When you read a new concept, ask yourself: ‚ÄúWhat is the macroscopic example? What does it look like microscopically? How would I write it symbolically?‚Äù

1.2 Classifications of Matter
States of matter and how we classify substances and mixtures.
Roots: States, elements, compounds, and mixtures.

Matter is first classified by physical state and then by composition.

  • Gas (vapor): no fixed volume or shape; expands to fill its container; highly compressible. Microscopically, particles are far apart and move rapidly.
  • Liquid: fixed volume, but takes the shape of its container; relatively incompressible. Particles are close together but can slide past one another.
  • Solid: fixed shape and volume; essentially incompressible. Particles are tightly packed in fixed positions and only vibrate.

By composition, matter is classified as:

  • Pure substances: have fixed composition and distinct properties.
    • Elements: cannot be chemically decomposed; made of one kind of atom.
    • Compounds: two or more elements chemically combined in fixed ratios.
  • Mixtures: physical combinations of substances with variable composition; each component retains its identity.
    • Heterogeneous mixtures: non-uniform (for example, granite, salad, sandy water).
    • Homogeneous mixtures (solutions): uniform throughout (for example, air, saltwater, brass).

The law of constant composition states that a given compound always has the same elemental composition by mass.

Law of constant composition (law of definite proportions):

If a substance is truly a pure compound, then every sample, regardless of source or amount, has the same mass ratio of elements because each molecule contains the same numbers of each type of atom.

Conceptually:

  • Every H‚ÇÇO molecule has 2 H atoms and 1 O atom.
  • Because H atoms are much lighter than O atoms, water is ~11% H and ~89% O by mass in every pure sample.

Classification tree for matter: start with ‚Äúmatter‚Äù ‚Üí ask if composition is uniform ‚Üí ask if composition can vary ‚Üí decide whether you have an element, compound, or mixture. This logical tree is a mental ‚Äúflowchart‚Äù that you can redraw quickly on exams.

Example: Is water a compound?

  • Electrolysis of pure water produces hydrogen gas and oxygen gas in a fixed volume ratio of 2:1 and mass ratio of about 1:8.
  • The constant mass ratio and reproducible decomposition products show that water is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen, not a mixture.

Example: White gold.

  • White gold is made by mixing gold with a ‚Äúwhite‚Äù metal like palladium. Different samples have different amounts of the two metals but look uniform.
  • Because the composition can vary but each sample is uniform, white gold is a homogeneous mixture, not a compound.

Tip: If you can vary the composition smoothly (a little more of one component, a little less of another) and still have a single phase, you almost certainly have a mixture, not a pure compound.

1.3 Properties of Matter
Physical vs chemical properties, intensive vs extensive, and separation methods.
Roots: What properties tell us and how we use them.

Physical properties can be observed without changing a substance‚Äôs identity.

  • Examples: color, odor, density, melting point, boiling point, hardness, electrical conductivity, state.

Chemical properties describe how a substance may react to form new substances.

  • Examples: flammability, tendency to rust, reactivity with acids or bases, toxicity.

Physical changes alter appearance or state, not composition (melting ice, boiling water, dissolving sugar).

Chemical changes (reactions) transform substances into chemically different substances (burning, rusting, reacting metals with acids).

Two useful categories:

  • Intensive properties: independent of sample size (density, temperature, boiling point).
  • Extensive properties: depend on how much matter is present (mass, volume, total energy).

Differences in physical and chemical properties allow us to separate components of mixtures.

Filtration uses differences in particle size and phase:

  • A mixture of a solid and liquid is poured through filter paper.
  • The liquid passes through; the solid is trapped on the paper.

Distillation uses differences in boiling point:

  • When a solution of salt in water is boiled, water vaporizes first because it has a lower boiling point.
  • The vapor is condensed back to liquid in a separate container, leaving the nonvolatile salt behind.

Chromatography uses differences in how strongly substances adhere to a solid vs move with a liquid or gas:

  • A mixture is dissolved in a solvent and passed through a column (or over paper) containing a solid.
  • Components that interact strongly with the solid move slowly; those that prefer the solvent move faster, leading to separation.

These are physical separation methods; the chemical identities of the components are unchanged.

Example: boiling water vs burning fuel.

  • Boiling water: liquid water ‚Üí water vapor; H‚ÇÇO molecules remain unchanged ‚Üí physical change.
  • Burning propane: C‚ÇÉH‚Çà reacts with O‚ÇÇ to form CO‚ÇÇ and H‚ÇÇO; new substances form ‚Üí chemical change.

Example: separating sand and salt.

  1. Add water: salt dissolves, sand does not.
  2. Filter: sand is trapped on filter paper; saltwater passes through.
  3. Evaporate water: salt crystals remain.

Tip: When deciding if a process is physical or chemical, ask: ‚ÄúAre the molecules themselves the same before and after?‚Äù If yes, it‚Äôs physical; if no, it‚Äôs chemical.

1.4 The Nature of Energy
Energy as work and heat, and its kinetic and potential forms.
Trunk: Work, kinetic energy, and units.

Energy is defined as the capacity to do work or transfer heat.

  • Work (w) is energy transferred when a force causes an object to move.
  • Heat (q) is energy transferred that results in a temperature change.

On the molecular level:

  • Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. Atoms and molecules have kinetic energy because they move; heating a substance increases their average speed.
  • Potential energy is stored energy due to position or composition. Chemical bonds and interactions between charges store potential energy.

In chemistry, electrical forces between charged particles dominate potential energy changes. Electrostatic potential energy arises from the attraction between opposite charges and repulsion between like charges.

Energy is conserved: it can change form (for example, from chemical to thermal) but cannot be created or destroyed.

Here w is work (J), F is force (N), and d is distance moved in the direction of the force (m).

1.5 Units of Measurement
SI base units, metric prefixes, temperature scales, and density.
Trunk: Units that make chemistry quantitative.

Chemistry relies on the SI (International System of Units) and metric prefixes to express measurements clearly.

  • Base units used in this chapter:

  • Mass vs weight:

1.6 Uncertainty in Measurement
Exact vs inexact numbers, precision, accuracy, and significant figures.
Roots: How we report and trust measurements.

In scientific work, we distinguish between exact and inexact numbers:

  • Exact numbers have no uncertainty (defined values like 1 kg = 1000 g, or counted quantities like 12 eggs).
  • Inexact numbers arise from measurements; all measurements carry some uncertainty.

Precision measures how closely repeated measurements agree with each other. Accuracy measures how close a measurement is to the true value.

You can have:

  • Good precision and good accuracy (ideal).
  • Good precision but poor accuracy (systematic error, for example, mis-calibrated instrument).
  • Poor precision and poor accuracy (random errors and poor technique).

The number of significant figures in a measured value reports how many digits are meaningful (certain digits plus one estimated digit).

Counting significant figures:

  • All nonzero digits are significant (7.35 has 3 sig figs).
  • Zeros between nonzero digits are significant (1005 has 4 sig figs).
  • Leading zeros are not significant (0.0026 has 2 sig figs).
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant (2.300 has 4 sig figs).
  • Trailing zeros in numbers without a decimal point are ambiguous (3000 may have 1‚Äì4 sig figs); use scientific notation to make intent clear (3.00 √ó 10¬≥ has 3 sig figs).

Significant figures in calculations:

  • Addition and subtraction: the result should have the same number of decimal places as the quantity with the fewest decimal places.
  • Multiplication and division: the result should have the same number of significant figures as the quantity with the fewest significant figures.

These rules ensure that you do not imply more precision in your answer than your measurements justify.

Example: reading a thermometer.

  • If the mercury column lies between the 25 ¬∞C and 30 ¬∞C marks, you might read it as 27 ¬∞C.
  • ‚Äú27‚Äù means 2 and 7 are significant; the ‚Äú7‚Äù is estimated. Writing 27.0 ¬∞C suggests you can estimate to tenths of a degree, which may not be justified.

Example: significant figures in a calculation.

  • Compute (12.53 g + 1.8 g) / 0.055 g.
  • Addition: 12.53 + 1.8 = 14.33 ‚Üí round to 14.3 g (1 decimal place).
  • Division: 14.3 g / 0.055 g ‚âà 260 (calculator gives ~259.1).
  • 14.3 has 3 sig figs; 0.055 has 2 sig figs ‚Üí answer should have 2 sig figs ‚Üí 2.6 √ó 10¬≤.

Common pitfalls:

  • Dropping units and focusing only on numbers.
  • Keeping too few significant figures early in a long calculation (round only at the end).
  • Reporting answers with more digits than your data support (overstating accuracy).
1.7 Dimensional Analysis
Using units systematically to convert and solve problems.
Roots: Units as algebra and conversion factors.

Dimensional analysis, also called the factor-label method, treats units like algebraic symbols that can cancel or combine.

A conversion factor is a fraction whose numerator and denominator are the same physical quantity in different units. Because the two forms are equal, the fraction is equal to 1 physically, even though it changes units.

Basic strategy for solving most numeric chemistry problems:

  1. Write down the starting (given) quantity with its unit.
  2. Multiply by one or more conversion factors so unwanted units cancel and the desired units remain.
  3. Check that units are correct and that the magnitude of the answer is reasonable.

This approach keeps track of both numbers and units and helps prevent common mistakes.

quantitydesired = quantitygiven √ó (desired unit / given unit)

The conversion factor (desired unit / given unit) is chosen so that the ‚Äúgiven unit‚Äù in the denominator cancels the given quantity‚Äôs unit.

Each factor changes units one step at a time. The units cancel like algebraic factors until only the target unit remains.

Dimensional analysis also serves as a check: if the final units are wrong, something is wrong with your setup.

  • Given: mass = 115 lb; conversion factor: 1 lb = 453.6 g.
  • Setup: 115 lb √ó (453.6 g / 1 lb) = 5.22 √ó 10‚Å¥ g.
  • Units: ‚Äúlb‚Äù cancels; answer is in grams.
