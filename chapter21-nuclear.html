Chapter 21: Nuclear Chemistry

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Classify common types of radioactive decay (α, β, γ) and write nuclear equations.
  • Use half‑life relationships to describe radioactive decay.
  • Distinguish nuclear fission and fusion conceptually.

Key Vocabulary

  • Radioactivity: spontaneous emission of particles or radiation from unstable nuclei.
  • Alpha (α) decay: emission of a He‑4 nucleus.
  • Beta (β) decay: emission of an electron (β−) or positron (β+); often changes Z by ±1.
  • Gamma (γ) emission: emission of high‑energy photons; nucleus goes to lower energy state.
  • Half‑life (t1/2): time for half the nuclei in a sample to decay.
  • Nuclear fission: splitting of heavy nucleus into smaller fragments with energy release.
  • Nuclear fusion: combining light nuclei into heavier ones with energy release.

1. Nuclear Equations and Decay Types

  • Balance both mass number (A) and atomic number (Z) in nuclear equations.
  • Recognize typical patterns: α emission decreases A by 4 and Z by 2; β− increases Z by 1.

2. Half‑Life and Decay

  • For a first‑order decay process: N_t = N_0(1/2)^{t/t_{1/2}}.
  • Activity and amount decrease exponentially with time.

3. Fission and Fusion (Qualitative)

  • Fission is used in current nuclear power plants; controlled chain reactions in reactors.
  • Fusion powers the sun; challenging to implement on Earth due to high temperature requirements.

4. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Write a balanced nuclear equation for α decay of ^{238}_{92}U.
  2. Write a balanced nuclear equation for β− decay of ^{14}_{6}C.
  3. A radioisotope has half‑life 5.0 days. What fraction of the original sample remains after 20.0 days?
  4. Explain in basic terms why both fission of heavy nuclei and fusion of light nuclei can release energy (hint: binding energy per nucleon).
