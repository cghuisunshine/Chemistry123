Chapter 21: Nuclear Chemistry

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Classify common types of radioactive decay (α, β, γ) and write simple balanced nuclear equations.
  • Use half‑life relationships to describe radioactive decay quantitatively at a basic level.
  • Distinguish nuclear fission and fusion conceptually and relate them to binding energy.

Key Vocabulary

  • Radioactivity: spontaneous emission of particles or radiation from unstable nuclei.
  • Alpha (α) decay: emission of a He‑4 nucleus (^4_2He or α).
  • Beta (β) decay: emission of an electron (β− = ^0_{−1}e) or positron (β+).
  • Gamma (γ) emission: emission of high‑energy photons; nucleus moves to lower energy state without changing Z or A.
  • Half‑life (t1/2): time for half the nuclei in a sample to decay.
  • Nuclear fission: splitting of heavy nucleus into smaller fragments with energy release.
  • Nuclear fusion: combining light nuclei into heavier ones with energy release.

1. Nuclear Equations and Decay Types

  • Balance both mass number (A) and atomic number (Z) in nuclear equations.
  • α emission: A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2.
  • β− emission: a neutron converts to a proton + electron; Z increases by 1, A unchanged.

Worked Example 1 – Alpha Decay of U‑238

Write a balanced nuclear equation for α decay of ^{238}_{92}U.

  1. α particle is ^4_2He.
  2. Mass number: 238 = Adaughter + 4 → Adaughter = 234.
  3. Atomic number: 92 = Zdaughter + 2 → Zdaughter = 90 (thorium).
  4. Equation: ^{238}_{92}U → ^{234}_{90}Th + ^4_2He.

2. Half‑Life and Decay

For a first‑order decay process:

N_t = N_0\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{t/t_{1/2}}

  • N0 = initial amount; Nt = amount remaining after time t.
  • Each additional half‑life reduces the amount by another factor of 1/2.

Worked Example 2 – Fraction Remaining

A radioisotope has half‑life 5.0 days. What fraction of the original sample remains after 20.0 days?

  1. Number of half‑lives: t/t1/2 = 20.0 / 5.0 = 4.
  2. Fraction remaining: (1/2)4 = 1/16 = 0.0625 (6.25%).

3. Fission and Fusion (Qualitative)

  • Fission: heavy nuclei (like U‑235) can undergo chain reactions, releasing large amounts of energy; used in nuclear power plants and weapons.
  • Fusion: light nuclei (like H isotopes) combine to form heavier nuclei (like He); powers the sun and stars; practical controlled fusion on Earth is still under development.
  • Both processes involve changes in nuclear binding energy per nucleon; products are often more tightly bound than reactants → energy release.
