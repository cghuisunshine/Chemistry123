Chapter 18 Tutorial — Chemistry of the Environment

18.1 Earth’s Atmosphere

Key ideas

  • The atmosphere is layered by temperature (troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere) and contains the mass and chemistry that support life.
  • Pressure drops exponentially with height, and high-energy solar radiation drives photochemistry in the upper layers.
  • Trace gases absorb UV, and their photodissociation (e.g., CFCs) releases reactive atoms that catalytically destroy ozone.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Photodissociation: CF₂Cl₂ + hν → CF₂Cl + Cl (Eq. 18.6) creates Cl radicals that destroy ozone via Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂ and regenerating Cl.
  • Ozone formation: O + O₂ + M → O₃ + M* (Eq. 18.14) and O₂ + hν → 2 O balance ozone destruction; therefore small halogen amounts rapidly deplete ozone.

Short example(s)

The Antarctic ozone hole arises when CFCs reach the stratosphere, photolyze, and release Cl that destroys ozone faster than it reforms (Eq. 18.10).

18.2 Human Activities and Earth’s Atmosphere

Key ideas

  • Sulfur and nitrogen oxides from fossil-fuel combustion and high-temperature engines cause acid rain and photochemical smog.
  • SO₂ oxidizes to SO₃ and forms H₂SO₄ in rain, acidifying lakes, dissolving CaCO₃, and corroding structures.
  • Nitrogen oxides generate NO₂ that photodissociates under sunlight, leading to ozone-rich smog.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Acid rain: SO₂ + ½O₂ → SO₃ then SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ lowers rain pH from ~5.6 to ~4.
  • Smog mechanism: NO₂ + hν → NO + O followed by O + O₂ + M → O₃ + M* (Eqs. 18.13–18.14).

Short example(s)

Pulverized limestone in power plants (CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂; CaO + SO₂ → CaSO₃) removes SO₂ before exhaust (Figure 18.8).

18.3 Earth’s Water

Key ideas

  • The global water cycle cycles water through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, and return, redistributing heat.
  • Seawater accounts for 97.2% of Earth’s water; salinity averages 35‰, dominated by Na⁺, Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻, Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺.
  • CO₂ dissolves into oceans forming H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻/CO₃²⁻ buffers; rising CO₂ lowers pH (ocean acidification), reducing carbonate for shells.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Carbonate buffering: CO₃²⁻ + H⁺ ⇌ HCO₃⁻. Increased CO₂ pushes equilibrium right, decreasing [CO₃²⁻] and dissolving CaCO₃ shells.
  • Salinity definition: grams of dissolved salt per kg seawater (~35 g/kg), used to compute ionic concentrations (Table 18.5).

Short example(s)

Seawater pH ~8.2, but absorbing anthropogenic CO₂ lowers it toward 7.9, threatening corals whose CaCO₃ skeletons dissolve when carbonate decreases.

18.4 Human Activities and Water Quality

Key ideas

  • Municipal water treatment includes screening, coagulation (Al₂(SO₄)₃), filtration, aeration, and disinfection (chlorine/ozone) to remove particles and microbes (Fig. 18.19).
  • Disinfection creates halogenated by-products (THMs, bromate) from reaction of HClO with organics/bromide; total THMs are limited to 80 ppb.
  • Contamination from sewage, nutrient runoff, and fracking wastes decreases dissolved oxygen and spreads pollutants; reverse osmosis and LifeStraw-type purification filter and disinfect water.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Chlorination: Cl₂ + H₂O → HClO + H⁺ + Cl⁻ (Eq. 18.15), with HClO forming THMs and, via HClO + Br⁻ → HBrO + Cl⁻ (Eq. 18.16), bromate.
  • Ozone vs bromide: O₃ + Br⁻ + H₂O → HBrO + O₂ + OH⁻ (Eq. 18.17) and HBrO + 2 O₃ → BrO₃⁻ + 2 O₂ + H⁺ (Eq. 18.18) showing ozonation can form carcinogenic bromate.

Short example(s)

Reverse osmosis forces seawater through semipermeable hollow fibers under pressure, leaving pure water while rejecting salts (Fig. 18.18).

18.5 Green Chemistry

Key ideas

  • Green chemistry’s 12 principles promote waste prevention, energy efficiency, safer chemicals, renewable feedstocks, catalysis, and degradability.
  • New processes (styrene from toluene + methanol; atom-economical hydroquinone; click reactions) showcase energy savings, reduced by-products, and recyclable reagents.
  • Supercritical fluids (CO₂, H₂O) replace volatile organic solvents; coupling catalysts and renewable feedstocks improves sustainability.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Atom economy: (mass of desired product)/(mass of reactants) × 100%; high values (close to 100%) indicate efficient resource usage.
  • Catalysis: Lower activation energy E_a raises rate constant k via the Arrhenius equation `k = A e^{-E_a/(RT)}` with less heat input.

Short example(s)

Styrene production via toluene + methanol (425 °C, base catalyst) reduces benzene/ethylene feedstocks, recycles H₂, and can use biomass-derived methanol.
