Chapter 5: Thermochemistry

5.1 The Nature of Chemical Energy

Key ideas

  • Chemical energy is stored as electrostatic potential energy between charged particles and is released or absorbed when bonds break or form.
  • Heat and work are the two observable ways chemical systems exchange energy with the surroundings.
  • Processes that move atoms apart or together alter the electrostatic potential energy, which is why bonded structures are sources of stored energy.

Important formulas & their meaning

Mechanical work w = F * d

Work equals the force F applied over a displacement d. If you push a cart with 50 N for 5 m, you transfer 250 J of energy into the cart; the system gains energy when work is done on it (positive w).

Electrostatic potential energy Eel = k * Q1 * Q2 / d

The energy between two charges depends on their magnitudes Q1, Q2, their separation d, and Coulomb’s constant k ≈ 8.99 × 10⁹ J·m/C². Like charges have positive Eel, opposite charges negative, so bonding lowers energy and releases heat.

Short example(s)

Bringing Na⁺ and Cl⁻ together drops their electrostatic potential energy, which is released as heat when NaCl forms. This stored chemical potential explains why combustion and other bond-making reactions can power engines and metabolism.

5.2 The First Law of Thermodynamics

Key ideas

  • The first law states that the change in a system’s internal energy equals the heat added plus the work done on the system: energy is conserved.
  • Systems can exchange energy with the surroundings as heat (q) or mechanical work (w), while matter exchange depends on whether the system is open, closed, or isolated.
  • Energy changes are measured for the system, but every gain for the system equals a loss for the surroundings and vice versa.

Important formulas & their meaning

First law ΔE = q + w

The internal energy change ΔE equals the sum of heat transferred q and work w. Both q and w are positive when energy flows into the system; negative if energy flows out. For example, if a reaction loses 1150 J of heat and the surroundings do 480 J of work on it, then ΔE = -1150 + 480 = -670 J.

Work for constant pressure w = -P ΔV

When gases expand against a constant external pressure P, the system performs work on the surroundings. Positive ΔV gives negative work, reducing the system’s energy.

Short example(s)

In a closed piston, burning H₂ and O₂ pushes the piston upward (ΔV positive). The gases do work on the environment, giving w = -PΔV, so the internal energy change combines the negative work and the heat released by the exothermic reaction.

5.3 Enthalpy

Key ideas

  • Enthalpy H = E + PV is a state function that keeps track of internal energy plus the PV work term, making it especially useful at constant pressure.
  • At atmospheric pressure, ∆H equals the heat exchanged (qP), simplifying calorimetry for many chemical reactions.
  • In endothermic processes ∆H is positive (heat absorbed); in exothermic processes ∆H is negative (heat released).

Important formulas & their meaning

Enthalpy definition H = E + PV

Enthalpy sums a system’s internal energy E and the product of its pressure and volume. At constant pressure, changes in H reflect heat flow because ΔH = ΔE + PΔV.

ΔH at constant pressure ΔH = qP

When pressure is fixed, the enthalpy change equals the heat transferred. Measuring the temperature change in a calorimeter lets us compute ΔH directly.

Short example(s)

Combustion of methane at constant atmospheric pressure releases heat so ΔH is negative and equals the heat flowing out of the system; this heat warms the surroundings and gives a clear enthalpy diagram with reactants above the products.

5.4 Enthalpies of Reaction

Key ideas

  • The enthalpy change of a reaction depends on the reactants, products, and their physical states, and is often reported per mole of a specified substance.
  • Balanced chemical equations can include an associated ΔH value to report whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic
  • Enthalpy diagrams visualize the relative energies of reactants and products, showing whether ΔH is positive or negative.

Important formulas & their meaning

Enthalpy of reaction ΔH = Hproducts - Hreactants

This difference gives the heat released or absorbed; the sign tells you whether the system loses energy (negative ΔH, exothermic) or gains energy (positive ΔH, endothermic).

Short example(s)

If the enthalpy of the products is lower than that of the reactants, ΔH is negative and heat exits the system. The combustion of C + O₂ → CO₂ has ΔH° = -393.5 kJ/mol, so energy flows to the surroundings.

5.5 Calorimetry

Key ideas

  • Calorimetry tracks the temperature change of a known mass to quantify heat flow because the heat absorbed/released equals the mass × specific heat × temperature change.
  • Bomb calorimeters hold constant volume, so the heat measured relates to ΔE; coffee-cup calorimeters operate at constant pressure and yield ΔH.
  • Heat absorbed by the calorimeter and solution are accounted for to determine reaction enthalpies.

Important formulas & their meaning

Heat q = Cs * m * ΔT

Multiply the specific heat Cs, mass m, and temperature change ΔT to calculate heat exchanged. In differential calorimetry, add together the heats for each component to find the reaction heat.

Short example(s)

Heating 25.0 g of water (Cs = 4.18 J/g·K) from 21.0 °C to 27.5 °C absorbs q = 4.18 × 25.0 × 6.5 ≈ 680 J, so a reaction that cools the water by 6.5 °C releases that same amount of heat to the surroundings.

5.6 Hess’s Law

Key ideas

  • Enthalpy is a state function, so the enthalpy change for a given reaction is path independent; you can add or reverse individual steps to obtain the target reaction.
  • Hess’s law lets you calculate ΔH for complex reactions by combining known ΔH values for simpler reactions.
  • Manipulating equations (reversing, multiplying, canceling) carries their ΔH values along with the same operations.

Important formulas & their meaning

Hess’s law ΔHtarget = Σ ΔHsteps

Add the enthalpy changes of the intermediate reactions that sum to the overall reaction. Reversing a step flips the sign of its ΔH; multiplying by a factor multiplies ΔH.

Short example(s)

To find the enthalpy for CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O, add ΔH of CH₄ combustion to CO₂ formation and subtract the ΔH of water condensation if you split the process into known steps.

5.7 Enthalpies of Formation

Key ideas

  • The standard enthalpy of formation ΔHf⁰ is the enthalpy change when one mole of a compound forms from its elements in their standard states.
  • Standard enthalpies (with the superscript °) use 1 atm pressure and 298 K temperature.
  • Using tabulated ΔHf⁰ values makes it easy to compute reaction enthalpies for systems that are hard to study directly.

Important formulas & their meaning

Standard reaction enthalpy ΔH° = Σ n ΔHf⁰(products) - Σ n ΔHf⁰(reactants)

The sum over products minus the sum over reactants gives ΔH° for the reaction. Multiply each ΔHf⁰ by its coefficient n in the balanced equation.

Short example(s)

The standard enthalpy of formation for water vapor is ΔHf⁰ = -241.8 kJ/mol. To compute the combustion enthalpy of H₂, combine ΔHf⁰ of H₂O (products) and subtract those of the elements (zeros) to get ΔH° = -286 kJ/mol (approx).

5.8 Bond Enthalpies

Key ideas

  • Average bond enthalpies quantify the energy required to homolytically break a bond in a gaseous molecule.
  • The reaction enthalpy approximated by bond enthalpies equals the energy cost to break bonds minus the energy released forming new bonds.
  • Stronger bonds have higher bond enthalpies; multiple bonds release more energy than single bonds when formed.

Important formulas & their meaning

Bond enthalpy estimate ΔHrxn ≈ Σ D(bonds broken) - Σ D(bonds formed)

Breaking bonds consumes energy (positive contributions), forming bonds releases energy. Use average bond enthalpies for the specific bonds in reactants and products.

Short example(s)

Breaking four C–H bonds in CH₄ costs about 4 × 413 = 1652 kJ, and forming the appropriate bonds in products gives another total; their difference approximates ΔH of the reaction.

5.9 Foods and Fuels

Key ideas

  • Chemical energy in fuels and foods comes from combustion that converts C–H and C–C bonds to CO₂ and H₂O, releasing large negative ΔH.
  • Fuel value is the energy released per gram or per mole when a substance combusts; fats, carbohydrates, and proteins have different caloric densities.
  • Food labels report kilocalories, where 1 Calorie (capital C) = 4184 J, so monitoring intake relates to the enthalpy of digestion (metabolism) of glucose or fat.

Important formulas & their meaning

Fuel value Fuel value ≈ ΔHcombustion per gram

Combustion enthalpy per gram gives the energy density of fuels or foods. For example, fats release ~38 kJ/g while carbohydrates ~17 kJ/g; these normalized values guide dietary planning and energy engineering.

Short example(s)

Glucose combustion (C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ → 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O) liberates about 2800 kJ/mol. Eating one mole of glucose thus releases roughly the same energy as burning that mole in a calorimeter, explaining why blood glucose feeds cells with chemical energy.
