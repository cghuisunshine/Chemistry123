Chapter 5: Thermochemistry

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish system vs. surroundings, and heat vs. work.
  • Use the first law of thermodynamics (ΔE = q + w).
  • Define enthalpy and solve basic enthalpy problems.
  • Use calorimetry data (coffee-cup and bomb calorimetry).
  • Apply Hess’s law and standard enthalpies of formation.

Key Terms

  • System: the part of the universe you you are studying (for example, chemicals in a beaker).
  • Surroundings: everything else (the beaker, lab air, etc.).
  • Open / Closed / Isolated system: describes whether matter and/or energy can be exchanged with surroundings.
  • Energy (E): capacity to do work or transfer heat.
  • Kinetic energy (Ek): energy of motion.
  • Potential energy (Ep): energy due to position or composition.
  • Heat (q): energy transfer due to temperature difference.
  • Work (w): energy transfer when a force moves an object (for example, gas expansion).
  • Internal energy (E): total energy of a system’s particles.
  • State function: depends only on initial and final states (ΔE, ΔH); path-independent.
  • Enthalpy (H): heat content at constant pressure; ΔH = q_p.
  • Exothermic: releases heat (ΔH < 0).
  • Endothermic: absorbs heat (ΔH > 0).
  • Calorimetry: experimental measurement of heat changes.
  • Heat capacity (C): heat needed to change temperature of an object by 1 K.
  • Specific heat (c): heat needed to change 1 g of a substance by 1 K.
  • Standard enthalpy of formation (ΔH°f): enthalpy change for forming 1 mol of a compound from its elements in their standard states.

1. Energy, Heat, Work, and the First Law

Sign Conventions (Chemistry)

  • q > 0: heat flows into the system (endothermic).
  • q < 0: heat flows out of the system (exothermic).
  • w > 0: work done on the system.
  • w < 0: work done by the system.

First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy is conserved:

ΔE = q + w

If the system loses energy, the surroundings gain it, and vice versa.

Pressure–Volume Work

For gas expansion or compression at constant external pressure:

w = -P ΔV

with P in atm and ΔV in L (convert to joules using 1 L·atm ≈ 101.3 J).

2. Enthalpy (ΔH) and Types of Processes

Enthalpy is defined as H = E + PV.

At constant pressure, the heat flow equals the change in enthalpy:

ΔH = q_p

  • Exothermic (ΔH < 0): system releases heat; surroundings warm up.
  • Endothermic (ΔH > 0): system absorbs heat; surroundings cool down.

Properties of ΔH

  • ΔH is a state function.
  • Reversing a process changes the sign of ΔH.
  • ΔH depends on the amount of substance (it is an extensive property).

Example: Sign of ΔH

Combustion of methane:

CH4(g) + 2 O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2 H2O(l) + heat

Heat is produced, so the reaction is exothermic and ΔH < 0.

3. Calorimetry and Specific Heat

Basic Relationships

For a sample of mass m (g), specific heat c, and temperature change ΔT (K or °C):

q = m c ΔT

Coffee-Cup Calorimetry (Constant Pressure)

Used for reactions in aqueous solution at (approximately) constant pressure.

Assuming no heat loss to the outside surroundings:

q_reaction + q_solution = 0

For the solution, q_solution = m c ΔT, so:

q_reaction = -q_solution

At constant pressure, ΔH ≈ q_reaction.

Example (Solution Calorimetry)

A 100.0 g water solution (assume c ≈ 4.18 J g−1 K−1) warms from 25.0 °C to 32.0 °C due to a reaction.

  1. ΔT = 32.0 − 25.0 = 7.0 K.
  2. q_solution = (100.0 g)(4.18 J g−1 K−1)(7.0 K) ≈ 2926 J.
  3. q_reaction = -2926 J (the system released heat; exothermic).
  4. If the reaction involved 0.0500 mol of reactant: ΔH ≈ (-2.926 kJ)/(0.0500 mol) = -58.5 kJ mol−1.

Bomb Calorimetry (Constant Volume)

Used for combustion reactions at constant volume.

  • Volume is constant → no PV work, so ΔE = q_v.
  • The calorimeter has a heat capacity C_cal: q_cal = C_cal ΔT.
  • For the reaction: q_reaction = -q_cal.

4. Hess’s Law

Because enthalpy is a state function, the overall enthalpy change for a process is the sum of the enthalpy changes for any sequence of steps that leads from the same initial to the same final state.

Rules When Manipulating Equations

  • Reverse a reaction → change the sign of ΔH.
  • Multiply a reaction by a factor n → multiply ΔH by n.
  • Add reactions → add their ΔH values.

Conceptual Example

If you know:

  1. C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g)    ΔH1
  2. 2 H2(g) + O2(g) → 2 H2O(l)    ΔH2

and you want ΔH for:

CH4(g) + 2 O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2 H2O(l)

You can build CH4 from C and H2 using known intermediate equations and then sum the corresponding enthalpy changes.

5. Standard Enthalpies of Formation and ΔH°rxn

Standard State

The standard state of a substance is its most stable form at 1 bar (approximately 1 atm) and a specified temperature (usually 298 K).

Examples: O2(g), H2(g), N2(g), C (graphite).

Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔH°f)

  • ΔH for forming 1 mol of a compound from its elements in their standard states.
  • By convention, ΔH°f of any element in its standard state is zero.

Using ΔH°f to Find ΔH°rxn

For a reaction:

aA + bB → cC + dD

The standard enthalpy of reaction is:

ΔH°rxn = [Σ n ΔH°f(products)] − [Σ n ΔH°f(reactants)]

Symbolic Example

For the combustion of methane:

CH4(g) + 2 O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2 H2O(l)

ΔH°rxn = [ΔH°f(CO2) + 2 ΔH°f(H2O)] − [ΔH°f(CH4) + 2 ΔH°f(O2)]

Since ΔH°f(O2) = 0, that term drops out.

6. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

Try these problems. You can check your work with a teacher or tutor, or ask for detailed solutions.

  1. Conceptual – Sign Conventions
    (a) A reaction warms the solution: is q for the system positive or negative?
    (b) A gas expands against an external pressure: is w positive or negative?
  2. q, w, and ΔE
    A system absorbs 150 J of heat and does 75 J of work on the surroundings.
    (a) What are q and w (with signs)?
    (b) Calculate ΔE.
  3. Specific Heat
    A 50.0 g metal sample is heated from 20.0 °C to 45.0 °C, absorbing 520 J of heat.
    (a) Calculate its specific heat.
    (b) If the same metal (50.0 g) cools from 45.0 °C to 25.0 °C, how much heat is released?
  4. Coffee-Cup Calorimetry
    When 1.00 g of NaOH is dissolved in 100.0 g water (assume c = 4.18 J g−1 K−1), the temperature rises from 25.0 °C to 31.0 °C.
    (a) Calculate q_solution and q_reaction.
    (b) Find ΔH (kJ·mol−1) for the dissolution of NaOH (M ≈ 40.0 g·mol−1).
  5. Bomb Calorimetry
    A 0.800 g hydrocarbon sample is burned in a bomb calorimeter (assume C_cal = 5.50 kJ K−1), causing a temperature rise of 2.15 K.
    (a) Find q_reaction.
    (b) What is the energy released per gram of the sample?
  6. Hess’s Law
    Use these data to find ΔH for:
    C(graphite) + ½ O2(g) → CO(g)
    Given:
      - C(graphite) + O2(g) → CO2(g)    ΔH1
      - 2 CO(g) + O2(g) → 2 CO2(g)    ΔH2
  7. ΔH°rxn from ΔH°f
    Write the formula for ΔH°rxn in terms of ΔH°f values and apply it to any combustion reaction you choose (for example, C2H6 or C3H8).
