Chapter 31: Nitrogen-Containing Organic Compounds

Learning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to:

  • Recognize amines, amides, and related nitrogen-containing functional groups.
  • Describe basicity of amines qualitatively.
  • Relate nitrogen-containing groups to biomolecules (conceptually).

Key Vocabulary

  • Amine: derivative of ammonia (NH3) where one or more H are replaced by carbon groups.
  • Amide: carbonyl compound where –NH2, –NHR, or –NR2 is attached to C=O.
  • Heteroatom: atom in an organic molecule that is not carbon or hydrogen (for example, N, O, S, halogens).
  • Basicity: tendency to accept a proton; relates to availability of lone pair on nitrogen.

1. Structures and Classes

  • Amines: primary (1°), secondary (2°), tertiary (3°) depending on how many carbons are attached to N.
  • Amides: less basic than amines due to resonance delocalization of the nitrogen lone pair into the carbonyl.

2. Biological Connections (Conceptual)

  • Amino acids contain both amine and carboxylic acid groups.
  • Peptide bonds linking amino acids are amide linkages.

3. Practice Problems (Self-Check)

  1. Identify whether each is an amine or an amide: CH3NH2, CH3CONH2, (CH3)3N.
  2. Explain qualitatively why amides are much less basic than amines.
  3. Describe in words how an amide (peptide) bond connects two amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
