26.1 Structure of Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

Alkenes use sp² carbon atoms with trigonal planar geometry; ethene’s remaining p orbitals overlap sideways to create a π bond above and below the molecular plane, locking the molecule’s shape.

Alkynes have sp carbons (linear) with two perpendicular p orbitals; the triple bond comprises one σ and two π bonds, each with less orbital overlap than σ bonds and thus higher reactivity.

CnH2n for alkenes and CnH2n−2 for alkynes—each double bond removes two hydrogens, each triple removes four compared to saturated alkanes.

Bond order progression: single (154 pm, 350 kJ/mol) < double (134 pm, 620 kJ/mol) < triple (121 pm, 840 kJ/mol), showing stronger attraction for higher bond orders.

Ethene’s planar geometry arises because the sp² orbitals lie in a plane while the unhybridized p orbitals overlap perpendicularly; rotating around the double bond would break that π overlap.

26.2 Isomerism and Nomenclature

Names come from the longest chain containing the multiple bond; double/triple bonds get the lowest-numbered locant and change the suffix (-ene, -yne).

Cyclic alkenes share the general formula CnH2n−2, the same as acyclic alkynes with the same number of carbons.

Assign priorities to the two substituents on each double-bonded carbon using atomic numbers; then compare radical positions: same side=Z, opposite=E.

Pentene yields pent-1-ene plus cis/trans pent-2-ene; hept-2-ene with a methyl on C4 becomes Z-4-methylhept-2-ene when the higher-priority groups lie together.

26.3 Arrow Notation and Resonance

Curved arrows show electron pair movement: bonds to atoms (lone pair), atoms to bonds (π formation), or bond to bond (redistribution). Always retain electron count, obey octets, and ensure charges/paired electrons stay consistent.

The nucleophile (Nu⁻) donates electrons to the electrophile (E⁺); the resulting bond follows the equation Nu⁻ + E⁺ → Nu–E.

Nitromethane has two resonance forms; the electron pair shifts from one oxygen to the other, delocalizing the negative charge and equalizing N–O bond lengths.

Vision depends on breaking a π bond in retinal—light energy cleaves the π component, allowing rotation and triggering the visual response.

26.4 Electrophilic Addition Reactions

Alkenes/alkynes react with electrophiles to form carbocations, which nucleophiles then attack; the first step is rate-determining (rate = k[alkene][HBr]).

Markovnikov’s rule: the electrophile (often H⁺) adds to the carbon with more hydrogens so that the positive charge rests on the more substituted carbon.

C=C + E⁺ + Nu⁻ → C–E + C–Nu (Equation 26.25) lets you design halogenation, hydrohalogenation, hydration, or halohydrin formation through appropriate electrophile/nucleophile pairs.

HBr adds to 2-methylpropene to give 2-bromo-2-methylpropane (tertiary carbocation) and bromohydrin formation from propene + Br₂ + H₂O yields 1-bromo-2-propanol via a cyclic bromonium ion and anti addition.

26.5 Alkanes from Alkenes: Catalytic Hydrogenation

H₂ adds across multiple bonds on metal catalysts (Pt, Pd, Ni); the H–H bond breaks on the surface, and hydrogen atoms add syn to the π bond, saturating the alkene or alkyne.

C=C + H₂ → C–C (Equation 26.29) is exothermic (~137 kJ/mol) because the weaker π bond is replaced with new σ bonds.

Hydrogenating linoleic/oleic esters converts kinks into straight chains, turning oils into solids (margarine); hydrogenation of 1,2-dimethylcyclohexene gives cis-1,2-dimethylcyclohexane due to syn addition.

26.6 Addition Polymerization

Addition polymers form when radicals or other reactive centers sequentially add monomers; the repeat unit is repeated n times, yielding macromolecules such as polyethene.

n CH₂=CH₂ → [−CH₂−CH₂−]ₙ (Equation 26.35), where n may be hundreds of thousands.

  • Initiation: peroxide (e.g., benzoyl) forms radicals using fishhook arrows.
  • Propagation: each radical adds another monomer, regenerating the radical.
  • Termination: two radicals combine to end growth.

LDPE (branched) is low density and amorphous; HDPE (linear) is crystalline and used for pipes. Vulcanized rubber cross-links chains with sulfur, turning sticky latex into strong, elastic material.
