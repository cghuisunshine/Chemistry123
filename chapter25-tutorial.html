Chapter 25 Tutorial — Stereochemistry of Organic Compounds

25.1 Stereochemistry in Organic Chemistry

Key ideas

  • Stereochemistry describes three-dimensional arrangements of atoms, which influences physical properties and biological activity (e.g., limonene enantiomers smell like lemons vs. oranges).
  • Isomers have the same molecular formula but differ in connectivity (constitutional) or spatial arrangement (stereoisomers: geometric, enantiomers, diastereomers, meso).
  • Enantiomers are nonsuperimposable mirror images; they behave identically in achiral environments but differently in chiral settings.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Enantiomeric relationship: Mirror-image tetrahedral centers giving opposite arrangements (R vs. S) are enantiomers and rotate plane-polarized light in opposite directions.

Short example(s)

Limonene exists as two enantiomers: one interacts with receptors to smell like lemons, the other like oranges, showing the biological importance of stereochemistry.

25.2 Cis–Trans Isomerism in Cycloalkanes

Key ideas

  • Cycloalkanes adopt chair conformations with axial and equatorial hydrogens; steric strain is minimized when bulky substituents occupy equatorial positions.
  • Cis/trans stereochemistry arises when substituents are on the same (cis) or opposite (trans) faces of a ring or double bond.
  • 1,3-diaxial interactions destabilize axial substituents; methylcyclohexane prefers the equatorial methyl.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Cycloalkane formula: `CnH2n`. Compared to alkanes, ring closure removes two hydrogens.

Short example(s)

In cyclohexane, the chair form is ∼30 kJ/mol more stable than the boat; equatorial substituents avoid 1,3-diaxial clashes.

25.3 Chirality in Organic Compounds

Key ideas

  • Chiral stereocenters have four different groups; molecules with such centers exist as enantiomeric pairs with identical connectivity but opposite spatial arrangements.
  • Chiral molecules become enantiomerically distinct only in chiral environments (e.g., enzymes, polarized light).
  • Meso compounds contain stereocenters but have internal symmetry, making them achiral despite stereochemistry.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Stereoisomer count: Maximum `2ⁿ` for n stereocenters; meso symmetry can reduce the actual number because one configuration is identical to its mirror image.

Short example(s)

2,3-dibromobutane has three stereoisomers: a meso form (achiral) plus a pair of enantiomers.

25.4 Measuring Optical Activity

Key ideas

  • Plane-polarized light is rotated by chiral molecules: dextrorotatory (d, +) rotates right, levorotatory (l, −) rotates left; racemic mixtures show zero net rotation.
  • Specific rotation `[α]` normalizes observed rotation by path length and concentration: `[α] = α_obs/(l·c)`.
  • Enantiomeric excess (ee) measures deviation from racemic mixtures; ee = |(%R − %S)|.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Specific rotation: `[α] = α_obs/(l·c)` where α_obs is measured rotation (degrees), l is path length (dm), and c is concentration (g/mL).

Short example(s)

Racemic mixtures (50:50 enantiomers) have zero optical rotation because equal and opposite effects cancel.

25.5 Absolute Stereochemistry

Key ideas

  • R/S configuration uses priority rules (Cahn-Ingold-Prelog) to assign absolute stereochemistry: orient the lowest-priority group away, trace 1→2→3, and assign R (clockwise) or S (counterclockwise).
  • Enantiomers have opposite R/S assignments at each stereocenter.
  • Meso compounds can have stereocenters but are achiral due to internal symmetry.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • R/S assignment steps: (1) Rank substituents by atomic number, (2) orient lowest priority away, (3) determine direction of 1→2→3. Clockwise = R, counterclockwise = S.

Short example(s)

A chiral carbon with substituents ranked to give clockwise order is R; reversing two groups or flipping orientation swaps to S.

25.6 Molecules with More than One Stereocenter

Key ideas

  • Multiple stereocenters produce diastereomers (non-mirror-image stereoisomers) and enantiomers; meso symmetry can reduce the STEM count.
  • Diastereomers have distinct physical properties, making them easier to separate than enantiomers.
  • Molecules such as tartaric acid illustrate meso forms (achiral) plus enantiomeric pairs despite multiple stereocenters.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Maximum stereoisomers: `2ⁿ` for n stereocenters when no symmetry; actual number may be lower if meso forms exist.

Short example(s)

Glyceraldehyde has one stereocenter (R/S). Tartaric acid’s two centers give three stereoisomers: meso (achiral) + enantiomeric pair.
