Chapter 22 Tutorial — Chemistry of the Nonmetals

22.1 Periodic Trends and Chemical Reactions

Key ideas

  • Nonmetals gather the highest electronegativities and small radii in the upper-right of the periodic table, so they favor molecular bonding instead of ionic lattices.
  • The first element in each group (H, C, N, O) is small enough to form strong π bonds and stays limited to fewer bonded neighbors compared with heavier congeners.
  • Combustion and proton-transfer reactions dominate nonmetal chemistry because O–H, C=O, and N≡N bonds are thermodynamically very stable.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Combustion (Equation 22.1): 4 CH₃NHNH₂ + 9 O₂ → 4 CO₂ + 10 H₂O + 2 N₂. Bond enthalpies for O–H (463 kJ/mol), C=O (799 kJ/mol), and N≡N (941 kJ/mol) mean the products are energetically favored and the reaction releases a lot of heat.
  • Strong-base proton acceptance (Equation 22.2): CH₃⁻ + H₂O → CH₄ + OH⁻. A very weak acid like methane forms the strong base OH⁻ when it removes a proton from water.
  • Nitride protonation (Equation 22.3): N³⁻ + 3 H₂O → NH₃ + 3 OH⁻. Nitride gains protons instantly, generating ammonia and hydroxide ions due to its extreme basicity.

Short example(s)

Burning CH₃NHNH₂ gives CO₂, H₂O, and N₂, while Mg₃P₂ + H₂O yields PH₃ plus Mg(OH)₂, illustrating the textbook combustion and acid–base templates.

22.2 Hydrogen

Key ideas

  • Hydrogen is unique: it can behave like a proton donor (H⁺) or an electron acceptor (H⁻), yet it stands apart from any specific family.
  • Three isotopes exist—protium (¹H), deuterium (²H), and tritium (³H)—and their mass differences produce measurable kinetic isotope effects.
  • Hydrogen forms a strong H–H bond (1436 kJ/mol) and must be activated by heat, irradiation, or catalysts before it participates in reactions.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Tritium decay (Equation 22.4): ³H → ²He + ⁻₁e with t₁/₂ = 12.3 yr, meaning each tritium nucleus halves every 12.3 years and decays to helium-3 while emitting a beta particle.
  • Acid‑metal reduction (Equation 22.7): Zn + 2 H⁺ → Zn²⁺ + H₂. An active metal plus acid liberates hydrogen gas, which is the usual lab preparation of H₂.
  • Steam reforming (Equation 22.8): CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3 H₂ followed by the water-gas shift (22.9): CO + H₂O → CO₂ + H₂. Together they describe industrial manufacture where methane and steam make CO and H₂, then CO is turned into CO₂ with more hydrogen.
  • Reduction of oxides (Equation 22.6): CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O. Hydrogen removes oxygen from solid copper oxide, yielding the metallic element and water.

Short example(s)

Zn + 2 HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂ is the classroom version of Equation 22.7; acid strength controls how quickly hydrogen appears above the flask.

22.3 Group 18: The Noble Gases

Key ideas

  • Noble gases have filled valence shells, so they exhibit high ionization energies and minimal chemical reactivity.
  • Physical applications—lightning displays, inert shielding, cryogenics—use their noble-gas properties rather than chemistry.
  • Heavier noble gases, especially xenon, can expand their valence shells and form stable fluorides and oxides under forcing conditions.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Xenon hydrolysis (Equation 22.16): XeF₆ + 3 H₂O → XeO₃ + 6 HF. Water converts xenon fluoride to xenon trioxide plus hydrofluoric acid, showing how even “inert” atoms can enter covalent chemistry when matched with strong oxidizers.

Short example(s)

The VSEPR model predicts square-planar XeF₄ because six electron domains (four bonds plus two lone pairs) arrange octahedrally, with lone pairs opposite each other to minimize repulsion.

22.4 Group 17: The Halogens

Key ideas

  • Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) are strong oxidizing agents because of their high electronegativities and high reduction potentials.
  • A halogen can oxidize the halide ions below it in the group, which explains halogen displacement reactions and practical uses like disinfecting water.
  • They form hydrohalic acids, oxyacids, and interhalogen compounds, giving rise to materials from bleach to Teflon®.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Reduction potential (Equation 22.17): F₂ + 2 e⁻ → 2 F⁻ with E° = +1.80 V. A large positive potential signals that fluorine strongly attracts electrons, making it the harshest oxidant in the group.
  • Chlorine hydrolysis (Equation 22.18): Cl₂ + 2 OH⁻ → Cl⁻ + ClO⁻ + H₂O. Chlorine reacting with base yields chloride and hypochlorite, the bleaching species in household bleaches.
  • HF + silica (Equation 22.19): SiO₂ + 6 HF → H₂SiF₆ + 2 H₂O. Hydrofluoric acid attacks silicates because fluoride forms strong bonds with silicon and pulls oxygen into soluble fluoro complexes.

Short example(s)

2 I⁻ + Br₂ → I₂ + 2 Br⁻ shows bromine oxidizing iodide, producing the brown iodine color that identifies the reaction.

22.5 Oxygen

Key ideas

  • Oxygen is produced by liquefying air or via photosynthesis, and it is the most-used industrial oxidant (steel, welding, bleaching).
  • Ozone (O₃) is a powerful oxidizer and forms/disintegrates readily; in the stratosphere it shields UV, while near Earth it damages lungs.
  • Oxides can behave as acids (nonmetal oxides), bases (metal oxides), or both (amphoteric), and special O–O species (peroxides, superoxides) fuel breathing apparatus and bleaching reactions.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Photosynthesis (Equation 22.20): 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂. Plants convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen using sunlight; the oxygen is released back into the atmosphere.
  • Ozone dynamics (Equations 22.21–22.22): O₃ → O₂ + O and 3 O₂ → 2 O₃. Ozone decomposes into molecular oxygen and reactive atoms, and it reforms when oxygen is energized (e.g., lightning).
  • SO₂ hydrolysis (Equation 22.23): SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃. Sulfur dioxide dissolves in water to form sulfurous acid, central to acid rain chemistry.
  • Basic oxide hydration (Equation 22.24): O²⁻ + H₂O → 2 OH⁻. The oxide ion from ionic oxides quickly forms hydroxide ions in water, justifying the basic nature of metal oxides.
  • Superoxide hydration (Equation 22.26): 4 KO₂ + 2 H₂O → 4 K⁺ + 4 OH⁻ + 3 O₂. Moisture in respiration triggers this reaction and cleans CO₂ with KOH, making KO₂ ideal for breathing masks.
  • Hydrogen peroxide disproportionation (Equation 22.28): 2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂ (ΔH° = −196.1 kJ). One oxygen atom is reduced (−1 to −2) and another oxidized (−1 to 0), making H₂O₂ both an oxidizer and reducer.

Short example(s)

Oxyacetylene welding uses 2 C₂H₂ + 5 O₂ → 4 CO₂ + 2 H₂O to make flames hotter than 3000 °C for cutting and joining metals.

22.6 The Other Group 16 Elements: S, Se, Te, and Po

Key ideas

  • Other Group 16 elements have ns²np⁴ valence electrons and can reach −2 or positive oxidation states up to +6, thanks to expanded valence shells.
  • Sulfur is central to industry (sulfuric acid, vulcanized rubber) and agriculture but also causes pollution when burned.
  • Selenium and tellurium appear in electronics and pigments, while polonium is radioactive and rare.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Phosphate reduction (Equation 22.43): 2 Ca₃(PO₄)₂ + 6 SiO₂ + 10 C → P₄ + 6 CaSiO₃ + 10 CO. Carbon and silica reduce phosphate rock to elemental phosphorus; the slag and carbon monoxide are removed.
  • SO₃ hydration (Equations 22.29–22.30): SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ followed by H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2 H₂SO₄. Instead of directly hydrating SO₃, it dissolves in sulfuric acid to form pyrosulfuric acid, which is safer to handle before the final acid step.

Short example(s)

Rust removal uses Fe₂O₃ + 6 H⁺ → 2 Fe³⁺ + 3 H₂O, dissolving iron oxide with acid so the metal can be recoated.

22.7 Nitrogen

Key ideas

  • Nitrogen exists mainly as N₂ with a triple bond; its inertness means special processes (like Haber) are needed to “fix” it into reactive forms.
  • The element spans oxidation states from −3 to +5, enabling a wide variety of redox chemistry in fertilizers, explosives, and biological molecules.
  • Hydrogen-rich molecules like NH₃, N₂H₄, and NH₂OH are key industrial feedstocks and reducing agents.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Haber process (Equation 22.34): N₂ + 3 H₂ ⇌ 2 NH₃. High pressure and catalysts push this equilibrium toward ammonia, making it the backbone of fertilizer manufacture.
  • Ammonia release (Equation 22.33): NH₄Cl + NaOH → NH₃ + H₂O + NaCl. Base abstracts a proton from ammonium, yielding volatile ammonia gas.
  • Hydrazine synthesis (Equation 22.35): 2 NH₃ + OCl⁻ → N₂H₄ + Cl⁻ + H₂O. Hypochlorite converts ammonia into hydrazine, which serves as rocket fuel and corrosion inhibitor.

Short example(s)

On reacting hydroxylamine with Cu²⁺, you obtain Cu²⁺ + 2 NH₂OH → Cu + N₂ + 2 H₂O + 2 H⁺, a balanced redox equation featuring N₂ as the oxidation product.

22.8 The Other Group 15 Elements: P, As, Sb, and Bi

Key ideas

  • These elements have ns²np³ valence electrons and smoothly transition from nonmetal (P) to metallic (Bi).
  • Phosphorus forms highly reactive P₄ molecules (white phosphorus) that convert to less reactive red phosphorus upon heating.
  • Halides (PCl₃, PCl₅) and oxyacids dominate phosphorus utility—in detergents, plastics, and fertilizers.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Phosphate reduction (Equation 22.43): repeated for context—see earlier description.
  • Phosphorus chlorides (Equations 22.44–22.45): 2 P + 3 Cl₂ → 2 PCl₃ and PCl₃ + Cl₂ ⇌ PCl₅. Chlorine oxidizes phosphorus to +3, and excess Cl₂ pushes it to the pentachloride, demonstrating controlled oxidation.

Short example(s)

White phosphorus spontaneously combusts in air, while heating it to ~400 °C yields red phosphorus, showing how bonding and strain dictate allotrope stability.

22.9 Carbon

Key ideas

  • Carbon catenates to form countless organic molecules and allotropic solids (diamond, graphite, nanotubes) with contrasting properties.
  • CO and CO₂ arise in fuels and metallurgy, while carbonates, carbonic acid, and carbides highlight inorganic carbon’s versatility.
  • Carbon dioxide is mildly acidic in water, making it responsible for carbonated drinks, greenhouse warming, and limestone dissolution.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Carbon monoxide formation (Equation 22.52): 2 C + O₂ → 2 CO. Limited oxygen gives CO, which binds strongly to hemoglobin and is still used as a reducing agent.
  • CO oxidation (Equation 22.53): 2 CO + O₂ → 2 CO₂ (ΔH° = -566 kJ). CO burns to CO₂, releasing energy and eliminating toxicity.
  • Metal reduction (Equation 22.54): Fe₃O₄ + 4 CO → 3 Fe + 4 CO₂. CO reduces iron oxide to metal in a blast furnace.
  • Carbonate decomposition (Equation 22.56): CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. Heating limestone gives lime and carbon dioxide for cement.
  • Acid + carbonate (Equation 22.57): CO₃²⁻ + 2 H⁺ → CO₂ + H₂O. Acid neutralization releases ferric gas, a classic lab demonstration.
  • Carbide hydrolysis (Equation 22.65): CaC₂ + 2 H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + C₂H₂. Water liberates acetylene from calcium carbide, useful for welding.

Short example(s)

Adding vinegar (acetic acid) to sodium bicarbonate makes bubbles of CO₂ via NaHCO₃ + H⁺ → Na⁺ + CO₂ + H₂O, the same reaction that lifts baked goods.

22.10 The Other Group 14 Elements: Si, Ge, Sn, and Pb

Key ideas

  • Group 14 moves from nonmetal (C) to semimetal (Si, Ge) to metals (Sn, Pb), with bond strengths that steadily decline.
  • Silicon is abundant, supplies semiconductors after careful purification, and always bonds to oxygen to form silicates/glass.
  • Silicones (polymers with Si–O backbones and organic side groups) and glass formulations exploit wide chemical stability and flexibility.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Silicon reduction (Equation 22.66): SiO₂ + 2 C → Si + 2 CO. Carbon reduces silica to elemental silicon plus carbon monoxide, the basic metallurgical route.
  • Purified Si (Equation 22.67): SiCl₄ + 2 H₂ → Si + 4 HCl. This gas-phase reaction regenerates solid silicon after distilling the volatile tetrachloride.
  • Glass precursors (Equations 22.68–22.69): CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ and Na₂CO₃ → Na₂O + CO₂. Lime and soda lower silica’s melting point for mass-market glass production.

Short example(s)

Zone refining sweeps impurities toward one end of a silicon rod by moving a molten zone; the remaining solid is ultrapure (up to 11 nines!), suitable for chips.

22.11 Boron

Key ideas

  • Boron is the only nonmetal in Group 13, forming electron-deficient clusters called boranes and extended borate networks.
  • Diborane (B₂H₆) features bridging hydrogens because each boron has only six electrons, so the hydrogens share bonding pairs between two boron atoms.
  • Borates and borohydrides are used in lifestyle products (borax laundry soap), reducing agents (NaBH₄), and neutralizing washes (boric acid).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Diborane combustion (Equation 22.70): B₂H₆ + 3 O₂ → B₂O₃ + 3 H₂O (ΔH° = −2030 kJ). The electron-poor borane is oxidized to stable boric oxide and water, releasing tremendous energy.
  • Boric acid condensation (Equation 22.71): 4 H₃BO₃ → H₂B₄O₇ + 5 H₂O. Heat drives water off boric acid, forming tetraboric acid (borate) plus water—the same process forms borax from boric acid.

Short example(s)

Sodium borohydride contains the BH₄⁻ ion, a strong hydride donor that safely reduces aldehydes/ketones and metal ions in organic synthesis.
