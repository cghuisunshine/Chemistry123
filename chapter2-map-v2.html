2.1 The Atomic Theory of Matter
Dalton‚Äôs postulates and laws of chemical combination.
Roots: Atoms and the laws they explain.

Dalton‚Äôs atomic theory connects the observed behavior of elements and compounds to atoms:

  • Each element consists of extremely small particles called atoms.
  • All atoms of a given element are identical, but atoms of different elements differ in mass and other properties.
  • Atoms are not created or destroyed in chemical reactions; they are only rearranged. Atoms of one element are not changed into atoms of another by chemical reactions.
  • Compounds are formed when atoms of different elements combine; a given compound always contains the same relative numbers and kinds of atoms.

These ideas help explain key laws:

  • Law of constant composition: a given compound always has the same composition by mass because it always contains the same types and ratios of atoms.
  • Law of conservation of mass: total mass before and after a chemical reaction is the same because atoms are conserved.
  • Law of multiple proportions: if two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in simple whole-number ratios, reflecting different small whole-number ratios of atoms.

There are no algebraic formulas in this section, but Dalton‚Äôs postulates translate into important qualitative rules:

  • Because atoms are indivisible in chemical reactions, any balanced equation must conserve the number of each type of atom.
  • A compound like CO‚ÇÇ always has a 1:2 C:O atom ratio; you cannot have a ‚ÄúCO‚ÇÅ.‚ÇÖ‚Äù compound.
  • Different compounds of the same elements, such as CO and CO‚ÇÇ, arise from different small whole-number atom ratios (1:1 vs 1:2), producing simple whole-number mass ratios.

Example: multiple proportions for carbon and oxygen.

Suppose:

  • In CO, 1.0 g C combines with 1.33 g O.
  • In CO‚ÇÇ, 1.0 g C combines with 2.66 g O.

The ratio of the masses of O that combine with a fixed mass of C is 1.33 : 2.66 ‚âà 1 : 2. This is a small whole-number ratio, consistent with atom ratios of 1:1 and 1:2 for CO and CO‚ÇÇ.

Tip: Whenever you see mass ratios that are near a small whole-number ratio (like 1:2, 2:3, 3:4), suspect different atomic combinations consistent with Dalton‚Äôs theory.

2.2 The Discovery of Atomic Structure
Electrons, radioactivity, and the nuclear model of the atom.
Roots: How we learned atoms have parts.

Cathode rays and electrons. Cathode-ray tubes produce beams when high voltage is applied across a low-pressure gas. J. J. Thomson showed that these beams are deflected by electric and magnetic fields, and always bend as if they carry negative charge. He concluded that they consist of negatively charged particles, later called electrons, and measured their charge-to-mass ratio.

Millikan‚Äôs oil-drop experiment. Small oil drops were allowed to fall between charged plates. By adjusting the electric field to suspend a droplet, Millikan found the exact charge on the drop. He noticed the charge was always a multiple of a minimum value (‚âà 1.602 √ó 10‚Åª¬π‚Åπ C), which he identified as the charge of a single electron.

Radioactivity. Becquerel and the Curies found that uranium compounds emit radiation spontaneously. Rutherford classified three types of radiation:

  • Œ± (alpha): positively charged, relatively massive particles.
  • Œ≤ (beta): negatively charged, high-speed electrons.
  • Œ≥ (gamma): high-energy electromagnetic radiation, no charge.
The different bending of Œ± and Œ≤ rays in electric fields proved their charges, while Œ≥ rays were unaffected.

Rutherford‚Äôs gold foil experiment. When Œ±-particles were shot at thin gold foil, most passed straight through, but some were deflected at large angles or even backwards. This result could not be explained by Thomson‚Äôs ‚Äúplum-pudding‚Äù model.

Rutherford proposed the nuclear model:

  • Most of an atom‚Äôs mass and all its positive charge are concentrated in a tiny nucleus.
  • Electrons move in the surrounding space, which is mostly empty.
  • Most Œ±-particles pass through without hitting the small nucleus; a few that come close to a nucleus are strongly deflected.

Electron mass from e/m and charge.

me = e / (e/m)

Using Thomson‚Äôs charge-to-mass ratio and Millikan‚Äôs measured charge:

me = (1.602 √ó 10‚Åª¬π‚Åπ C) / (1.76 √ó 10‚Å∏ C¬∑g‚Åª¬π) ‚âà 9.10 √ó 10‚Åª¬≤‚Å∏ g

Meaning: the electron‚Äôs mass is tiny compared to atomic masses (~10‚Åª¬≤‚Å¥ g), so electrons contribute very little to the atom‚Äôs mass and cannot explain the atom‚Äôs density; the mass must reside in the nucleus.

Rutherford‚Äôs scattering data. If the atom were a uniform ‚Äúpudding‚Äù of positive charge, Œ±-particles would mostly pass through with only slight deflection. The observed large-angle scattering means:

  • The positive charge is concentrated in a very small region (nucleus).
  • The rest of the atom is largely empty space; hence most Œ±-particles pass through unaffected.

Tip: When reasoning from scattering experiments, always ask: what arrangement of charges and masses would produce the observed deflection pattern?

2.3 The Modern View of Atomic Structure
Protons, neutrons, electrons, atomic number, and isotopes.
Roots: What is inside an atom and how we label it.

Atoms contain three main subatomic particles:

  • Protons (p‚Å∫): charge +1 (in units of electron charge); mass ‚âà 1 amu.
  • Neutrons (n‚Å∞): charge 0; mass ‚âà 1 amu.
  • Electrons (e‚Åª): charge ‚àí1; mass ‚âà 1/1836 amu.

Nucleus: contains protons and neutrons; extremely small compared to the overall atom but holds nearly all the mass. The surrounding electron cloud determines the atom‚Äôs size and chemical behavior.

Atomic number (Z): the number of protons in the nucleus. This identifies the element. In a neutral atom, Z also equals the number of electrons.

Mass number (A): the total number of protons and neutrons (A = Z + N).

Isotopes: atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A). They have the same chemical behavior but different masses and often different nuclear stability.

Notation: an atom is written as ^A_ZX, where X is the element symbol, Z is atomic number (subscript), and A is mass number (superscript). Often Z is omitted if it is known from the symbol.

Mass number and subatomic particle counts:

A = Z + N

For a neutral atom: number of electrons = Z.

Atomic mass unit (u):

1 u = 1.66054 √ó 10‚Åª¬≤‚Å¥ g

By definition, a ¬π¬≤C atom has a mass of exactly 12 u, and other atomic masses are measured relative to this.

Example 1: ¬π‚Åπ‚Å∑Au.

  • Gold (Au) has atomic number Z = 79 ‚Üí 79 protons.
  • Mass number A = 197 ‚Üí N = 197 ‚àí 79 = 118 neutrons.
  • Neutral atom ‚Üí 79 electrons.

Example 2: strontium-90.

  • Strontium (Sr) has Z = 38.
  • A = 90 ‚Üí N = 90 ‚àí 38 = 52.
  • Neutral Sr-90 has 38 electrons.

Tip: When you see a notation like ‚Äúoxygen-18‚Äù, the number 18 is its mass number A. Use the periodic table to find Z (for O, Z = 8), then compute neutrons: N = 18 ‚àí 8 = 10.

2.4 Atomic Weights
Atomic mass unit, isotopes, and average atomic masses.
Trunk: From isotope masses to the periodic table numbers.

Atoms of different elements have different masses. Rather than listing a separate mass for every isotope, the periodic table gives a single **atomic weight** for each element, which is a weighted average of isotope masses according to their natural abundances.

The **atomic mass unit (u)** is defined so that an unbound ¬π¬≤C atom has exactly 12 u. Other atomic masses are measured relative to this standard.

Most elements occur naturally as mixtures of isotopes, so atomic weights are rarely whole numbers.

Atomic mass unit to grams:

1 u = 1.66054 √ó 10‚Åª¬≤‚Å¥ g

Atomic weight from isotope data:

[2.1] Atomic weight = Œ£ (isotope mass √ó fractional abundance)

Where each isotope‚Äôs percentage abundance is converted to a fraction (e.g., 75.78% ‚Üí 0.7578) before multiplication.

Example: atomic weight of chlorine.

Naturally occurring Cl: 75.78% ¬≥‚ÅµCl (34.969 u) and 24.22% ¬≥‚Å∑Cl (36.966 u).

AW(Cl) = (0.7578 √ó 34.969) + (0.2422 √ó 36.966) ‚âà 35.45 u

This value lies between the two isotope masses and is closer to 34.969 u, as expected because ¬≥‚ÅµCl is more abundant.

Tip: If the isotopic percentages are 99% of one isotope and 1% of another, the atomic weight will be very close to the mass of the major isotope.

2.5 The Periodic Table
Arranging elements by atomic number and grouping similar behaviors.
Roots: Plan of the elements.

The periodic table arranges elements in order of increasing atomic number, placing elements with similar properties in the same vertical columns.

Periods are horizontal rows:

  • Period 1: H and He.
  • Periods 2‚Äì3: 8 elements each.
  • Periods 4‚Äì5: 18 elements each.
  • Periods 6‚Äì7: 32 elements each (with lanthanides and actinides often shown separately).

Groups are vertical columns, numbered 1‚Äì18. Elements in a group share similar chemical and physical properties.

Some groups have common names:

  • Group 1: alkali metals.
  • Group 2: alkaline earth metals.
  • Group 17: halogens.
  • Group 18: noble gases.
  • Group 11: coinage metals (Cu, Ag, Au).

Elements to the left and center are mostly metals (lustrous, good conductors, usually solid). Elements to the upper right are nonmetals. A zig-zag line separates them, with metalloids along the border showing intermediate properties.

Again, no algebraic formula, but important patterns:

  • Elements in the same group have similar valence electron configurations and similar chemistry.
  • Moving across a period (left to right), properties gradually change from metallic to nonmetallic.
  • Group location helps predict typical ion charges (Group 1 ‚Üí +1, Group 2 ‚Üí +2, Group 17 ‚Üí ‚àí1, etc.).

Example: reactive metals vs noble gases.

  • Li, Na, K (Group 1): soft, very reactive metals, especially with water; form +1 cations.
  • He, Ne, Ar (Group 18): colorless, nonreactive gases; rarely form compounds under normal conditions.

Tip: When you see an unfamiliar element, locate it in the periodic table. Its group tells you how it will likely behave (metal vs nonmetal, likely ion charges, relative reactivity).

2.6 Molecules and Molecular Compounds
Molecules, molecular vs empirical formulas, and structural representations.
Roots: How we write and visualize molecules.

A molecule is a combination of two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds. Examples include H‚ÇÇO, CO‚ÇÇ, NH‚ÇÉ, CH‚ÇÑ.

Molecular formula: gives the actual number of each type of atom in a molecule (e.g., C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÜO).

Empirical formula: gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound (e.g., for C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÜO the empirical formula is C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÜO itself; for C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ it is CH‚ÇÇO).

Structural formula: shows the connectivity of atoms (who is bonded to whom). They do not usually show true 3D shape or bond angles.

3D models: perspective drawings, ball-and-stick models, and space-filling models make it easier to visualize geometry and relative sizes.

Key relationships:

  • If all subscripts in a molecular formula share a common factor, dividing by that factor yields the empirical formula.
  • Structural formulas and 3D models highlight that compounds with the same molecular formula can differ in connectivity (isomers).

There is no single algebraic formula here, but the link from molecular formula ‚Üí empirical formula ‚Üí structural formula is central to understanding composition and structure.

Example: lactic acid. Molecular formula C‚ÇÉH‚ÇÜO‚ÇÉ.

  • Divide all subscripts by 3 ‚Üí CH‚ÇÇO, the empirical formula.

Example: n-butane vs isobutane.

  • Both have molecular formula C‚ÇÑH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄ.
  • n-Butane: straight chain of 4 carbons.
  • Isobutane: 3-carbon chain with one carbon branching off the middle.
  • Same molecular formula, different structures ‚Üí structural isomers.

Tip: When given only a molecular formula, you often cannot tell how atoms are connected. Additional information (like structural formula, 3D model, or context) is needed to distinguish isomers.

2.7 Ions and Ionic Compounds
How ions form and how we write ionic compound formulas.
Roots: Electrons lost or gained, and neutral salts.

An ion is formed when electrons are added to or removed from a neutral atom:

  • Cation: positively charged ion formed when an atom loses one or more electrons.
  • Anion: negatively charged ion formed when an atom gains one or more electrons.

In general, metals form cations and nonmetals form anions. For example:

  • Na atom (11 p, 11 e) ‚Üí Na‚Å∫ (11 p, 10 e) by losing an electron.
  • Cl atom (17 p, 17 e) ‚Üí Cl‚Åª (17 p, 18 e) by gaining an electron.

Polyatomic ions are groups of atoms bonded together that carry a net charge (e.g., NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫, SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª).

An ionic compound is composed of cations and anions. Although it contains charged particles, the overall compound is electrically neutral (total positive charge = total negative charge).

Neutral ionic compound rule: The sum of all positive and negative charges must be zero.

  • Na‚Å∫ and Cl‚Åª ‚Üí NaCl (1(+1) + 1(‚àí1) = 0).
  • Ca¬≤‚Å∫ and Cl‚Åª ‚Üí CaCl‚ÇÇ (1(+2) + 2(‚àí1) = 0).
  • Al¬≥‚Å∫ and O¬≤‚Åª ‚Üí Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ (2(+3) + 3(‚àí2) = 0).

Predicting common charges from the periodic table:

  • Group 2 metals ‚Üí +2.
  • Group 17 nonmetals ‚Üí ‚àí1.
  • Group 16 nonmetals ‚Üí ‚àí2.

  • 16 p, 16 n, 18 e: Z = 16 ‚Üí S; A = 32; charge = 16 ‚àí 18 = ‚àí2 ‚Üí ¬≥¬≤S¬≤‚Åª.

Tip: When building ionic formulas, write the ions with their charges, then ‚Äúcross‚Äù charges to become subscripts (simplified to the lowest ratio). Always check that total charge sums to zero.

2.8 Naming Inorganic Compounds
Systematic names for ionic compounds, acids, and binary molecular compounds.
Trunk: Names that reflect composition.

Chemical nomenclature is a set of rules used to name chemical substances so that their names convey their composition.

We focus on three categories of inorganic compounds:

  • Ionic compounds: usually metal cations + nonmetal or polyatomic anions.
  • Acids: hydrogen-containing compounds that yield H‚Å∫ in water.
  • Binary molecular (covalent) compounds: combinations of nonmetals.

Organic compounds (those containing carbon and hydrogen) are treated separately in Section 2.9.

Ionic compounds:

  • Cation first, anion second.
  • Monatomic cations (single metal ions): use the metal‚Äôs name (Na‚Å∫ = sodium ion, Ca¬≤‚Å∫ = calcium ion).
  • Metal cations with multiple possible charges: use Roman numerals in parentheses (Fe¬≤‚Å∫ = iron(II), Fe¬≥‚Å∫ = iron(III); Cu‚Å∫ = copper(I); Cu¬≤‚Å∫ = copper(II)).
  • Monatomic anions: root of element + ‚Äú-ide‚Äù (Cl‚Åª: chloride, O¬≤‚Åª: oxide).
  • Polyatomic ions use specific names (NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª: nitrate, SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª: sulfate, NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫: ammonium).
  • Examples: NaCl = sodium chloride; FeCl‚ÇÉ = iron(III) chloride; CaSO‚ÇÑ = calcium sulfate.

Acids:

  • Acid = H‚Å∫ plus an anion.
  • If the anion ends in -ide, acid name = ‚Äúhydro- [root] -ic acid‚Äù (Cl‚Åª ‚Üí HCl = hydrochloric acid).
  • If the anion ends in -ate, acid name = ‚Äú[root] -ic acid‚Äù (NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª ‚Üí HNO‚ÇÉ = nitric acid; SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª ‚Üí H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ = sulfuric acid).
  • If the anion ends in -ite, acid name = ‚Äú[root] -ous acid‚Äù (SO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª ‚Üí H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÉ = sulfurous acid).

Binary molecular compounds (nonmetals only):

  • The more ‚Äúmetal-like‚Äù element (further left, or lower in the same group) is named first.
  • The second element is named as an anion (root + ‚Äú-ide‚Äù).
  • Use Greek prefixes (mono-, di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa-, hepta-, octa-, nona-, deca-) to indicate the number of atoms. Do not use ‚Äúmono-‚Äù for the first element.
  • Drop the final ‚Äúa‚Äù or ‚Äúo‚Äù of a prefix if the element name begins with a vowel (dinitrogen tetroxide, not ‚Äútetraoxide‚Äù).

Ionic examples:

  • Li‚ÇÇO: Li‚Å∫ + O¬≤‚Åª ‚Üí lithium oxide.

  • Na‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ: Na‚Å∫ + SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª ‚Üí sodium sulfate.

  • HNO‚ÇÉ: NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª is nitrate ‚Üí nitric acid.
  • H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ: SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª is sulfate ‚Üí sulfuric acid.
  • H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÉ: SO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª is sulfite ‚Üí sulfurous acid.

Binary molecular examples:

Tip: To decide if a given formula is likely ionic or molecular, look at the elements: metal + nonmetal (ionic) vs nonmetal + nonmetal (molecular). Then apply the appropriate naming rules.

2.9 Some Simple Organic Compounds

Organic compounds are carbon-containing compounds, typically with C‚ÄìH bonds and often containing O, N, or other elements.

Hydrocarbons contain only C and H. The simplest class is the alkanes, where each carbon has four single bonds (saturated hydrocarbons).

C‚ÇôH‚ÇÇ‚Çô‚Çä‚ÇÇ

This pattern applies to saturated, single-bonded hydrocarbons in the simplest series.

Alcohol naming: replace the -ane of the corresponding alkane with -ol.

Example: n-butane vs isobutane.
